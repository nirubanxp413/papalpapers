# Pius VI’s *Cum nos superiori* (1798): Emergency Papal Constitutionalism in an Age of Revolution

Issued from a Carthusian monastery near Florence on 13 November 1798, while Pope Pius VI lived in forced exile after the French occupation of Rome, the bull *Cum nos superiori* stands as a remarkable exercise in papal crisis management at the intersection of canon law, international politics, and revolutionary warfare.[3][14][18] Written against the backdrop of the Roman Republic’s suppression of religious houses, the dispersal and imprisonment of cardinals, and the disintegration of the Papal States under French pressure, the document suspends and rewrites long‑standing conclave legislation in order to guarantee that, whatever might befall the aging pope, the College of Cardinals could still elect his successor.[3][9][11] It is intensely reflective in its detailed catalogue of institutional injuries inflicted upon the Church during the revolutionary decade, yet even more strongly prospective in its juridical engineering of a future papal election likely to occur outside Rome, under foreign protection, and with only a fraction of the college present.[2][11] This report situates *Cum nos superiori* within the military and diplomatic crises of 1796–1799, analyzes its content and tone in relation to earlier conclave law, reconstructs how it was implemented at the 1799–1800 conclave in Venice, and evaluates its reflective and prescriptive gradients: as a deeply present‑oriented response to revolutionary persecution and as a short‑horizon, but highly saturated, constitutional intervention designed to carry the papal office through a moment when its temporal foundations seemed to be collapsing.

## Structured

### Document metadata and genre

*Cum nos superiori* (often cited in Latin as *Quum nos, superiore anno*, and in Italian as *Bolla Cum Nos superiori*) is a papal bull promulgated by Pope Pius VI on 13 November 1798, from the Charterhouse (Certosa) of San Casciano near Florence.[11][14][18] It explicitly recalls and modifies a prior bull, *Christi Ecclesiae*, which Pius VI had issued on 30 December 1797 in Rome as a first emergency adjustment to conclave rules in view of impending dangers.[5][12][14] Both bulls belong to the highly formal genre of papal constitutions governing papal elections, standing in a line that includes Gregory X’s *Ubi periculum* (1274) and later constitutions of Clement V, Clement VI, Urban VIII, Pius IV, Gregory XV, and Clement XII, all of which are cited or referenced in *Cum nos superiori* as legal precedents being derogated in part.[2][11] The bull is thus not a doctrinal encyclical in the modern sense, but a piece of constitutional legislation that rewrites the “organic law” of the papacy at a time when the pope’s own physical safety and temporal sovereignty were under direct attack.[2][11]

The primary text survives in Latin and Italian in the Vatican archives and is accessible via the Vatican website and papalencyclicals.net, with critical commentary found in canon law and conclave histories.[1][11][14] The bull was issued under the Fisherman’s Ring, the formal sign of papal authority, and was meant to be read at the opening of the next conclave to confirm its provisions.[11] In historiography, it is routinely treated as the legal basis that made possible the conclave of 1799–1800 in Venice, held far from Rome under Austrian protection after Pius VI’s death in French captivity at Valence on 29 August 1799.[3][6][11][15] The genre and formal features of the bull underscore its institutional purpose: it is not a meditation on persecution or a sermon about revolution, but a manual for how to elect a pope when revolution and persecution have destroyed the conditions presupposed by earlier conclave law.

### Central argument and purpose

The core argument of *Cum nos superiori* is that the unprecedented persecution and dispersion of the papal court in 1797–1798 have rendered earlier conclave constitutions inadequate, and that the salvation of the Church requires derogating certain procedural rules to ensure that the College of Cardinals can elect a successor even under conditions of exile, coercion, and incomplete attendance.[11][14] Pius VI argues that when he issued *Christi Ecclesiae* in December 1797, he anticipated grave dangers, but events since then have confirmed and exceeded his fears: Rome has been occupied, the patrimony of St Peter plundered, religious houses dissolved, cardinals expelled or imprisoned, and he himself driven from the See and held under effective arrest in Tuscany.[3][9][11][14][16] These concrete injuries, presented as facts rather than abstract possibilities, constitute the reflective groundwork for his prospective legislative intervention.

From this diagnosis, the bull proceeds to legislate. It allows the cardinals, after the pope’s death, to gather not necessarily in Rome but in any city of a Catholic prince where the greatest number of free cardinals can assemble, thereby abandoning the traditional requirement of election in the Papal States.[2][11][14] It declares that the election will be valid with a two‑thirds majority of the cardinals actually present, even if many are absent due to imprisonment, illness, distance, or secular interference.[11] It permits the cardinals, for this emergency, to dispense with many of the ceremonial and procedural prescriptions of earlier constitutions if observing them would be impossible or dangerous, while still insisting on key elements such as secret ballot and freedom from secular compulsion.[2][11] Most strikingly, it suspends the ancient prohibition, enforced by Paul II’s constitution *Cum secundum*, against cardinals discussing the choice of a future pope while the reigning pope is still alive; instead, Pius VI actively authorizes them to confer in advance about how and where to hold the next conclave so that no time will be lost when death comes.[2][11][14]

### Key reflective elements

The bull’s reflective character centres on its description of the Church’s condition in the late 1790s and its implicit interpretation of revolutionary violence against ecclesiastical institutions. Pius VI recounts that cardinals have been forced out of Rome, imprisoned, or deported; that monasteries and convents have been suppressed; that the ecclesiastical patrimony has been confiscated; and that priestly immunity has been violated by secular authorities.[14][16] These statements correspond to verifiable events in the Roman Republic established under French auspices in February 1798, which suppressed numerous religious houses, confiscated church property, and treated clergy as ordinary citizens liable to civil jurisdiction.[3][16] The bull also reflects on the pope’s own exile, noting that he has been torn from Rome and is now living under the surveillance of a secular power, unable to exercise his temporal sovereignty.[3][4][14]

In its legal commentary, the bull reflects backward on centuries of conclave regulation, invoking previous papal constitutions by name and arguing that they were crafted for times of peace and ecclesial liberty, not for an era when the very freedom of the electors is imperilled.[2][11][14] This retrospective survey does not become a full historical narrative, but it situates the current legislative act within a tradition, presenting Pius VI as both heir and necessary reformer of conclave law. The reflective saturation is high, because the document grounds its prescriptions in detailed references to the immediate past fifteen to twenty months and in a sober catalogue of injuries to the Church’s institutional life. However, its temporal density is shallow: the gaze is focused on very recent events—the French campaigns in Italy, the Treaty of Tolentino (February 1797), and the Roman Republic (1798)—rather than on deeper longue durée narratives.[3][9][16]

### Key prospective and prescriptive elements

The prospective character of *Cum nos superiori* is even more pronounced, as the bulk of the document is devoted to authorizing future actions by the College of Cardinals in a hypothetical but clearly anticipated scenario: the pope’s death in exile, with the papal court scattered and hostile powers in control of Rome. Pius VI prescribes that, upon his death, the cardinal dean or senior cardinal present must notify the others by the quickest means available, and then all free cardinals are to assemble in the specified refuge city under the protection of a Catholic prince.[11][14] He prescribes a waiting period of ten days after the arrival of the majority of cardinals before the formal opening of the conclave, following earlier custom but allowing discretion if circumstances demand.[11] He orders that, at the first session, *Cum nos superiori* be read aloud to ensure that all electors are aware of the derogations and that no one can contest the validity of their proceedings on procedural grounds.[11]

The bull’s most innovative prescriptions concern the legal validity of elections conducted under duress. Pius VI declares that secular interference, occupation of Rome, or the inability of many cardinals to attend does not invalidate an election, provided that the cardinals present are free in conscience, deliberate without direct coercion, and reach the usual two‑thirds majority.[11] He warns against the danger of rival elections if cardinals in different places attempt to act independently and instructs them to act as one body wherever the greatest number and freedom can be achieved, thus aiming to pre‑empt a schism.[11][14] In the final paragraphs, he issues a strong exhortation that, when the time comes, the cardinals should set aside personal ambition, act swiftly, and choose a successor who can guide the “barque of Peter” through storms and protect the flock from “wolves,” imagery that casts the future pope as a necessary bulwark against ongoing revolutionary and anti‑clerical threats.[14]

These prescriptions proved immediately consequential. When Pius VI died in French captivity in Valence on 29 August 1799, the College of Cardinals, scattered across Europe, invoked *Cum nos superiori* as legal authorization to meet in Venice, then under Austrian control, rather than in occupied Rome.[3][6][11][15] The conclave opened on 30 November 1799 in the Benedictine monastery of San Giorgio Maggiore and, after a long and politically fraught process, elected Cardinal Barnaba (Gregorio) Chiaramonti as Pope Pius VII on 14 March 1800, in a manner that contemporaries and later canonists regarded as valid precisely because it followed the emergency procedures authorized by Pius VI.[6][11][15] The bull’s prospective saturation is therefore extremely high: it does not merely imagine a possible future but effectively designs the canonical script that the Church would follow within a year of its promulgation.

### Gradient scores and their rationale

On the reflective axis, *Cum nos superiori* merits a saturation score of about 8 out of 10 because a substantial portion of the text is devoted to recounting and interpreting recent events that have harmed the Church’s institutional life, especially in Rome and the Papal States.[14][16] The bull names concrete injuries, ties them explicitly to earlier fears expressed in *Christi Ecclesiae*, and presents them as proof that the pope was not reacting to imaginary dangers but to a genuine, ongoing persecution.[5][14] The density on this axis is modestly negative, around −2, because the backward gaze rarely extends more than one or two years into the past, focusing on 1797–1798 and only glancingly on the earlier decades of Pius VI’s long pontificate or on deeper church history.[3][9][14]

On the prospective axis, saturation is even higher, plausibly 9 out of 10, because the explicit purpose of the document is to shape future behaviour: it legislates for the next conclave, relaxes previously binding prohibitions, and directly addresses the cardinals’ future responsibilities up to the point of exhorting them about the spiritual and political qualities they should seek in the next pope.[11][14] The density of its forward gaze is short‑ to medium‑term, estimated here at about +5 years, because the bull’s normative horizon is essentially the period between its promulgation and the successful election and installation of a successor, a span that in fact turned out to be less than two years but could reasonably have been imagined to stretch somewhat longer under conditions of war and occupation.[3][6][11] Unlike later social encyclicals that speculate about long‑term economic or ideological trends, *Cum nos superiori* is tightly focused on one looming institutional event, and its temporal horizon is bounded by the politics of the revolutionary decade.

## Unstructured

### Pius VI and the revolutionary decade: a fragile sovereignty

To understand the world that *Cum nos superiori* both reflects and attempts to shape, it is necessary to situate Pius VI’s pontificate within the broader upheavals of the French Revolutionary and Napoleonic era. Born Count Giovanni Angelo Braschi in 1717, Pius VI was elected pope in 1775 and reigned until his death in 1799, overseeing a Papal State that still functioned as a temporal principality in central Italy.[3] For much of his pontificate, he confronted Enlightenment‑inspired reforms in Catholic monarchies, Jansenist controversies, and tensions over the suppression of the Jesuits, but the decisive rupture came with the French Revolution and its export to Italy through military conquest.[3][9]

Revolutionary France’s attitude toward the Catholic Church oscillated between attempts to nationalize and subordinate it, as in the Civil Constitution of the Clergy (1790), and outright persecution during the Terror, when thousands of clergy were exiled or executed.[3] By the mid‑1790s, the Directory had adopted a more pragmatic approach, but hostility to the papacy as a symbol of the ancien régime and as a landholding temporal power remained strong.[3][4] When French armies under General Bonaparte invaded northern Italy in 1796, the Papal States became a key bargaining chip in French diplomacy, both as a source of financial indemnities and as an ideological target whose humiliation could demonstrate the triumph of revolutionary principles.[9]

The Treaty of Tolentino, signed on 19 February 1797, marked a turning point.[9] It imposed on the Papal States not only large territorial losses—ceding Avignon, the Comtat Venaissin, Ferrara, Bologna, and the Romagna to France or its client republics—but also a massive financial indemnity of 36 million livres and the transfer of significant artistic and cultural treasures to France.[9] Historians have widely interpreted the treaty as a forced capitulation that stripped the Papal States of strategic depth and economic resources, leaving them vulnerable to further encroachments.[9] For Pius VI, it was a humiliating but perhaps survivable blow, one that he accepted to preserve his spiritual authority and residual temporal sovereignty.

It was in the wake of Tolentino’s devastations that Pius VI issued *Christi Ecclesiae* in December 1797, already contemplating the possibility that his death might occur in a context of political turmoil or exile.[5][12][14] The fears expressed there were quickly vindicated. In early 1798, after an incident involving the killing of a French general in Rome, French troops under General Berthier marched into the city, declared the deposition of Pius VI’s temporal power, and on 15 February 1798 proclaimed the Roman Republic.[3][4][16] The pope was ordered to renounce his temporal sovereignty, and when he refused, he was taken prisoner and eventually transported north, first to Siena, then to the Certosa near Florence, and later across the Alps into France.[3][4][18]

The Roman Republic instituted sweeping secularizing reforms. It suppressed numerous monasteries and convents, nationalized church property, and reorganized ecclesiastical structures according to revolutionary models.[16] One Italian study of “suppressions” and cultural heritage notes that an 11 May 1798 proclamation suppressed thirty‑one conventual buildings in Rome alone, a striking symbol of the regime’s determination to dismantle the old ecclesiastical landscape.[16] These measures mirrored, and in some ways radicalized, earlier secularizing policies in France and other revolutionary republics. For the clergy and religious of Rome, they meant the loss of property, community, and legal immunities, and for the papal court, they meant dispersion, as cardinals fled or were expelled, arrested, or placed under surveillance.[3][16][17]

By the time Pius VI arrived at the Certosa of San Casciano, he was an elderly, physically frail prisoner of a hostile power, deprived of his capital, his temporal state, and the normal apparatus of papal governance.[3][18] It was from this position of weakness that he issued a series of documents addressed to the Church at large and to the cardinals in particular, including the breve *Constantiam vestram* of 10 November 1798, which encouraged the faithful to persevere amid persecution, and three days later, the bull *Cum nos superiori*, which addressed the practical problem of papal succession.[14][18] The juxtaposition is telling: one document consoles and exhorts; the other legislates in a coldly legal register. This dual response reflects a papacy that, even while stripped of worldly power, sought to exercise its spiritual and juridical authority in the face of revolutionary disruption.

### The making and content of *Cum nos superiori*

The text of *Cum nos superiori* opens with a reference to the earlier bull *Christi Ecclesiae*, issued “last year” when Pius VI, still in Rome, had already feared that extraordinary measures would be needed to safeguard the papal succession.[5][14] He recalls that in that document he had taken certain steps to facilitate a future conclave in case of his death, relaxing some procedural rules of earlier constitutions. However, he immediately notes that the dangers imagined then have not only come to pass but have been surpassed by events, prompting him to review and extend his earlier provisions.[14] This rhetorical move establishes continuity with, and yet superiority over, the previous bull, portraying *Cum nos superiori* as a second, more drastic stage in emergency legislation.

The bull then embarks on a sobering catalogue of harms. Pius VI laments that the cardinals have been constrained in various ways: some are in exile, others in prison, others robbed of their revenues; the “Patrimony of Peter” has been despoiled; religious orders have been suppressed; monasteries have been profaned for secular uses; and priests have been subjected to secular tribunals, stripped of traditional immunities.[14][16] The language is formal but vivid. It is not a generalized complaint about “persecution,” but a specific enumeration of institutional injuries, each of which has implications for the normal functioning of the papal monarchy. If cardinals are scattered and impoverished, they cannot easily gather in Rome; if monasteries and palaces are occupied or destroyed, there may be no suitable venue for a conclave; if secular authorities claim jurisdiction over clergy, they may seek to control the election itself.

On this basis, the bull proceeds to its legislative core. It declares that, because of these grave obstacles, the Church’s welfare demands a new constitution that will apply in the present crisis.[11][14] Pius VI states that he is acting “motu proprio,” of his own initiative, in the fullness of apostolic power, and that he intends to derogate from, suspend, or modify certain provisions of previous conclave constitutions while leaving their fundamental principles intact.[2][11] He reaffirms the importance of a secret and free election by the cardinals, but he insists that the place, forms, and certain ceremonial elements must be adapted to the circumstances.

One of the most significant provisions concerns the place of election. Earlier conclave law strongly presupposed, if not strictly required, that the cardinals would meet in Rome, usually in the Apostolic Palace or another major Roman site, with the Papal States providing the secure environment for their deliberations.[2][11] *Cum nos superiori* declares that, in the current situation, the cardinals are authorized to meet in any city under the control of a Catholic prince where the largest number of free cardinals can be assembled.[11][14] This clause reflects a realistic assessment of the options available in 1798–1799. Rome was under the control of a revolutionary republic allied to France; the Papal States no longer existed as an independent bloc; and only foreign Catholic powers—Austria, Spain, Naples, Portugal—could offer a secure haven. By making the “territory of a Catholic prince” the key criterion, the bull effectively internationalized the papal election process, acknowledging that the papacy might have to seek refuge under the protection of monarchs whose own policies Pius VI had often criticized in earlier years.

Another crucial provision concerns the quorum and validity of the election. Earlier constitutions assumed that, barring illness or other obstacles, most cardinals would attend the conclave, and they were often designed to ensure that all had time to travel to Rome.[2][11] *Cum nos superiori* notes that, under present persecutions, many cardinals may be physically prevented from travelling or may find their journey impeded by war, and thus it declares that the election will be valid with a two‑thirds majority of those actually present, whatever their number, provided that they are free in conscience and not under direct coercion.[11] This reinforces the principle that the essence of validity lies in the free suffrage of the College, not in the presence of a predetermined number or the observance of certain ceremonies. The clause also attempts to forestall future challenges to the legitimacy of the elected pope on procedural grounds, a real concern in an era when secular rulers had previously attempted to influence papal elections through vetoes and diplomatic pressure.[2][11]

Perhaps the most audacious innovation is the explicit suspension of penalties forbidding cardinals to discuss the future papal election while the reigning pope is still alive. Since the fifteenth century, the constitution *Cum secundum* of Paul II had imposed censures on cardinals who plotted or even discussed papal succession before the see fell vacant, in order to prevent factional maneuvering and simony.[2] Pius VI, acknowledging that these rules were appropriate in normal times, nevertheless decrees in *Cum nos superiori* that, given the current crisis, cardinals are not only permitted but encouraged to confer about the practical arrangements for the next conclave while he still lives, without incurring any canonical penalties.[11][14] He justifies this startling relaxation of discipline by appeal to necessity: the Church’s welfare requires that they settle questions of place, procedure, and logistics in advance, so that the election may take place quickly after his death, minimizing the danger of a prolonged vacancy or schism.

The bull also contains detailed provisions about communications, the reading of the bull in conclave, and the preservation of its text in authentic copies, including translations, in case the original is lost or inaccessible.[11][14] These clauses reflect an awareness that physical documents, like people, may be seized, destroyed, or prevented from circulating under conditions of war and censorship. By validating authenticated copies and translations, Pius VI attempts to ensure that his constitution cannot easily be suppressed by hostile powers.

The closing paragraphs shift from dry legislation to spiritual exhortation. Pius VI addresses the cardinals as men who have vowed to defend the Church even unto blood and reminds them that the barque of Peter is in a storm, threatened by waves and by wolves breaking in on the flock.[14] He urges them, when the time comes, to act swiftly and unanimously, guided by the blood of Christ’s mercy, seeking not private advantage but the common good of the Church. The imagery is traditional, but the tone carries the urgency of a man who senses that his own life is nearing its end and that the institutional survival of the papacy depends on the fidelity and courage of his electors.

### Reflective dimensions: reading the crisis of 1798 through *Cum nos superiori*

When assessing the reflective character of *Cum nos superiori*, it is important to note that the bull is not a broad theological commentary on the French Revolution or a philosophical reflection on modernity. Instead, it reflects on a specific set of institutional and political conditions that affect the papal office and the Church’s governance structures. The text’s backward‑looking gaze is tightly focused on the immediate past, roughly 1797–1798, and on the concrete legal and material changes imposed by revolutionary regimes in Italy.

The first reflective dimension concerns the status of Rome and the Papal States. By lamenting the plunder of the “Patrimony of St Peter,” Pius VI alludes to both the territorial losses of the Treaty of Tolentino and the subsequent confiscation of properties in Rome under the Roman Republic.[9][16] The Papal States had long been the territorial basis of papal temporal power, providing revenues, diplomatic leverage, and a secure environment for the exercise of spiritual authority. The bull’s emphasis on their violation thus reflects a consciousness that the traditional synthesis of spiritual and temporal authority in the papacy had been shattered. In this sense, *Cum nos superiori* documents the twilight of the pre‑modern papal monarchy, even if it does not theorize it as such.

A second reflective dimension relates to the status of religious communities. The bull explicitly notes the suppression of convents and monasteries and their profanation for secular uses.[14][16] This corresponds to documented decrees of the Roman Republic, which closed many religious houses, nationalized their assets, and often converted buildings to barracks, hospitals, or administrative offices.[16] The language of profanation conveys not only a loss of property but a violation of sacred space, a theme that had been central in Catholic polemics against revolutionary iconoclasm in France. By incorporating this grievance into a constitutional text, Pius VI effectively treats the suppression of religious orders not merely as a pious lament but as a factor that requires adaptation of the Church’s governance structures—for example, by recognizing that the usual Roman sites for conclave might no longer exist or be available.

Third, the bull reflects on the status of the cardinals themselves as both ecclesiastical dignitaries and political actors. It notes that many have been forced into exile, imprisoned, or deprived of their revenues, which historically came largely from benefices and territorial holdings.[14][17] Contemporary biographical entries, such as that on Cardinal Michele Di Pietro, confirm that cardinals experienced exile, dispersal, and political surveillance during this period, as revolutionary authorities sought to prevent them from acting collectively.[17] By highlighting this, Pius VI is not simply complaining about noblemen’s misfortunes; he is diagnosing a structural issue: the very body responsible for electing popes is being targeted as a political threat by secular regimes, making its free functioning precarious.

A fourth reflective element is the bull’s implicit evaluation of prior papal legislation. By reviewing earlier conclave constitutions and judging them insufficient for present circumstances, Pius VI engages in a restrained but real critique of his predecessors’ assumptions.[2][11][14] He suggests that those laws were crafted “for peaceful times” and under conditions of ecclesiastical liberty, where it could be assumed that the cardinals would meet in Rome, that their communications would be unimpeded, and that the pope’s temporal sovereignty would shield them from external coercion. The current persecution, he implies, invalidates those assumptions. This is not a denunciation of earlier popes, but it is a recognition that the papal office must adapt its own legal frameworks in response to changed political realities. As such, the bull stands at a hinge where the papacy begins to conceive of its institutional continuity as something that must be secured even in the absence of its traditional temporal scaffolding.

The bull is more reticent in naming the agents of persecution. It does not explicitly mention France, Bonaparte, the Directory, or the Roman Republic.[14] Instead, it speaks of enemies of the Church, of those who have expelled and imprisoned cardinals, and of persecutors of religion. This circumlocution may reflect prudence: naming specific states or leaders could have further endangered Catholics under their rule or aggravated Pius VI’s own captors. It may also reflect a reluctance to grant revolutionary regimes the dignity of explicit acknowledgment in a papal constitution. In any case, the effect is to frame the crisis in juridical and moral, rather than geopolitical, terms. The persecutors are defined by their actions—plunder, suppression, coercion—rather than by their ideological self‑descriptions as republicans or revolutionaries.

From the standpoint of the Papal Papers project, which is particularly interested in papal engagement with the secular world, this reticence is significant. *Cum nos superiori* is deeply engaged with secular political events, but it refracts them through a lens of institutional liberty rather than overt political theory. The document speaks repeatedly of the “liberty of the Church,” the “liberty of apostolic see,” and the “liberty of the electors,” treating freedom primarily as the absence of coercion in religious governance rather than as a broader citizen right.[14] In this, it mirrors earlier Catholic rhetoric about ecclesiastical liberty vis‑à‑vis monarchies, but now directed against revolutionary republics. The bull thus reflects on the secular world in a narrow but intense way: it sees states primarily as actors that either respect or violate the Church’s freedom to govern itself.

### Prospective and prescriptive dimensions: designing a future conclave in exile

If the reflective dimension of *Cum nos superiori* is tightly bounded in time and scope, its prospective dimension is expansive within its chosen horizon. The bull is not content merely to lament the crisis; it constructs a detailed script for how the papacy should navigate the likely scenario of a conclave in exile, under hostile political conditions.

One prospective element is the geographical reimagining of the papal election process. By authorizing the conclave to convene “in the city of some Catholic prince” rather than in Rome, Pius VI effectively anticipates the relocation of the papal electoral apparatus across national borders.[11][14] This is not a purely theoretical move. Already in 1798, cardinals were finding refuge in various Catholic courts, notably Vienna, Venice, Naples, and Spain, and diplomatic correspondence indicates that these powers were considering how to respond to the pope’s captivity.[11][15] The bull presumes that one of these princes will be willing to host the conclave and provide the necessary security, and it gives the cardinals the flexibility to choose the most suitable location when the time comes. When the conclave eventually met in Venice, on Austrian territory, this provision proved crucial.[6][11][15]

A second prospective element concerns the timing and communication logistics of the election. Pius VI instructs that, upon his death, the cardinal dean or senior cardinal must send notice to all cardinals as quickly as possible, using whatever means are available.[11][14] In a world of war‑disrupted postal services and slow transportation, this is hardly trivial. The bull’s recognition of potential delays and obstacles, and its authorization of a ten‑day waiting period after the arrival of the majority, reflects a realistic assessment of the communication infrastructure of late eighteenth‑century Europe, strained by military campaigns and political fragmentation.[11] It thus prescribes not only legal norms but a time‑bound process calibrated to what the pope and his advisors judge to be feasible.

A third and central prospective dimension is the insistence on freedom of election under conditions of external pressure. The bull repeatedly states that, for an election to be valid, the cardinals must be free in conscience and not subject to direct coercion or intimidation, even if their broader political circumstances are constrained.[11][14] This reflects centuries of canon law on free ecclesiastical elections, but it takes on new urgency when the papacy itself is at the mercy of a hostile power. Pius VI cannot prevent revolutionary armies from occupying Rome or imprisoning cardinals, but he can declare in advance that any election obtained by threats or force is canonically null, and conversely that an election by a minority of free cardinals in distant exile is valid. This anticipatory legal framing aims to pre‑empt both secular manipulation and internal doubts about legitimacy.

The suspension of the prohibition on pre‑electoral discussions is perhaps the most striking example of forward‑looking pragmatism. By authorizing cardinals to plan in advance for the conclave’s logistics, Pius VI effectively invites them to act as constitutional co‑designers of the succession process, something earlier law had treated as dangerous factionalism.[2][11][14] This reflects a judgment that, under revolutionary persecution, the greater danger lies in disorganization and indecision, not in excessive planning. The bull thus attempts to harness the cardinals’ political and diplomatic networks, encouraging them to secure commitments from Catholic courts and to prepare the ground for a swift election. In doing so, it tacitly recognizes that the papal succession is no longer an event that can be assumed to occur in a well‑ordered Roman setting; it is a vulnerable process that must be secured through proactive coordination across borders.

A final prospective dimension lies in the spiritual exhortation about the qualities to be sought in the next pope. While *Cum nos superiori* does not list specific criteria, its imagery of storms, wolves, and the need for a firm helmsman suggests that Pius VI expects his successor to face continued persecution and to require both courage and diplomatic skill.[14] He urges the cardinals to seek the common good of the Church and to act swiftly, implying that prolonged vacancies would be especially damaging in such times. In retrospect, the election of Pius VII—an Italian Benedictine bishop known for his prudence and moderate openness to concordats with secular powers—fits this profile reasonably well, though it is unclear how directly Pius VI’s exhortation shaped the cardinals’ thinking.[6][15] Nonetheless, the bull’s prospective tone is that of a ruler who sees the office, not his own person, as the essential locus of the Church’s resilience, and who is willing to loosen traditional disciplines to ensure that office’s continuity.

### From text to event: *Cum nos superiori* and the conclave of 1799–1800

The most concrete test of *Cum nos superiori*’s prescriptive efficacy is its role in the organization and conduct of the conclave that followed Pius VI’s death. After being moved from the Certosa of San Casciano to various locations, Pius VI arrived in Valence in southeastern France, where he died on 29 August 1799, still a prisoner of the French government.[3][4] His death left the Holy See vacant and raised the practical question of how and where the cardinals should elect a successor.

At that time, the College of Cardinals was scattered. Some were still in Italy, others at Catholic courts across Europe, and some under various forms of surveillance or house arrest.[6][11][17] Rome was no longer available as a neutral meeting place, being under French influence and subject to the shifting fortunes of war. The Austrian emperor Francis II, seeking to shape the post‑revolutionary order in Italy, offered to host the conclave in Venice, which had recently come under Austrian control after the fall of the Venetian Republic.[6][11] Venice, with its strong Catholic culture and monastic infrastructure, offered both a symbolically charged and practically secure site.

Canon law historians note that the cardinals explicitly invoked *Quum nos, superiore anno* when accepting the Austrian proposal, seeing in Pius VI’s bull the canonical authorization needed to convene outside Rome.[2][11] The conclave opened on 30 November 1799 in the Benedictine monastery of San Giorgio Maggiore in Venice, where twenty‑three of the available thirty‑four cardinals eventually assembled, a number lower than in many previous conclaves but sufficient under the terms of *Cum nos superiori*.[6][11][15] The bull’s provision that a two‑thirds majority of those present, regardless of total numbers, would suffice for validity was crucial in calming doubts about the reduced attendance.

The election itself was protracted and entangled in European power politics. Austrian and Neapolitan factions promoted different candidates, and the French, though temporarily weakened after Bonaparte’s Egyptian campaign and the fall of the Directory, still cast a shadow over the proceedings.[6] After several months of deadlock, a compromise candidate emerged in the person of Cardinal Barnaba Chiaramonti, bishop of Imola, whose pastoral letters had earlier suggested a cautious willingness to live with certain aspects of republican government under conditions of religious freedom.[6][15] On 14 March 1800, the cardinals elected him pope, and he took the name Pius VII, explicitly honoring his predecessor and fellow native of Cesena.[6][15]

Sources emphasize that the conclave followed the essential procedural norms of secrecy and freedom, even as it adapted certain ceremonies to local circumstances, again in keeping with the latitude granted by *Cum nos superiori*.[6][11] The bull’s requirement that it be read at the opening of the conclave served to remind all participants of the legal framework under which they acted and to forestall later challenges. From the perspective of later canonists and historians, the legitimacy of Pius VII’s election was generally accepted, and *Cum nos superiori* is frequently credited as the constitutional instrument that made it possible under such unusual circumstances.[2][11]

This episode illustrates how the bull’s prospective prescriptions translated into concrete institutional practice. It functioned as a kind of emergency constitutional amendment that, far from remaining a dead letter, shaped the very real choices of cardinals and Catholic princes at a time when the traditional nexus of papal sovereignty—Rome, the Papal States, and their protective structures—had collapsed. In that sense, the bull can be seen as one of the few papal documents of the era whose prescriptions were almost fully realized within a short time frame, giving it a rare correspondence between normative intention and historical outcome.

### Reception, legacy, and limits

In terms of broader reception, *Cum nos superiori* did not become a widely cited or debated text in the secular public sphere of its time. Newspapers and pamphlets in 1798–1800 focused more on the geopolitical drama of Bonaparte’s campaigns, the fluctuating fortunes of the Roman Republic and its successors, and the symbolic humiliation of Pius VI’s captivity and death.[3][4][8] Some Protestant and secular commentators saw the pope’s downfall as emblematic of the collapse of ancien régime clericalism, while Catholic loyalists portrayed him as a martyr to revolutionary impiety.[4][8] However, the technicalities of conclave law rarely entered public discourse. The bull’s impact was primarily intra‑ecclesial, affecting canon law and the internal organization of the papacy rather than the wider political imagination.

Within the Church, the bull has been treated in canon law manuals and histories of papal elections as a notable precedent for conclaves held under persecution or in exile.[2][11] The Catholic Encyclopedia’s article on the conclave notes Pius VI’s 1798 legislation as an example of the Church’s capacity to modify electoral procedures according to circumstances, while maintaining the essential principle of free choice by the cardinals.[2] The CSUN “Sede Vacante 1799–1800” study reproduces key passages and emphasizes its role in enabling the Venice conclave.[11] However, after the restoration of papal rule in Rome under Pius VII and the eventual consolidation of the papal court there, the bull’s practical relevance diminished. Later popes, such as Pius IX during the Italian Risorgimento, faced comparable threats to their temporal power and again considered the possibility of conclaves in exile, but they crafted their own legal instruments and did not simply rely on *Cum nos superiori*.[3][11]

In that sense, the bull’s legacy is limited and specific. It was an effective solution to a particular crisis, not a long‑term blueprint for papal elections in the modern era. When the 1917 Code of Canon Law and later conclave norms were codified, they did not explicitly incorporate its provisions, although they allowed for the possibility of elections outside Rome in cases of necessity.[2] The bull thus occupies a somewhat marginal place in the broader history of papal law—important for understanding the Venice conclave and the late eighteenth‑century crisis, but not foundational for later constitutional developments.

From a social and political perspective, the bull’s impact on the secular world was correspondingly modest. It neither challenged the ideological premises of revolutionary republicanism nor proposed a theological critique of modern sovereignty. Instead, it functioned as a defensive maneuver, adjusting internal church procedures to ensure continuity in the face of external upheaval. In that respect, it exemplifies a broader pattern in papal engagement with revolutionary modernity during the 1790s: a focus on institutional survival rather than comprehensive social teaching. More conceptual critiques of liberalism and secularism would emerge in later papal documents, especially in the nineteenth century, once the immediate survival of the papal office was less in question.

Yet even in its limited sphere, *Cum nos superiori* has a certain symbolic significance. It captures a moment when the papacy, stripped of much of its worldly power, asserted its capacity to legislate for its own continuity independently of any particular territorial regime. By declaring that the pope could be validly elected anywhere that a group of free cardinals could gather, Pius VI implicitly de‑territorialized the papal office. The pope’s authority, in this vision, no longer depended on specific lands or fortresses but on the persistence of a transnational college of electors bound by canonical rules. This anticipates, in a rough way, the later nineteenth‑ and twentieth‑century reality of a papacy increasingly defined by spiritual and moral authority rather than by temporal sovereignty.

### Anthropological and institutional reading: vulnerability, adaptation, and narrative

Reading *Cum nos superiori* anthropologically, as a cultural artefact of an institution under stress, highlights several dimensions of vulnerability and adaptation. The bull’s very existence suggests an acute awareness within the papal curia that the inherited scripts for succession were fragile when confronted with modern forms of state power—standing armies, ideological revolutions, and the centralized capacity to suppress or co‑opt ecclesiastical structures. The bull can be seen as a ritualized reaffirmation of continuity, a narrative that inscribes the promise of future order into the legal fabric of the Church at a time when the present order was collapsing.

The detailed invocation of persecution—imprisoned cardinals, profaned monasteries, plundered patrimony—serves not only as factual backdrop but as a way of sacralizing the institution’s suffering. By including these elements in a constitutional document, the papacy weaves its own vulnerability into the story of the Church’s perseverance across ages of persecution. The bull thus performs a double function: it offers practical instructions and it contributes to a collective memory of the revolutionary crisis as a time when the papacy stood firm in adversity, even as it adapted its rules. In later Catholic narratives, Pius VI would sometimes be portrayed as a martyr‑like figure whose humiliation foreshadowed, and justified, later papal resistance to secular encroachment.[4][8] *Cum nos superiori* provides some of the raw material for that narrative, though it does so in legalistic rather than hagiographic tones.

The bull also reveals tensions within the Church’s self‑understanding regarding law and necessity. Traditional canon law placed great weight on the stability of constitutions, especially those governing sacraments and the papal office. Yet here, a reigning pope explicitly derogates from well‑established conclave legislation, invoking necessity and the salus Ecclesiae as justification.[2][11][14] This move both reaffirms and stretches the doctrine of papal plenitude of power. On the one hand, it underscores that the pope can legislate for nearly any ecclesiastical matter; on the other, it signals that even solemn constitutions are not immutable but contingent on historical circumstances. Anthropologically, this can be seen as an instance of an institution renegotiating its own sacred norms in the face of external pressure, while framing the renegotiation itself as an act of continuity with the past.

In terms of engagement with the secular world, the bull’s silence on the ideological content of the revolution is as important as its detailed response to its institutional effects. *Cum nos superiori* does not argue about rights, popular sovereignty, or the legitimacy of republics; it does not, for example, condemn the Roman Republic per se as a form of government. Instead, it condemns specific actions—confiscation, suppression, coercion—that violate ecclesiastical liberty.[14][16] This reflects a pre‑liberal Catholic conception in which the primary lens for evaluating regimes is their treatment of the Church. From an anthropological viewpoint, this suggests that, at this stage, the papacy had not yet fully articulated a theological politics of modernity; it was still reacting piecemeal to assaults on its property and jurisdiction, using older categories of liberty and persecution rather than engaging the new language of rights and citizenship that the revolution had popularized.

### Evidence gaps and historiographical cautions

The study of *Cum nos superiori* faces some evidentiary limits. The bull itself is accessible, and its role in the Venice conclave is reasonably well documented in ecclesiastical sources and modern historiography on papal elections.[2][6][11][15] However, there is relatively little contemporaneous secular commentary on the document as such. Revolutionary and post‑revolutionary newspapers in France and Italy, where they survive, rarely mention the technicalities of papal constitutional legislation, focusing instead on the dramatic narrative of the pope’s captivity and death.[4][8] This makes it difficult to assess how widely the bull was known beyond ecclesiastical circles or how lay Catholics perceived its significance.

Furthermore, the internal deliberations that led to the bull’s drafting are only partially recoverable. We lack detailed minutes of discussions among Pius VI’s advisors at the Certosa, and thus must infer from the text and from broader patterns of papal governance how the key decisions were made. The bull’s reliance on earlier conclave constitutions suggests that the pope and his curia drew on existing canon law expertise, but the degree to which specific cardinals or canonists influenced particular clauses remains unclear.[2][11] In this sense, the bull functions as a public, authorized narrative of papal adaptation, but the backstage processes of negotiation, disagreement, or competing proposals are largely hidden.

There is also a risk of retrospective inflation of the bull’s importance. Because the Venice conclave ultimately took place and produced a widely recognized pope, later historians have tended to highlight *Cum nos superiori* as the necessary precondition for that success.[2][6][11] While this is accurate at the level of formal legality, it may obscure other factors that contributed to the conclave’s feasibility, such as the diplomatic calculations of Austria, the relative weakening of French power after military setbacks, or the personal networks among cardinals who facilitated communication and travel. The bull did not create the willingness of Catholic princes to host the conclave; it provided a canonical framework that legitimized their offer. Disentangling these various causal strands requires caution in attributing too much agency to a single text.

Finally, from the perspective of the Papal Papers project, which is interested in social and economic engagement, *Cum nos superiori* is somewhat narrow. It does not address the broader social consequences of the dissolution of monasteries, such as the impact on education, poor relief, or local economies, topics that some modern historians of the French and Italian revolutions have explored.[7][16] Nor does it comment on the experiences of lay Catholics under revolutionary regimes, beyond general references to persecution. While references to plunder and confiscation implicitly touch on economic issues, the bull treats them primarily as violations of ecclesiastical rights rather than as social disruptions affecting wider populations.[14][16] This limits the extent to which the document can be used as a window into the papal perception of wider social change, as opposed to institutional self‑defence.

## Context: Church, revolution, and the dissolution of religious property

To deepen understanding of how *Cum nos superiori* fits within its secular context, it is helpful to consider the broader phenomenon of the dissolution of religious property in early modern and modern Europe. The suppression of monasteries, convents, and other religious houses by secular authorities was not unique to the French and Roman republics. For example, in England under Henry VIII, the Dissolution of the Monasteries in the 1530s saw the crown disband all Catholic monastic institutions, seize their wealth, and redistribute their lands, radically altering the religious and economic landscape.[7] That earlier episode, driven by a mix of financial motives and the creation of a national church independent of Rome, left long‑lasting scars in Catholic memory as a paradigmatic case of state aggression against religious life.

The revolutionary suppressions in France and Italy drew on similar financial incentives—church lands were a valuable resource for cash‑strapped regimes—and on ideological hostility to corporate religious bodies seen as obstacles to civic equality and rational administration.[3][16] The Roman Republic’s decree of 11 May 1798 suppressing thirty‑one conventual buildings in Rome fit within this pattern, freeing up urban real estate and resources for the new regime.[16] From the secular perspective, these measures were intended to break the power of the clergy, assert state control, and mobilize resources; from the Church’s perspective, they were experienced as unjust expropriations and acts of sacrilege.

Pius VI, as a ruler of the Papal States, was particularly sensitive to these trends. The Papal States had long resisted the deeper secularization of church property that had occurred in some Catholic monarchies, such as Joseph II’s reforms in Austria, which suppressed “unproductive” contemplative orders.[3] The revolutionary wave in Italy threatened to subject the Papal States to even more radical confiscations. The Treaty of Tolentino had already stripped the papacy of key territories and imposed heavy indemnities; subsequent revolutionary regimes went further, targeting religious houses as repositories of wealth and as symbols of the old order.[9][16] *Cum nos superiori* reflects this by listing the suppression and profanation of monasteries as one of the major grievances necessitating emergency legislation.[14]

The bull’s focus on institutional liberty, however, does not translate into a defence of the broader social roles of monasteries in education, charity, and local economies. Modern scholarship has shown that the dissolution of religious houses in various contexts, including England and revolutionary France, had significant social consequences, altering patterns of land ownership, weakening traditional networks of poor relief, and transforming urban and rural landscapes.[7][16] Yet in *Cum nos superiori*, Pius VI treats these suppressions primarily as obstacles to the functioning of the papal monarchy and the religious life of the Church, rather than as threats to social welfare. This may reflect the genre of the document, which is narrowly constitutional, but it also indicates a certain institutional self‑referentiality in papal perceptions at the time.

The dissolution of religious property also complicates the relationship between the Church and Catholic princes, who sometimes engaged in similar policies. Austrian and some Italian rulers had, prior to the revolution, pursued secularizing reforms under the banner of enlightened absolutism, seeking to subordinate the Church to the state while maintaining Catholic doctrine.[3] Pius VI had opposed some of these reforms, especially those of Joseph II, but had often been forced to acquiesce. By 1798–1799, however, these same Catholic princes became potential protectors of the papacy against revolutionary republics, which posed a more radical threat. *Cum nos superiori*’s reliance on the “territory of a Catholic prince” as the safe space for a conclave thus embodies an ambivalent relationship: the papacy is placing itself under the protection of powers that have sometimes eroded its temporal and property rights, but which now appear as defenders against atheistic or anti‑clerical regimes.

This ambivalence would continue to shape papal diplomacy in the nineteenth century, as popes navigated between suspicion of state control and reliance on Catholic monarchs for protection. Pius VII’s subsequent concordats with Napoleon and various states attempted to negotiate a modus vivendi in which the Church retained spiritual authority while recognizing the state’s reorganization of ecclesiastical property and jurisdictions.[3] In hindsight, *Cum nos superiori* can be seen as an early, defensive iteration of this negotiation, in which the papacy concedes that its survival as an office may require dependence on secular powers beyond its own territory.

## Reflective character: scoring and elaboration

Given the preceding analysis, the reflective character of *Cum nos superiori* can be further elaborated and justified. The bull’s reflective saturation is high because it not only mentions but structurally relies on a detailed diagnosis of recent events. Its opening paragraphs recount the sequence from *Christi Ecclesiae*’s anticipations to the subsequent escalation of persecution, making these facts the explicit motive for new legislation.[5][14] Without this narrative of injury—plundered patrimony, suppressed houses, dispersed cardinals—the bull’s derogations from earlier law would appear arbitrary. By embedding the legal changes in a story of suffering, Pius VI legitimizes them as necessary responses to an objective crisis.

The density of this reflection, however, is temporally thin. The bull does not reach back to older conflicts between papacy and empire, such as the Investiture Controversy, nor to earlier cases of conclaves held in unusual circumstances, such as those at Viterbo or Perugia in thirteenth‑century Italy.[2] It alludes generally to earlier conclave constitutions but treats them as static texts rather than as products of specific historical crises. The only truly historicizing gesture is the contrast between “peaceful times” and the present persecution.[14] Thus, while the reflection is intense regarding 1797–1798, it does not situate that crisis within a broader longue durée narrative; it treats the revolution as an unprecedented rupture rather than as a new chapter in an ongoing story.

From a methodological standpoint, this narrow temporal focus affects the gradient density estimation. A density of around −2 suggests that the backward gaze extends a few years rather than decades. The references to *Christi Ecclesiae* (1797) and to the Treaty of Tolentino’s aftermath support this.[5][9][14] There is no attempt to interpret the revolution in light of earlier Enlightenment controversies, such as Jansenism or Febronianism, which had marked Pius VI’s earlier pontificate.[3] This reinforces the view that *Cum nos superiori* is a crisis‑management document rather than a comprehensive reflection on the pope’s era.

It is also worth noting what the bull does not reflect upon. It does not offer self‑critique of papal policies that may have contributed to the Papal States’ vulnerabilities, such as financial mismanagement or reluctance to reform.[3] Nor does it acknowledge the grievances—real or perceived—that revolutionary actors held against the Church, such as accusations of clerical abuses or financial exploitation. The reflective narrative is unilateral: the Church is the victim; secular powers are aggressors. This is understandable in context but limits the bull’s usefulness as a nuanced historical analysis. For the purposes of the Papal Papers project, which values investigative scrutiny, this asymmetry is important to flag. The bull’s account of events is accurate in terms of describing specific acts of suppression and plunder, but it omits the socio‑political drivers and popular dynamics behind them.[9][16]

## Prospective character: scoring and elaboration

On the prospective side, the bull’s saturation is even more intense. Nearly every paragraph after the initial reflection introduces some forward‑looking clause: authorizing future actions, prescribing procedures, warning against schism, exhorting about the next pope’s qualities.[11][14] The future is not an abstract eschatological horizon; it is concretely envisaged as the period after Pius VI’s death, when the Church will need a new head amid ongoing persecution. The bull’s very reason for being is this future event. That justifies a saturation score of 9: the document is almost wholly organized around shaping coming actions rather than simply describing current realities.

The temporal density of the prospective gaze is similarly focused but slightly broader. A density of about +5 years reflects the idea that Pius VI envisions the emergency measures as necessary until a successor is elected and can re‑establish more stable norms. The text does not specify an expiry date, but its practical horizon is bounded by the next conclave. In reality, that conclave took place within less than two years; thus, the actual forward reach was shorter. However, it is plausible to think that, had war and persecution persisted, the bull’s provisions would have remained relevant somewhat longer, perhaps for an additional generation, until the Church either regained secure territory or codified new norms. The absence of any deep speculation about the Church’s situation decades into the future supports the view that the prospective density is short‑ to medium‑term rather than long‑term.

The bull’s prescriptive content also reveals certain implicit assumptions about future secular developments. Pius VI seems to assume that Catholic princes will remain willing and able to protect the Church, even as revolutionary forces threaten it.[11][14] He does not anticipate, or at least does not address, scenarios in which Catholic monarchies themselves collapse or secularize further, as would happen in the nineteenth century. The horizon of his imagination is still the old European order of Catholic courts, albeit under strain. In that sense, the bull’s prospective vision is conservative in structure: it imagines adaptation within the existing framework of Catholic princes, not a new relationship to modern nation‑states or democratic regimes.

At the same time, by de‑linking the papal election from a specific territory and anchoring it instead in the transnational College of Cardinals, the bull inadvertently gestures toward a more mobile, less territorially constrained papacy. It suggests that, even if the Papal States are lost, the papal office can persist wherever cardinals and a willing host state can be found. This anticipates, in embryonic form, the later reality of a papacy that, after the loss of the Papal States in 1870, nevertheless continued to function as a spiritual authority headquartered in Rome but without temporal territory. The bull does not explicitly predict that outcome, but its logic is compatible with it.

From today’s vantage point, one can also assess the accuracy or success of *Cum nos superiori*’s prospective elements. In the immediate term, it was highly successful: the conclave took place, a pope was elected, and the continuity of the office was secured under the conditions anticipated.[6][11][15] In the longer term, however, the bull did not inaugurate a lasting transformation of conclave law; its emergency measures were not directly incorporated into later codes, and the papacy eventually developed other ways of thinking about elections in crisis, drawing on broader experiences of political exile. Thus, while the bull’s prescriptions were prescient for the narrow window of 1799–1800, they did not shape the Church’s trajectory over subsequent decades in a sustained way.

## Conclusion

*Cum nos superiori* emerges, in this investigation, as a densely focused document of papal crisis management at the intersection of ecclesiastical law and revolutionary politics. Written from exile in 1798, with Rome occupied and the Papal States dismembered, the bull reflects intensely on the immediate past—particularly the Treaty of Tolentino, the establishment of the Roman Republic, and the suppression of religious institutions—but it does so primarily in order to justify a radical, though narrowly framed, reconfiguration of conclave law.[3][9][14][16] Its reflective saturation is high in relation to the years 1797–1798, but its backward gaze is temporally shallow, largely ignoring deeper historical analogies and structural critiques.

Prospectively, the bull is even more saturated, almost entirely oriented toward securing the conditions for a future papal election under persecution. By authorizing the conclave to meet outside Rome, validating elections by whatever number of free cardinals can assemble, and suspending the long‑standing prohibition on pre‑electoral planning, Pius VI effectively constitutionalizes a model of papal succession in exile.[11][14] This model proved immediately efficacious when, after his death in French captivity, the College of Cardinals met in Venice and elected Pius VII in 1800, an event whose canonical legitimacy rested heavily on *Cum nos superiori*’s provisions.[6][11][15] The bull thus stands as an example of a papal document whose prescriptive intent and historical realization align unusually closely.

In terms of engagement with the secular world, *Cum nos superiori* is both deeply embedded in and somewhat insulated from its context. It takes as its premise the disruptive power of revolutionary regimes, their capacity to confiscate church property and imprison clergy, and it pragmatically seeks protection from Catholic princes whose own histories with the Church are ambivalent.[9][16] Yet it refrains from naming political actors or offering a theoretical critique of revolutionary ideology, focusing instead on the narrower category of ecclesiastical liberty. This reveals a papacy still thinking in pre‑modern categories even as it faces modern state power.

For the purposes of the Papal Papers project’s visual and conceptual mapping, *Cum nos superiori* should be represented as a document of high saturation in both reflective and prescriptive modes, with a modest backward temporal density and a short‑ to medium‑term forward horizon. It is profoundly present‑oriented, capturing a brief but critical window in which the papacy, stripped of much of its temporal power, nevertheless asserted its capacity to legislate for its own continuation. In doing so, it offers a poignant snapshot of an institution caught between an ancien régime self‑understanding and an emergent, more de‑territorialized future—an image perhaps well suited to artistic exploration of fragility, adaptation, and the thin legal filaments by which complex institutions survive political storms.
