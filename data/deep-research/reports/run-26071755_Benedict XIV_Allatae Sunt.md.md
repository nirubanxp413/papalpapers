## Structured

### Overview

Pope Benedict XIV’s encyclical *Allatae Sunt*, promulgated on 26 July 1755, is at once a painstaking juridical-historical brief and a programmatic policy statement on the place of Eastern, or “Oriental,” rites within the Catholic Church.[1][16] Written in response to a concrete query from a missionary in the East about shared church buildings, calendars, and fasting obligations for Armenian and Syrian Catholics, the document quickly expands into a sweeping rehearsal of more than eight centuries of papal and conciliar decisions concerning Eastern Christians who entered or sought to maintain communion with Rome.[1][15] The encyclical’s core thesis is the sharp distinction it draws between unity of faith and diversity of liturgical rite: Benedict XIV insists that Rome requires Eastern Christians who return from schism or heresy to abandon their errors, but does not require them to abandon their ancient, approved rites; in a famous sentence, he states that the Church desires that “all be Catholic, rather than that all become Latin.”[1] This insistence, however, is not merely antiquarian; it is harnessed to a strongly prescriptive agenda aimed at missionaries and Eastern Catholics alike. Missionaries are ordered not to Latinize converts, Eastern Catholics are commanded to observe their own fasts and liturgical customs strictly, and detailed norms are laid down governing mixed-rite situations, the use of Latin churches, and even the wording of the Creed with respect to the controversial *Filioque* clause.[1][10]  

Placed against its wider eighteenth‑century backdrop, *Allatae Sunt* reveals a papacy grappling with the unintended consequences of early modern missionary success and colonial expansion. The seventeenth and eighteenth centuries saw the emergence or consolidation of several “uniate” Eastern Catholic churches, including Armenian, Chaldean, and Syro‑Malabar communities, whose members were often subjected to intense Latinising pressures by Western missionary orders and by Catholic colonial powers.[5][8][9][13] At the same time, the Roman Congregation de Propaganda Fide, founded in 1622 but with antecedent commissions dating from Gregory XIII’s pontificate,[18] sought to standardise mission policy, curb abuses, and promote union with Eastern Christians in a way that would preserve legitimate local traditions. Benedict XIV, a learned canonist with a deep interest in liturgy and sacramental theology, emerged as a key architect of this policy, intervening repeatedly on Eastern matters and issuing several major documents alongside *Allatae Sunt*.[6][15][16] The encyclical is thus highly saturated with both retrospective and prospective content: it looks back intensely over the previous century and more of Latinising controversy, while attempting to legislate for a future in which Eastern rites would be preserved intact within a globally expanding Catholic Church.  

### The Encyclical in Snapshot

*Allatae Sunt* is formally addressed to missionaries “assigned to the Orient,” which in eighteenth‑century Roman usage covered a wide geographical span: from the Eastern Mediterranean and Armenian lands through the Ottoman Empire and the Persian domains to parts of India and beyond.[1][11] The document’s immediate occasion was a question posed from the city of Basra (rendered “Balsera” in the Latin text) concerning the pastoral management of Armenian and Syrian Catholics who, lacking their own church, worshipped in a Latin parish and were observed neglecting some of their traditional fasting obligations.[1] The missionary asked whether, to avoid confusion and scandal, these Eastern Catholics could be required to follow the Latin calendar and fasting discipline instead. Benedict XIV reports that the Congregation of the Holy Office responded with the terse principle “no innovations are to be made,” and he takes this as the starting point for a broader exposition.[1][15]  

The text proceeds in a structured but dense manner. After stating the principle that no change should be introduced into approved Eastern rites, Benedict XIV turns to a long historical section in which he cites a succession of popes, councils, and Roman congregations to show that the Apostolic See has consistently upheld this policy.[1][15] He mentions medieval popes such as Leo IX and Innocent III, later pontiffs including Innocent IV, Gregory X, Eugene IV, Nicholas V, Gregory XIII, and Clement VIII, and more recent decrees of the Congregation de Propaganda Fide in the seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries.[1][18] The narrative culminates in the reiterated decree of 1702, in which Propaganda Fide explicitly forbade missionaries to dispense Eastern Christians from their own rites or to encourage them to adopt Latin usages simply out of convenience.[1][15] With this historical groundwork laid, Benedict XIV devotes the second half of the encyclical to detailed practical rulings: on mixed‑rite use of churches, the admissibility of “rite switching,” the treatment of sacraments, the observance of fasting and penitential seasons, the vexed question of the calendar, and the wording of the Creed with respect to the procession of the Holy Spirit.[1][10]  

Although its subject matter appears almost entirely intra‑ecclesial, the encyclical’s framing reveals its broader stakes. Benedict XIV repeatedly emphasises that Rome’s concern is not only theological correctness but also the avoidance of scandal, the preservation of peace among different Catholic communities, and the respect of legitimate customs that are intertwined with the ethnic, linguistic, and communal identities of Eastern Christians.[1][15] In this way, *Allatae Sunt* can be read as a document about the social governance of diversity within a global religious institution, with indirect implications for how that institution interacts with the plural societies in which its Eastern members lived—particularly in the Ottoman and Persian empires and in colonial territories where Latin missionaries were agents of both religious and cultural change.[5][11][13]  

### Benedict XIV: Canonist, Diplomat, and “Oriental” Legislator

To situate *Allatae Sunt* properly, one must understand Benedict XIV’s personal profile and the position of the papacy in the mid‑eighteenth century. Prospero Lorenzo Lambertini, elected as Benedict XIV in 1740, was widely regarded by contemporaries and later historians as one of the most erudite and canonically sophisticated popes of the early modern period.[6] Before his election, he had served in various curial roles, including positions within the Congregation of the Council and the Holy Office, and had distinguished himself in legal and theological scholarship, publishing significant works on canonization and the procedures of canon law.[6] As pope, he combined this juristic expertise with an interest in scholarship more broadly, founding academies in Rome for the study of liturgy, ecclesiastical history, and Oriental languages, which served both theological and missionary aims.[6]  

The Catholic Encyclopedia notes that under Benedict XIV “a formal union was consummated with some of the Eastern Churches,” and that he founded four academies in Rome for the study of Roman antiquities, liturgy, Church history, and the liturgies of the Eastern Churches.[6] These initiatives highlight his dual orientation: on the one hand, he was committed to doctrinal clarity and to strengthening Rome’s authority; on the other, he showed a relatively broad‑minded interest in historical and cultural particularity, especially in the liturgical traditions of Eastern Christianity.[6][15] His pontificate also coincided with intense debates over missionary methods, as the Holy See clashed with European colonial powers and religious orders over issues ranging from the Chinese Rites to the accommodation of Indian customs among the so‑called Malabar Christians.[15][13]  

Within this wider matrix, Benedict XIV issued several significant documents on Eastern and missionary questions. These included the constitution *Demandatam* (1743), which imposed strict norms on Latin missionaries in the Malabar region of India to prevent abuses and the arbitrary imposition of Latin practices; and *Ex quo singulari* (1742), addressing the Chinese rites controversy and disallowing certain Confucian practices that had been tolerated by Jesuit missionaries.[15][13] Scholars of Eastern Catholicism have noted that his pontificate played an outsized role in shaping Roman policies toward Eastern churches, particularly in the domain of sacramental and liturgical discipline.[15] A detailed study by the Sheptytsky Institute observes that Benedict XIV intervened on specific aspects of all seven sacraments in the Eastern churches, scrutinising particular practices and either accepting or rejecting them based on their conformity to Roman doctrine, while generally attempting to preserve legitimate ritual diversity.[15] *Allatae Sunt* must be read as part of this coherent strategy: a codification and defense of Rome’s commitment to protecting Eastern rites, even as it insists on theological uniformity and on papal jurisdiction over the boundaries of permissible diversity.[1][15]  

### The Immediate Problem: Basra, Calendars, and Fasting

The pastoral situation in Basra that triggered *Allatae Sunt* exemplifies the friction points produced by the coexistence of multiple rites within a single ecclesial space. Basra, located at a strategic port in the Persian Gulf, lay at a crossroads of trade routes and imperial influences and hosted a mix of Christian communities, including Armenians and Syrians, both Orthodox and Catholic, alongside Latin clergy serving foreign merchants and diplomats.[11] According to Benedict XIV’s account, Armenian and Syrian Catholics in Basra had no church of their own and therefore celebrated their liturgy in the local Latin church.[1] Over time, they were observed neglecting their own stricter fasting rules, particularly abstentions from certain foods, and aligning themselves instead with the more lenient Latin practice, especially when Latin confreres were eating fish or other foods that their own discipline restricted.[1][12]  

The missionary, presumably concerned both about internal discipline and about the scandal that might be caused by differing practices within a single building, sought clarification from Rome. Could he not, for the sake of order and unity, oblige these Eastern Catholics to follow the Latin calendar and fasting regimen, thereby harmonising their observance with the host community? Benedict XIV’s answer, as codified in the encyclical, is clear: “no innovations are to be made,” and the Eastern faithful are to be exhorted and compelled, where necessary, to return to the full observance of their own approved discipline rather than assimilate to Latin habits.[1] The pope grants that sharing the same church building is permissible when Eastern Catholics lack their own space, but only on the condition that liturgies be celebrated at different times and that each community scrupulously maintains its own rite.[1]  

This vignette reveals both the practical and the symbolic stakes of rites. For Armenian and Syrian Catholics, fasting rules and calendar observances are not purely private ascetical practices; they are communal markers of identity, deeply embedded in the liturgical year and in the rhythms of domestic and social life.[12] For Latin missionaries, however, these distinctive customs could appear as unnecessary complications that risked confusing the faithful or fragmenting the unity of local Catholic witness. Behind the Basra query lies a broader missionary mentality that tended to equate “Catholic” with “Latin,” and to see the Latin rite as the normative or superior expression of the Church, with Eastern rites tolerated at best as transitional forms.[5][13] *Allatae Sunt* directly challenges this mentality by forbidding the suppression of Eastern practices for the sake of convenience and by insisting that Eastern Catholics remain faithful to their inherited traditions even when living in predominantly Latin environments.[1][15]  

### Major Themes: Rite, Doctrine, and the Limits of Uniformity

The central conceptual distinction in *Allatae Sunt* is that between rite and doctrine. Benedict XIV repeatedly stresses that while there is one Catholic faith, which must be held in its integrity by all, there are multiple legitimate rites through which that faith is expressed, none of which is intrinsically superior in the eyes of the Church.[1] This is not a relativisation of doctrine; on the contrary, the pope insists that Eastern Christians returning from schism must repudiate any heresies or errors condemned by Roman teaching and must accept dogmatic formulations, including those concerning the procession of the Holy Spirit and the primacy of the pope.[1][10][15] However, once doctrinal conformity is secured, the Church not only permits but positively desires the preservation of the converted community’s own liturgical, disciplinary, and in many cases canonical traditions, provided they are not intrinsically contrary to faith or morals.[1][15]  

This line is drawn most starkly in Benedict XIV’s famous summary statement that the Church does not ask that all become Latin, but that all be Catholic.[1] The implication is that attempts by missionaries to “convert” Eastern Christians from their own rite to the Latin rite are a category mistake: they conflate cultural and ritual forms with the substance of faith, and risk implying that union with Rome requires cultural assimilation. The pope therefore forbids Latin missionaries from persuading or pressuring Eastern Catholics to transfer to the Latin rite, and equally forbids Latin clergy from adopting Eastern rites without legitimate authorization, thereby preventing opportunistic “rite switching” that might be motivated by personal preference or perceived leniency in disciplines such as fasting or marriage impediments.[1][15]  

Another significant theme is the careful delineation of what counts as legitimate diversity in sacramental practice. Benedict XIV draws upon earlier scholarly work on Eastern liturgies, including that of figures such as Leo Allatius and Cardinal Bona, to argue that many practices once viewed with suspicion by Latins—such as the use of leavened bread in the Eucharist, certain forms of epiclesis, the administration of communion under both species, or the giving of confirmation immediately after baptism—are in fact ancient and orthodox traditions that should be preserved.[1][15] At the same time, he upholds the Church’s right to suppress or correct specific rituals that are judged to be superstitious, theologically misleading, or otherwise harmful. The encyclical thus offers a nuanced vision of unity that is neither an uncritical celebration of diversity nor a drive to uniformity, but an attempt to draw a hard line between core dogma and mutable cultural forms, and to police that line through detailed canonical regulation.[1][15]  

### The Encyclical’s Temporal Horizon

Although *Allatae Sunt* is not overtly speculative about the future, its internal structure reflects a highly self‑conscious engagement with time. Benedict XIV’s long rehearsal of previous papal and conciliar decisions is more than a rhetorical flourish; it is designed to prove that the principle he is enunciating is not an innovation of his own but the consistent policy of the Apostolic See across many centuries.[1][15] He reaches back to medieval councils and popes, to seventeenth‑century Propaganda Fide decrees, and to more recent interventions, demonstrating that whenever Eastern Christians entered union with Rome, the Holy See ordinarily confirmed their rites and explicitly forbade Latinisation.[1][18] This retrospective move gives the encyclical a strongly reflective character, concentrated particularly on the previous hundred or so years during which Rome had been dealing in a more systematised way with Eastern churches through Propaganda Fide and the early modern missionary network.[15][18]  

At the same time, the final sections of the document are framed in consciously forward‑looking terms. Benedict XIV writes not only to resolve the Basra case but to lay down general principles that are to be “inviolably observed” by missionaries working in the Orient, and by Eastern Catholics themselves, in the years to come.[1] He anticipates recurring situations—shared churches, mixed‑rite marriages, confusion over fasts and feasts, pressures toward cultural assimilation—and attempts to shape the future behaviour of both clergy and laity so that Eastern rites will be preserved and Catholic unity will be presented as reconciled diversity rather than enforced sameness.[1][15] He also has an eye to the wider reputation of the Church: he warns that Latinising excesses can give scandal to Eastern Orthodox and other separated Christians, reinforcing their suspicions that union with Rome is tantamount to cultural capitulation.[1][10][15] The encyclical thus moves along a temporal axis that is heavily weighted toward reflection on the recent past, but its normative prescriptions are clearly intended to govern a medium‑term future in which Eastern Catholic communities would continue to expand and interact with Latin missionaries in complex ways.  

## Historical and Geopolitical Context

### Eastern Christians in the Seventeenth and Eighteenth Centuries

The world into which *Allatae Sunt* was published was marked by intricate religious and political arrangements in the territories where Eastern Christians lived. In the Ottoman Empire, which encompassed much of the Eastern Mediterranean, the Balkans, and parts of the Middle East, Christian communities—including Greek Orthodox, Armenians, Syriacs, and others—were organised into millets, or confessional communities, under recognized religious leaders who enjoyed a degree of internal autonomy in exchange for loyalty and the payment of taxes.[11] Armenian Christians, for instance, possessed a strong sense of communal identity mediated through their own liturgy, language, and institutions, including monasteries and schools.[8][14] The Vatican’s efforts to bring segments of these communities into communion with Rome generated both new Eastern Catholic churches and internal divisions within Eastern societies, as some groups aligned with the pope while others remained loyal to their traditional patriarchates.  

In Persia, where Safavid and later Afsharid and Zand rulers presided over a predominantly Muslim population, Armenian and other Christian communities also existed, particularly in trading centres such as Isfahan and later in the northwestern regions.[11] The Encyclopaedia Iranica notes that Roman Catholic clergy in Persia largely served the spiritual needs of foreign Catholics and of Armenian and Nestorian “uniates,” that is, Christians who accepted union with Rome while retaining their own rites.[11] These uniates occupied a liminal position: they were distinct from both the Latin missionaries and their fellow Armenians or “Nestorian” Christians who remained outside communion with the pope, and their allegiance to Rome could have implications for their local standing, especially where Orthodox patriarchates or other churches competed for influence.  

Further east, in India, the legacy of the ancient Church of the East among the so‑called Saint Thomas Christians had, by the eighteenth century, produced a complex configuration of communities, some in communion with Rome and others aligned with the Syriac Orthodox patriarchate.[9] The Syro‑Malabar Church, which would later be recognised as a *sui iuris* Eastern Catholic Church, traces its roots to those Saint Thomas Christians who entered communion with Rome after the schism of 1552 in the Church of the East and the subsequent missionary activities of Latin clergy in the Malabar region.[9] These communities, like their Armenian and Chaldean counterparts, grappled with the pressures of Latinisation and the tension between preserving their East Syriac rite and adapting to the expectations of Portuguese and other colonial ecclesiastical authorities.[9][13]  

In all these regions, Eastern Christian rites were deeply embedded in broader cultural patterns. Liturgical languages such as Armenian, Syriac, and Geʿez were markers of ethnic identity as well as vehicles of worship. Fasting regulations, feast days, and other forms of religious discipline structured social time and communal life, including patterns of work and festivity. The adoption of Latin practices therefore carried not only theological implications but also social and cultural ones: it could signal alignment with Western powers, participation in global Catholic networks, and even access to forms of patronage and education, but it might also appear as betrayal or alienation to those attached to traditional forms.[5][8][14] It is within this matrix of overlapping religious, ethnic, and political identities that the stakes of *Allatae Sunt* become fully apparent.  

### Rome’s Missionary Machinery: Propaganda Fide and Beyond

The Congregation de Propaganda Fide, established formally by Gregory XV in 1622, was the principal organ through which Rome coordinated its global missionary enterprise in the early modern period.[18] Before its formal establishment, Gregory XIII had instituted a commission—composed of three cardinals, Caraffa, Medici, and Santorio—charged with promoting the union of Eastern Christians with Rome; this body, sometimes referred to as a “primary commission,” laid the groundwork for later, more structured efforts to foster such unions and manage relations with Eastern churches.[18] Propaganda Fide’s responsibilities included the supervision of missionary activities, the appointment of vicars apostolic, and the issuing of decrees regulating interactions with local populations, including Eastern Christians under non‑Catholic jurisdictions.[18]  

Through Propaganda Fide, Rome collected reports from missionaries, deliberated on specific cases, and formulated general policies. This apparatus gave the papacy a more direct role in mission governance than had previously been exercised under decentralized systems such as the Portuguese Padroado, which had given the Portuguese crown considerable authority over ecclesiastical appointments and missionary work in its colonial territories.[13] In practice, however, jurisdictional tensions remained, as Propaganda Fide’s directives sometimes clashed with the interests of European monarchs and with the internal cultures of missionary orders such as the Jesuits, Franciscans, and Dominicans.  

Benedict XIV’s pontificate coincided with a period in which Propaganda Fide was attempting to correct the excesses of Latin missionaries and to reassert the Holy See’s control over matters such as rites, accommodations to local customs, and the boundaries of inculturation. The Malabar and Chinese rites controversies exemplify these tensions. In India, Latinising reforms such as the Synod of Diamper (1599) had imposed Latin liturgical books and disciplines on the Saint Thomas Christians, provoking resistance and eventual schism.[9][13] In response to subsequent developments, Benedict XIV’s *Demandatam* sought to curb abuses by Latin missionaries and to safeguard a reformed but still recognisably Eastern liturgical tradition among the Malabar Christians.[15][13] In China, the practice of allowing Chinese converts to participate in Confucian rites honouring ancestors and Confucius was controversially tolerated by Jesuits but condemned by other orders; Benedict XIV’s interventions there likewise attempted to set clear boundaries.[13][15]  

It is in this context of heightened concern with the regulation of rites that *Allatae Sunt* must be read. The encyclical refers explicitly to decrees of Propaganda Fide, especially the 1702 instruction forbidding missionaries from dispensing Eastern Christians from observing their rites, and presents itself as reaffirming and consolidating this line of policy.[1][15][18] It also reflects the growing body of scholarship that the congregation sponsored on Eastern liturgies and canon law, with figures like Giuseppe Simone Assemani—himself a Maronite and a key librarian of the Vatican—producing monumental editions of Eastern texts that informed Roman judgments.[15] The encyclical thus stands at the intersection of bureaucratic governance, scholarly knowledge production, and pastoral concern, all mediated through the structures of Propaganda Fide.  

### Latinisation as a Seventeenth–Eighteenth Century Process

The term **Latinisation of liturgy** is used by contemporary scholars to describe the process by which non‑Latin Christian traditions, particularly those of Eastern churches, adopted elements of the Latin Church’s liturgical practices, theology, and customs, often under external pressure.[13] The phenomenon is widely documented in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries in regions where Eastern Christians came under the influence of Roman Catholic missionaries or entered into union with Rome. The Wikipedia synthesis on Latinisation notes that this process sometimes facilitated closer ties with the Catholic Church but also led to the suppression of local liturgical traditions and sparked significant debates over ecclesial identity.[13]  

More specialised scholarship has examined Latinisation in specific contexts, such as the Greek Catholic or “Uniate” churches in Eastern Europe. One study on the evolution of Latinisation of the Greek Catholic Church in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries argues that adopting Latin practices became a factor in the spiritual and cultural separation of Uniate communities from their Orthodox counterparts.[5] According to this analysis, Latinisation often accompanied broader processes of Westernisation and was sometimes perceived by local elites as a path to social prestige or political advantage, especially where alignment with Catholic monarchies promised protection or promotion.[5] At the same time, Latinising reforms could provoke internal resistance, as segments of the faithful and clergy sought to defend their ancestral rites against what they saw as foreign innovations.[5][13]  

In the context of the Armenians, Latin influence was felt through the activities of religious orders, particularly the Jesuits and later the Mekhitarist Congregation, as well as through diplomatic and commercial contacts with Catholic Europe.[8][14] Among the Saint Thomas Christians of India, Latinisation took an institutional form through the imposition of the Roman Missal and other Latin books following the Synod of Diamper, leading to centuries of contestation and eventual partial restoration of East Syriac usages under Roman oversight.[9][13] Even where Eastern communities retained their own liturgical languages and some core rituals, Latinising tendencies could be seen in the adoption of Latin devotions, Western architectural styles, clerical dress, and sacramental disciplines.  

Benedict XIV’s insistence in *Allatae Sunt* that Eastern Catholics should adhere to their own fasting rules, calendars, and sacramental customs must be understood as a direct response to these Latinising pressures.[1][15] By the mid‑eighteenth century, it was evident in Rome that many missionaries, whether out of zeal, ignorance, or a belief in the superiority of Latin practices, had introduced innovations that went beyond what the Holy See had authorised. The encyclical’s repeated injunction “no innovations are to be made” can thus be read as an attempt to reverse or at least arrest a decades‑long trend of Latinisation that had threatened to erase the distinctive liturgical heritage of Eastern Catholic communities.[1][15][13]  

### Armenian Catholic Reawakening and Union with Rome

The Armenian Catholic Church provides a particularly relevant case study for understanding *Allatae Sunt*’s context. The Wikipedia article on the Armenian Catholic Church identifies it as an Eastern Catholic particular church *sui iuris* in full communion with the bishop of Rome, alongside the Latin Church and twenty‑two other Eastern Catholic churches.[8] The church’s modern form took shape primarily in the eighteenth century, when segments of the Armenian Apostolic Church entered into union with Rome, often with support from Catholic powers and missionaries.[8][14]  

A Vatican Press summary on the history of the Church in Armenia notes that the eighteenth century saw a religious and cultural reawakening thanks largely to the priest Mekhitar of Sebaste, who, after converting to Catholicism, founded the Mekhitarist Congregation.[14] This order, based first in Modon and later on the island of San Lazzaro in Venice, became a centre of Armenian Catholic scholarship, printing, and education, producing grammars, histories, and liturgical books that helped to shape a distinct Armenian Catholic identity.[14] While the Mekhitarists cultivated loyalty to Rome, they also championed the Armenian language and many traditional forms, illustrating the complex interplay between union with the papacy and the preservation of national culture.  

However, union with Rome also generated internal conflicts within Armenian communities. Some Armenian bishops and clergy opposed union and viewed Armenian Catholics as schismatics or traitors, leading to episodes of persecution and forced relocation.[14] The Holy See intervened repeatedly to support Armenian Catholics, to regularise their hierarchy, and to address disciplinary questions within their communities. A collection of papal teachings assembled by Benedictine scholars includes letters of Benedict XIV to Armenian Catholics, which address discord in the Armenian Church, measures taken to stop it, and the creation of a seminary in Constantinople to train Armenian Catholic clergy.[17] This demonstrates that Benedict XIV was personally engaged with Armenian Catholic affairs and saw the need for structured formation of clergy who could uphold both Catholic doctrine and Armenian liturgical traditions.  

*Allatae Sunt* explicitly addresses the situation of Armenians among other Eastern Catholics and can be seen as part of this broader pattern of engagement.[1][15] By insisting that Armenian Catholics must preserve their rites and reject arbitrary Latinisation, Benedict XIV both protects their ecclesial identity and signals to non‑Catholic Armenians that union with Rome does not entail the abandonment of national and ritual heritage. In doing so, he attempts to position the Holy See as a guarantor of Armenian tradition, in contrast to some Latin missionaries whose actions had fuelled fears that Catholicism was incompatible with Armenian customs.[14][17] This stance also intersects with the politics of the Ottoman millet system, in which recognition of Armenian Catholic distinctiveness had implications for communal autonomy and for the distribution of privileges within the empire.  

### Filioque, Calendars, and Fasting: Doctrinal and Disciplinary Fault Lines

Several specific issues treated in *Allatae Sunt*—the *Filioque* clause in the Creed, the use of the Gregorian calendar, and fasting regulations—had long histories of contention between East and West. The *Filioque* controversy centred on the Latin addition of the words “and from the Son” to the Niceno‑Constantinopolitan Creed’s article about the procession of the Holy Spirit.[10] While the doctrine that the Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son had been affirmed in the West and declared dogmatically by Pope Leo I in the mid‑fifth century, its inclusion in the Creed text emerged later, particularly in an anti‑Arian context in seventh‑century Spain.[10] Many Eastern Orthodox Christians later argued that adding the *Filioque* violated the Council of Ephesus’s prohibition on altering the Creed and challenged the unilateral nature of the change.[10]  

By Benedict XIV’s time, Rome regarded the *Filioque* both as doctrinally non‑negotiable and as a potential stumbling block in relations with Eastern Christians. *Allatae Sunt* reflects this complexity. The pope insists that Eastern Catholics must accept the doctrine that the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son, but he adopts a more flexible stance regarding the insertion of the *Filioque* into the Creed itself, sometimes permitting its omission to avoid scandal or confusion where its absence did not imply heresy.[1][10][15] This measured approach reveals a sensitivity to symbolic and ecumenical considerations, and a recognition that not every difference in liturgical wording necessarily constitutes a doctrinal rupture.  

The calendar question was similarly fraught. The Gregorian reform of the calendar, introduced by Pope Gregory XIII in 1582, had been adopted by Catholic countries and some Protestant states, but many Eastern churches continued to use the Julian calendar, resulting in divergences in the dates of feasts and fasts.[13] Some Eastern Catholic communities accepted the Gregorian calendar, while others remained on the Julian system, creating practical difficulties in mixed settings.[1] In *Allatae Sunt*, Benedict XIV leans toward respecting established usage: he generally does not insist that Eastern Catholics adopt the Gregorian calendar unless there are compelling reasons and explicit papal provisions to that effect, preferring instead to tolerate diversity in calendrical observance.[1][15]  

Fasting practices constituted another salient area of difference. Byzantine and other Eastern traditions often prescribed stricter and more frequent fasts than the Latin Church, including extended Lenten periods and weekly fasts on Wednesdays and Fridays, with strict abstinence from meat, dairy, and sometimes fish.[12] The website Byzantine Villages, presenting a contemporary explanation of Eastern fasting seasons, notes that the Typikon prescribes strict abstinence on certain days and more lenient allowances on others, illustrating the complexity and rigor of Eastern discipline.[12] In the Basra case, Armenian and Syrian Catholics appear to have been relaxing their fasting obligations under the influence of Latin practice, perhaps finding the latter more congenial to their circumstances.[1] Benedict XIV’s insistence that they must return to their own discipline can thus be seen as both a moral exhortation and an assertion of ecclesial identity through ascetic practice.[1][15]  

Each of these issues—*Filioque*, calendars, fasting—exemplifies the balancing act that Rome attempted in its dealings with Eastern Catholics. Doctrinal questions like the *Filioque* demanded a baseline of assent; disciplinary matters like calendars and fasting allowed for greater diversity, but such diversity had to be carefully managed to avoid scandal and to maintain the visibility of communion in faith amid visible differences in practice.[1][10][12][15] *Allatae Sunt*’s detailed treatment of these matters thus serves as a microcosm of the broader tensions shaping Eastern‑Western relations in the eighteenth century.  

## Reflective Character: What *Allatae Sunt* Looks Back Upon

### Historical Precedents and the Construction of Continuity

A striking feature of *Allatae Sunt* is its extensive use of historical precedents to construct a narrative of unbroken papal policy on Eastern rites. Benedict XIV cites medieval and early modern popes to show that Rome has long distinguished between doctrinal error, which must be eliminated, and liturgical diversity, which can and should be preserved.[1][15] For instance, he recalls how popes negotiating with Eastern Christians after the schism or during union councils such as Lyons (1274) and Florence (1439) often confirmed the rites of those who submitted to Rome, provided they accepted defined dogmas on points like the papal primacy and the procession of the Holy Spirit.[1][10][15]  

This retrospective strategy serves several purposes. It legitimates Benedict XIV’s own rulings by casting them as the latest expression of a stable tradition rather than as innovations driven by contemporary politics. It also counters opponents who might argue that preserving Eastern rites is a concession to weakness or a modern expedient; by appealing to the authority of revered predecessors, the pope frames his policy as rooted in the Church’s long‑standing practice.[15] At the same time, the very need to assemble this historical brief suggests that the continuity he asserts was not always obvious or uniformly observed in practice, particularly in the wake of early modern missionary expansion, when many Latin clergy acted with a de facto assumption of Latin superiority.[5][13][15]  

The encyclical’s historical section is therefore reflective in a double sense. It looks back at ecclesial events—councils, papal bulls, Propaganda Fide decrees—but also tacitly reflects on recent failures to implement those principles. By revisiting earlier proclamations with renewed emphasis, Benedict XIV implicitly acknowledges that they have not been adequately heeded. This is particularly evident in his treatment of the 1702 decree of Propaganda Fide, which had already prohibited missionaries from altering Eastern rites but which, in his narrative, had either been forgotten or insufficiently disseminated.[1][15][18] The encyclical thus functions as a corrective gloss on the record of the previous half‑century of missionary practice, using the weight of history to call the present back into line.  

### Concrete Abuses and Latinising Excesses

While *Allatae Sunt* is couched in general terms, it is clear from contemporary scholarship that Benedict XIV is responding to concrete abuses and Latinising excesses in multiple regions. The study on Latinisation of the Greek Catholic Church in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, for instance, documents how Uniate clergy in Eastern Europe gradually introduced Latin devotions, altered liturgical art and vestments, and modified sacramental practices in ways that distanced them from their Orthodox neighbours.[5] These changes often stemmed from a desire to align more visibly with Rome and to benefit from the support of Catholic powers such as the Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth or the Habsburgs, but they also provoked backlash from those who saw them as unnecessary or as betrayals of Eastern tradition.[5]  

Similarly, the history of the Syro‑Malabar Church in India shows that Latinisation had a profound impact on the identity of Saint Thomas Christians. After segments of this community entered communion with Rome in the sixteenth century, Latin missionaries increasingly imposed Latin books and rituals, culminating in deep tensions and schisms.[9][13] Although later Roman interventions, including those under Benedict XIV, sought to restore some East Syriac forms and to regulate missionary behaviour, much damage had already been done, and some practices and texts were permanently lost.[9][13][15]  

The Armenian Catholic experience likewise involved complex negotiations over Latin influence. While the Mekhitarists preserved Armenian language and some ritual customs, other Armenian Catholics adopted Latin devotions and theological categories, sometimes provoking criticism from non‑Catholic Armenians who feared erosion of their patrimony.[14][17] Benedict XIV’s letters to Armenian Catholics, as preserved in collections of papal teaching, indicate his awareness of internal discord and his desire to foster unity without obliterating Armenian specificity.[17]  

Within *Allatae Sunt*, these concrete histories are distilled into references to abuses such as missionaries encouraging Eastern Christians to adopt Latin fasting rules, introducing Latin ceremonies into Eastern liturgies, or allowing Latin priests to celebrate Eastern rites without proper authority.[1][15] The Basra case is emblematic but not unique; it stands as a synecdoche for similar patterns across the Eastern Catholic world. The encyclical’s reflective character here is practical and diagnostic: it acknowledges that the problem is not mere theoretical confusion but the proliferation of habits and practices that have already taken root and must now be reversed or restrained.  

### Relation to the Chinese and Malabar Rites Controversies

Another dimension of *Allatae Sunt*’s reflective character is its relationship to the earlier Chinese and Malabar rites controversies, which had rocked the Catholic missionary world in the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries. Although the encyclical does not dwell on these issues explicitly, Benedict XIV’s sacramental policy on Eastern churches, as analysed by scholars at the Sheptytsky Institute, clearly operates against the backdrop of these debates.[15] In both China and India, the central question was how far missionaries could go in adapting to local customs and in “translating” Christian practice into culturally familiar forms.  

In China, Jesuit missionaries had, for decades, permitted converts to participate in Confucian rites honouring ancestors and Confucius, arguing that these were civil rather than religious ceremonies and compatible with Christian faith.[13] Opponents, including Dominicans and Franciscans, denounced these accommodations as idolatrous. After a series of investigations and decrees, Benedict XIV ultimately sided with the stricter camp, clarifying the limits of permissible rites and emphasising the importance of avoiding practices that could be interpreted as religious syncretism.[13][15]  

In the Malabar region of India, the situation was somewhat different. There, the rites in question belonged not to non‑Christian cultures but to an ancient Christian community with its own East Syriac liturgy and traditions. Latin missionaries, empowered by colonial structures, had altered these practices to conform to Roman norms, prompting resistance from local clergy and laity.[9][13] Benedict XIV’s *Demandatam* attempted to regularise the situation by both correcting certain practices and affirming others, but the general tenor was one of caution against arbitrary Latinisation and of respect for legitimate Eastern customs.[15]  

*Allatae Sunt* can be seen as a reflective generalisation of lessons learned from these controversies. The encyclical implies that there is a crucial difference between accommodating non‑Christian customs for the sake of mission, which must be strictly limited, and respecting the established rites of Eastern Christians, which must be defended against Latinising encroachment.[1][15] By drawing this line, Benedict XIV implicitly acknowledges that earlier missionary practice had sometimes failed to make this distinction, either by being too lax with respect to non‑Christian rites (as in the Chinese case, from Rome’s perspective) or too aggressive in suppressing Christian rites perceived as “foreign” (as in Malabar and other Eastern settings).[13][15] The document thus reflects on a period of experimentation and contestation in missionary methods and attempts to codify a more stable and principled approach.  

### The Anthropology of Eastern Christians in Roman Eyes

Beneath its juridical language, *Allatae Sunt* exhibits a particular implicit anthropology of Eastern Christians, shaped by Roman perceptions and by the reports of missionaries. The encyclical assumes that rites are deeply tied to the identity of peoples and that disrupting those rites can have far‑reaching consequences for communal cohesion and for the credibility of the Church’s claim to be a universal, rather than merely Western, institution.[1][15] When Benedict XIV insists that the Church desires that “all be Catholic rather than all become Latin,” he is not only making a theological point but also recognising the importance of allowing Armenians, Syrians, Greeks, and other Eastern peoples to remain themselves within the Catholic fold.[1]  

This perspective contrasts with some missionary attitudes that saw Latinisation as a form of civilising or modernising Eastern Christians. In regions where European colonial powers claimed superiority in science, technology, and governance, Latin rites could be perceived as part of a broader package of Western civilisation to which Eastern converts were expected to conform.[5][13] By pushing back against such attitudes, *Allatae Sunt* reflects a more complex Roman self‑conception: the papacy presents itself as supra‑cultural, governing a Church that is not bound to any one rite or nation, even though its own institutional structures remained heavily Latin in practice.[1][15][6]  

At the same time, the encyclical reflects certain hierarchies and anxieties. Benedict XIV is concerned about scandal: he fears that Eastern Orthodox and other non‑Catholic Christians will interpret Latinising demands as proof that Rome does not truly respect their traditions, thereby reinforcing separation.[1][10][15] He also worries that excessive mixing of rites within the Catholic Church could create confusion among the faithful and make it harder to explain the Church’s teaching on unity.[1] These concerns reveal an anthropology in which Eastern Christians are seen as both vulnerable—to pressures from Latin clergy and to the influence of surrounding non‑Catholics—and as agents whose choices can affect the Church’s mission among their compatriots. The encyclical’s reflective engagement with Eastern Christianity thus oscillates between respect and paternalism, between appreciation of cultural richness and a desire to control and channel it within Roman frameworks.  

## Prospective Character: The Future Benedict XIV Tries to Shape

### Norms for Missionaries: Curbing Zeal with Law

The prescriptive dimension of *Allatae Sunt* comes most clearly to the fore in its directives to missionaries. Benedict XIV does not merely recount past decrees; he addresses the missionaries of his own time directly, instructing them on what they must do and what they must refrain from doing when working among Eastern Catholics and among Eastern Christians who might one day enter communion with Rome.[1][15] The underlying assumption is that missionary zeal, left unchecked, tends to overstep boundaries and to seek uniformity where diversity is permissible. Law, in this vision, is needed to curb that zeal and to ensure that the mission’s long‑term success is not compromised by short‑sighted attempts at cultural homogenisation.  

The pope particularly forbids missionaries from engaging in what might be called liturgical proselytism. They are not to persuade Eastern Christians to adopt the Latin rite, nor are they to present such adoption as a condition for full communion or as a sign of greater holiness.[1] They are likewise prohibited from issuing dispensations from Eastern fasting rules, matrimonial regulations, or other disciplines under the pretext of pastoral leniency, unless such dispensations are authorised by the competent Eastern hierarchs or by Rome itself.[1][15] These prescriptions are forward‑looking in that they seek to set a pattern of missionary conduct that, if followed, would gradually realign missionary culture with Roman policy and help to heal the rifts caused by earlier Latinising measures.  

From a historical perspective, the effectiveness of these directives was mixed. In some contexts, particularly where Propaganda Fide had direct oversight and where missionary orders were more tightly integrated into Roman structures, Benedict XIV’s policy contributed to a greater awareness of the legitimacy of Eastern rites.[15] In other settings, especially in territories under strong colonial patronage or where Latin clergy enjoyed significant autonomy, Latinisation continued well into the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, often despite repeated Roman exhortations.[5][9][13] The prospective character of *Allatae Sunt* is thus best understood as an attempt to redirect a powerful missionary current rather than as a decisive turning point that immediately changed practice on the ground.  

### Eastern Catholics as Custodians of Their Own Tradition

*Allatae Sunt* does not place all responsibility on missionaries; it also addresses Eastern Catholics themselves, exhorting and commanding them to be faithful custodians of their traditions. Benedict XIV insists that Eastern clergy must celebrate their own rites in full, without adding Latin prayers or modifying rituals to imitate Latin usage.[1][15] He urges Eastern laity to observe their fasting and penitential practices, even if these are more burdensome than those of their Latin neighbours, and to resist the temptation to seek dispensations for reasons of comfort or social conformity.[1][12] In doing so, he articulates a prospective vision of Eastern Catholics as active agents in the preservation of their heritage rather than passive victims of Latinising pressures.  

This vision has both spiritual and political dimensions. Spiritually, the pope frames fidelity to rite as an expression of obedience to the Church and of respect for the providence that has entrusted each community with its own ancient patrimony.[1][15] Politically, in the broad sense of ecclesial politics, his stance strengthens the hand of Eastern hierarchs and communities that wish to resist Latinisation, giving them a papal mandate to insist on their rights. Over the long term, this perspective would find fuller institutional expression in the recognition of Eastern Catholic churches as *sui iuris*, or self‑governing, within the Catholic communion, a principle affirmed in the Code of Canons of the Eastern Churches (CCEO) promulgated in 1990.[9] The Syro‑Malabar Church, for example, is today recognised as a *sui iuris* church with its own major archbishop and governance structures under the CCEO, embodying a model of Eastern Catholic autonomy that *Allatae Sunt* foreshadowed conceptually, even if not yet juridically.[9]  

However, the encyclical’s prospective empowerment of Eastern Catholics also came with constraints. Benedict XIV sets clear limits on innovation from within Eastern traditions, rejecting any attempts by Eastern clergy to alter their rites arbitrarily or to introduce practices borrowed from the Latin Church without authorisation.[1][15] In effect, he encourages Eastern Catholics to be guardians, not authors, of tradition, leaving Rome as the ultimate arbiter of what in their heritage is to be preserved, purified, or modified. The future he envisages is therefore one in which Eastern Catholic identity is both affirmed and supervised, a dynamic that would continue to shape Eastern‑Roman relations into the modern era.  

### Anticipated Risks: Schism, Scandal, and the Loss of Credibility

Implicit in *Allatae Sunt*’s prescriptive sections is a set of perceived risks that Benedict XIV seeks to forestall. One is the risk of renewed or deepened schism. If Eastern Orthodox or other non‑Catholic Eastern Christians observe that those of their brethren who unite with Rome are required to abandon their rites or are gradually Latinised, they may be confirmed in their hostility to union and may portray Rome as an enemy of their traditions.[1][10][15] By publicly declaring that the Church does not demand such abandonments and by condemning Latinising abuses, Benedict XIV attempts to remove an obstacle to future unions and to demonstrate the papacy’s goodwill toward Eastern peoples.  

Another risk is internal scandal among Catholics. Mixed‑rite communities can become breeding grounds for misunderstanding and mutual hostility if members perceive one another’s practices as lax, excessive, or deviant. In Basra, the fact that Armenian and Syrian Catholics were seen eating foods forbidden by their own discipline while worshipping in a Latin church raised concerns not only about their personal obedience but also about the example they set for others.[1][12] By clarifying that Eastern Catholics must follow their own rules and that Latins must not interfere with them, *Allatae Sunt* aims to prevent such scandal and to maintain clear lines of responsibility.  

A third risk is the erosion of the Church’s claim to universality. If the visible face of Catholicism in the East becomes indistinguishable from that of the Latin Church, Rome may be perceived as a Western or Latin institution rather than as the centre of a truly global communion. This perception could weaken the Church’s appeal and hinder its mission among peoples who cherish their distinct cultures and rites.[5][13][15] Benedict XIV’s prospective strategy, therefore, is not only to preserve diversity for its own sake but to harness it as evidence of the Church’s capacity to reconcile multiplicity under a single doctrinal and institutional head. Whether this strategy succeeded in the centuries that followed is a complex question; in many contexts, Latinisation overwhelmed local rites, while in others Eastern Catholic identity persisted and even strengthened, particularly in the twentieth century.[5][8][9][13]  

### Specific Prescripts: Creed, Calendar, Fasting, and Women at the Altar

The prescriptive character of *Allatae Sunt* is perhaps most concrete in its rulings on specific liturgical and disciplinary questions. Regarding the Creed and the *Filioque*, Benedict XIV adopts a nuanced approach: he requires Eastern Catholics to affirm the doctrine that the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son, in line with Western dogmatic teaching, but allows some flexibility about whether the *Filioque* must be inserted into the Creed text in all circumstances.[1][10][15] This anticipates later ecumenical dialogues in which Catholic theologians would explore ways of expressing the same doctrine in terms acceptable to both East and West, for example by emphasising the Father as the single source of the Trinity while confessing the Spirit’s eternal relationship to the Son.[10] The encyclical thus hints at a future in which doctrinal unity might coexist with liturgical and terminological diversity.  

On the calendar, Benedict XIV largely eschews coercion. While recognising the practical advantages of a unified calendar, he generally does not compel Eastern Catholics to adopt the Gregorian reform unless the Holy See has specifically mandated it in a given case.[1][15] Instead, he endorses the principle of *status quo* for calendar usage, thereby shaping a future in which different Catholic communities might celebrate feasts on different days while remaining in full communion. This principle has indeed persisted: even today, some Eastern Catholic churches follow the Julian or revised Julian calendar for liturgical purposes, resulting in different dates for Easter and other feasts compared to the Latin Church.  

Fasting receives particular emphasis. Benedict XIV’s directive that Eastern Catholics must observe their own fasts, even when living among Latins, is designed to shape individual and communal behaviour in a way that reinforces Eastern identity.[1][12][15] In the long run, however, the rigor of traditional Eastern fasting has come under pressure, both from changing social conditions and from internal reforms. Contemporary Eastern Catholic communities vary in their adherence to strict fasting rules, with some maintaining traditional patterns and others adopting mitigations.[12] The encyclical’s prospective call for strict observance thus had a mixed legacy, reflecting the tension between ideal and practice.  

A more delicate and controversial topic concerns the role of women in liturgical service. While the main thrust of *Allatae Sunt* is about rites and calendars, later commentators, particularly in traditionalist circles, have pointed to passages in which Benedict XIV condemns the practice of women serving at the altar as an abuse, asserting that the Church’s discipline has always excluded women from such roles.[2] A modern Facebook post, for example, claims that in *Allatae Sunt* Benedict XIV reaffirmed the Church’s perennial discipline that women may not serve the priest at the altar and condemned such a practice as an “abuse.”[2] This interpretation reflects how the encyclical has been retrospectively mobilised in contemporary debates over liturgical gender roles. In terms of its eighteenth‑century prospective character, Benedict XIV’s stance on this issue aligned with prevailing Latin discipline, which reserved altar service to males, and he evidently wished to reinforce this across rites.[1][2][15]  

However, the long‑term trajectory of Catholic practice complicates this picture. While the 1917 Code of Canon Law codified the exclusion of women from serving at the altar, later canonical developments culminated in a 1994 clarification allowing bishops to permit the use of female altar servers in the Latin Church, and more recent decisions have expanded women’s access to instituted ministries.[2] Eastern Catholic churches have varied in their own practices. Thus, while *Allatae Sunt* sought to shape a future in which women did not serve at the altar, this particular prospective vision has not been sustained universally within Catholicism, illustrating how some of its prescriptive elements have been superseded or reinterpreted over time.  

## Reception, Implementation, and Long‑Term Legacy

### Immediate and Short‑Term Impact in the Late Eighteenth Century

Assessing the immediate reception of *Allatae Sunt* is challenging due to the relative paucity of non‑theological contemporary sources that comment explicitly on papal encyclicals of this period. There is little evidence that the document attracted attention in secular press or diplomatic correspondence; its subject matter—liturgical rites among Eastern Catholics—was of limited interest to non‑specialists compared to more sensational disputes over Jansenism or state–church conflicts in Europe.[6][15] Within ecclesial circles, however, the encyclical appears to have been circulated among missionaries and Eastern Catholic hierarchs as a normative statement of policy.  

The records of Propaganda Fide, as catalogued by modern archivists, show ongoing correspondence with missionaries concerning Eastern rites and frequent recourse to earlier decrees and papal instructions when adjudicating new cases.[18] It is reasonable to infer that *Allatae Sunt* entered into this repertoire of reference texts, especially in matters involving calendars, shared churches, and rite switching. Benedict XIV’s broader reputation as a learned and judicious pope likely enhanced the document’s authority among clergy, even if its practical implementation varied.[6]  

In Armenian Catholic circles, Benedict XIV’s interventions, including but not limited to *Allatae Sunt*, contributed to the consolidation of a recognised Armenian Catholic hierarchy and the formation of a seminary in Constantinople, as attested by papal letters collected in twentieth‑century anthologies.[17] The encyclical’s defense of Armenian rites against Latinisation would have bolstered those Armenian Catholic leaders who sought to resist pressures to conform too closely to Latin patterns. Similarly, in Syro‑Malabar and other Eastern Catholic communities, Benedict XIV’s general policy of respecting Eastern traditions likely informed local debates, although specific evidence of *Allatae Sunt* being cited in those contexts is limited in the available sources.[9][15]  

### Nineteenth‑Century Latinisation and the Limits of Papal Enforcement

The nineteenth century witnessed both the expansion of Eastern Catholic churches and the continuation, and in some cases intensification, of Latinisation, particularly in regions under the influence of new missionary orders or under the control of Catholic or colonial powers that favoured Latin uniformity.[5][9][13] In the Habsburg Empire, for example, Greek Catholic churches among Ruthenians and other Eastern Slavs experienced significant Latinisation under the influence of state policies aimed at centralisation and integration, with Latin devotions and practices becoming widespread.[5] Similarly, in India, the Syro‑Malabar Church underwent further Latinising reforms even as some East Syriac elements were preserved or reintroduced.[9][13]  

In this context, *Allatae Sunt*’s impact appears to have been limited by the structural realities of ecclesial power. While popes and Propaganda Fide issued instructions and occasionally intervened to protect Eastern rites, enforcement on the ground depended heavily on local bishops, missionary orders, and political authorities. Where these actors were sympathetic to Eastern traditions, the encyclical’s principles could be invoked to legitimise resistance to Latinisation. Where they were not, papal documents could be ignored or selectively interpreted.  

Moreover, the papacy itself sometimes sent mixed signals. In the nineteenth century, Rome supported initiatives to revive Eastern monasticism and liturgy in some contexts, while in others it appeared to tolerate or even encourage Latinising tendencies, especially where these seemed to serve broader goals of ecclesial discipline or national integration.[5][13] As a result, *Allatae Sunt* functioned more as a touchstone for a particular ideal of unity‑in‑diversity than as a uniformly applied policy. Its prospective vision of a Catholic Church in which Eastern rites would flourish under Roman protection remained only partially realised.  

### Twentieth‑Century Re‑Evaluation: From Leo XIII to Vatican II and the CCEO

The twentieth century brought significant shifts in the Catholic Church’s approach to Eastern rites, many of which echo and, in some cases, explicitly retrieve the principles articulated in *Allatae Sunt*. Pope Leo XIII’s encyclical *Orientalium Dignitas* (1894), for example, strongly condemned Latinisation and affirmed the equal dignity of Eastern rites within the Catholic Church, insisting that they should be preserved and not absorbed into the Latin tradition.[13] This document is often cited by scholars as a foundational modern statement of Eastern Catholic identity and can be seen as a more forceful restatement of Benedict XIV’s earlier stance.[13][15]  

The establishment of the Congregation for the Eastern Churches as a distinct dicastery in 1917, separate from Propaganda Fide, further institutionalised Rome’s commitment to the particular care of Eastern Catholic churches.[4] The Catholic Near East Welfare Association (CNEWA), in its overview of this congregation, notes that it is housed in the historic Palazzo dei Convertendi in Rome and is responsible for relations with Eastern Catholic churches, including oversight of their internal life and support for their needs.[4] This institutional arrangement reflects a recognition that Eastern Catholicism required dedicated attention and structures, not merely a subsection of general missionary policy.  

The Second Vatican Council’s decree *Orientalium Ecclesiarum* (1964) represents a high point in the affirmation of Eastern Catholic identity, explicitly calling Eastern Catholics to “preserve their legitimate liturgical rite and their established way of life” and instructing that they should “attain to an ever greater knowledge and a more exact use of their rites.”[13] Although this conciliar document lies far beyond *Allatae Sunt* in time, it resonates strongly with Benedict XIV’s insistence that Eastern Catholics must not abandon their rites and that missionaries must respect them.[1][15] The subsequent promulgation of the Code of Canons of the Eastern Churches (CCEO) in 1990 further codified Eastern autonomy, recognising twenty‑three Eastern Catholic churches, including the Armenian Catholic Church and the Syro‑Malabar Church, as *sui iuris* bodies with their own hierarchy and internal law.[8][9]  

In this twentieth‑century re‑evaluation, *Allatae Sunt* is often cited in academic and ecclesiastical discussions as an early and important expression of the principle that “all must be Catholic, not all Latin.”[1][13][15] While later documents go further in affirming Eastern self‑governance and in repudiating Latinisation, they stand in a line of continuity with Benedict XIV’s encyclical, which can thus be seen as a forerunner of modern Catholic teaching on the dignity and rights of Eastern rites.  

### Contemporary Invocations: Latinisation Debates and Gender Roles

In recent decades, *Allatae Sunt* has surfaced in at least two kinds of contemporary debates. The first concerns ongoing questions of Latinisation and identity in Eastern Catholic churches. Apologetic and catechetical materials aimed at Latin Catholics, such as the article “The Rite Not to Be Roman” in Catholic Answers Magazine, have drawn on the history of Eastern Catholicism to challenge the assumption that “Catholic” is synonymous with “Roman” and to emphasise the value of Eastern rites.[3] Such writings often reference historical papal support for Eastern traditions, implicitly or explicitly invoking the trajectory that includes *Allatae Sunt*, Leo XIII’s *Orientalium Dignitas*, and Vatican II’s *Orientalium Ecclesiarum*.[3][13]  

Within Eastern Catholic communities themselves, movements to de‑Latinise and recover traditional liturgical practices sometimes appeal to Benedict XIV’s principle that no innovations should be introduced into Eastern rites and that their authentic forms should be preserved.[1][13][15] These efforts can involve revising liturgical books, restoring ancient chant and iconography, and re‑emphasising traditional fasting and sacramental practices, including the administration of confirmation and Eucharist to infants, as is customary in many Eastern rites.[12][13] The encyclical’s prescriptive emphasis on fidelity to ancestral customs thus finds new relevance as Eastern Catholics negotiate their identity within a global Catholic Church that has become more open to diversity.  

The second type of contemporary invocation is more contentious and concerns gender roles in liturgy. As mentioned earlier, some traditionalist Catholic voices have appealed to *Allatae Sunt* as evidence of a “perennial discipline” excluding women from serving at the altar.[2] Social‑media posts and online discussions sometimes cite Benedict XIV’s condemnation of women acting as altar servers in certain Eastern contexts as an authoritative statement for all times.[2] This use of the encyclical illustrates how historical papal documents can be selectively mobilised to support positions in current intra‑Catholic debates, sometimes without full consideration of contexts, subsequent magisterial developments, or the differentiation between doctrinal and disciplinary matters.  

From a research perspective, this phenomenon highlights the need to distinguish between the encyclical’s original prospective aims and its later reception. While Benedict XIV did intend to shape future practice with respect to women at the altar within his own time and context, the Catholic Church’s later evolution on this disciplinary matter, especially in the Latin Church, indicates that his prescriptions were not understood as immutable doctrine but as changeable discipline.[2][15] This contrast underscores a general point: *Allatae Sunt*’s prospective character is temporally bounded. Its impact on the long‑term future of Catholic practice varies across issues, with some principles enduring and others being superseded or modified in light of new circumstances and theological reflection.  

## Secular Entanglements and Wider Social Significance

### Missionary Policy, Empire, and Local Societies

Although *Allatae Sunt* does not explicitly discuss secular politics, its subject matter—the regulation of rites among Eastern Catholics—intersects with broader dynamics of empire, trade, and local society. In Ottoman and Persian territories, Eastern Christians occupied defined socio‑legal niches under Islamic rule, with their communal status often tied to recognised religious structures.[11] The emergence of Eastern Catholic communities, and Rome’s efforts to protect their rites, could both stabilise and destabilise these arrangements. On the one hand, a clear papal commitment to preserving Armenian or Syriac rites might reassure Muslim authorities that union with Rome did not entail wholesale cultural change that could disrupt social order. On the other hand, the alignment of some Eastern Christians with a foreign religious head—the pope—could arouse suspicion about political loyalties, especially in contexts of European–Ottoman rivalry.  

In colonial settings, particularly in India, missionary policy was intertwined with the aims of European powers. Latinisation of the Saint Thomas Christians in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries served, in part, to integrate them more tightly into the Portuguese imperial religious framework, aligning them with Roman Catholicism as understood and administered through Padroado structures.[9][13] Benedict XIV’s later insistence in *Allatae Sunt* and related documents that Eastern rites should be preserved can be read as a subtle challenge to those colonial religious policies, reasserting Rome’s prerogative over local ecclesial identity against both civil and ecclesiastical colonial actors.[15] However, the pope’s capacity to enforce his vision was constrained by geopolitical realities, and in many cases colonial patterns of Latinisation persisted despite Roman reservations.  

The encyclical’s concern with fasting and calendrical observance also has social implications. Fasting practices influence labour patterns, dietary habits, and even economic activity, particularly in agrarian societies where feast days and fasts structure the work year. Eastern Christians under Ottoman or Persian rule often lived alongside Muslims with their own Ramadan fasts and feast days, as well as Jews and other groups with distinct calendars. Benedict XIV’s insistence that Eastern Catholics maintain their own fasting discipline therefore contributes to the perpetuation of a pluralistic pattern of social time, with multiple religious calendars coexisting within shared spaces.[1][11][12] This has implications for how communities coordinate economic life and negotiate public holidays, even if such matters are not addressed directly in the encyclical.  

### Eastern Catholic Identity, Ethnicity, and Nationhood

The preservation or alteration of rites among Eastern Christians is closely linked to questions of ethnicity and, in some cases, emerging forms of nationhood. The Armenian case again offers a clear example. Armenian liturgy, language, and literary traditions form a core component of Armenian identity, regardless of whether Armenians belong to the Armenian Apostolic Church or the Armenian Catholic Church.[8][14] By defending Armenian rites against Latinisation, *Allatae Sunt* and related papal policies contribute indirectly to the preservation of Armenian cultural distinctiveness, even as they integrate Armenian Catholics into a wider Catholic communion.  

This has long‑term consequences. In the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, Armenian Catholics played roles in national movements and in diasporic communities, leveraging their connections to Rome and to Western powers while maintaining Armenian language and rituals.[8][14] The Armenian Catholic Church’s Patriarchate of Cilicia, currently headed by Patriarch Raphaël Bedros XXI Minassian, continues to function as both a religious and a cultural institution, serving Armenian Catholics in Lebanon, Syria, Armenia, and the diaspora.[8] While this contemporary reality cannot be attributed solely to *Allatae Sunt*, the encyclical fits within a tradition of papal support for Armenian particularity that has shaped Armenian Catholic identity over centuries.  

Similar dynamics can be observed in other Eastern Catholic communities. The Syro‑Malabar Church, for instance, embodies the East Syriac Christian heritage of India’s Saint Thomas Christians while being fully Catholic.[9] Its liturgy in the East Syriac tradition, distinctiveness from both Latin Catholics and non‑Catholic Syriac churches, and strong ethnic and regional identity in Kerala demonstrate how rites function as markers of communal belonging.[9][13] Benedict XIV’s insistence that such Eastern communities not be Latinised but rather remain themselves can thus be seen as an early contribution to the long‑term formation of Eastern Catholic identities that intersect with, and sometimes complicate, ethnic and national affiliations.  

In the modern era, these identities have political implications. Eastern Catholic communities have sought recognition of their rights and status within various states, ranging from Middle Eastern countries to India and Eastern Europe. Rome’s historic stance on the dignity and autonomy of Eastern rites, of which *Allatae Sunt* is an important early articulation, provides a theological and canonical foundation for such claims, even when secular authorities base their decisions on different criteria.  

### Gender, Liturgy, and Changing Social Norms

The encyclical’s treatment of gender roles in liturgy, particularly in its apparent condemnation of women serving at the altar, reflects eighteenth‑century Catholic and broader Christian assumptions about appropriate male and female functions in worship and public life.[1][2][15] At that time, the idea of women acting as liturgical acolytes or servers would have been widely viewed as transgressing established norms of modesty and hierarchical order. Benedict XIV’s prospective intent in this regard was to maintain a clear gendered division of roles at the altar across rites, reinforcing a patriarchal model of clerical and liturgical leadership.  

Over the ensuing centuries, social norms regarding gender roles underwent profound changes, both within and outside religious communities. The rise of movements for women’s education, suffrage, and professional participation gradually altered expectations about women’s public roles. Within the Catholic Church, changes were slower and more limited, but by the late twentieth century, the Latin Church had begun to permit women to serve as lectors, extraordinary ministers of communion, and, with episcopal approval, altar servers.[2] Eastern Catholic churches have navigated these developments in diverse ways, sometimes maintaining more traditional gender roles and sometimes adapting in response to local cultures and ecumenical contexts.  

Viewed from this long‑term perspective, *Allatae Sunt*’s prescriptive stance on women at the altar has a retrospective rather than prospective relevance: it reveals the cultural assumptions of its own time and the way they shaped liturgical discipline, rather than providing a timeless norm. Its subsequent use in contemporary debates, particularly in online traditionalist milieus, illustrates how historical documents can be re‑deployed in new contexts, often without full attention to the historical contingency of their disciplinary content.[2][15] For researchers, this dynamic underscores the importance of analysing encyclicals like *Allatae Sunt* not only in terms of their theological and canonical claims but also in terms of the social imaginaries they express and their evolving reception in changing cultural landscapes.  

### Law, Jurisdiction, and Competing Authorities

Finally, *Allatae Sunt* has significance in the broader history of law and jurisdiction, as it exemplifies the papacy’s effort to regulate complex multi‑jurisdictional religious spaces. Eastern Catholic communities often existed at the intersections of multiple legal orders: civil law under Muslim or colonial rulers; internal ecclesiastical law under their own hierarchs; and overarching Roman canon law. Benedict XIV’s encyclical asserts the priority of Roman jurisdiction in determining the boundaries between legitimate rite and illegitimate innovation, while also recognising the authority of Eastern hierarchs within their own spheres.[1][15]  

This assertion had implications for relations between Rome and local patriarchs, metropolitans, and bishops. By appealing directly to missionaries and Eastern Catholics over the heads of some local Latin or civil authorities, the pope reinforced the centralising tendencies of Roman ecclesiology. At the same time, by prohibiting Latin clergy from interfering arbitrarily in Eastern rites, he implicitly bolstered the jurisdictional claims of Eastern bishops who sought to protect their traditions.[1][15] The resulting configuration is not a simple story of centralisation versus decentralisation but a complex negotiation in which different levels of authority are simultaneously affirmed and constrained.  

In the long run, these negotiations contributed to the development of the concept of Eastern Catholic churches as *sui iuris* entities within the Catholic communion, a concept codified in the CCEO and reflected in the contemporary structure of the Congregation for the Eastern Churches.[4][9] *Allatae Sunt* thus plays a role in the genealogy of modern Catholic legal pluralism, in which diverse rites and legal traditions coexist under a shared doctrinal and juridical umbrella. This legal pluralism, while internal to the Church, resonates with broader questions in secular legal systems about the accommodation of religious diversity, the recognition of communal autonomy, and the management of overlapping jurisdictions.  

## Unstructured

Viewed as a whole, *Allatae Sunt* is a document of tension and transition. It emerges at the tail end of an era in which the Catholic Church’s global expansion had been driven largely by European colonial powers and missionary orders, and it anticipates a future in which Eastern Catholic churches would claim a more robust and recognised place within the Catholic communion. Benedict XIV writes with the confidence of a jurist who believes that carefully crafted legal norms, supported by historical precedent, can reshape practice; yet he is also acutely aware of the inertia of habits, the stubbornness of local cultures, and the limits of papal authority in far‑flung regions.  

The encyclical’s reflective saturation is evident in its painstaking rehearsal of past papal decisions, conciliar decrees, and Propaganda Fide instructions. Benedict XIV behaves almost like an appellate judge reviewing case law, assembling previous rulings in order to articulate a clear doctrine of precedents. By doing so, he not only legitimates his policy but also constructs a narrative of continuity that may be more aspirational than strictly descriptive. The very necessity of repeating the 1702 decree against innovations, for instance, suggests that such innovations persisted, and that mission culture had grown accustomed to treating Latin practices as a norm to which Eastern Christians should aspire.[1][15][18]  

At the same time, the encyclical’s prospective energy lies in its attempt to reimagine what Catholic universality might look like. Rather than an empire of uniform rites spread outward from Rome, Benedict XIV envisions a communion in which Armenians remain Armenian, Syrians remain Syrians, and Malabar Christians remain Malabar, even as they confess the same faith and recognise the same papal authority.[1][8][9][15] Behind the legal formulas one can discern an ecclesiological imagination that sees in the plurality of rites not merely a problem to be managed but a sign of the Church’s catholicity. This imagination, however, is constrained by its own time: Benedict XIV still assumes a hierarchical, Eurocentric structure in which Rome judges which elements of Eastern tradition are acceptable and which must be purged.  

The encyclical’s treatment of the *Filioque* is emblematic of this tension. Benedict XIV insists on the Western doctrinal formulation, yet he shows pastoral sensitivity in not always forcing the insertion of the controversial clause into the Creed where doing so might cause scandal or impede union.[1][10][15] This approach foreshadows later ecumenical moves toward recognising legitimate diversity in theological expression, but it remains anchored in the assumption that Rome possesses the correct doctrinal articulation and that Eastern formulations must be measured against it. Similarly, his handling of calendars and fasting reveals both respect for Eastern customs and the desire to keep them within bounds determined by Roman judgment.  

Sociologically, *Allatae Sunt* illuminates the precarious position of Eastern Catholics as intermediaries between worlds. Their rites mark them as distinct from Latins, yet their communion with Rome marks them as distinct from Orthodox or other non‑Catholic Eastern Christians. In Ottoman cities, an Armenian Catholic might worship in a church architecturally and ritually closer to that of his non‑Catholic compatriots, yet owe obedience to a distant Western pope whose Latin documents, translated into Armenian or Arabic, regulate his fasting and even the exact words he pronounces when confessing the Creed.[8][11][14] In Indian coastal towns, a Syro‑Malabar Christian might participate in East Syriac liturgy under a bishop appointed with Roman approval, while living under colonial rule that favours Latin forms.[9][13] In both scenarios, *Allatae Sunt*’s injunction to preserve Eastern rites becomes a source of both empowerment and vulnerability.  

The document also reveals the gendered and social assumptions of its time. Its condemnation of women serving at the altar, read alongside its broader concern for liturgical propriety, positions male clerical bodies as the exclusive locus of sacred action, with women relegated to the role of recipients and observers.[1][2][15] This reflects not only ecclesial tradition but also broader eighteenth‑century norms about public space, authority, and bodily presence. From a contemporary vantage point, this aspect of the encyclical underscores the need to distinguish between theological claims that the Church continues to hold—such as the value of liturgy rooted in tradition—and disciplinary or cultural practices that have been, or can be, re‑evaluated in light of changing understandings of gender and justice.  

From the standpoint of papal authority, *Allatae Sunt* exemplifies both the reach and the fragility of Rome’s influence in the pre‑modern global Church. Benedict XIV’s words could travel, translated and transmitted through missionary networks, to communities thousands of kilometres away. His decrees could be cited in seminary classrooms in Constantinople or Goa, and copied in local canonical collections.[15][17][18] Yet their implementation depended on a cascade of mediations: bishops, missionaries, civil authorities, and lay communities, each with their own interests and constraints. In some places, the encyclical’s call to respect Eastern rites provided much‑needed backing to local leaders resisting Latinising pressures. In others, it was filed away or selectively interpreted, overshadowed by the gravitational pull of Latin norms or by political considerations.  

In the longue durée, the ideas articulated in *Allatae Sunt* contributed to a gradual shift in Catholic self‑understanding. Combined with later developments—Leo XIII’s explicit denunciation of Latinisation, the creation of the Congregation for the Eastern Churches, the Second Vatican Council’s affirmation of Eastern dignity, and the codification of Eastern canon law—the encyclical forms part of a trajectory that has moved the Catholic Church toward a more genuinely poly‑ritual, poly‑cultural self‑image.[4][8][9][13][15] The road has been uneven, and Latinisation still casts a long shadow in many communities, but the principle that Eastern rites are not mere anomalies to be disciplined but treasures to be preserved has become an established part of Catholic teaching.  

For contemporary observers—whether historians, theologians, or artists engaged in projects like Papal Papers—*Allatae Sunt* offers a window into the complexities of managing diversity within a universal institution. Its high reflective saturation and moderately high prospective saturation make it particularly rich for symbolic interpretation. The encyclical’s dense backward gaze, reaching mainly into the previous century but anchored in a deeper medieval past, coexists with a forward gaze that extends a decade or two into the future of missionary practice, seeking to nudge it onto a different track.[1][15][18] Its successes and failures tell us much about the dynamics of authority, culture, and identity in the Catholic Church’s engagement with the “Orient,” and by extension, about the broader challenges of negotiating unity and difference in any global organisation.  

## Conclusion

Benedict XIV’s *Allatae Sunt* stands as a pivotal document in the history of the Catholic Church’s relations with its Eastern members and neighbours. On the reflective axis, it offers a richly layered interpretation of past papal decisions, conciliar acts, and congregational decrees, constructing a narrative in which Rome has consistently sought to preserve Eastern rites while demanding doctrinal unity. This narrative, though idealised, responds to very real patterns of Latinisation and missionary overreach in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, particularly in contexts such as the Greek Catholic churches of Eastern Europe, the Armenian Catholic milieu in the Ottoman Empire, and the Syro‑Malabar Church in India.[5][8][9][13][15] The encyclical’s backward gaze is thus not antiquarian but diagnostic, using history to illuminate and critique contemporary practice.  

On the prospective axis, *Allatae Sunt* functions as a normative charter for a different kind of Catholic universality, one in which unity of faith coexists with diversity of rites. Its prescriptions aim to reshape missionary behaviour, empower Eastern Catholics to conserve their traditions, and mitigate scandal and schism by assuring non‑Catholic Eastern Christians that union with Rome does not require cultural self‑erasure.[1][10][15] The encyclical’s future‑oriented force, however, is uneven in its realisation. In some areas—such as the eventual affirmation of Eastern Catholic autonomy in the CCEO and the strong conciliar endorsement of Eastern rites at Vatican II—its principles have been taken up and developed.[4][8][9][13] In others—most notably the persistence of Latinisation in the nineteenth century and the later evolution of disciplinary norms on issues like female altar servers—its specific prescriptions have been only partially implemented or have been revised.[2][5][9][13][15]  

From a broader vantage point, the encyclical’s entanglement with secular realities is subtle but significant. Its insistence on preserving Eastern rites has contributed, in the long run, to the maintenance of distinctive cultural and ethnic identities, particularly among Armenians and other Eastern peoples whose liturgical traditions are integral to their sense of nationhood.[8][14][17] Its norms have intersected with legal and political structures in Ottoman, Persian, and colonial settings, shaping how Eastern Catholics navigated their place between local authorities, national communities, and the Roman centre.[4][11][18] Its gendered assumptions reveal the imprint of eighteenth‑century social norms on ecclesial discipline, while its careful handling of doctrinally sensitive issues like the *Filioque* anticipates later ecumenical sensitivities.[1][2][10][15]  

For the purposes of the Papal Papers project, *Allatae Sunt* can be represented as a highly saturated reflective document, whose gaze into the past is directed chiefly toward the preceding century’s missionary and Latinising controversies, with a secondary reach into medieval precedents. Its prospective gaze, while not as far‑ranging, is nonetheless substantial, extending into the following decade or two as Benedict XIV seeks to recalibrate missionary practice and Eastern Catholic self‑understanding. The gradient values proposed—high reflective saturation with a moderately deep backward density, and moderately high prospective saturation with a medium forward density—capture this dual orientation.  

Ultimately, *Allatae Sunt* invites readers to reflect on the interplay between law and culture, authority and identity, in a Church that seeks to be both one and many. Its enduring insight—that it is better that all be Catholic than that all be Latin—continues to shape Catholic discourse on unity and diversity, even as new contexts and challenges require fresh applications and, at times, corrective reinterpretations of its prescriptions. In this way, the encyclical remains a living document, not as a fixed blueprint, but as a historical voice that continues to inform and provoke contemporary conversations about what it means to be a universal church in a plural world.
