# Paterno Iam Diu and the Hungry Children of Central Europe: A Humanitarian Encyclical in a Shattered Post‑War Order

Pope Benedict XV’s 1919 encyclical *Paterno Iam Diu* is a compact but revealing document that sits at the intersection of catastrophic post–First World War hunger, the political disintegration of Central Europe, and the early formation of modern, child‑centred international humanitarianism.[1][7][13] Issued on 24 November 1919, it responds to reports of extreme deprivation among children in Austria, Germany and the wider region, where malnutrition rates in places like Vienna reached staggering levels and parliamentary debates in Britain spoke frankly of women and children dying of starvation.[10][14] The encyclical is at once reflective, acknowledging that hopes for a swift post‑war recovery and the restoration of “Christian charity” had been disappointed, and sharply prospective, issuing a concrete, time‑bound instruction to the global Catholic episcopate to organise a worldwide day of prayer and collection of money, food, medicine, and clothing on the Feast of the Holy Innocents, 28 December 1919.[1][12] Its language is theologically sparse and strategically cautious: Benedict XV invokes the image of the Divine Infant and the liturgy of Christmas to mobilise emotion, but he avoids naming political culprits such as the Allied blockade or punitive peace terms that historians now highlight as key drivers of post‑war hunger.[1][12][13] Instead, he frames the crisis in terms of innocent child victims and “future generations,” aligning his appeal with new secular initiatives like Save the Children and the American Relief Administration that were simultaneously constructing children as a privileged focus of international concern.[5][6][9] In this report, I situate *Paterno Iam Diu* within that broader landscape, assess how much it looks backward versus forward, and measure its effective “gradient” as a reflective interpretation of a devastated Central Europe and as a prescriptive intervention seeking to shape both immediate relief and longer‑term norms of humanitarian responsibility.

## Structured

### Document overview and thematic core

*Paterno Iam Diu* was promulgated on 24 November 1919 by Pope Benedict XV and addressed formally to “the Patriarchs, Primates, Archbishops, Bishops, and other Ordinaries in peace and communion with the Apostolic See.”[1][12] Its Latin incipit, translated as “For a long time now,” signals that the pope is not announcing a new doctrine but returning to a concern that has pressed upon him throughout the war years: the suffering of innocent populations, and particularly of children, under conditions of modern total war and its aftermath.[1][7] The text is strikingly short for an encyclical and almost entirely practical in scope. Instead of presenting a lengthy theological treatise, Benedict XV sketches the devastation of Central Europe, emphasising that the end of formal hostilities in 1918 has not brought the hoped‑for relief from hunger and cold.[1][12][13] He identifies children as the most pitiable victims, “altogether innocent and even ignorant of the sanguinary conflict,” and he recalls a previous papal appeal on behalf of Belgian children during the war, presented as a precedent that met with admirable generosity from Catholics worldwide.[1][7]

From this brief reflective frame, the encyclical pivots quickly to a prescriptive programme. Benedict XV commands that on 28 December, the Feast of the Holy Innocents—a liturgical commemoration of the children slain by Herod in the Gospel of Matthew—bishops in all dioceses should organise public prayers and collections for the children of Central Europe.[1][12] These collections are to go beyond money and should include food, medicines and clothing, items which he notes are acutely lacking in the stricken regions.[1][12] Rather than building a new Vatican bureaucracy, he instructs that the practical work of dividing and forwarding these goods should be entrusted to existing committees already formed for this purpose, implicitly recognising secular and Catholic lay organisations active in relief, such as Save the Children or local Catholic charities.[5][6][12] He broadens the appeal beyond Catholicism by explicitly expressing the hope that all who share “sentiments of humanity” will respond, and he announces a personal donation of 100,000 lire from the Holy See’s own limited funds as an example to others.[1][12] The encyclical closes with an Apostolic Blessing, but the weight of the document clearly lies in its humanitarian injunction rather than its spiritual consolations.

Thematically, therefore, *Paterno Iam Diu* is driven by a single dominant concern: the physical survival and future prospects of children in a devastated Central Europe. It treats the suffering of children as a moral scandal and a threat to the “germs” of future generations in the region, framing relief not only as an act of charity but as an investment in Europe’s social and civilisational recovery.[1][12] The document presupposes, without argument, that Catholics have a duty to respond, but it also situates this duty within a larger, quasi‑universal humanitarian ethic that it hopes non‑Catholics will share. Although issued in a spiritual register and timed to the Christmas season, its content is deeply entangled with secular realities: collapse of food supplies, breakdown of railways, inflation, and the fragility of new post‑imperial states.[10][13][14] It is this entanglement that makes the encyclical especially significant for a project concerned with papal engagement in the political, economic, and social spheres.

### Historical field of reference

The encyclical’s reflective horizon is relatively narrow but intense. When Benedict XV looks backward, he does so across the five years of the First World War, describing it as a “terrible conflict” whose aftereffects continue to haunt societies even after guns have fallen silent.[1][7] Historians now emphasise that the war did not end hunger in Europe; rather, hunger had been used as a weapon during the conflict through the Allied naval blockade, which restricted food and fuel imports to Central Powers like Germany and Austria‑Hungary and continued in varying forms into 1919.[13] According to research associated with the “Hunger Draws the Map” project at Oxford, malnutrition and starvation persisted across Europe and the Ottoman Empire long after the armistice, especially in urban centres cut off from food‑producing hinterlands.[13] In Austria, a parliamentary debate in the British House of Lords in December 1919—the same moment as the encyclical—reported that women and children were dying of starvation in Vienna, that rations had been cut in half from already inadequate wartime levels, and that railways were so damaged that even where food existed, it could not be transported.[10] A contemporary nutrition study cited by later historians suggested that by 1919 some 90 percent of schoolchildren in Vienna were experiencing moderate to severe malnutrition.[14] These empirical findings sharply corroborate Benedict XV’s depiction of Central Europe as a zone of acute child suffering and validate his assertion that the end of military conflict had not yet brought normal conditions of life.[1][12]

At the same time, Benedict’s reflective gaze does not delve into the deeper structural or political causes of this hunger. He does not mention the continued effects of the blockade, the punitive reparations demanded of defeated states, the break‑up of imperial economic systems, or the role of new national borders in disrupting trade and supply.[10][13] Nor does he explicitly refer to parallel crises in Russia and Eastern Europe, where civil war, drought and policy failures would culminate in the Russian Famine of 1921–1922, which eventually killed an estimated five million people, primarily in the Volga and Ural regions.[4] His silence on these elements does not mean that he was unaware of them—indeed, the Holy See maintained diplomatic and ecclesiastical networks that provided information about political and economic conditions—but the encyclical’s rhetorical choice is to foreground humanitarian symptoms rather than political diagnoses.[7][11] This choice reflects both the Vatican’s constrained diplomatic position in 1919, excluded from the Versailles peace conference, and Benedict’s own preference for appearing above partisan blame.[7] As a result, the historical field of reference is tightly focused on the immediate post‑war suffering of children rather than on a broader critique of the international order.

### Axes of reflection and projection

When mapped onto the project’s conceptual axes of reflection and projection, *Paterno Iam Diu* displays a distinctive profile. On the reflective axis, the encyclical is moderately saturated. It mourns the devastation of the recent war, acknowledges that hopes for a natural resurgence of Christian charity after the armistice have not been fulfilled, and recognises that children are bearing a disproportionate burden of deprivation despite their innocence.[1][7][12] It refers explicitly to earlier papal initiatives, such as an appeal for Belgian children, thereby situating itself within an ongoing pattern of Vatican concern and implicitly assessing that previous efforts, though laudable, have proven insufficient in scale.[1][7] Its temporal density on this axis is limited by the fact that it looks back chiefly to events within the preceding five years, from the outbreak of war in 1914 to the still‑unresolved humanitarian crisis in 1919, rather than to remote historical epochs. Thus, a reflective density score of around −5 years is appropriate.

On the prospective axis, by contrast, the encyclical is highly saturated. Its organising purpose is prescriptive: it demands a specific course of action on a specific date and gives concrete instructions about what should be collected, how it should be organised, and who should oversee distribution.[1][12] Moreover, it is not merely instrumental; it articulates a forward‑looking concern for the long‑term consequences of failing to nourish today's children, using the language of “germs of future generations” to evoke the idea that the physical and moral health of societies decades hence depends on the care extended to their current young.[1][12] This conceptual move extends the temporal gaze well beyond the immediate winter of 1919–1920. Although Benedict does not quantify this horizon, the generational framing reasonably points toward a time span on the order of twenty years or more, when today’s children will themselves be adults, parents, workers and citizens. Consequently, while the encyclical’s practical prescriptions focus on an imminent event in December 1919, its normative anticipation of social consequences justifies a prospective density score around +20 years.

### Justifying the gradient scores

The numerical gradient scores assigned earlier—reflective saturation 5 with density −5, and prospective saturation 9 with density +20—require explicit justification in light of both the encyclical’s text and the historical record. The moderate reflective saturation score reflects the fact that *Paterno Iam Diu* does engage in retrospective reasoning, but always as a prelude to action rather than as an end in itself. Benedict XV recalls the horrors of the “terrible conflict” and the failure of post‑armistice charity to emerge spontaneously, and he references previous successful appeals as part of a narrative of experience and learning.[1][7][12] However, he does not undertake a detailed diagnosis of how and why Central Europe has reached its current state, nor does he engage in theological or moral evaluation of treaties, blockades, or revolutions. Those absences mark a limit to his reflective ambitions when compared with more expansive social encyclicals.

The high prospective saturation score stems from the encyclical’s character as a mobilisation order. Almost every paragraph is oriented toward what should be done next: prayers to be said, alms to be gathered, committees to be entrusted, personal example to be offered.[1][12] Benedict’s own donation of 100,000 lire, while modest in absolute terms, illustrates that he is not simply exhorting others but concretely participating in the future‑shaping act he advocates.[1][12] The choice of the Feast of the Holy Innocents, and the appeal to “all who have sentiments of humanity,” further signal an attempt to choreograph a collective, transnational gesture that might influence not only the distribution of resources but the moral imagination of Catholics and non‑Catholics alike.[1][12] Academic studies of the Holy See’s humanitarian diplomacy in these years confirm that Benedict XV increasingly saw such appeals as a way to carve out a distinctive role for the papacy in a world where it had lost temporal power but could still claim moral leadership.[7][11] The density score of +20 rests on the generational language and on the historical fact that post‑war famine and relief would shape European politics and international norms for at least two decades, up to and including the responses to the Russian famine and later crises.[4][11][13]

In sum, *Paterno Iam Diu* is best understood as an encyclical whose reflective dimension, though present, is compressed and subordinated to a prescriptive, future‑oriented project: the immediate alleviation of child suffering and the longer‑term preservation of Central Europe’s human capital. Its gradients capture a gaze that is short but intense when looking backward and longer, more imaginative, and more normative when looking forward.

## Historical Context: Hunger, Collapse, and Children in Central Europe

### Disintegration of the old order and economic breakdown

To grasp the world to which *Paterno Iam Diu* responds, one must begin with the collapse of the old imperial order in Central Europe. The First World War shattered the Austro‑Hungarian Empire, producing successor states such as Austria, Hungary, Czechoslovakia and the Kingdom of Serbs, Croats and Slovenes (later Yugoslavia), whose borders and economies were unsettled and often contested.[13] These new political entities inherited devastated infrastructures: railways damaged by war, industrial plants starved of raw materials, and agricultural regions whose labour force had been decimated by conscription and casualties. In Austria, the truncated republic centred on Vienna lacked sufficient domestic food supplies, having lost access to the imperial agricultural hinterlands that had previously fed the capital.[10][13] British parliamentary debates in December 1919 describe Austria as “in a state of famine,” with food needing to be imported because “there are no crops” in sufficient quantity and the regions still part of the Austrian state—Lower Austria, Styria, Carinthia, and Tyrol—being unable to provide enough sustenance.[10] The same debate notes that Austria had been “practically blockaded as regards both fuel and food” for many months, compounding the misery left by the war.[10]

German territories likewise suffered from agricultural shortfalls, industrial dislocation and the lingering effects of the Allied blockade, which had restricted imports of food and fertiliser during the war and whose lifting in 1919 did not immediately restore normal trade or prices.[13] Dr. Heather Jones and Dr. Tamara Glenny, associated with the “Hunger Draws the Map” project, emphasise that blockade‑induced malnutrition was not simply a wartime phenomenon but bled into the post‑war years, with hunger effectively “drawing the map” of social unrest and political instability across Europe and the former Ottoman Empire.[13] In their interpretation, women and children paid a particular price, as mothers limited their own intake to feed children and as urban families struggled to cope with soaring food prices and rationing. While these historians write from a contemporary vantage point, their analysis is grounded in contemporaneous testimonies and data that make the scale of suffering clear.

Inflation and currency collapse further undermined purchasing power in Central Europe. The British House of Lords debate on Austrian distress highlights the precipitous decline of the Austrian krone, which had fallen from being worth tenpence before the war to only about 3 percent of its face value by late 1919, effectively “worth no more than a farthing.”[10] Even in cases where food was theoretically available in markets, such currency collapse meant that ordinary people could not afford to buy it. This dynamic exacerbated class tensions and left children in poor households particularly exposed to malnutrition and disease. The combination of physical destruction, economic disintegration, and monetary chaos created a landscape in which traditional coping mechanisms—family networks, local charity, municipal welfare—were overwhelmed.

### Hunger as a weapon and its lingering effects

The use of hunger as a weapon during the First World War forms a crucial backdrop to the post‑war crisis described in *Paterno Iam Diu*. Britain and France imposed a naval blockade on Germany, Austria‑Hungary, Bulgaria and the Ottoman Empire, aiming to cut off both military supplies and civilian food imports.[13] While justified in Allied discourse as a means of shortening the war and weakening the enemy, the blockade had devastating humanitarian consequences. According to Dr. Cox, quoted in the Oxford analysis, the blockade caused malnutrition and starvation in the Central Powers for years and was maintained in various forms until 1919, even after the armistice.[13] The intentional restriction of food blurred the line between military and civilian targets, making children collateral victims of strategic policy.

The persistence of hunger after the armistice reflects both the inertia of such large‑scale economic measures and the political will of the victorious powers to use food relief as leverage in post‑war negotiations.[13] The hunger could not simply be turned off like a tap; shipping routes, trade agreements, and domestic production all required time to adjust. In some cases, the provision of food relief was contingent upon political concessions or upon acceptance of treaty terms, placing populations in a form of economic hostage situation. In public debate, there emerged a growing unease about the morality of continuing to “fight with food” when the shooting war had ended. Humanitarian activists, including those who would found organisations like Save the Children, began to argue that the blockade and its aftermath inflicted unacceptable suffering on innocent children and should be lifted or mitigated.[5][13]

Benedict XV’s encyclical does not explicitly mention the blockade, but his description of children suffering from “privation of food and clothing” in Central Europe is entirely consistent with the documented consequences of blockades, crop failures, and disrupted trade.[1][12][13] His choice to avoid naming specific policies may have reflected diplomatic considerations—criticising the Allies directly could have jeopardised the Vatican’s delicate relations with them—but his humanitarian focus aligns with a broader contemporary movement that sought to separate child welfare from wartime enmities.[7][13] This separation is especially visible in British activism for the relief of “enemy” children, where figures like Eglantyne Jebb of Save the Children deliberately framed German and Austrian children as deserving subjects of British compassion despite recent hostilities.[5][9] Benedict’s encyclical, in addressing children in Central Europe without distinguishing between victors and vanquished, falls squarely within this emergent ethic.

### Children as primary victims: malnutrition, disease, and “future generations”

The specific condition of children in Central Europe in 1919–1920 has been reconstructed by historians using school medical records, municipal health statistics, and contemporary social surveys. One study cited in modern scholarship on Vienna indicates that in 1919 some 90 percent of schoolchildren in the city showed signs of moderate to severe malnutrition.[14] This level of deprivation manifests not only in underweight and stunted growth, but also in increased susceptibility to infectious diseases such as tuberculosis, measles, and respiratory infections, which thrive in overcrowded, cold, and poorly nourished populations. In famine conditions, children’s bodies, still developing, are especially vulnerable to permanent damage, including cognitive impairments and chronic illness that can affect them throughout their lives.

In Austria, parliamentary testimony from British observers described women and children dying of starvation in Vienna in late 1919 and noted that existing rations were not sufficient to “properly support life,” with those rations being halved and sometimes not even delivered.[10] The same debate drew attention to the interplay between malnutrition and other infrastructure failures, such as the breakdown of railway systems which prevented food from reaching urban centres even when it existed elsewhere.[10] These accounts create a grim picture of daily life in which mothers might skip meals or sell personal possessions to feed their children, only to find that there was nothing to buy or that currency inflation had rendered their purchasing power almost meaningless.

Benedict XV’s decision to focus his encyclical on children rather than on the general population reflects both the objective vulnerability of children and their symbolic power in moral discourse.[1][12] Children, especially small children, are universally recognised as dependent and innocent; they did not vote for war, did not participate in combat, and bore no responsibility for diplomatic decisions. By portraying them as the unintended casualties of adult conflicts, Benedict taps into a potent rhetorical resource that can transcend national and confessional boundaries. His phrase about the “germs of future generations” condenses both a biological insight—that malnourished children will grow into weaker adults—and a civilisational anxiety—that a whole region’s future may be blighted if its children are allowed to wither.[1][12] This dual framing parallels secular discussions in the same period, where doctors, child welfare advocates, and social reformers warned that failing to invest in child health and nutrition would have long‑term consequences for labour productivity, military readiness, and social stability.

The idea of the child as a bearer of the future would later become more formally codified in documents like the 1924 Geneva Declaration of the Rights of the Child and, much later, the 1989 UN Convention on the Rights of the Child, but the post‑war famine debates already display a proto‑rights discourse in which children are presented as having special claims on the conscience and resources of societies.[5][9][11] Benedict XV’s encyclical participates in this shift by elevating child welfare to a central ecclesial concern and linking it to the Church’s understanding of human dignity and social order, even if it does not articulate this in the legalistic vocabulary of rights.

### Emergence of organised humanitarian responses

The years immediately surrounding 1919 witnessed the rapid growth of organised humanitarian responses to post‑war hunger, in which religious and secular actors often operated side by side. In May 1919, the Save the Children Fund was established at a public meeting in London’s Royal Albert Hall, with the explicit aim of raising funds for children suffering from famine and deprivation in Central Europe.[5] The organisation, founded by Eglantyne Jebb and her sister Dorothy Buxton, took a deliberately internationalist stance and sought to challenge the narrative that former enemy populations should be left to suffer as punishment.[5][9] By August 1921, Save the Children in the UK had raised over £1,000,000, a substantial sum for the time, and its efforts were credited with improving conditions for children in parts of Central Europe.[9] The group also contributed to shaping public opinion through posters, pamphlets and media campaigns that emphasised children’s innocence and the cruelty of using hunger as a post‑war weapon.[5]

Alongside such NGOs, governmental and quasi‑governmental bodies played critical roles. The American Relief Administration (ARA), headed by Herbert Hoover, organised large‑scale food relief throughout Europe, using funds appropriated by the US government and voluntary contributions.[6] The ARA’s European operational records note that agreements were made with various countries in Central and Eastern Europe, stipulating that any balance in relief funds after initial operations would be used for general European children’s relief.[6] This provision reflects an emerging recognition that children’s needs warranted special prioritisation. The ARA worked through local committees, including religious and civic organisations, to distribute food, thereby creating practical networks that could be mobilised by appeals like *Paterno Iam Diu*.[6][11] 

The Holy See was itself an active participant in this burgeoning humanitarian ecosystem. Historians of Vatican diplomacy in the period emphasise that Benedict XV devoted considerable energy to organising and supporting relief for war victims, refugees, and famine‑stricken populations, often acting as a moral broker and coordinator among Catholic charities and international agencies.[7][11] In Central and Eastern Europe, the Vatican used its nuncios, bishops, and religious orders to collect information, channel funds, and advocate for aid.[7][11] The encyclical’s explicit mention of committees already formed for the purpose of helping children suggests that Benedict was aware of and willing to cooperate with these structures, whether they were Catholic, ecumenical, or secular.[12] He does not insist on exclusive control by ecclesiastical authorities, but instead encourages bishops to entrust the practical work of dividing and forwarding relief to these mixed bodies, demonstrating a pragmatic openness to collaboration.

In this context, *Paterno Iam Diu* should be seen not as the origin of Catholic concern for hungry children but as a public articulation of a wider, multifaceted relief effort in which the Vatican sought to mobilise its unique global network of parishes, religious orders and lay associations. Its novelty lies in the tight coupling of liturgical timing, child‑centred rhetoric, and explicit alignment with non‑ecclesial committees, making it a distinctively modern papal intervention into a global humanitarian moment.

## Benedict XV and the Humanitarian Papacy

### A “Pope of Peace” in wartime and after

Giacomo della Chiesa, who became Benedict XV in 1914, quickly acquired the reputation of a “Pope of Peace,” due to his persistent, though largely unsuccessful, efforts to mediate between the warring powers during the First World War.[7] According to the authoritative online encyclopaedia of the war, Benedict XV issued several interventions, including his 1917 Peace Note, in which he proposed a set of principles for a negotiated peace, and he condemned the war as a “useless slaughter.”[7] His appeals were largely ignored by the major belligerents, who viewed the Vatican with suspicion or treated it as lacking the political leverage to matter. Nevertheless, Benedict persisted in advocating for prisoners of war, refugees, and civilian victims, using the limited tools of moral persuasion and diplomatic representation still available to the Holy See.[7][11]

During the war, Benedict also established and supported practical relief efforts. He encouraged Catholics to contribute to aid for Belgian and other occupied populations, organised information bureaux to assist families in tracing missing soldiers, and called upon religious orders to assist in caring for the wounded and displaced.[7] This combination of moral rhetoric and concrete humanitarian activity set a pattern that would continue into the post‑war years. The Holy See could not command armies or resources on the scale of great powers, but it could seek to alleviate suffering and to shape the conscience of Catholic populations. In doing so, it also hoped to maintain and even enhance its international standing at a time when its loss of the Papal States and its exclusion from formal peace negotiations highlighted its political marginality.[7]

Benedict’s position was complicated by the fact that Catholics were present on all sides of the conflict—Italian, Austrian, German, French, and others—making overt partisanship both theologically problematic and pastorally dangerous. He adopted a rhetoric of impartial compassion, insisting that the Church’s care extended to all victims regardless of nationality.[7][11] This was not always appreciated; some French and Italian Catholics felt he was insufficiently supportive of their national causes, while some Germans suspected him of favouring the Allies. Yet this very insistence on standing above the fray laid the groundwork for the kind of universalist humanitarian appeal evident in *Paterno Iam Diu*, where Central European children are presented as deserving subjects of compassion without regard to their parents’ prior status as enemy or ally.

### Humanitarian diplomacy and the “Russian world”

Recent scholarship on the Holy See’s humanitarian diplomacy has highlighted Benedict XV’s engagement not only with Central Europe but also with the “Russian world,” especially in response to the Russian Famine of 1921–1922.[11] The famine, caused by a combination of severe drought, the cumulative devastation of World War I, the Russian Revolution and Civil War, and policy failures associated with War Communism, led to an estimated five million deaths from hunger and associated diseases in the Volga and Ural regions.[4] The Soviet state initially resisted foreign aid but eventually accepted some international assistance, including from religious organisations.[4][11] The Holy See, despite ideological opposition from the Bolshevik regime, attempted to organise relief for famine victims and to negotiate access for Catholic aid agencies.[11]

The article on the Holy See’s humanitarian diplomacy toward the Russian world argues that humanitarian questions gradually took precedence as a crucial topic in the history of international relations and that religious actors, including the Vatican, played significant roles in this evolution.[11] Benedict XV’s appeals on behalf of Russian famine victims, like his earlier efforts for Central Europe, combined spiritual language with practical fundraising and coordination. His diplomacy in this arena built upon networks and experience developed during and immediately after the war, including the use of nuncios, bishops, and Catholic charitable organisations as intermediaries.[7][11] Although the encyclical *Paterno Iam Diu* predates the Russian famine, it reflects the same underlying conviction that the Holy See has both a right and a duty to intervene in global humanitarian crises, not in a partisan way but as a voice for the suffering.

This broader humanitarian orientation is critical for understanding the encyclical’s prescriptive ambition. Benedict is not simply reacting ad hoc to reports of child hunger; he is pursuing a consistent strategy of using encyclicals and other public communications to mobilise the Church’s global resources and to assert Catholicism’s relevance in a new international order increasingly structured by secular nation‑states and emerging international organisations like the League of Nations.[7][11] His humanitarian diplomacy, though constrained by limited political leverage, sought to carve out a moral space for the Holy See as an interlocutor on questions of war, peace, and reconstruction.

### Papal authority, reputation, and the tone of the encyclical

Benedict XV’s personal authority and the papacy’s political situation in 1919 significantly shaped the tone and reach of *Paterno Iam Diu*. The Vatican was not a signatory to the Treaty of Versailles and had been excluded from the Paris Peace Conference, reflecting both anti‑clerical sentiments in some Allied circles and the unresolved “Roman Question” concerning the Italian state’s occupation of Rome and the Papal States.[7] As a result, the Holy See lacked formal standing in the new League of Nations and was relegated to the margins of official diplomacy. This institutional marginalisation heightened the importance of “soft power” tools such as encyclicals, which could still reach millions of believers and influence public opinion.

Within the Church, Benedict’s wartime efforts had earned him a reputation for compassion and moral consistency, though not necessarily for political effectiveness.[7] Some Catholics were disappointed that his peace initiatives had failed; others admired his refusal to sanctify the war. This ambivalent legacy likely encouraged him to adopt a modest and practical tone in *Paterno Iam Diu*. The encyclical does not trumpet Vatican achievements or issue grand pronouncements about the future of Europe. Instead, it speaks in the language of duty, compassion, and concrete action, aligning with Benedict’s self‑presentation as a servant of peace rather than a triumphant spiritual monarch.[1][12] His decision to include a specific personal donation to the cause underscores his desire to be seen as contributing rather than merely commanding.[1][12]

At the same time, the encyclical’s tone avoids overt criticism of governments or policies, which might have been interpreted as overstepping the Vatican’s diminished political role. This restraint contrasts with some secular activists’ more confrontational rhetoric about the blockade or the terms of the peace settlement.[5][13] Benedict’s criticism is implicit rather than explicit: by highlighting the scandal of starving children in ostensibly Christian societies, he invites readers to question the structures that produce such outcomes without directly naming culprits. This indirectness may have blunted the encyclical’s potential to influence policy, but it also allowed it to be received in multiple countries without provoking diplomatic crises.

### The Church’s internal debates and priorities

Historically, internal Catholic debates in 1919 included questions about the Church’s relationship to new democratic regimes, socialist movements, and the rising influence of secular ideologies. Benedict XV and his advisers had to balance concern for social justice and workers’ rights—already themes in earlier social encyclicals like Leo XIII’s *Rerum Novarum*—with fear of revolutionary movements that threatened property and religious institutions.[7] In Central Europe, Catholic parties and associations were engaged in fierce political struggles over the shape of new states, particularly in countries like Austria and Germany where confessional divisions overlapped with class and regional cleavages.[7]

*Paterno Iam Diu* largely brackets these internal debates. It does not engage with questions of socialism, democracy, or class conflict; nor does it frame child hunger in terms of social justice in the more structural sense that would become characteristic of later Catholic social teaching.[1][12] Instead, it adopts a language of charity and humanitarian duty that, while not incompatible with justice discourse, remains more traditional. This choice may have been strategic: by focusing on the most uncontroversial and urgent aspect of the crisis—hungry children—the pope could appeal across intra‑Catholic factions and avoid getting entangled in highly charged political debates. It also reflects his assessment that immediate relief took precedence over longer‑term structural reform, at least in the context of this particular document.

In this way, the encyclical’s silence on certain ecclesial and socio‑political issues is as revealing as its explicit content. It shows a papacy prioritising humanitarian action as a bridge between an older world of confessional politics and a new world of secular internationalism, using the unifying symbol of the suffering child to cut across internal and external divisions without resolving deeper ideological conflicts.

## Reading *Paterno Iam Diu* against its Time

### Structure and rhetorical trajectory

The internal structure of *Paterno Iam Diu* follows a simple but effective arc, beginning with lament, moving through moral framing, and culminating in a concrete directive. The opening paragraphs situate the encyclical in the aftermath of “the terrible conflict” and express Benedict XV’s disappointment that the restoration of peace has not brought the expected relief.[1][12] He notes that Christian charity, which should have blossomed naturally after the cessation of hostilities, has been insufficient to meet the vast needs of those who lost homes, property, and livelihoods during the war. This initial reflection serves to name the gap between moral ideal and lived reality.

The text then narrows its focus to children, describing them as “altogether innocent and even ignorant of the sanguinary conflict,” yet being forced to suffer in body and soul from hunger, cold, and the lack of basic necessities.[1][12] Benedict invokes the image of the Divine Infant in Bethlehem and the biblical story of the Holy Innocents, thus linking the plight of contemporary children to sacred narratives that Catholics would soon be hearing in the Christmas liturgy. This theological imagery does not introduce new doctrine but functions as a powerful rhetorical bridge between religious emotion and humanitarian concern.

From there, the encyclical transitions to a more pragmatic register. Benedict recalls his previous appeal for Belgian children during the war, emphasising the generosity with which Catholics responded and the good that was accomplished.[1][7] This precedent establishes a sense of capacity and responsibility: if the Church could aid Belgian children in wartime, it can and should now aid Central European children in the post‑war crisis. Finally, he announces the specific directive: on 28 December, the Feast of the Holy Innocents, bishops are to organise public prayers and collections; the faithful are to contribute money, food, clothing, and medicines; existing committees are to oversee distribution; and the pope himself will donate 100,000 lire.[1][12]

The rhetorical trajectory thus moves from diagnosis (there is suffering and insufficient charity) to identification of the most vulnerable victims (children), to invocation of sacred exemplars (Christ Child, Holy Innocents), to deployment of institutional memory (Belgian appeal), and finally to actionable instruction (Holy Innocents collections). Each step is calibrated to minimise resistance and to maximise the likelihood of concrete response. The brevity of the document reinforces its urgency, avoiding diluting the message with extended theological or philosophical discussions.

### Reflective content: war, disappointment, and the image of the child

Within this structure, the explicitly reflective content plays a significant but supporting role. Benedict’s description of the war as a “terrible conflict” and his acknowledgment that its “cruelty” has left deep scars both recall his earlier condemnation of the war as a “useless slaughter.”[1][7][12] He does not rehearse the full horror of trench warfare, mass casualties, or chemical weapons; by 1919, his audience hardly needed reminders. Instead, he focuses on the less visible but equally devastating post‑war suffering, especially among civilian populations in Central Europe, where hunger and deprivation had not ended with the armistice.[10][13][14] This shift of attention from the battlefield to the home front reflects a broader post‑war reorientation in public discourse, where the enduring social and economic consequences of the war began to loom as large as its military battles.

Benedict also reflects on the failure of Christian charity to rise adequately to the occasion. There is an implicit critique of the Christian nations of Europe, which had plunged into war and now seemed reluctant to bear the full cost of reconstruction and relief.[1][7] Yet this critique remains understated; Benedict does not dwell on hypocrisy or sin but instead presents the current crisis as an opportunity for Christians to reawaken their charity and to act in accordance with their professed beliefs. The reflective element here lies in naming the gap between ideal and reality without assigning detailed blame.

The image of the child functions as a reflective mirror as well as a prospective symbol. In recalling the Divine Infant and the Holy Innocents, Benedict invites believers to see in the starving children of Vienna or Berlin not only victims but living icons that shame adult complacency.[1][12] The juxtaposition of the warm, comforting imagery of Christmas with the cold, hungry reality of Central European children produces a moral dissonance that the faithful are expected to resolve through concrete action. This use of religious imagery to reframe perception is reflective in that it asks readers to reinterpret familiar stories in light of contemporary suffering.

### Prospective content: mobilisation, committees, and universal appeal

The most distinctive feature of *Paterno Iam Diu* is its prospective, prescriptive thrust. Benedict is not content with general exhortations to charity; he specifies a date, a method, and a cooperative framework. The choice of 28 December, the Feast of the Holy Innocents, is symbolically rich: it situates the act of giving within the liturgical cycle, ensuring that the appeal will be heard at a time when attendance at Mass is high and when the theme of child suffering is already present in the readings and prayers.[1][12] This synchronisation multiplies the impact of the encyclical beyond what a random date would have achieved.

The instruction to gather not only money but also food, clothing and medicines reflects practical awareness of on‑the‑ground needs.[1][12] In many famine situations, currency is of limited use if goods cannot be procured locally or imported; sending actual supplies can be more immediately beneficial. Benedict does not micromanage the logistics of collection and transport, but he does mandate that bishops take responsibility for organising efforts in their dioceses and that they entrust practical oversight to committees already formed for this purpose.[1][12] This is a notable recognition of the value of lay expertise and existing organisational capacity. The committees he likely had in mind would have included Catholic charities, but also possibly broader relief committees that cooperated with agencies like the ARA or with secular groups such as Save the Children.[5][6][12] The encyclical thus encourages an integrated, networked approach rather than isolated, duplicative efforts.

By explicitly addressing “all who have the sentiments of humanity” and expressing the hope that his exhortation will be “benevolently listened to” beyond Catholic circles, Benedict extends the prospective horizon of his appeal to include non‑Catholic and even non‑Christian audiences.[1][12] This universalist tone resonates with the broader humanitarian discourse of the period, in which suffering children were increasingly portrayed as a common responsibility of humankind rather than of any one nation or confession.[5][9][13] It also anticipates later papal interventions that appeal to “all people of good will,” a formula that would become standard in Catholic social teaching. In 1919, such language signalled an openness to collaboration in the public sphere that contrasted with older models of Catholic insularity.

The pope’s personal donation of 100,000 lire carries both symbolic and practical significance. Given the Vatican’s straitened finances after the loss of the Papal States and the lack of compensation from Italy, this sum, while not enormous in absolute terms, represented a real sacrifice.[7] It also served as a prospective model for wealthy Catholics and institutions, indicating that they too should stretch their resources for the sake of Central European children. In a world where fundraising campaigns increasingly employed matching grants and challenge gifts, Benedict’s donation can be seen as an early ecclesial form of such incentivising.

### Strategic silences and political caution

An important part of reading *Paterno Iam Diu* against its time is recognising what it does not say. The encyclical does not mention the Allied blockade, the Treaty of Versailles, the break‑up of the Austro‑Hungarian Empire, or the specific responsibilities of particular governments for post‑war suffering.[1][12] It does not distinguish between “victor” and “vanquished” populations or address the widespread sentiment in some countries that former enemies deserved to suffer as a consequence of their wartime actions. Nor does it address the role of emerging international institutions like the League of Nations in coordinating relief and reconstruction.

These silences reflect both strategic caution and theological positioning. By avoiding direct criticism of powerful states or explicit discussion of controversial treaties, Benedict preserves a space for the Vatican to act as a neutral humanitarian actor, potentially acceptable to all sides.[7][11] Any explicit political critique could have compromised relations with governments, endangered Catholic minorities, or diminished the likelihood of cooperation in humanitarian projects. At the same time, by treating all children in Central Europe as equally deserving, the encyclical implicitly challenges narratives that would justify collective punishment or preferential treatment.

The absence of explicit engagement with the League of Nations or with secular international law also reveals the limits of the Vatican’s integration into the new international system in 1919. While some Catholic thinkers were beginning to reflect on the implications of the League and on the possibilities of developing a Christian approach to international organisation, Benedict’s encyclical remains firmly within a pre‑League frame in which the primary actors are the Church, states, and private relief organisations.[7][11] Nevertheless, by addressing a global audience and encouraging cross‑border humanitarian solidarity, *Paterno Iam Diu* participates in the emerging internationalist ethos, even if it does not reference its institutional manifestations.

### Relationship to secular initiatives and the humanitarian “imaginary”

The encyclical’s reference to committees already formed for the purpose of aiding children situates it within a dense landscape of secular and religious initiatives. Save the Children’s founding in May 1919 and its rapid mobilisation of public support, particularly in Britain, indicate that the idea of assisting Central European children had already gained traction.[5][9] The ARA’s contracts with European countries, stipulating that surplus funds would go to children’s relief, show that child‑focused aid was becoming institutionalised at governmental levels as well.[6] Benedict’s encyclical does not claim to initiate these trends; rather, it plugs the Catholic Church into them by mobilising the faithful and offering episcopal structures as channels for donations and aid.

From an anthropological perspective, one can say that *Paterno Iam Diu* participates in the construction of a new humanitarian “imaginary” in which the suffering child stands at the centre. Posters, photographs, and reports from NGOs and governments portrayed gaunt, wide‑eyed children as the emblem of war’s cruelty and as the rightful recipients of compassion.[5][9][13] The Church’s own iconography of the Christ Child and the Holy Innocents provided a deep reservoir of symbolic resources that could be re‑mapped onto this new humanitarian field. Benedict’s encyclical explicitly performs this mapping, aligning the liturgical calendar with secular relief efforts and thereby sacralising humanitarian action.

There is no clear evidence in the available sources that secular organisations like Save the Children explicitly cited or coordinated with *Paterno Iam Diu* in their campaigns.[5][9] The encyclical seems to have functioned more as an internal Catholic mobilisation document than as a major event in broader public discourse. Nonetheless, by echoing and reinforcing the child‑centred framing, it contributed to the diffusion of that humanitarian imaginary within Catholic communities, which constituted a significant portion of the populations in affected and donor countries alike.

## Forward Trajectories: Relief, Influence, and Legacy

### Immediate relief campaigns and their reach

Assessing the concrete impact of *Paterno Iam Diu* on relief campaigns is challenging, due to the paucity of surviving, non‑theological documentation that directly links specific donations or programmes to the encyclical. Catholic diocesan archives and parish records, which might contain reports of the Holy Innocents collections, are unevenly preserved and not easily accessible through general historical surveys. The sources at hand do not provide direct quantitative data on funds or goods raised specifically in response to the encyclical. This constitutes a clear evidentiary limitation and must be acknowledged as such.

However, we can situate the encyclical within the broader wave of relief campaigns that peaked around 1919–1921. Save the Children’s records report that by August 1921 the organisation had raised over £1,000,000 in the UK alone, contributing significantly to improving conditions for children in Central Europe.[9] The ARA’s operations distributed millions of tons of food across Europe, including to children through school feeding programmes and special rations.[6] Various national Red Cross societies and church‑based charities also conducted appeals, often collaborating with governmental efforts. Within this ecosystem, Catholic appeals like *Paterno Iam Diu* likely channelled substantial resources, even if these are not easily disaggregated in the historical record.

The British parliamentary debate on Austrian distress, held in December 1919, reflects a growing awareness among policymakers of the urgent need for relief and the inadequacy of existing measures.[10] Speakers acknowledged that if help were not given very speedily, Austrians would face “absolute starvation” and that the only adequate measure in the long term would be to re‑establish normal economic processes so that populations could support themselves.[10] This recognition of both the need for immediate aid and long‑term reconstruction parallels the dual concern in Benedict’s encyclical for immediate child survival and the future of “generations” to come.[1][12] While there is no indication that the debate was influenced by the encyclical, it illustrates the convergence of secular and ecclesial diagnoses.

In some respects, the encyclical may have been more significant in donor countries than in recipient ones. Catholics in countries less affected by famine, such as the United States, parts of Italy, and Latin America, could respond to Benedict’s call by contributing funds and goods that would be channelled through international networks.[6][7][11] To the extent that bishops and Catholic media publicised the encyclical, it might have prompted an upsurge in charitable giving and enhanced cooperation with secular agencies. However, without systematic studies of Catholic press coverage or parish collections explicitly tied to *Paterno Iam Diu*, these possibilities remain hypotheses rather than established facts.

### Evolution toward a global child‑centred humanitarian regime

The longer‑term legacy of *Patero Iam Diu* lies less in measurable relief outputs and more in its contribution to a broader shift in humanitarian norms. Historians of Save the Children argue that the post‑First World War period marked the birth of a new, child‑centred international humanitarian regime, in which children were defined as a special category of victims meriting specific protections and resources.[5][9] Save the Children’s efforts, along with those of other NGOs and international bodies, culminated in the 1924 Geneva Declaration of the Rights of the Child, adopted by the League of Nations, which asserted that mankind “owes to the Child the best that it has to give.”[5][9] Although drafted and promoted by secular actors, this declaration resonates strongly with the moral intuition articulated in Benedict’s encyclical: that the wellbeing of children is both a present imperative and a determinant of the future health of societies.[1][12]

The encyclical thus stands as an early ecclesial instance of the child‑centred humanitarian discourse that would later become ubiquitous. Its emphasis on children as “germs of future generations” parallels the declaration’s insistence that children’s development is a collective responsibility.[1][12] By aligning religious symbolism with child welfare, the Church helped to embed this norm in popular consciousness among Catholics. Over time, Catholic organisations would become important actors in child‑focused humanitarian work, from Caritas Internationalis to various religious orders running schools, orphanages, and feeding programmes. While these later developments cannot be causally attributed to *Paterno Iam Diu* alone, the encyclical can be seen as a milestone in the Church’s articulation of child welfare as a central social concern.

### The Holy See and subsequent famines

The Vatican’s humanitarian engagement did not end with Central Europe’s post‑war crisis. As noted earlier, Benedict XV and his successors turned their attention to other crises, notably the Russian famine of 1921–1922.[4][11] The Holy See sought to organise relief for famine victims in the Volga region and to negotiate with Soviet authorities for access and the protection of Catholic workers and clergy involved in the effort.[11] These activities, which involved complex diplomacy and collaboration with other international actors, were shaped by the experience of post‑war European relief and by the networks developed during those efforts. The conceptual and practical toolkit that *Paterno Iam Diu* presupposed—a global Church mobilised by encyclical appeals, cooperating with mixed committees and secular agencies—was thus applied in new contexts.

Moreover, the memory of hunger in the immediate post‑First World War period influenced the thinking of political leaders who would later confront the challenge of preventing a similar catastrophe after the Second World War. Parliamentary debates in 1942, for example, recalled the “very great distress” in Russia, Austria, Poland and other countries after the previous war and discussed the need for relief measures that would avoid repeating the mistakes of punitive post‑war policies.[15] While these debates did not cite *Paterno Iam Diu*, they demonstrate that the humanitarian crises of 1919–1922—of which Central European child hunger was a core component—remained a reference point in mid‑twentieth‑century policy discussions.

### Limits of influence and relative invisibility

Despite its moral clarity and practical intent, *Paterno Iam Diu* appears to have had limited direct influence on secular policy or public discourse beyond Catholic circles. The available sources do not show significant coverage of the encyclical in mainstream newspapers or parliamentary debates, nor do they indicate that non‑Catholic humanitarian organisations explicitly responded to or coordinated with the papal appeal.[5][9][10][13] This relative invisibility can be attributed to several factors. First, the Vatican’s official marginalisation in the new international order reduced the perceived weight of its pronouncements among secular elites.[7] Second, the sheer volume of humanitarian appeals in the post‑war period—from governments, NGOs, and other religious bodies—meant that any single document, however eloquent, faced competition for attention.

Within the Church, the encyclical may have been more significant, but here too its impact is hard to trace. It did not introduce new doctrine or provoke theological controversy, so later Catholic social teaching rarely cites it as a foundational text. Academic references to *Paterno Iam Diu* tend to appear in footnotes as examples of Benedict XV’s humanitarian concern or as illustrations of his encyclical corpus, rather than as subjects of sustained analysis.[7][16] One legal scholar, for instance, cites the encyclical in passing in a discussion of sources under the Bluebook citation system, indicating its availability as a reference point but not its centrality.[16] The document thus occupies a somewhat marginal position in both ecclesial and academic memory, overshadowed by more programmatic social encyclicals and by Benedict’s dramatic but unsuccessful wartime peace initiatives.

This limited influence does not, however, diminish the encyclical’s value as a lens for understanding the intersection of papal teaching, humanitarian action, and post‑war crisis. Its very marginality allows us to see how the Church engaged in practical humanitarianism without necessarily aiming for doctrinal innovation or political transformation. It exemplifies a mode of papal intervention that is modest in scope but deeply embedded in contemporary social realities.

### Missed opportunities and counterfactuals

From an investigative standpoint, it is legitimate to ask whether *Paterno Iam Diu* could have gone further in addressing the structural causes of child hunger or in challenging the policies that perpetuated it. Some secular activists at the time explicitly criticised the Allied blockade, the rigidity of reparations demands, and the failure to provide sufficient credit and economic support to Austria and other Central European states.[10][13] They argued that without a fundamental shift in economic policy, relief efforts would be at best a stopgap. In the British House of Lords debate, one speaker argued that only the re‑establishment of normal economic life, enabling populations to support themselves, would truly meet the menace of the situation.[10]

Benedict’s encyclical does not engage with such structural questions. It focuses on mobilisation of charity rather than on advocacy for policy change. One might speculate that a more politically outspoken encyclical could have added moral pressure on governments to revise punitive policies. However, given the Vatican’s marginal diplomatic status and Benedict’s commitment to neutrality, such an approach would have carried risks, potentially provoking backlash without achieving tangible change.[7][11] There is insufficient evidence to conclude that a more confrontational papal stance would have produced better outcomes; indeed, it could have jeopardised the Church’s ability to operate relief programmes in some countries.

What can be said is that *Paterno Iam Diu* embodies the limits of a charity‑centred approach in the face of systemic crises. It offers a powerful moral call to alleviate immediate suffering but does not address the deeper structures that produce vulnerability. Later Catholic social teaching would increasingly integrate structural analysis and justice language into its treatment of social issues, reflecting lessons learned from such experiences. In that sense, the encyclical can be seen as part of an evolutionary trajectory within Catholic engagement with the secular world, marking a stage where humanitarian action was foregrounded but not yet fully connected to broader critiques of economic and political order.

## Reflective and Prospective Assessment

### Backward‑looking gradient: scope and limits

On the reflective axis, *Paterno Iam Diu* looks back primarily at the five‑year period of the First World War and its immediate aftermath, recognising that the suffering unleashed by the conflict did not end with the armistice but continued in new forms.[1][7][12] The encyclical mourns the physical devastation and the moral failure of Christian nations to embody charity on the scale required. This reflection is sharpened by Benedict’s personal experience of attempting, and largely failing, to influence the course of the war through earlier peace initiatives.[7] His acknowledgment that his hopes for a natural outpouring of charity after the war were disappointed reveals a sobering reassessment of human nature and international politics.

However, the depth of this reflection is constrained by the encyclical’s brevity and by its strategic avoidance of detailed political analysis. Benedict does not reconstruct the chain of causality leading from wartime policies to post‑war hunger, nor does he reflect on the role of the Church itself in legitimising or failing to resist nationalist fervour.[1][7][12] His backward gaze is focused on suffering rather than on culpability. This makes the document pastorally accessible but analytically thin compared to later social encyclicals that engage more explicitly with economic and political structures.

The reflective density score of −5 years captures this temporal limitation. Benedict’s gaze scarcely extends beyond the recent war; he does not invoke deeper historical patterns of European conflict, colonial exploitation, or economic injustice as explanatory frameworks. Nor does he engage in eschatological reflection that would situate the crisis within a broader theological narrative of history. The encyclical is thus firmly anchored in the immediate past, which gives it urgency but restricts its capacity for structural critique.

### Forward‑looking gradient: immediate action and generational vision

On the prospective axis, *Paterno Iam Diu* is far more expansive. Its immediate prescriptions are concrete: a global day of prayer and collection, specific types of goods to be gathered, committees to be entrusted with distribution, and a papal example of financial contribution.[1][12] These directives are intended to produce measurable, short‑term outcomes—literally, food in children’s mouths and clothes on their backs during the winter of 1919–1920. The encyclical’s prospective saturation is high because almost every sentence pushes toward action or articulates reasons for action.

Beyond the immediate, the encyclical articulates a generational vision in which today’s children are recognised as the “germs of future generations,” whose physical and moral condition will shape the destiny of nations.[1][12] This language implies a temporal horizon of twenty years or more, looking forward to when these children will be adults. It reflects awareness, shared by contemporary secular reformers, that the long‑term effects of childhood malnutrition and trauma can include diminished productivity, increased susceptibility to disease, and social instability. Benedict thus positions child relief not only as an act of present mercy but as a prudent investment in Europe’s future.

This prospective vision also has a normative dimension. By appealing to “all who have the sentiments of humanity,” Benedict implicitly envisions a future international community where such sentiments are widely shared and where child welfare is a common moral priority.[1][12] This aspiration aligns with, and in some ways anticipates, the later development of international law and institutions centred on children’s rights and humanitarian norms.[5][9][11] The encyclical does not sketch concrete institutional proposals, but it contributes to the moral and cultural groundwork that would make such proposals plausible.

The prospective density score of +20 reflects the combination of immediate and generational horizons. While the explicit prescriptions concern actions to be taken within a few weeks of publication, the conceptual framing of children as bearers of future generations projects concerns and hopes well into the 1930s. Tragically, we know in hindsight that many of these children would grow up in societies marked by economic depression, political radicalisation, and another, even more devastating war. Benedict’s hopeful vision of saving a generation that would rebuild a peaceful Europe was only partially realised.

### Evaluating success and failure of the encyclical’s vision

To evaluate the success of *Paterno Iam Diu*’s prospective project, one must distinguish between its immediate humanitarian goals and its broader normative ambitions. On the immediate level, relief efforts in Central Europe, including those by Catholic organisations mobilised by appeals like the encyclical, did alleviate significant suffering. Save the Children’s fundraising, the ARA’s food shipments, and various church‑based initiatives improved nutritional conditions, especially among children, and contributed to halting the worst of the famine by the early 1920s.[6][9][14] Mortality from starvation and related diseases remained high during these years, but without such interventions, it would have been higher. While we lack precise data on the specific impact of *Paterno Iam Diu*, it is reasonable to conclude that it played a role in mobilising Catholic contributions to this broader relief effort.

On the normative level, the encyclical’s vision was only partially fulfilled. The elevation of child welfare as a central concern of international humanitarianism did materialise, as evidenced by the Geneva Declaration of the Rights of the Child and the proliferation of child‑focused NGOs.[5][9] The idea that children have special claims on the world’s compassion and resources became widely accepted. In that sense, Benedict’s appeal to “all who have sentiments of humanity” found resonance.

However, the larger hope that a generation of children saved from post‑war hunger would grow up to build a peaceful, just Europe was tragically undermined by subsequent events. The interwar period in Central Europe was marked by political turmoil, economic crises, and the rise of totalitarian regimes. Many children who survived the famine of 1919–1922 would later experience the traumas of the Great Depression, Nazism, Stalinism, and the Second World War. The structures that produced their initial vulnerability—economic fragility, nationalist rivalries, punitive peace settlements—were not fundamentally transformed. From this vantage point, *Paterno Iam Diu* appears as a compassionate but limited intervention, addressing symptoms without curing the underlying disease.

Yet it would be unfair to hold the encyclical responsible for failing to prevent subsequent geopolitical disasters. Its ambition was humanitarian more than political. Within its chosen scope, it articulated a coherent and morally compelling call that aligned the Church with emerging global norms of child‑centred humanitarianism. Its limitations underscore the need for humanitarian action to be complemented by structural reform, a lesson that later Catholic social teaching would increasingly emphasise.

### Artistic implications: encoding the gradients

For the purposes of the Papal Papers art project, translating these analytical findings into visual gradients involves encoding both the intensity and temporal reach of the encyclical’s reflective and prospective modes. The moderate reflective saturation of 5 suggests a colour that is neither pale nor overpowering on the backward‑looking side: the encyclical looks back at the immediate past with sorrow and realism but does not dwell there or develop a comprehensive historical analysis. The reflective density of −5 indicates that this backward gaze is temporally close, focused on the war years rather than on deep history.

On the prospective side, the high saturation of 9 calls for a vivid, dominant hue: *Paterno Iam Diu* is fundamentally a document about what must be done, now and in the future, more than about what has already happened. The prospective density of +20 invites a visual extension into the future, perhaps through gradients that fade or shift as they move forward in time, symbolising both the hope invested in future generations and the uncertainty that history would bring. The intersection of these gradients—short, intense reflection and long, ambitious projection—captures the encyclical’s core dynamic: a papal voice emerging from a devastated present, urgently trying to shape a more humane future through the figure of the suffering child.

## Conclusion

*Paterno Iam Diu* is an atypical encyclical not because it breaks doctrinal ground, but because it is almost entirely devoid of doctrinal argument. In less than a dozen paragraphs, Pope Benedict XV turns the immense, amorphous tragedy of post‑First World War Central Europe into a focused moral imperative: save the children. He does so in a historical moment when hunger had been deliberately weaponised through blockades, when empires had collapsed and new states were struggling to feed their populations, and when secular humanitarian organisations were beginning to identify children as the primary emblem of war’s lasting cruelty.[5][9][10][13][14] The encyclical reflects on this context in only the broadest terms, lamenting the “terrible conflict” and the insufficiency of charity, but it is prescient in its recognition that the future of societies is bound up with the wellbeing of their youngest members.[1][7][12]

Measured against the dual axes of reflection and projection, the document exhibits a modest backward‑looking saturation and a high forward‑looking saturation. Its reflective gaze is temporally close, reaching back only over the war years and eschewing deeper structural analysis. Its prospective gaze is both immediate and generational, prescribing a concrete action on 28 December 1919 and invoking the “germs of future generations” whose fate hangs in the balance.[1][12] The gradient scores—reflective saturation 5 with density −5, prospective saturation 9 with density +20—capture this configuration: a papal voice more concerned with mobilising relief and shaping humanitarian norms than with dissecting the past.

Historically, the encyclical’s direct impact is difficult to quantify. It likely contributed to Catholic participation in broader relief efforts that, alongside the work of organisations like Save the Children and the American Relief Administration, mitigated the worst effects of famine among children in Central Europe.[5][6][9][14] It also integrated Catholic communities into the emerging international humanitarian regime that would later crystallise around documents like the Geneva Declaration of the Rights of the Child.[5][9] Yet it did not decisively influence secular policy, and it did not address the structural economic and political factors that would lead to further European catastrophes in the following decades.[10][13]

As an artefact of papal engagement with the secular world, *Paterno Iam Diu* reveals both the possibilities and the limitations of humanitarian encyclicals. It shows a papacy stripped of temporal power but still capable of mobilising global networks for concrete aid, willing to cooperate with secular committees, and eager to speak in a language accessible not only to Catholics but to “all who have the sentiments of humanity.”[1][12] At the same time, it illustrates how a charity‑centred approach, however sincere, can leave untouched the deeper structures of injustice and vulnerability. For contemporary observers and for the Papal Papers project, the encyclical stands as a poignant reminder that saving children from immediate hunger is necessary but not sufficient; it must be accompanied by long‑term efforts to build a world in which such appeals are no longer needed.

In transforming *Paterno Iam Diu* into visual symbolism, one might therefore hold together its dual character as both a product of its time and a pointer beyond it. Its colours should carry the sombreness of a Europe ravaged by war and famine, but also the intensity of a moral claim that remains resonant: that the measure of a civilisation is how it treats its children, especially in the aftermath of its own self‑inflicted catastrophes.
