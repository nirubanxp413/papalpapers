# In Supremo Apostolatus: Gregory XVI, Slavery, and the Limits of Papal Intervention in a Changing Atlantic World

In *In Supremo Apostolatus* (1839), Pope Gregory XVI issued one of the most forceful papal denunciations of the transatlantic slave trade and the enslavement of Indigenous and African peoples, framing these practices as **“absolutely unworthy of the Christian name”** and tightly binding the prohibition to a lineage of earlier papal interventions on slavery stretching back to the fifteenth century.[1][11] The document emerged in a world where Britain and the United States had already outlawed the transatlantic trade, yet slavery itself remained entrenched across the Americas and the illegal traffic in human beings persisted, especially to Brazil and Cuba.[3][17] By explicitly recalling prior papal bulls such as Paul III’s *Sublimis Deus* (1537) and later pronouncements by Urban VIII, Benedict XIV, Pius II, and Pius VII, Gregory XVI’s brief was strongly reflective, constructing a moral narrative in which Christian charity had gradually weakened slavery only to see a shameful resurgence under the lure of “sordid gain.”[4][11] At the same time, the letter was sharply prescriptive, warning clergy and laity that they must neither participate in nor defend the trade and new enslavements, and forbidding any teaching that attempted to justify such practices.[1][11] Its gaze backward thus spans several centuries of papal engagement with slavery, while its gaze forward seeks to halt ongoing Christian complicity in slave trading and to anticipate a future in which new enslavements among Christians cease, even though the document stops short of demanding immediate emancipation of existing slaves.[3][11] Historically, *In Supremo Apostolatus* had uneven worldly effects: in some quarters it was celebrated as abolitionist; in others, notably among American Catholic leaders such as Bishop John England, it was narrowed to a condemnation of the trade alone and domesticated in ways that left the institution of slavery intact.[9][14][16] This report situates the brief within its social and political moment, dissects its reflective and prospective dimensions, and assesses its actual impact and limits in the broader history of the Catholic Church and slavery.

### Document metadata and textual overview

*In Supremo Apostolatus* is formally an Apostolic Letter, sometimes referred to as a papal brief, issued by Pope Gregory XVI on December 3, 1839, following a consultation with the College of Cardinals.[1][11] The brief addresses “all the faithful of Christ” and bears the incipit “In supremo apostolatus,” drawn from its opening phrase that situates the pope at “the summit of the Apostolic power, holding the place of Jesus Christ,” and frames the letter as an exercise of that pastoral authority.[1][11] In its structure, the document opens with theological and historical reflections on slavery in the early Church, moves to a narrative of how Christian charity over time reduced slavery in many nations, and then laments the reappearance of slave trading and new enslavements among Christians in distant lands driven by the pursuit of profit. This is followed by a substantial section recalling earlier papal pronouncements on slavery, and culminates in a juridical set of prohibitions and exhortations directed at clergy and laity across the Catholic world.[1][11]

The text is notable for its explicit naming of the groups most affected by contemporary Christian slave trading, referring to “Indians, Blacks and other such peoples” who are reduced to servitude and treated “as if they were not men but rather animals.”[1][11] Gregory XVI describes the slave trade as an “inhuman traffic” that violates “the rights of justice and humanity,” and he emphasizes not only the personal cruelty of enslavement but also its social consequences, such as the “dissensions and almost perpetual conflicts” aroused in regions where traders purchase enslaved people from original captors.[1][11] At the level of legal language, the pope “reproves” these practices “by virtue of Our Apostolic Authority” and then “prohibits and strictly forbids” any ecclesiastic or lay person from defending the trade as permissible, under any pretext or excuse, or from teaching opinions contrary to what the letter sets forth, whether in public or private.[1][11] New enslavements and slave trading by Catholics are thus clearly and absolutely forbidden, even though the document does not contain a direct, formal command to emancipate all existing slaves within Christian societies.[3][11]

In contemporary reference works, *In Supremo Apostolatus* is often described as the culminating nineteenth-century papal condemnation of chattel slavery and the transatlantic slave trade.[3][11] Wikipedia’s synthesis, which draws on both the papalencyclicals text and modern scholarship, emphasizes that Gregory XVI condemned slavery as contrary to human dignity, with specific reference to New World slavery and the trade, while acknowledging that the document did not mirror the British and French model of calling for immediate emancipation of slaves.[3][11] The brief itself, as presented on papalencyclicals.net, underscores continuity with earlier papal documents, naming Paul III’s *Sublimis Deus* (1537), Urban VIII’s 1639 letter, Benedict XIV’s 1741 engagement with Brazilian slavery, Pius II’s intervention in 1462, and Pius VII’s efforts around the Congress of Vienna, thereby situating Gregory XVI’s own action within a long, if uneven, line of papal concern.[1][11] This self-conscious positioning is central to the document’s reflective dimension, while the prohibitive language drives its prescriptive character.

### Historical background: The papacy and slavery to 1839

To understand *In Supremo Apostolatus*, it is essential to situate it within the broader trajectory of papal responses to slavery from the fifteenth century onward. Early modern Europe saw the issuance of papal bulls that both facilitated and later sought to restrain aspects of slavery and colonial conquest. In the mid-fifteenth century, Pope Nicholas V issued bulls, including *Romanus Pontifex* (1455), that granted Portugal rights over newly discovered lands in Africa and, in practice, legitimized the enslavement of non-Christian peoples there, contributing to what later became known as the “Doctrine of Discovery.”[6] Contemporary media commentary, such as a widely circulated social media post from Sojourners Magazine, notes that these documents were invoked to justify the dispossession and subjugation of Indigenous populations, and that the Vatican has recently acknowledged that such papal texts were used in this way, even if this acknowledgment comes centuries after the fact.[5][6] These fifteenth-century bulls stand in tension with later papal efforts to protect certain categories of non-European peoples from enslavement, illustrating a complex and often contradictory papal relationship to slavery.

A significant turning point came with Paul III’s bull *Sublimis Deus* of June 2, 1537, which explicitly forbade the enslavement of Indigenous peoples of the Americas and other newly discovered or previously known Indigenous groups.[4][11] *Sublimis Deus* declared that the “Indians of the West and the South” are “truly men” capable of understanding the Catholic faith and possessing rights to freedom and property, even if they remain heathen, and condemned any claim to the contrary as inspired by the “enemy of the human race.”[4] The bull denounced their reduction to slavery in strong terms, declared such enslavements null and void, affirmed their right to liberty and property, and called for their evangelization rather than subjugation.[4] Its principles influenced the Valladolid debate on the morality of Spanish conquest and were incorporated into the New Laws of 1542 issued by Charles V of Spain, although these laws were frequently ignored by colonists and conquistadors.[4] Gregory XVI’s brief explicitly cites *Sublimis Deus* as the first major papal pronouncement in the historical chain he invokes, using it as evidence that papal solicitude for enslaved peoples is longstanding.[1][11]

Subsequent popes issued further interventions concerning slavery, often in regional contexts. Urban VIII in 1639, for example, sent a document to Portuguese authorities condemning the enslavement of Indigenous peoples in South America, and Benedict XIV in 1741 addressed the bishops of Brazil and other regions, seeking to mitigate the cruelties of slavery and protect certain rights of enslaved people, though not abolishing the institution outright.[3][11] Pius II in 1462 is remembered for condemning the enslavement of newly converted Christians by Christian pirates, and in the early nineteenth century Pius VII urged European powers to end the slave trade and participated in the Congress of Vienna’s call for its suppression.[7][11][13] A chapter in the *Cambridge History of the Papacy* notes that in 1814 Pius VII wrote to the restored French king Louis XVIII urging the end of slavery in France, and that in 1815 he joined the Congress of Vienna in calling for an end to the slave trade, thereby aligning the papacy with emerging diplomatic efforts to curtail the traffic in enslaved Africans.[7][13] Gregory XVI’s decision in 1839 to issue *In Supremo Apostolatus* can thus be seen as both a continuation and an intensification of these earlier, often more limited, papal gestures.

Modern syntheses of the Catholic Church’s relationship to slavery underscore that by the nineteenth century the Church had reached a relative consensus condemning chattel slavery and praising its abolition, even though earlier centuries had witnessed both toleration and complicity.[3][11] The current *Catechism of the Catholic Church*, reflecting this later development, situates slavery within the violations of the Seventh Commandment, stating that acts or enterprises that lead to the enslavement of human beings—treating them as merchandise and reducing them by violence to their productive value or profit source—are sins against the dignity of persons and their fundamental rights.[3] Gregory XVI’s brief occupies a transitional space in this history: more forceful than many predecessors, more explicitly universal in its condemnation of new enslavement and slave trading among Christians, yet not fully aligned with later Catholic doctrine that treats slavery per se as intrinsically evil in all forms. This background is vital for evaluating the reflective and prospective elements of the document.

### Immediate social and political context: Transatlantic slave trade and abolition circa 1839

When Gregory XVI issued *In Supremo Apostolatus* in December 1839, the transatlantic slave trade was officially illegal for key Atlantic powers but still functionally alive, especially in its illegal forms, and slavery itself remained deeply entrenched across the Americas.[3][17] Historical overviews of the transatlantic trade note that its volume peaked in the eighteenth century, with millions of Africans transported to the New World, and that the British and United States governments formally outlawed the trade in 1807.[17] Britain later pressured other nations to follow suit and, by the 1840s, committed about ten percent of its naval forces to suppressing the trade along the West African coast, seizing slave ships and entering into bilateral treaties aimed at curtailing the traffic.[17] Yet despite these measures, illegal slaving continued, particularly to Brazilian and Cuban markets, where plantation economies and demand for enslaved labor persisted, and the enforcement of anti-slave trade laws was uneven and often undermined by local elites.

Within the United States, the 1807 ban on the international trade did not end the domestic institution of slavery, which remained central to the economy of the Southern states and was protected by federal and state law.[17] The internal slave trade flourished, and the forced migration of enslaved people from the Upper to the Lower South intensified, fueling cotton expansion and deepening the entrenchment of slavery in American society. In Latin America, slavery was still legal in Brazil and other regions, and abolitionist movements were only beginning to gather strength; Brazil would not abolish the slave trade until 1850 and slavery itself until 1888, and Cuba would maintain slavery into the later nineteenth century.[3] Against this backdrop, papal concern about slavery had both a European diplomatic dimension—aligned with British-led efforts to suppress the trade—and a pastoral dimension directed toward Catholic populations in slaveholding societies, including the Americas.

Diplomatically, the Congress of Vienna (1814–1815) had already treated the slave trade as a moral and political issue, with British diplomats and abolitionists placing great hopes in the influence of Pope Pius VII.[13] A conference panel on “The Congress of Vienna and the Slave Trade” notes that Pius VII was assigned a key role by British abolitionists, who believed that papal support for ending the trade could sway Catholic powers such as Portugal and Spain.[13] Pius VII’s participation in the Congress’s call to suppress the trade illustrates the papacy’s willingness to intervene in international debates on slavery, albeit within the constraints of its spiritual authority and the complex politics of the era.[7][13] Gregory XVI’s brief came roughly a quarter-century after these events, at a time when abolitionist sentiment was stronger in Britain and parts of Europe but Catholic slaveholding societies in the Americas remained largely resistant to change.

Internally, the Papal States themselves were not major slaveholding territories, and the papacy’s direct economic stake in slavery was limited. However, Catholic religious orders and institutions in colonial regions sometimes owned enslaved people, and Catholic elites in Brazil, Cuba, and the American South were deeply embedded in slave economies.[3][9] Abolitionism within Catholic circles was uneven: while some Catholic thinkers and missionaries opposed slavery, others defended it or accommodated it as a regrettable but legally and socially entrenched institution. In the United States, Catholic bishops had to navigate a political landscape in which anti-Catholic sentiment was strong in the North and where Catholic communities in the South were often slaveholders.[9][14] This made a blunt papal condemnation of slavery politically sensitive, and the reception of *In Supremo Apostolatus* there would reflect those tensions.

### Internal Church context: Theology, authority, and slavery

The internal ecclesial context of *In Supremo Apostolatus* involves both theological developments and papal authority structures. Theologically, the Catholic tradition had long tolerated slavery under certain conditions, viewing it as a social reality that could be mitigated by Christian virtue rather than abolished outright. Early Christian writings advised masters to treat slaves kindly, and medieval canon law recognized forms of servitude.[3][11] However, from the late medieval period onward, the Church also fostered practices of manumission and discouraged extreme abuses, and over time, Christian charity was increasingly invoked as a factor in the gradual erosion of slavery in many European societies.[3][11] Gregory XVI’s brief reflects this trajectory by noting that, as pagan superstition faded and the manners of barbarous peoples were softened through faith operating by charity, slavery disappeared from many Christian nations over several centuries.[1][11]

Institutionally, Gregory XVI had to negotiate the complex role of the papacy in a period marked by political upheaval and growing challenges to papal temporal power. He was elected in 1831, taking the name Gregory XVI, and his pontificate was characterized by strong opposition to modern liberalism, nationalism, and certain technological innovations, but also by a concern to assert papal authority in matters of faith and morals.[3] The issuance of *In Supremo Apostolatus* was a significant exercise of this moral authority, particularly because he consulted widely with the College of Cardinals before promulgating the brief, signaling that the condemnation of slavery and slave trading was not a personal opinion but a collective judgment of the Church’s highest leadership.[11] The document’s juridical tone—its use of prohibitive language and its extension of the ban to teaching and publication—reflects a desire to legislate the behavior of Catholics worldwide on this issue.

At the doctrinal level, *In Supremo Apostolatus* did not represent a formal and explicit declaration that slavery in all its forms is intrinsically evil, but it did move the Church closer to that position by condemning the slave trade and new enslavements as violations of justice, humanity, and Christian brotherhood.[3][11] Later Catholic teaching would build on this foundation. As noted earlier, the *Catechism of the Catholic Church* now explicitly lists enslavement under violations of the Seventh Commandment, describing as sinful any enterprises that lead to human beings being bought, sold, or exchanged like merchandise, in disregard of their dignity.[3] Pope Leo XIII, in an 1888 letter to the bishops of Brazil, would further clarify the Church’s stance by commending the abolition of slavery in that country and condemning the slave trade’s cruelties, marking an additional step toward explicit recognition of slavery’s incompatibility with Christian social teaching.[3] In this sense, Gregory XVI’s brief can be seen as a pivotal node in a developing doctrinal arc.

The tensions within this ecclesial context are evident in later debates about what exactly *In Supremo Apostolatus* condemned. Some commentators, including an author cited by EWTN in an article on “The Popes and Slavery,” have claimed that Gregory XVI condemned the slave trade but not slavery itself.[2] Others, including historians of the Catholic Church and slavery, argue that the brief’s language condemns both the trade and the reduction of people to servitude, even if it does not call for immediate emancipation, and that it marked a clear moral stance against chattel slavery as practiced in the Americas.[3][11] This ambiguity would have practical consequences, as local Catholic leaders in slaveholding societies sought to interpret the document in ways that fit their political and social circumstances, underscoring the complex interplay between papal authority and local ecclesial-political realities.

### Reflective character: Backward-looking narrative and historical framing

The reflective character of *In Supremo Apostolatus* is pronounced and multi-layered. Gregory XVI does not simply issue a condemnation of contemporary slave trading; he constructs a historical narrative that stretches from the apostolic era to his own time, thereby situating his action within a broader story about Christianity and slavery.[1][11] He begins by recalling that, in the time of the Apostles, slavery existed as a social institution and was tolerated by Christian communities, but that believers were exhorted to treat slaves with kindness and justice, recognizing that they shared a common Master in heaven who made no distinction of persons.[11] This sets up a contrast between an earlier phase of toleration tempered by charity and a later phase of moral progress.

Gregory XVI then describes how, as Christian faith spread and pagan superstition declined, the customs of barbarous peoples were softened and, over several centuries, slavery disappeared from many Christian nations.[1][11] This depiction of history serves two purposes. First, it underscores the transformative power of Christian charity and suggests that slavery is not an inevitable or permanent fixture of human society, but one that can be overcome through moral development. Second, it creates a sense of tragedy and scandal regarding the re-emergence of slave trading among Christians in more recent times. The pope expresses “profound sorrow” that, despite this progress, there are now faithful Catholics who, driven by the desire for “sordid gain,” have reduced Indians, Blacks, and other peoples to slavery in distant lands or have facilitated the trade in those already enslaved, thereby reversing the earlier trajectory of Christian moral progress.[1][11]

Most strikingly, Gregory XVI engages in a detailed recollection of earlier papal interventions on slavery. He cites Paul III’s *Sublimis Deus* of May 29, 1537, addressed to the Cardinal Archbishop of Toledo, which prohibited the enslaving of Indigenous peoples and affirmed their rights to liberty and property.[4][11] He recalls Urban VIII’s 1639 letter to the Collectors of the Apostolic Chamber of Portugal, Benedict XIV’s 1741 letter to the bishops of Brazil and other regions, Pius II’s intervention of October 7, 1462, and the efforts of Pius VII in the early nineteenth century, particularly his alignment with the Congress of Vienna’s call to suppress the slave trade.[7][11][13] By weaving these specific dates, names, and documents into his brief, Gregory XVI crafts a narrative of papal continuity and persistence: the Holy See has “never failed” to intervene on behalf of enslaved peoples, even if these efforts have not always met with complete success.[1][11]

This backward-looking emphasis is reflective in a deeper sense as well. Gregory XVI explicitly acknowledges that, despite repeated papal admonitions, Christian involvement in slavery and slave trading has continued. He laments that “no small number of Christians” have ignored earlier papal warnings and have persisted in treating Blacks as if they were animals, buying and selling them without regard for justice or humanity.[1][11] This acknowledgment of failure—of the limits of papal authority in changing entrenched social and economic practices—adds a sober tone to the document and underscores the reflective saturation of the brief. It is not merely celebratory of past papal actions; it is critical of how those actions have been disregarded and of the scandal this brings to the Christian name.

The temporal density of this backward gaze extends far beyond Gregory XVI’s own lifetime. By evoking papal documents from the fifteenth century (Pius II), sixteenth century (Paul III), seventeenth century (Urban VIII), eighteenth century (Benedict XIV), and early nineteenth century (Pius VII), the brief spans roughly three to four centuries of ecclesial history, even though the main practical concern is the nineteenth-century transatlantic trade.[4][7][11] This depth is captured in the reflective density score of −25, indicating that the document’s reflective horizon reaches well beyond the immediate past and into long-term historical memory. Yet, at the same time, the brief is selective in its recall: it does not mention earlier papal bulls, such as Nicholas V’s *Romanus Pontifex*, that authorized enslavement of non-Christians, perhaps because doing so would complicate the narrative of consistent papal opposition to slavery.[6][11] This selective memory is itself a feature of the reflective character: it reflects on history, but in a way that emphasizes continuity of condemnation rather than admits outright contradictions.

### Prospective character: Forward-looking prescriptions, warnings, and expectations

The prospective character of *In Supremo Apostolatus* is even more intense than its reflective dimension. After narrating the history of Christian engagement with slavery and recalling earlier papal interventions, Gregory XVI turns to a series of clear prescriptions aimed at the future conduct of Catholics. He warns and “earnestly adjures” faithful Christians of every condition that no one in the future should dare to vex anyone, despoil them of their possessions, reduce them to servitude, or lend aid and favor to those who engage in such practices.[1][11] He condemns the inhuman traffic by which Blacks are brought into servitude and are bought, sold, and devoted to the hardest labor without distinction, in contempt of justice and humanity.[1][11] These exhortations are explicitly future-oriented, seeking to prevent any repetition of such acts among Christians.

Moreover, Gregory XVI does not limit his prescriptions to direct perpetrators of the slave trade. He extends the prohibition to those who, for gain, make propositions of purchase to the first owners of Blacks, thereby fueling dissensions and conflicts in the regions where these transactions occur.[1][11] He then moves into a juridical register, reproving “all the practices above mentioned as absolutely unworthy of the Christian name” by virtue of his apostolic authority, and prohibiting and strictly forbidding any ecclesiastic or lay person from defending the traffic in Blacks as permissible or from publishing or teaching opinions contrary to the brief’s teaching, whether publicly or privately.[1][11] This demand that Catholics refrain not only from participation but also from intellectual defense of slavery and slave trading is prescriptive in a strong sense: it seeks to shape future discourse, eliminating theological or moral arguments that might justify such practices.

Importantly, the brief’s prescriptions are framed not merely as moral advice but as authoritative commands. Gregory XVI invokes his position as Vicar of Christ and uses formal language of prohibition—“We prohibit,” “strictly forbid”—that carries, within the Church’s legal framework, a binding character on the conscience of Catholics.[1][11] He grounds these prescriptions in the theological principles of charity, justice, and the universal brotherhood of humanity in Christ, but he also emphasizes their disciplinary consequences by targeting the teaching and publishing of contrary opinions. In doing so, he anticipates future debates and seeks to forestall the development of pro-slavery theological justifications within the Church.

Despite this strong prescriptive thrust, the brief’s prospective horizon is not unlimited. It does not explicitly call for the emancipation of all existing slaves in Christian societies, nor does it lay out a detailed program for how Catholic states should transition away from slave-based economies.[3][11] Modern scholars note that while Gregory XVI condemned both the slave trade and the enslavement of new victims, his brief stopped short of demanding immediate abolition of slavery itself, unlike British and French policies that explicitly legislated emancipation in their empires.[3] The prospective density score of +5 reflects this limited yet real forward horizon: the document is primarily concerned with the immediate and near-term future—the cessation of slave trading and new enslavements—but implicitly supports a longer-term movement toward abolition by delegitimizing Catholic defense of slavery and aligning the Church with broader humanitarian trends.

From the vantage point of subsequent history, some of Gregory XVI’s implicit expectations were partially fulfilled, while others were not. Over the following decades, more Catholic-majority societies abolished the slave trade and, eventually, slavery itself; Brazil ended the trade in 1850 and slavery in 1888, and the Vatican never reversed its moral stance against slavery.[3] However, Catholic participation in slaveholding persisted for decades after the brief, especially in the United States and Brazil, and Catholic leaders sometimes interpreted *In Supremo Apostolatus* in ways that minimized its practical demands, as will be discussed in more detail below.[9][14][16] In this sense, the prescriptive saturation of the document reflects Gregory XVI’s strong attempt to shape the future, while the limits of its practical impact reveal the constraints on papal foresight and authority in the face of entrenched social systems.

### Reception and impact: Europe, the Americas, and contested meanings

The reception of *In Supremo Apostolatus* varied across regions and social contexts, illustrating both its influence and its limitations. In Europe, where abolitionist sentiment was stronger and the slave trade had already been officially curtailed by major powers, the brief aligned the papacy more clearly with humanitarian and diplomatic efforts to end the trade. Papal support for suppression of the slave trade at the Congress of Vienna and subsequent diplomatic actions by Pius VII had already paved the way for a more abolitionist posture, and Gregory XVI’s explicit condemnation of the trade and new enslavements reinforced this orientation.[7][13] In Catholic-majority countries like France and Spain, the brief contributed to the moral climate that would eventually support abolition, although national politics, economic interests, and colonial dynamics played significant roles independent of papal influence.

In the Americas, the impact was more complex. The United States had already banned the international slave trade in 1807 but remained deeply committed to domestic slavery, especially in the South. The Catholic Church there was a relatively small minority, and bishops had to navigate a political environment in which anti-Catholic prejudice was widespread and where Catholic communities in slaveholding regions often participated in the institution.[9][14] An article in the *Catholic Historical Review* titled “Three Cheers for the Abolitionist Pope!: American Reaction to Gregory XVI’s Condemnation of the Slave Trade, 1840–1860” examines how the brief was reported and debated in the United States, noting that reactions ranged from praise to efforts at reinterpretation.[15][16] Some American Catholics and abolitionists welcomed the papal condemnation as aligning the Church with broader moral movements, while others sought to limit its scope to the slave trade and new enslavements, leaving domestic slavery untouched.[14][16]

A key figure in the American reception was Bishop John England of Charleston, South Carolina. England wrote a letter to U.S. Secretary of State John Forsyth on September 29, 1840, in which he interpreted *In Supremo Apostolatus* as condemning the slave trade but not domestic slavery per se, and defended the South’s slave society within certain moral limits.[9][12][14] The article “Slavery and American Catholicism” notes that England framed the papal brief in a way that allowed Southern Catholics to remain loyal to the pope while continuing to uphold slavery as a legal institution.[9] A more detailed study, “Bishop John England’s Defense of Domestic Slavery and the ‘Incongruous’ Bull,” observes that England did not present the South’s slave society as an alternative political or economic system but sought to reconcile it with Catholic teaching by emphasizing the brief’s focus on the trade and new enslavements rather than on existing slaves.[12][15] This interpretive strategy illustrates how local ecclesial leaders could domesticate papal pronouncements to fit regional contexts, thereby limiting their transformative potential.

Rome’s response to slavery in the United States more broadly has been analyzed in another *Catholic Historical Review* article, “Rome’s Response to Slavery in the United States,” which points out that Gregory XVI’s condemnation was clear but that subsequent papal interventions remained cautious and often indirect.[16] The article notes that while the pope’s letter was disseminated and discussed, the Holy See did not follow it with strong disciplinary actions against slaveholding Catholics or bishops who defended slavery, and that Rome’s communications often focused on broader moral principles rather than specific policy mandates.[14][16] This pattern underscores the tension between the prescriptive aspirations of *In Supremo Apostolatus* and the practical constraints of the papacy’s diplomatic and pastoral strategies, especially in regions where Catholicism was a minority and where open confrontation might have exacerbated anti-Catholic sentiment.

In Brazil and other Latin American countries, reception was likewise mixed. Benedict XIV’s earlier letter to the bishops of Brazil had addressed slavery’s abuses without abolishing the institution, and Brazilian society remained heavily slave-dependent well into the nineteenth century.[3][11] Gregory XVI’s brief added moral pressure by explicitly condemning the trade and new enslavements, and over time, Catholic voices contributed to abolitionist movements. Leo XIII’s 1888 letter to the Brazilian bishops, written as Brazil moved to abolish slavery, praised the abolition and condemned the slave trade’s cruelties, indicating that by then the Church’s position had hardened against slavery itself.[3] Yet throughout the period between 1839 and 1888, Catholic participation in Brazilian slavery remained significant, and papal pronouncements did not automatically translate into immediate social change. The brief thus had a normative impact—shaping what could be defended morally within the Church—but its practical effects on slaveholding elites were gradual and constrained.

In the longer term, *In Supremo Apostolatus* has been reinterpreted within debates about the Catholic Church’s complicity and resistance to slavery. Some modern apologetic accounts, such as the EWTN article “The Popes and Slavery: Setting the Record Straight,” emphasize the continuity of papal opposition to slavery, highlighting documents like *Sublimis Deus* and *In Supremo Apostolatus* as evidence that the Church condemned slavery and the slave trade.[2][4][11] Critics, however, point to earlier papal bulls that authorized enslavement and to the slow pace of practical reforms, arguing that the Church’s record is ambivalent and marked by moral contradictions.[3][5][6] Recent Vatican statements acknowledging that fifteenth-century papal documents were used to justify colonial dispossession and enslavement, and distancing the Church from those texts, indicate a growing willingness to confront this ambivalence.[5][6] Within this contested memory, Gregory XVI’s brief occupies an important, but not unproblematic, place.

### Gaps, ambiguities, and tensions in the document

Despite its forceful condemnations, *In Supremo Apostolatus* contains notable gaps and ambiguities that have shaped both historical and contemporary interpretations. One key ambiguity concerns the scope of its denunciation of “reducing to servitude.” The brief clearly forbids Catholics from reducing Indians, Blacks, and other peoples to slavery in the future, and from participating in the trade in those already enslaved.[1][11] It condemns the inhuman traffic by which people are bought and sold without distinction, and reproves such practices as incompatible with the Christian name.[1][11] However, it does not explicitly declare the continued ownership of existing slaves to be intrinsically immoral, nor does it call for their immediate emancipation. This silence has allowed some commentators to argue that Gregory XVI condemned only the trade and new enslavements, not slavery as an institution, while others see the logic of his arguments as implicitly undermining all forms of chattel slavery.[2][3][11]

Another tension lies in the document’s selective historical memory. Gregory XVI recalls papal interventions that condemned or mitigated slavery—Paul III, Urban VIII, Benedict XIV, Pius II, Pius VII—but does not mention earlier bulls such as Nicholas V’s *Romanus Pontifex* that granted Portuguese monarchs the right to conquer and subjugate non-Christian lands and, in practice, to enslave their inhabitants.[6][11] Contemporary critical commentary has highlighted these earlier bulls as foundational to the “Doctrine of Discovery” and colonial enslavement, and recent Vatican statements acknowledge that these documents were misused to justify dispossession.[5][6] By omitting them, *In Supremo Apostolatus* constructs a narrative of consistent papal opposition to slavery that is historically incomplete, reflecting a tension between the desire to present the Holy See as a moral leader and the reality of its earlier complicity in systems that facilitated enslavement.

The brief also navigates a delicate balance between condemning Christian slave traders and avoiding direct confrontation with specific nations or economic systems. Gregory XVI refers to “lonely and distant countries” where Christians reduce people to slavery and develop the trade, and to “regions” where dissensions arise due to the purchase of Blacks from their first owners, but he does not name particular states such as Brazil, Cuba, or the United States.[1][11] This generality may have been diplomatically prudent but also renders the document less pointed as a political intervention. It condemns practices in principle but leaves considerable room for local authorities to interpret its practical implications for their own societies.

Furthermore, the brief frames enslaved peoples primarily as passive victims rather than active agents. Gregory XVI speaks of Indians and Blacks as “wretched peoples” who are reduced to slavery and subjected to harsh labor, and he emphasizes the harm done to them and the scandal caused among “infidel peoples” who witness Christian cruelty and therefore hate the Christian name.[1][11] Yet he does not discuss the perspectives, resistance, or aspirations of enslaved people themselves, nor does he engage with abolitionist movements led by those directly affected by slavery. This omission reflects the paternalistic lens of nineteenth-century papal documents and limits the brief’s capacity to acknowledge enslaved people as subjects of history rather than objects of charity.

Finally, the tension between the document’s strong prescriptive language and the absence of concrete enforcement mechanisms raises questions about its practical efficacy. Gregory XVI forbids any Catholic from defending or teaching the permissibility of slavery and the trade, but the brief does not specify canonical penalties for violating these prohibitions, nor does it outline procedures for disciplining clergy or laypeople who ignore its commands.[1][11] In practice, local bishops and religious superiors retained significant discretion in handling pro-slavery voices within their jurisdictions, and as the American case shows, some used that discretion to reinterpret the brief in ways that minimized its disruptive potential.[9][12][14][16] This tension between moral authority and institutional enforcement is central to assessing the document’s prospective impact.

### Comparative positioning among papal slavery-related texts

Within the corpus of papal documents on slavery, *In Supremo Apostolatus* occupies a distinctive position as both a culmination of earlier interventions and a bridge to later, more explicit condemnations. Compared to Paul III’s *Sublimis Deus*, which focused on Indigenous peoples of the Americas and grounded its prohibition of their enslavement in a recognition of their rationality and capacity for faith, Gregory XVI’s brief has a broader scope, targeting both Indigenous and African peoples and condemning the entire slave trade among Christians.[4][11] While *Sublimis Deus* centered on the rights of Indigenous peoples and their evangelization, *In Supremo Apostolatus* emphasizes the scandal caused by Christian participation in slavery and the moral obligation of Catholics to abstain from and oppose these practices.[1][4][11] In this sense, Gregory XVI expands the range of condemned practices and moves closer to a universal denunciation of chattel slavery.

Compared to Benedict XIV’s 1741 letter to the bishops of Brazil, which addressed abuses within slavery and sought to protect certain rights of enslaved people without abolishing the institution, Gregory XVI’s brief is more radical in its explicit prohibition of the trade and new enslavements.[3][11] Benedict XIV’s intervention reflects a pastoral strategy of regulating and mitigating slavery, whereas Gregory XVI seeks to halt its expansion and delegitimize its moral defense among Catholics. Yet both documents share a reluctance to demand immediate emancipation, revealing a continuity of caution in papal engagement with economic structures deeply entwined with slavery.

In relation to Pius VII’s diplomatic actions at the Congress of Vienna, *In Supremo Apostolatus* translates papal support for suppressing the slave trade from the realm of international diplomacy into a more explicit moral and ecclesial command.[7][13] Pius VII’s letters to European monarchs and his participation in the Congress’s call for ending the trade demonstrate papal involvement in state-level negotiations, while Gregory XVI’s brief speaks directly to the faithful and forbids Catholics from participating in or defending the trade.[1][11] This shift from diplomatic advocacy to internal moral legislation marks an important development in papal strategy, even if it remains constrained by the limitations already noted.

Looking forward, *In Supremo Apostolatus* can be seen as a precursor to Leo XIII’s 1888 letter to the Brazilian bishops, which condemned the slave trade’s cruelties and praised abolition.[3] Leo XIII’s letter speaks in a context where slavery was on the verge of elimination in Brazil and thus emphasizes the Church’s support for that process, whereas Gregory XVI wrote at a time when slavery was still entrenched and abolition uncertain. The continuity between the two documents lies in their shared condemnation of the trade and recognition of slavery’s moral wrong; the difference lies in their prospective horizons and the political circumstances that shaped their impact.

In the modern era, the Catholic Church’s teachings and Vatican statements on slavery and colonialism retrospectively frame *In Supremo Apostolatus* as part of a trajectory toward full condemnation of slavery. The Catechism’s language, cited earlier, and recent Vatican acknowledgments that papal documents were misused to justify colonization and enslavement, illustrate a more self-critical stance.[3][5][6] Within this context, Gregory XVI’s brief is often held up as an abolitionist milestone, though critical scholarship reminds us that its limitations and ambiguities must be kept in view. The encyclical’s gradients of reflection and prescription thus capture both its historical importance and its constrained efficacy within a world that took many more decades to dismantle slavery fully.

## Unstructured

### Gregory XVI: Personal authority, political situation, and the choice to act

To grasp the deeper dynamics behind *In Supremo Apostolatus*, one must consider Gregory XVI himself—Mauro Cappellari—and the political situation of the papacy in the 1830s. Gregory XVI was elected in 1831 at a time when the Papal States were under pressure from liberal and nationalist movements, and his pontificate is often remembered for its conservative stance on political modernity and certain technologies, as well as its firm defense of papal temporal sovereignty.[3] Yet his conservatism in political matters did not prevent him from taking a relatively bold stand on slavery, a social issue that intersected with both moral theology and international politics. This dual posture—politically reactionary, morally assertive on certain questions—complicates any simplistic narrative about papal engagement with the secular world.

Gregory XVI’s authority was both spiritual and temporal, but his temporal power was geographically confined to the Papal States and increasingly contested by Italian nationalist aspirations. He did not command armies or fleets that could enforce his moral teachings across oceans, nor did he hold direct sway over the economic policies of slavery-dependent nations like Brazil or the American South. His influence was largely moral and ecclesial, exercised through letters, briefs, and diplomatic communications. Nonetheless, as head of a global Church, his words carried weight among Catholics and could, in principle, shape their consciences and behaviors, particularly in questions of faith and morals. It is within this limited yet significant sphere that *In Supremo Apostolatus* must be understood: as an attempt by a pope with constrained worldly power to intervene in a vast Atlantic system of human exploitation.

The decision to issue a comprehensive condemnation of the slave trade and new enslavements suggests that Gregory XVI recognized both the moral urgency of the issue and the inadequacy of piecemeal responses. Earlier papal interventions had often been context-specific, addressing particular abuses or regions, and had frequently been ignored by colonial authorities or undermined by economic interests.[4][7][11] By the late 1830s, abolitionist movements and diplomatic efforts had made suppression of the trade a prominent international concern, but Catholic participation in slavery persisted. Gregory XVI’s choice to consult the College of Cardinals, as noted in the brief, indicates that he sought broad consensus within the Church’s leadership before acting, perhaps to strengthen the authority of the condemnation and to pre-empt internal opposition.[11] This consultative process also reflects the encyclical’s reflective character: it is the product of institutional memory and deliberation, not merely of individual conscience.

At the same time, Gregory XVI’s political situation may have influenced the document’s cautiousness regarding immediate emancipation. The papacy depended on maintaining diplomatic relations with Catholic monarchs and avoiding direct confrontation that might further weaken its position. Calling for the instantaneous abolition of slavery in all Catholic countries could have provoked resistance from powerful elites and states whose economies were deeply intertwined with slave labor. By focusing on the trade and new enslavements, and by framing the condemnation as a continuation of earlier papal concern, Gregory XVI could take a strong moral stance while leaving some room for gradualism in practice. This compromise did not satisfy all abolitionists, but it may have been seen within the Vatican as necessary to preserve both moral integrity and political viability.

### The Atlantic system of slavery: Geography, economy, and human cost

The world to which *In Supremo Apostolatus* spoke was structured by an Atlantic system of slavery that had, for centuries, intertwined Europe, Africa, and the Americas in a web of coerced labor and racialized exploitation. The transatlantic slave trade had transported millions of Africans to the Americas, where they were forced to work on plantations, in mines, and in domestic settings, generating wealth for colonial powers and local elites.[17] Although Britain and the United States had outlawed the trade in 1807, illegal slaving continued, and the internal dynamics of slavery persisted. In Brazil, sugar and later coffee plantations relied heavily on enslaved labor, and the country became one of the last major slave societies in the hemisphere.[3] In the Caribbean, islands like Cuba operated slave-based economies that were deeply integrated into global trade networks.

The economic logic of this system presented a formidable obstacle to moral reform. Slave labor was cheap, coerced, and exploited, and the profits from plantation agriculture and mining underwrote the political power and social status of slaveholding elites. Many European and American Catholics participated in this system, either as direct slaveholders, investors, or beneficiaries of colonial trade. In some cases, religious orders owned plantations and enslaved people, using the proceeds to fund their missions and institutions.[3][9] The Catholic Church as an institution was thus not entirely separate from the system it was condemning; its own members were implicated in the web of exploitation.

Gregory XVI’s brief speaks into this system by targeting the moral conscience of Catholics, but it does not directly address the economic structures that sustain slavery. The document condemns the trade and new enslavements as violations of justice and humanity and as sources of social conflict, but it does not analyze the political economy of slavery or suggest concrete alternatives.[1][11] It assumes, perhaps, that ending the trade and new enslavements will gradually weaken the institution, but it does not venture into the realm of economic redesign. This gap reflects both the genre of papal moral teaching and the limitations of the Vatican’s expertise and authority in economic matters.

From an anthropological perspective, the Atlantic system of slavery also created complex cultural and social worlds. Enslaved Africans and their descendants forged communities, religions, and cultural practices under conditions of extreme oppression, and their resistance took many forms—from open rebellion to everyday acts of defiance.[17] Catholicism itself was sometimes embraced by enslaved people, who found in its rituals and symbols both comfort and potential resources for resistance. Yet *In Supremo Apostolatus* does not address these lived realities; it sees enslaved people mainly through the lens of victimhood and the scandal their mistreatment causes among “infidel peoples” who may be less inclined to accept Christianity when they see Christians enslaving others.[1][11] This omission is significant: it reveals a limitation in the papal gaze, which is focused more on the moral purity of Christians than on the agency and perspectives of those they enslave.

### American Catholicism and the “incongruous bull”: Misinterpretation and resistance

The reception of *In Supremo Apostolatus* in the United States offers a vivid case study of how papal pronouncements can be reshaped and resisted in local contexts. As noted earlier, Bishop John England of Charleston, a prominent leader of American Catholicism, responded to the brief by interpreting it narrowly as a condemnation of the slave trade rather than of slavery itself.[9][12][14] In his letter to Secretary of State John Forsyth, England sought to reassure the U.S. government that the pope’s proclamation did not directly challenge American domestic slavery, which remained a legal institution.[9][14] He argued that Gregory XVI’s concern was with the traffic in enslaved people across borders and with new enslavements, not with established social arrangements in the South.

Scholars writing in journals such as the *Catholic Historical Review* and other academic venues have described England’s interpretation as “incongruous,” given the breadth of Gregory XVI’s language forbidding Catholics from reducing anyone to servitude.[12][15][16] Watt’s study, “The Incongruous Bull: In Supremo Apostolatus,” highlights how England and other American Catholic leaders managed to domesticate the brief, aligning it with Southern interests while nominally honoring papal authority.[15] England did not present the South’s slave society as a utopian or superior social order; rather, he treated it as a given that could be morally managed, emphasizing that Catholic slaveholders should treat their slaves humanely and that the abolition of the trade did not necessarily entail the abolition of slavery itself.[12] This posture allowed Southern Catholics to remain loyal to Rome while avoiding a direct challenge to their way of life.

The broader American reaction to Gregory XVI’s condemnation between 1840 and 1860 encompassed a range of responses. Some Catholic abolitionists and Northern newspapers hailed the pope’s letter as aligning the Church with humanitarian principles, using it to counter Protestant accusations that Catholicism was morally backward.[14][15][16] Others downplayed its significance or emphasized its focus on the trade. Rome’s subsequent interventions were cautious, and the Holy See did not impose strong disciplinary measures on American bishops who defended slavery.[16] This pattern illustrates the limits of papal prescriptive authority in a context where Catholics were a minority and where slavery was intertwined with national identity and economic power.

From the perspective of gradient analysis, the American case shows that the high prescriptive saturation of *In Supremo Apostolatus* did not necessarily translate into high practical saturation. The document was prescriptive in intent, forbidding Catholics from participating in or defending slavery and the trade, but local interpretations could reinterpret its scope and soften its demands. In effect, the reflective narrative of papal continuity and moral outrage was recontextualized in a way that preserved existing social structures, revealing the complex interplay between central authority and local agency within the Church.

### Brazil and Latin America: Gradualism, abolition, and papal support

In Brazil and other Latin American contexts, the trajectory of slavery and abolition unfolded over a longer chronology, and the impact of *In Supremo Apostolatus* must be assessed within that broader arc. Brazil’s economy relied heavily on slave labor, particularly in sugar and coffee plantations, and the country became one of the largest recipients of enslaved Africans during the nineteenth century.[3][17] Although international pressure and treaties gradually constrained the slave trade, illegal slaving persisted, and the institution of slavery itself remained deeply entrenched. Catholic elites, including clergy and religious orders, were involved in this system, and Catholic theology had to grapple with its moral implications.

Gregory XVI’s condemnation added moral weight to abolitionist arguments by framing the trade and new enslavements as incompatible with Christian justice and charity. However, like in the American case, local Catholic responses varied. Some clergy and laypeople embraced the papal teaching and participated in abolitionist movements, while others defended slavery as a legally recognized institution that could be regulated but not immediately abolished. The Vatican did not issue explicit, immediate commands for Brazilian abolition, and the process of ending slavery took decades, shaped by internal political struggles and economic transitions.

The culmination of this process from a papal perspective came with Leo XIII’s letter to the Brazilian bishops in 1888, coinciding with Brazil’s abolition of slavery. In that document, Leo XIII condemned the cruelties of the slave trade and commended the abolition as a morally laudable step, aligning the Church more explicitly with anti-slavery outcomes.[3] This later intervention reflects a shift from Gregory XVI’s focus on prohibiting the trade and new enslavements to celebrating actual abolition, suggesting that the prospective horizon of papal teaching had advanced. Nonetheless, the path from 1839 to 1888 in Brazil reveals the slow and contested nature of change, and the limited capacity of papal pronouncements to directly transform deeply rooted social systems.

In other Latin American countries, similar dynamics played out. Slavery was abolished at different times and under varying conditions, often influenced by political independence movements, economic changes, and internal reforms. Catholic discourse on slavery and abolition was part of these processes but not always decisive. *In Supremo Apostolatus* contributed a clear moral condemnation of the trade and new enslavements and reinforced the illegitimacy of Catholic defense of slavery, but it did not dictate specific policy paths or timelines. This disjunction between moral teaching and policy outcomes underscores the need to distinguish the prescriptive saturation of the document from its practical efficacy.

### Modern Catholic memory and the re-evaluation of papal documents on slavery

In the twentieth and twenty-first centuries, the Catholic Church has re-evaluated its historical relationship to slavery and colonialism, leading to new interpretations of documents like *In Supremo Apostolatus* and earlier bulls. Modern syntheses of the Church’s teaching emphasize that by the late nineteenth and twentieth centuries, slavery was widely condemned within Catholic doctrine, and the Church praised its abolition.[3] The current *Catechism* frames slavery as a violation of human dignity and fundamental rights, building on the moral logic that underpinned Gregory XVI’s condemnation of the trade and new enslavements but extending it more clearly to the institution itself.[3] In this narrative, *In Supremo Apostolatus* is often portrayed as a key step in an evolution toward full denunciation.

Alongside this positive memory, there has been growing critical scrutiny of earlier papal documents that facilitated colonial conquest and enslavement. The fifteenth-century bulls of Nicholas V, including *Romanus Pontifex*, are now widely seen as contributing to the “Doctrine of Discovery,” which justified European domination of non-Christian lands and peoples.[6] Contemporary media commentary, such as posts from Sojourners Magazine and other sources, reports that the Vatican has formally acknowledged that these documents were used to justify dispossession and has distanced itself from them, signaling a willingness to confront the darker aspects of its historical record.[5][6] This re-evaluation complicates the narrative of consistent papal opposition to slavery and underscores the ambivalence in the Church’s role.

Within this modern memory, *In Supremo Apostolatus* assumes a dual character. On the one hand, it is celebrated as an abolitionist milestone, a clear condemnation of slavery and the slave trade that aligns the Church with humanitarian principles and foreshadows later doctrine.[2][3][11] On the other hand, critical scholars point out its limitations—its lack of explicit demand for immediate emancipation, its selective recall of papal history, its indirectness in naming slaveholding nations—and stress that the Church’s practical record in opposing slavery remained uneven. This double view is important for artistic and interpretive projects like Papal Papers, which seek not to canonize papal documents uncritically but to situate them within their messy historical contexts.

The recent Vatican statements on the misuse of papal documents in colonial contexts also invite a re-reading of Gregory XVI’s brief. If earlier bulls contributed to systems of enslavement and dispossession, then *In Supremo Apostolatus* can be seen, in part, as a corrective—an attempt to steer the Church away from those legacies and toward a more consistent defense of human dignity.[5][6] Yet, given its own ambiguities and limited impact, it does not fully resolve the tensions. The gradients of reflection and prescription in the brief thus resonate with contemporary efforts to reckon with history: they show a pope looking backward at a complex past and forward toward a hoped-for future, but constrained by the realities of his time.

### Reflective saturation and density: Historical depth and selective memory

Returning to the gradient framework, it is useful to unpack more concretely why *In Supremo Apostolatus* scores a reflective saturation of 7 and a reflective density of −25. The saturation score reflects the fact that the document devotes significant textual space to describing and interpreting what already exists or has occurred. Gregory XVI recounts the existence of slavery in the apostolic era, the gradual erosion of slavery in many Christian nations, the re-emergence of the slave trade and new enslavements among Christians, and the history of papal interventions on slavery.[1][11] These elements are not peripheral; they form the backbone of the brief’s argument and provide the context for its prescriptions. The pope is not simply reacting to an abstract moral principle; he is situating his condemnation within a narrative of Christian moral development and papal engagement with slavery.

The reflective density score of −25 captures the temporal breadth of this backward gaze. By invoking papal documents from 1462 (Pius II), 1537 (Paul III), 1639 (Urban VIII), 1741 (Benedict XIV), and the early nineteenth century (Pius VII), Gregory XVI reaches back over approximately three centuries.[4][7][11] He does so not merely as a footnote but as a central rhetorical strategy: the continuity of papal concern is presented as evidence that the Holy See has consistently opposed slavery, even if its interventions have met with limited success. This depth of recall is unusual for papal documents, which often focus more narrowly on recent events or theological debates. It underscores the anthropological richness of the brief, as it reflects on the longue durée of Christian engagement with slavery.

At the same time, the reflective dimension is shaped by selective memory. Gregory XVI does not mention papal bulls that authorized enslavement or significantly facilitated colonial domination, such as *Romanus Pontifex*.[6][11] His narrative presents a line of condemnation and amelioration rather than a more ambivalent history. This selectivity does not nullify the reflective character but complicates it: the brief reflects on history from a particular vantage point that emphasizes moral continuity and downplays contradictions. For gradient purposes, this nuance is important. The document is highly reflective, but its reflection is curated, aligning with the papacy’s desire to present itself as a consistent moral authority.

### Prospective saturation and density: Commands, hopes, and constrained foresight

On the prospective axis, the saturation score of 9 indicates that forward-looking prescriptions are the dominant organizing purpose of the document. After establishing the historical and theological context, Gregory XVI moves decisively into issuing commands for the future. He warns Catholics not to vex, despoil, or reduce anyone to servitude, condemns participation in the trade, and forbids any defense or teaching of its permissibility.[1][11] These prescriptions are comprehensive: they target direct perpetrators, facilitators, and intellectual defenders of slavery. The language is strong, invoking apostolic authority and employing juridical formulas that mark the brief as a legislative act within the Church.

The prospective density score of +5 suggests that the document’s forward gaze is primarily focused on the near-term future, with implicit extensions into a somewhat longer horizon. Gregory XVI wants Catholics to stop participating in and defending slavery immediately, but his hopes for the complete abolition of slavery in Christian societies are more implicit and long-term. He does not set specific timelines or outline detailed pathways for economic and social transition, nor does he predict concrete political events. His foresight is thus limited by both genre and context. Yet the brief’s logic—that new enslavements and defense of slavery are forbidden—lays groundwork for later developments, such as Leo XIII’s celebration of abolition and the Catechism’s explicit condemnation of slavery.[3] In this sense, the document’s prospective horizon extends beyond the immediate moment, even if it is not explicitly articulated.

The constrained foresight is evident in the brief’s failure to anticipate the degree of resistance and misinterpretation it would encounter, particularly in contexts like the American South. Gregory XVI may have hoped that Catholic slaveholders would respond by ceasing participation in the trade and gradually moving away from slavery, but the actual trajectory involved continued defense of domestic slavery, reinterpretation of his condemnation, and, in some cases, Catholic complicity in the institution until secular political events—such as the Civil War—forced change.[9][12][14][16] This mismatch between prescriptive intent and historical outcome underscores the limitations of papal predictive power and reveals the structural obstacles to moral reform.

### Investigative and anthropological reflections: Papal documents as social artifacts

From an investigative and anthropological standpoint, *In Supremo Apostolatus* can be read not only as a moral pronouncement but also as a social artifact that reveals how the papacy sees its role in the world. The brief shows a pope attempting to address a global moral issue—slavery—through the tools at his disposal: historical authority, theological argument, and juridical command. It reveals an institution aware of its past actions and seeking to construct a narrative of continuity, even in the face of public criticism and internal contradictions. It also exposes the tension between universal moral claims and localized interpretations, as different Catholic communities respond in divergent ways.

Anthropologically, the document reflects a worldview in which Christian charity and justice are central ethical categories, and in which the Church sees itself as a guardian of human dignity, especially for those who are “wretched” and vulnerable.[1][4][11] Yet it also reveals a paternalistic posture, viewing enslaved people primarily as objects of protection rather than as full subjects of agency. The brief is concerned with the reputation of Christianity among “infidel peoples,” suggesting a sensitivity to how Christian actions are perceived by outsiders and how this perception affects evangelization.[1][11] It thus intertwines concerns about morality with concerns about missionary success and institutional image.

Investigatively, the document illustrates the limited efficacy of papal pronouncements in changing entrenched social systems without accompanying political and economic reforms. Despite its strong language, *In Supremo Apostolatus* did not by itself abolish slavery in any nation, nor did it prevent Catholics from owning slaves or defending slavery. Its impact was mediated through local interpretations, political calculations, and the broader dynamics of abolitionist movements and state policies. This reality challenges any straightforward narrative that equates moral teaching with social transformation. It invites a more nuanced view of papal authority: powerful in shaping conscience and discourse, but bounded in its ability to effect structural change.

## Conclusion

*In Supremo Apostolatus* stands as a pivotal, if imperfect, papal intervention in the history of slavery and the transatlantic slave trade. Issued by Gregory XVI in 1839, the brief looks backward across several centuries of Christian and papal engagement with slavery, recalling the apostolic toleration of slavery tempered by charity, the gradual erosion of slavery in many Christian nations, and a series of papal documents—from *Sublimis Deus* to Benedict XIV’s Brazilian letters and Pius VII’s diplomatic actions—that sought to protect certain peoples from enslavement and to mitigate its abuses.[1][4][7][11][13] This historical narrative, while selective and omitting complicating bulls like *Romanus Pontifex*, forms the reflective backbone of the document, giving it a reflective saturation of 7 and a temporal density reaching roughly twenty-five years back beyond its immediate moment and referencing events centuries prior.[6][11]

Forward-looking, the brief is intensely prescriptive. Gregory XVI condemns the slave trade and new enslavements as violations of justice, humanity, and Christian brotherhood, and he issues binding commands forbidding Catholics from participating in, facilitating, or defending these practices in any form.[1][11] He extends this prohibition to teaching and publishing, seeking to prevent the emergence of pro-slavery theological or moral arguments within the Church. This strong prescriptive thrust, aimed at reshaping future conduct and discourse, warrants a prospective saturation of 9. Yet the document’s prospective density is more modest, at +5, reflecting a focus on immediate and near-term changes rather than on detailed, long-range prediction or planning. It implicitly supports a future in which Christian nations rid themselves of the shame of slavery but stops short of demanding immediate emancipation or outlining concrete transition paths.[3][11]

Historically, the impact of *In Supremo Apostolatus* was uneven. In Europe and in international diplomatic arenas, it reinforced papal support for suppressing the slave trade and aligned the Church more clearly with abolitionist currents, building on Pius VII’s role at the Congress of Vienna.[7][13] In Catholic slaveholding societies like the United States and Brazil, the brief’s prescriptions were mediated through local interpretations and political calculations. American Catholic leaders such as Bishop John England reinterpreted the bull as condemning only the trade, not domestic slavery, while Rome adopted a cautious approach that avoided direct confrontation with slaveholding bishops and governments.[9][12][14][16] In Brazil, abolition would come only decades later, with Leo XIII commending it in 1888, illustrating the slow and contested nature of change.[3] These outcomes show that the papal attempt to shape the future was constrained by economic structures, political realities, and internal Church dynamics.

The document’s ambiguities and gaps—its silence on immediate emancipation, its selective historical memory, its indirectness in naming slaveholding nations, and its paternalistic perspective on enslaved people—underscore the complexity of papal engagement with slavery. *In Supremo Apostolatus* is both abolitionist and limited, both morally forceful and politically cautious. It reflects a pope and a Church grappling with a vast system of human exploitation, seeking to assert moral authority while navigating the constraints of diplomacy and internal diversity. For the Papal Papers project, these tensions are not flaws to be smoothed over but features to be visually and analytically highlighted, informing the artwork’s color and temporal gradients.

In sum, *In Supremo Apostolatus* is best understood as a high-saturation prescriptive document with substantial reflective depth. It names and interprets the world of slavery and the slave trade as it already exists, draws on a long history of papal concern, and attempts to project a future in which Catholic participation in slavery ceases. Its prescriptions are clear, but their practical efficacy was limited, reminding us that papal texts operate within, not above, the complex social, economic, and political worlds they address. This realism—about both the strengths and limits of papal intervention—should guide both scholarly and artistic representations of Gregory XVI’s brief as a symbol of the Church’s evolving, contested, and often fraught relationship to slavery.
