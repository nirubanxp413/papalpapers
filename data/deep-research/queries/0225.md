# Dum nos

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Pius VI commends the faithful clergy and laity of Avignon and the Comtat Venaissin for resisting the French National Assembly's annexation of papal territories, extends canonical warnings to schismatic clergy, and formally declares null and void all French decrees against his principality.
- **Summary:** Written on 19 April 1792, this letter addresses the Archbishop of Avignon and the bishops, clergy, and people of Avignon and the Comtat Venaissin — territories Pius VI rules as sovereign. He praises those who endured exile, imprisonment, loss of property, and death rather than submit to the French National Assembly's annexation decrees. He condemns the pseudo-vicar Benedetto Francesco Malierio and other clergy who collaborated with the Assembly. He extends to Avignon the canonical warnings (sixty-day notice periods) already issued to France, grants special faculties to the legitimate bishops, and solemnly declares all French annexation decrees null and void, invoking both divine law and over five centuries of papal possession.
- **Response to:** The French National Assembly's decree of 14 September 1791 annexing Avignon and the Comtat Venaissin to France; the subsequent decree of 3 March 1792 dividing those territories into two French departments; the activities of pseudo-vicar Malierio (suspended in a prior letter); the release by the Assembly of criminals responsible for atrocities on 16 October 1791; and ongoing persecution of faithful Catholics including threats against sacred images.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Continued perseverance by the faithful, even to the sacrifice of life ("non chi ha incominciato ma colui che avrà perseverato fino in fondo sarà salvo," Mt 10:32); conversion of those still in rebellion within the sixty-day warning period; distribution of this letter and the attached canonical warning to all chapters, clergy, and people; and hope that the rebels themselves will eventually recognise "l'orrore dei propri crimini" once the passion of fanaticism subsides.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between legitimate sovereign authority — both religious (the Apostolic See as guardian of true religion) and temporal (the Pope as lawful ruler of Avignon for over five centuries) — and the revolutionary French National Assembly's claim to override both divine and human law by annexing papal territory and imposing the Civil Constitution of the Clergy. A secondary tension runs between those the Assembly calls "seditious" (the faithful) and those it praises (the collaborators), which Pius inverts: loyalty to God and to the legitimate sovereign is the true mark of honour.

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument.** The letter moves in three interlocking registers: consolation, exhortation, and formal legal declaration.

Pius opens (§§1–5) by noting that his previous letter of 23 April 1791 produced remarkable results: the vast majority of people in Avignon, and even more so in Carpentras and the Comtat, refused to recognise the schismatic clergy and the annexation. He cites as evidence a report presented to the French Assembly itself on 11 February 1792, which — trying to discredit the resisters by calling them "seditious and seducers" — inadvertently confirmed that the spirit of the population had turned sharply against the Constitution. Pius turns this hostile testimony into a badge of honour, quoting Augustine: "even the Lord Jesus Christ was called a seducer, to console his servants when they too are called seducers."

The middle section (§§6–10) pivots to urgency. New French decrees (3 March 1792) have partitioned the territories into departments, imposed French law wholesale, renewed the military garrison, and freed the criminals of the October massacre. Pius acknowledges that persecution will intensify — including, he states with horror, attacks on sacred images — and responds pastorally: he extends to Avignon the same canonical warning letters already issued to France (fixing sixty-day and then further sixty-day deadlines for schismatic clergy to recant), and grants the legitimate bishops special faculties to continue spiritual governance under these extreme conditions.

The closing section (§§11–14) shifts to the temporal sovereign register. Pius reminds his subjects of their oath of loyalty and of the divine precept to obey legitimate powers (Rom 13:1; Heb 13:17). He catalogues his own prior leniency toward the rebels, frames the ancien régime of the Holy See as a government uniquely "free and exempt from every tax" that provoked envy, and then issues a solemn juridical declaration: the decree of 14 September 1791 was already declared null in his chirograph of 5 November 1791; now he equally declares null and void the 3 March 1792 decree and every past and future French act against his principality. He orders this letter appended to that chirograph for perpetual record.

**Tone.** Predominantly authoritative and juridical, but with marked pastoral warmth toward the faithful sufferers and paternal patience toward the rebels. The pope explicitly describes himself as acting "in quel modo d'agire paterno che abbiamo usato finora." The emotional register rises at moments of persecution (§7: "con immenso dolore del Nostro animo") and drops into formal legal language at the declaration of nullity (§13).

**Notable framing.** The document consistently conflates two kinds of authority: the Pope as head of the universal Church (spiritual sovereign) and as ruler of Avignon/Comtat (temporal sovereign). Resistance to the French Assembly is simultaneously a religious act (fidelity to God and to the Church) and a civic act (loyalty to one's legitimate prince). The Assembly's labels are consistently inverted: those it calls "seditious" are the truly loyal; those it praises are the rebels. The appeal to five centuries of possession ("possesso di oltre cinque secoli") grounds the temporal claim in historical legitimacy rather than purely theological argument. The scriptural citations (Mt 10:32, Rom 13:1, Heb 13:17) anchor both the call to perseverance and the duty of obedience within a clearly religious framework.
