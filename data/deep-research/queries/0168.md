# Ubi Nos

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Pope asserts that the civil rule of the Apostolic See is a divinely ordained necessity for the free exercise of papal authority, and rejects any "guarantees" offered by the Piedmont Government as fraudulent substitutes that leave the Church subordinate to secular power.
- **Summary:** Issued in May 1871, following the military seizure of Rome and the fall of the Papal States, *Ubi Nos* addresses the world's bishops to formally reject the Italian government's "Law of Guarantees" as an illegitimate imposition. Pius IX argues that temporal sovereignty is not a privilege of convenience but a God-given requirement: without independent civil rule, the Roman Pontiff would be subject to a secular ruler who could be a heretic or an enemy of the Church. He calls on bishops and the faithful to intensify prayers, and urges secular rulers to unite in restoring the rights of the Holy See, framing the entire situation as a spiritual and moral crisis for the universal Church.
- **Response to:** The military seizure of Rome and the overthrow of the papal civil government ("the ill-starred day of the City's capture"); the Piedmont Government's enactment of so-called "guarantees" to compensate for stripping the Pope of temporal power; blasphemies and errors introduced into public debate that the document says are "at variance with the Catholic faith and with the very foundations of natural law."
- **Prescriptive towards:** Continued and intensified prayer by the faithful worldwide; refusal by the Pope to accept any guarantee or agreement that diminishes his rights; an appeal for secular rulers to unite, suppress rebellion, and work to restore the Holy See's rights; a call to "fight for justice with your life, and struggle for justice even to death."
- **Tension:** The central tension is between divinely grounded papal authority (spiritual and juridical) and the secular state's claim to legislate the conditions under which the Pope may exercise that authority. A secondary tension runs between the faithful's demonstrated unity and piety on one side, and the advancing "wickedness" of impious forces on the other — framed as an eschatological struggle ("the hour of wickedness and the power of darkness") that the document nonetheless treats as one the Church will survive.

## Unstructured

**Core argument and flow**

The letter opens by recalling the Pope's earlier communication of November 1869 and noting that conditions have only worsened since then. This backward glance serves to establish continuity and urgency: the letter is not a new alarm but a deepening of one already sounded. From this bleak opening, Pius IX pivots — somewhat unexpectedly — to consolation: the worldwide response of the faithful in prayer, pilgrimage, and sacramental participation is described in warmly affective terms ("wonderful enthusiasm," "never until now, since the first days of the Church, did the multitude of the faithful so clearly show that they are of one heart and one soul"). This movement from lament to consolation, and then back to polemical confrontation, gives the letter a rhythmic structure.

The polemical core occupies sections 4–11. The "guarantees" offered by the Piedmont Government are subjected to sustained mockery and theological refutation. The document's word for them — "empty immunities," "cunning and mockery," "vain attempt" — is consistently dismissive. The deeper argument is theological: God gave the papacy civil rule as a providential instrument; to accept a secular substitute is to allow one's God-given rights to be granted by human rulers, which would subordinate their source to their grantor. The Pope states this with notable force: "We would not be valuing the divine blood of Our Redeemer if We borrowed Our rights from the rulers of the earth."

**Use of authorities**

The text is heavily scaffolded with patristic quotation — St. Bernard appears at least four times, St. Anselm twice. The citations are not merely decorative; they are chosen to make specific argumentative moves. St. Anselm's address to rulers ("Christian rulers are sons, not lords, of the Church") is deployed to reframe the political situation in terms of the proper ordering of sacred and secular authority. St. Bernard's repeated appearances lend an air of ancient consensus to what was in fact a novel political crisis.

**Tone**

The tone is authoritative throughout — this is a papal declaration, not a pastoral guideline — but it modulates between prophetic denunciation (of the Piedmont Government and "impious minds"), paternal consolation (of the bishops and faithful), and almost eschatological urgency in the final sections. The closing quote from St. Bernard ("Now indeed is the hour of wickedness and the power of darkness. But it is the final hour and the power quickly passes away") introduces an apocalyptic register that frames the political situation as a moment of divine testing rather than merely a political setback.

**Notable framing**

The document consistently frames what could be described as a geopolitical defeat (the loss of Rome) as a spiritual opportunity and test. The Pope never concedes political weakness as such; instead, the faithful's response is presented as evidence that "malice abounds, [but] the grace of faith, charity, and confession abound too." The crisis is real, but it is also, in this framing, a demonstration of the Church's vitality. Similarly, the Pope's refusal of the guarantees is framed not as intransigence but as fidelity to a sworn trust — "We would be violating Our sworn trust" — giving the rejection a moral rather than merely political character.
