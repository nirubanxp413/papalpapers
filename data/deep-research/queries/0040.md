## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Summa providit altitudo consilii

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Social
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document addresses a structural gap in the University of Salamanca's authority to formally confer academic degrees and magisterial honors, resolving it by delegating that power to the cathedral scholasticus.
- **Summary:** Pope John XXII, responding to a petition from the rectors, university, and council of Salamanca (and supported by King Alfonso of Castile), issues this bull to remedy an institutional deficiency: while a prior privilege from Alexander IV had allowed Salamanca graduates to teach in other general studia without re-examination, no specific person had ever been empowered to actually grant the degree insignia and licentia regendi. The document resolves this by formally vesting that conferring authority in the scholasticus of the Church of Salamanca, current and future, for all faculties in which candidates are examined and found fit.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to the petition of the rectors, university, and council of Salamanca, and to King Alfonso of Castile's repeated humble supplications. It also responds to the existing gap left by Alexander IV's privilege, which granted recognition of Salamanca degrees elsewhere but omitted assigning any person the authority to actually confer those degrees — an omission the document states "derogates in many ways from the honour of the said studium."
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document is directed toward the future flourishing and growth of the University of Salamanca ("nunc et successivis temporibus"), establishing a permanent mechanism for degree conferral. It anticipates an ongoing institutional order: the current and future scholasticus is empowered to grant degrees according to the customs, solemnities, and ordinances observed in other general studia.
- **Tension:** The core tension is institutional and jurisdictional: the University of Salamanca had been granted external recognition for its degrees (parity with other general studia, excepting Paris and Bologna) but lacked the internal authority to formally bestow those degrees. The document also names a latent tension between the university's honour and the incomplete prior privilege — the unresolved status "derogated from the honour" of the studium. There is also an implicit tension between secular (royal) and ecclesiastical authority, resolved here by anchoring degree-conferral in the cathedral's scholasticus rather than a civil or purely academic officer.

## Unstructured

The bull opens with a brief theological preamble — "Summa providit altitudo consilii" — affirming that divine wisdom has arranged for humans to have knowledge of divine and human things, without which life cannot be rightly ordered. The "pearl of wisdom and knowledge" is found through devoted study. This framing is brief and conventional for the genre, serving primarily as legitimating preamble rather than as a sustained theological argument.

The body of the document is almost entirely procedural and juridical. It recites the history: Alexander IV had granted Salamanca graduates the right to teach in any general studium (except Paris and Bologna) without re-examination. However, that privilege had not designated anyone to actually confer the magisterial or doctoral insignia — the formal ceremony of degree-granting. The rectors, university, and council of Salamanca identified this gap and petitioned the pope to remedy it.

John XXII's solution is precise and jurisdictionally grounded: the scholasticus of the Cathedral of Salamanca (who had long held jurisdiction over the university's scholars) is empowered, personally or through delegates, to grant the licentia regendi ubique (licence to teach everywhere) and associated honours to those examined and approved in any faculty at Salamanca. The formula follows the modes, solemnities, and customs of other general studia.

The tone is administrative and authoritative rather than pastoral or exhortatory. The document closes with a standard anathema clause threatening divine displeasure and the wrath of Saints Peter and Paul against anyone who contravenes it — a formulaic but firm assertion of papal authority over the matter.

The document is notably brief and single-purpose. Its primary significance lies not in theological content but in institutional architecture: it closes a loophole in Salamanca's academic constitution, anchoring degree-conferral in the cathedral's scholasticus and thereby reinforcing the connection between the university and ecclesiastical governance. The mention of the king's intercession is notable — royal authority is acknowledged but channelled through and subordinated to the papal grant, with the actual conferral power placed in a church officer rather than a royal or civic one.
