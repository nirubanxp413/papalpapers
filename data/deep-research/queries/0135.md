# Sicut Dudum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document condemns the enslavement and forcible capture of the Canary Island natives — particularly those already baptized or seeking Baptism — as a grave sin against God, the Christian faith, and the persons themselves, and commands their immediate restoration to liberty under pain of excommunication.
- **Summary:** Eugene IV issues a direct papal command in response to reported abuses: Christian men arrived by ship at the Canary Islands and, using "fictitious reasoning," seized inhabitants of both sexes, carried them overseas, deprived them of their property, and subjected them to perpetual slavery. Some victims were already baptized; others had been deceived by false promises of Baptism. The pope calls on all Christian princes, lords, soldiers, and faithful to cease these acts, and mandates that within fifteen days all enslaved Canary Islanders be freed, fully and permanently, without payment. Failure to comply results in automatic excommunication, from which absolution is withheld until freedom and goods are restored.
- **Response to:** Reports from the bishop of Rubicon (Ferdinand) and other "trustworthy informers" concerning Christians seizing Canary Island natives by armed force, enslaving them, stealing their property, and deceiving some with false promises of Baptism; also responding to the consequent spiritual damage — namely that the remaining islanders, horrified by the slavery, were withdrawing from their intention to receive Baptism, thereby "offending the majesty of God" and endangering their souls.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The immediate liberation of all enslaved Canary Islanders (within fifteen days); the cessation of any future capture, sale, or enslavement of baptized residents or those freely seeking Baptism; the obedience of all Christian faithful — from princes and barons down to soldiers and communities — to this papal command; and the spiritual reward (eternal happiness, placement "at the right hand of God") for those who comply.
- **Tension:** A sharp tension between the obligations of Christian faith — which, in the document's framing, should protect and welcome converts — and the actual conduct of "some Christians" who exploit the islanders' "simplicity" and the absence of adequate governors to enslave and rob them. A secondary tension is between the integrity of missionary work (bringing the islanders into the Faith) and the actions of these same Christians, which actively caused the remaining islanders to recoil from Baptism, turning a missionary success into a scandal.

## Unstructured

**Key themes and argument flow**

The document opens with a narrative of cause and effect: the Canary Islanders had "recently" (the Latin formula "not long ago") embraced the Catholic Faith through God's mercy, following the natural law. This is cast as a positive development. The pope then introduces the crisis: the absence of "suitable governors and defenders" created a vacuum that unscrupulous Christians exploited. The language is deliberately sharp — "fictitious reasoning," "armed forces," "perpetual slavery," "various illicit and evil deeds" — making clear this is not a gray area but a categorical wrong.

The document names a precise spiritual consequence of these physical crimes: the remaining islanders are drawing back from Baptism. This is the hinge of the argument. The enslaving of converts and the deception of would-be converts does not merely injure the victims materially; it "offends the majesty of God," endangers souls, and causes "no little harm to the Christian religion." The missionary and the moral injury are thus fused.

**Tone**

The tone is unambiguously authoritative rather than pastoral or advisory. Eugene speaks from his office explicitly: "to whom it pertains … to rebuke each sinner about his sin." The document uses the vocabulary of command throughout — "We order and command," "they incur the sentence of excommunication by the act itself." The fifteen-day deadline and the automatic, non-dispensable nature of the excommunication (not removable even by the Holy See or local bishops until restitution is made) indicate that this is framed as a binding juridical act, not a moral appeal.

There is, however, a brief pastoral register in paragraph 5: those who obey "deserve … the blessings which follow" and are promised "eternal happiness." The carrot follows the very large stick.

**Notable framing**

- The islanders are described as "imitating the natural law alone" before conversion — a framing that grants them moral standing within the Catholic anthropology of the time, as persons capable of responding to divine grace.
- The category of persons protected is precisely defined: (1) those already baptized, (2) those "freely seeking Baptism." This scoping is significant — the document's protection is tied to the relationship to the Church.
- The enslaving Christians are not named collectively as a nation or political entity but as a moral category ("some Christians … we speak of this with sorrow"), framing the abuse as a betrayal of Christian identity.
- The exhortation in paragraph 3 addresses a wide social hierarchy — "temporal princes, lords, captains, armed men, barons, soldiers, nobles, communities" — suggesting the problem was not isolated to a few bad actors but potentially systemic enough to require papal intervention across all levels of Christian society.
- The document closes with a date and place (Florence, January 13, 1435), grounding it as a specific juridical act rather than a general teaching letter.
