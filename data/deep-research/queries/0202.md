# Auspicato Concessum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The centenary of St. Francis of Assisi provides an occasion to revive Franciscan spirituality — especially the Third Order — as a remedy for the moral, social, and anti-religious disorders of Leo XIII's own time.
- **Summary:** Leo XIII uses the seventh centenary of St. Francis of Assisi's birth to call the Catholic world to genuine devotion beyond mere commemoration: imitating Francis's virtues is presented as the most efficacious remedy for contemporary evils. The encyclical traces Francis's historical context (12th-century moral collapse, factionalism, materialism) and argues that the present age suffers from analogous ills — cold charity, materialism, socialism, irreligion, and class conflict. The Third Order of St. Francis is proposed as an accessible institution for all people that can restore Christian morality, social peace, and right relations between rich and poor without requiring rupture of family or civic life.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to: (1) the suppression of religious orders in various states ("unbefitting acts of violence" against Religious Orders); (2) the spread of Materialism and those who "obstinately deny that submission to the Church is due"; (3) advocates of socialism, agrarian revolt, and sedition who "flatter the desires of the proletariat" and "weaken the foundations of domestic and public order"; (4) the cooling of Christian charity and spread of luxury and self-seeking in contemporary society; (5) the damaged condition of the First and Second Franciscan Orders who have been "struck recently with a heavy blow."
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates and directs toward: (1) widespread enrollment in the Third Order of St. Francis by men and women of all ranks; (2) bishops and clergy actively teaching the faithful about the Third Order's rules, privileges, and practical advantages; (3) Italians in particular renewing devotion to Francis; (4) a restoration of social peace — quenching class hatred, dissolving causes of revolution — through Franciscan fraternal charity; (5) resolution of "the question that politicians so laboriously aim at solving" (relations between rich and poor) through the Franciscan principles that poverty has dignity and wealth carries obligation.
- **Tension:** The text names or clearly implies several tensions: (a) **Spiritual vs. worldly attachment** — between Christian detachment from earthly goods and the contemporary "greedily following after pleasures" and covetousness; (b) **Fraternity proclaimed vs. fraternity lived** — the document notes that contemporaries "extol the name of human fraternity" yet "speak more fraternally than they act," driven by self-love rather than genuine charity toward the poor; (c) **Church authority vs. materialist/socialist resistance** — those who deny submission to the Church are depicted as escalating to attacks on civil power and promoting violence and sedition; (d) **Rich and poor** — class conflict is named as a central social wound, with the Franciscan vision of merciful rich and contented poor offered as the resolution; (e) **Honored commemoration vs. hollow observance** — the Pope warns that festive honors to Francis will be "not enough" and even futile unless they translate into genuine imitation of his virtues.

## Unstructured

*Auspicato Concessum* is structured as a pastoral-exhortatory letter built around a historical parallel: the 12th-century crisis that Francis addressed mirrors the 19th-century crisis Leo XIII perceives. The argument moves in three broad phases.

**Phase 1 — The occasion (§§1–5):** The letter opens by linking the Benedictine and Franciscan centenaries as providential moments of memory. Leo frames the celebration instrumentally from the outset: honoring Francis is praiseworthy, but "not enough" — the real purpose is to draw "profit" from the saint's example. This immediately signals that the encyclical is not primarily hagiographic but diagnostic and prescriptive.

**Phase 2 — Francis as model and historical agent (§§6–21):** Leo provides a sustained account of Francis's life and significance. The rhetorical strategy is to portray Francis as the divinely appointed physician for a diseased age, deploying language of providential necessity ("God raises up a man… to whom He assigns the salvation of all"). The description of 12th-century ills — licentiousness, coveting of honors and wealth, extinction of charity, factionalism, city-states at war — reads as a thinly veiled description of Leo's own time, making the parallel explicit by §22. Key virtues highlighted: poverty voluntarily embraced, the "folly of the Cross," charity extended especially to the despised and repulsive, likeness to Christ (culminating in the stigmata). The Third Order receives particular attention (§§20–21) as the institutional vehicle that brought Franciscan reform into lay civic life, with notable historical examples (King Louis IX, St. Elizabeth of Hungary, popes and princes) underscoring its respectability across all social strata.

**Phase 3 — Application to the present and exhortation (§§22–27):** Here the parallel is made explicit: "the tone and temper of our times seem for many reasons to be similar to those" of the 12th century. The specific contemporary threats named are materialism, denial of Church authority, socialism, agrarian agitation, and luxury/covetousness. The Third Order is presented as the remedy institution. The tone shifts to direct pastoral command: Leo exhorts bishops to teach the Third Order, promote enrollment, and increase zeal — with added urgency because the First and Second Orders have recently been "struck with a heavy blow" (a reference to suppression of religious orders in Italy and elsewhere).

**Tone:** The encyclical blends authoritative papal teaching with genuine pastoral warmth (Leo notes his personal devotion to Francis and his own climbs to Alvernia). The argument is rational-historical in structure, moving from premise (Francis succeeded in a parallel crisis) through evidence (narrative of his life and the Third Order's fruits) to prescription (enroll, imitate, teach). The tone toward opponents is firm but not polemical — socialists and materialists are named as dangers, not addressed directly; the document speaks to the Catholic faithful, not to adversaries.

**Notable framing:** The encyclical frames the class question in explicitly spiritual-moral rather than structural-economic terms: the solution to the tension between rich and poor is interior conversion (the rich to mercy, the poor to contentment), mediated by Franciscan fraternity. This is consistent with Leo's broader approach, anticipating the more developed social teaching of *Rerum Novarum* (1891) while remaining anchored in a devotional-imitation framework rather than a rights-based one.
