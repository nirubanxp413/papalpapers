# Iniquis Afflictisque

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document bears witness to the systematic persecution of the Catholic Church in Mexico by the civil government, while praising the heroic resistance of bishops, clergy, religious, and laity, and calling the wider Catholic world to prayer and solidarity.
- **Summary:** Pius XI addresses the global episcopate to document the severe anti-Catholic legislation and violence carried out by the Mexican government following the 1917 Constitution and the 1926 presidential decree. The encyclical catalogs specific legal measures stripping the Church of property, civil status, and freedom of worship, records the expulsion of apostolic delegates and arrests of bishops and priests, and celebrates the fortitude of clergy and lay organizations who have maintained passive resistance. The pope frames this suffering within the broader narrative of Church history — persecution as a sign that the Church endures — and closes with an appeal to Our Lady of Guadalupe.
- **Response to:** The encyclical explicitly responds to the Mexican government's 1917 "Political Constitution," which separated Church and State and stripped the Church of civil rights; the presidential decree of 2 July 1926 which nearly eliminated the Church's ability to function; the expulsion of three Apostolic Delegates; the closure of seminaries, schools, and convents; the arrest and killing of priests and laypeople; and the suppression of Catholic public expression in the press and public assembly.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs the faithful to continue and intensify prayers for divine intervention; it urges bishops worldwide to keep the situation before their flocks; it implicitly directs Mexican Catholics to maintain "passive resistance" — refusing to comply with unjust laws while not abandoning the Church's unity or the Apostolic See. It also anticipates that the day of relief will eventually come through divine providence, not human political calculation.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between legitimate civil authority and the divine constitution of the Church as a "perfect society" with rights that the state cannot abrogate. A secondary tension runs between passive endurance (patience, prayer, legal petitions) and the impossibility of acquiescing to laws the hierarchy deems fundamentally illegitimate. A third tension appears in the Church's historical relationship with Mexico: the text asserts that all of Mexico's "progress, civilization, the good and the beautiful" was built by the Church, while the contemporary government treats that same Church as a criminal institution.

## Unstructured

**Central argument and flow:** The encyclical moves through four broad phases. First (§§1–16), Pius XI narrates the persecution in precise detail — the 1917 Constitution, the 1926 decree, expulsions, arrests, killings, and the silencing of Catholic voices — building a record of deliberate, escalating oppression. He frames the persecutors as "deserters from the army of Jesus Christ" acting with "insane hatred," using legal forms to cloak lawless acts. Second (§§17–27), the tone shifts to praise: the bishops' joint pastoral letters, their suspension of public worship, their doomed but principled petition to Congress, and above all the constancy of priests (only "one or two" of four thousand defected) and lay organizations (Knights of Columbus, Catholic Society of Mexican Youth, Union of Catholic Women, Federation for the Defense of Religious Liberty). Third (§§28–30), Pius situates Mexico within the universal history of Church persecution — invoking the bark of Peter, the gates of hell, and St. Hilary's paradox that the Church "conquers at the very moment when she is abandoned by all." He turns this into a counter-indictment: persecutors who would study history would see the Church as the source of Mexico's own civilization. Fourth (§§31–32), the letter closes with an appeal to Our Lady of Guadalupe as heavenly patroness and a paternal blessing.

**Tone:** The document is predominantly authoritative and juridical in its central sections (cataloguing legal violations, citing specific dates and decrees), but shifts to pastoral warmth when praising the faithful and to prophetic confidence when invoking scripture. The papal voice is never detached: Pius writes of being moved to tears ("We can scarcely keep back Our tears," §27) and of profound grief at the "perversion of civil authority" (§11). This combination — precise legal grievance plus emotional solidarity plus theological confidence — gives the encyclical its rhetorical force.

**Notable framing choices:**
- The persecutors are consistently described as acting from "hatred of religion" or "insane hatred," not merely political ideology. This rules out any sympathetic political reading and frames the conflict as fundamentally spiritual.
- The document uses martyrological language deliberately: the beatification of French Revolution martyrs is cited as a prompt for thinking about Mexico (§4), and young lay members dying with "the rosary in their hands and the name of Christ King on their lips" (§27) echo hagiographic conventions.
- "Catholic Action" is mentioned as a target of hostile newspapers (§12), and the named lay organizations (§§25–27) are described in terms of Catholic Social Action, suggesting the encyclical partly serves to publicly validate and encourage these structures under duress.
- The appeal to Our Lady of Guadalupe in the closing paragraph is theologically and culturally specific: she is addressed not merely as a Marian devotion but as the "heavenly patroness of the Mexican people," implicitly reclaiming Mexican national identity for Catholicism against the secular-nationalist government.
- The rhetorical question "With what justice can We apply to the authors of these enormities the words which Jesus Christ spoke to the leaders of the Jews: 'This is your hour, and the power of darkness'" (§11) is the encyclical's sharpest polemical moment, directly equating the persecutors with those responsible for the Passion.
