## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Adeo nota

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document simultaneously defends papal temporal sovereignty over Avignon and the Comtat Venaissin and condemns the ecclesiastical subversion wrought by the forced civic oath and coerced appointment of an illegitimate vicar capitular, while appealing for the rebels' return to obedience.
- **Summary:** Pius VI addresses the clergy and people of Avignon and the Comtat Venaissin — territories under direct papal rule — following a rebellion in which both populations expelled his legates, submitted to French sovereignty, adopted the Civil Constitution of the Clergy, and forcibly extracted a fraudulent canonical election at gunpoint. The document catalogues these acts in detail, declares them null and sacrilegious, imposes canonical penalties on the pseudo-vicar Malierio and his accomplices, and instructs the faithful to disregard illegitimate appointments and obey their legitimate pastors. It closes with a dual assertion of apostolic authority — spiritual over the clergy, princely over the civil order — while expressing ongoing paternal clemency and hope for reconciliation.
- **Response to:** The armed rebellion of the people of Avignon and Comtat Venaissin against papal sovereignty; the expulsion of the papal pro-legate and other pontifical ministers; the adoption of the French Revolutionary Civil Constitution of the Clergy in the Pope's own territories; the civic oath imposed on clergy; and specifically the forced "election" of Benedetto Malierio as vicar capitular by municipal officer Duprazio with sword in hand and soldiers present.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Clergy and faithful are directed to refuse communion with Malierio and any other intruder; to maintain obedience to the Archbishop of Avignon and their legitimate pastors even in exile; to resist the spread of the Civil Constitution of the Clergy; rebels are exhorted to abandon sedition and return to obedience; the Pope anticipates potentially escalating canonical and civil remedies ("rimedi più forti") if the rebellion persists.
- **Tension:** (1) Papal temporal sovereignty vs. popular/revolutionary sovereignty — the document insists no transfer of civil allegiance can occur without the Pope's "suprema autorizzazione," while the people assert the right to choose French rule; (2) Legitimate ecclesiastical hierarchy vs. state-coerced church appointments — the election of Malierio is described as a "military act, not a synodal one," invoking the patristic precedents of Athanasius and George of Alexandria; (3) Paternal clemency vs. canonical justice — the Pope repeatedly offers forgiveness to rebels while also declaring nullities and imposing suspensions; (4) Obedience to God's law vs. the civic oath — clergy are caught between civil compulsion and canonical prohibition.

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument:** The document moves in three broad movements. The first (§§1–15) is a detailed historical narrative — almost a juridical brief — recounting the sequence of rebellion: Avignon leads, Carpentras follows under duress, legates are expelled, the Civil Constitution is adopted, and the Malierio episode is described with granular procedural detail (names, dates, vote counts, the sword in the hand of officer Duprazio). This section is notable for its tone of controlled indignation; the Pope names individuals, cites specific acts and their dates, and builds a record. The second movement (§§16–29) shifts to pastoral address: the Pope affirms that he knows many among them remain faithful, expresses grief rather than vengeance, offers forgiveness unconditionally, and exhorts good clergy to persevere and rebels to return. Patristic citations (Eleazar, Athanasius, Pope Julius) are deployed to frame Malierio's capitulation as a failure of moral heroism and to place the forced election within a deep tradition of condemned usurpations. The third movement (§§30–34) reasserts temporal sovereignty in explicit terms, annulling all acts taken against his principate, distinguishing the Avignon/Comtat situation from France proper (where he "did not wish to speak" about civil matters), and closing with exhortation to return to obedience and a final apostolic blessing.

**Tone:** The document is simultaneously authoritative and pastoral — perhaps more forensic than either. The Pope adopts the register of a sovereign issuing formal declarations of nullity (declarations appear in §§18–21, 31–33 using language of "annulliamo," "condanniamo," "reproviamo"), while also addressing his subjects as a father ("paternamente," "diletti figli," "in seno"). The section on Malierio (§§10–11) is the most rhetorically charged: the comparison to Eleazar, the old man who chose glorious death over compromise, makes Malierio's acceptance of the vicar's role a form of moral cowardice framed in biblical terms.

**Notable framing:** The document insists throughout that the rebellion was motivated not by genuine grievance — the Pope notes the populations paid almost no taxes and were admired by neighbouring nations for their mild governance — but by "il desiderio di una sfrenata libertà" (the desire for unbridled freedom), which is identified as the true cause. This framing delegitimises the revolution as a phenomenon of appetite rather than justice. The "rights of man" articles adopted by the Comtat assembly are described as "contrari alla Religione e alla società" — opposed to both religion and society — a pointed double condemnation. The schism risk is made concrete: if an invalid archbishop or invalid pastors are installed, the faithful must still follow the exiled legitimate ones, even across physical absence, citing the Sorbonne declaration of 25 February.

**Key textual moments:** The forced election scene (§§7–9) reads almost like a court deposition — specific names, vote tallies, the barring of notaries from recording protests — suggesting the document was designed to serve as a canonical record as well as a pastoral letter. The citation of Pope Julius I against the Arian intrusion of George in Alexandria (§20) is a deliberate elevation of the Avignon situation into the long history of state-coerced church appointments, framing the French Revolutionary intervention as a recurrence of a heretical pattern the Church has always condemned.
