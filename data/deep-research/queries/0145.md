# Exivi de Paradiso

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** A papal declaration clarifying the binding force and practical limits of the Franciscan Rule, with especial focus on the absolute poverty required of the Friars Minor regarding property, money, buildings, and governance.
- **Summary:** "Exivi de Paradiso" (1312, Pope Clement V, as transcribed in the source file under the collection entry for Paul VI) is a formal declaratory constitution addressed to the Order of Friars Minor (Franciscans). It responds to persistent doubts among the friars about which provisions of their Rule carry the weight of precept and which are merely counsel. Working through a series of concrete cases — the scope of the three vows, handling of money and inheritances, fasting obligations, clothing, the size of church buildings, storage of food, and the election of Provincial Ministers — the document defines what counts as mandatory observance and condemns specific abuses that had crept into the Order. The tone is juridical and pastoral simultaneously: the pope expresses deep personal devotion to the Order while imposing strict obligations on its members.
- **Response to:** The document itself says it responds to (a) doubts that had circulated within the Order for a long time regarding the meaning of the Rule, (b) petitions submitted by the friars themselves ("humiliter supplicatum") to the Apostolic See for clarity, (c) previous but incomplete declarations by prior popes (especially Nicholas III), and (d) specific reported abuses — receiving inheritances and annuities, maintaining large orchards and vineyards for sale, stockpiling grain and wine, erecting excessively grand churches and furnishings, involvement in legal proceedings, and handling money through proxies. The friars' own leaders had asserted that many of these abuses were rare and were already penalised, but the pope judged that a formal declaration was still needed.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document prescribes stricter and clearer future observance of Franciscan poverty in all material respects; it forbids receiving inheritances (since "dominium" passes to heirs), prohibits annual revenues and possessions outright, bars friars from attending lawsuits for temporal gain, limits gardens and vineyards to personal sustenance only, requires that church buildings and furnishings remain modest and proportionate to a community of poor men, restricts money-handling to the narrow cases already permitted by Nicholas III, and establishes a formal procedure for electing and confirming Provincial Ministers so that governance disputes will not arise.
- **Tension:** Several tensions are named or clearly implied: (1) Between the literal text of the Rule (which some read as binding friars to all evangelical counsels) and a more restricted reading (binding them only to the three vows and explicitly stated obligations) — the document adjudicates this as the restricted reading. (2) Between the ideal of absolute poverty ("totaliter alieni a denariis") and the practical needs of community life (illness, clothing, food supply), which the document resolves by specifying only the narrow exceptions already granted. (3) Between the inward spiritual disposition of poverty and the outward visible signs — the pope repeatedly insists that external behaviour must mirror the interior vow, because outsiders judge by what they see and scandal is a real harm. (4) Whether "poor use" (usus pauper) of things is an integral part of the vow of poverty itself, a disputed theological question the document declines to resolve dogmatically, only saying it is not heretical to hold either side — but obligates friars to the specific poor uses named in the Rule.

## Unstructured

**Document identity and context within the text**

The source file's frontmatter attributes the document to "Paul VI" with a publication date of 2017-07-13, but the body of the text identifies itself unmistakably as a 1312 declaration by "Clemens Papa" (Pope Clement V), transcribed from the *Annales Minorum* of Luke Wadding. The opening metaphor ("Exivi de Paradiso") is drawn from Sirach 24:43 (Vulgate), where Wisdom says "I went out of Paradise" to water the garden — here reapplied to Christ who "came out" to redeem humanity, and the Franciscan Order is cast as the enclosed garden he waters with grace and doctrine.

**Flow of argument**

The document proceeds in a methodical, scholastic manner through a numbered series of "doubts" (*dubia*):

1. §§1–3: Preamble — the garden metaphor, history of prior declarations, the pope's personal devotion to the Order.
2. §4: Are friars bound to *all* evangelical counsels, or only to the three vows and what modifies them? Answer: only the three vows and the rule's explicit obligations; St Francis's intent can be inferred from the fact that he included *some* counsels in the Rule while omitting others.
3. §5: Which provisions stated in imperative mood carry the force of precept? A list is given (number of tunics, footwear, fasting calendar, Divine Office, care of the sick, preaching approval, etc.) — all are declared obligatory.
4. §6: May friars accept donations from those entering the Order? Carefully restricted: friars must not solicit or advise about distributions of goods from novices; if an entrant freely gives alms, the friars may receive them modestly but must avoid any appearance of a financial motive.
5. §§7–8: Clothing and fasting clarified. "Vileness" of clothing is relative to local custom; the minimum is set by local superiors. Fasting beyond the rule's stated seasons follows common Church law.
6. §9: Detailed rules on money. Friars may not handle coins in any form beyond the narrow cases permitted by Nicholas III. All forms of money collection (chest/trunk in church, seeking funds for non-permitted purposes) are "simpliciter interdicta."
7. §10: Property, inheritances, and buildings. Friars cannot receive inheritances (since inheritance transfers ownership). Annuities and possessions are equally forbidden. They may not assist in legal proceedings for temporal advantage. Excessive or sumptuous church buildings are condemned; furnishings must be decent but not exceeding what a cathedral might have.
8. §11: Gardens and vineyards. Modest gardens for recreation and personal vegetables are lawful; cultivating them for sale is not. Storing grain or wine beyond immediate need is suspect but may be permitted by superiors in genuine necessity.
9. §12–13: The "usus pauper" question. Friars are bound to the specific poor uses named in the Rule, in the manner the Rule names them. The pope explicitly refuses to declare it heretical to hold either side of the broader theological debate — calling both positions "praesumptuosum et temerarium" if raised as dogmatic claims.
10. §§14–17: Governance — election and confirmation of Provincial Ministers by Provincial Chapter, with the General Minister confirming or annulling; provisions for overseas provinces; destitution of ministers follows existing custom.

**Tone**

The document combines three registers: (a) devotional/pastoral — the opening garden allegory and the pope's protestation of his "pia devotione" towards the friars from youth; (b) juridical/legislative — numbered articles, declaratory formulae, lists of prohibited acts; (c) conscientious — the text repeatedly appeals to "conscience" (*conscientia*), noting that scrupulous friars are troubled even where there is no objective fault, and that the "safer path" (*pars securior*) must be taken in matters of soul.

**Notable framing**

- The metaphor of the enclosed garden (*hortus*) frames the Franciscan Order as a sheltered space set apart from "the stormy waves of the world" — a place of contemplation sustained by divine grace. This positions the Rule not as a legal burden but as a gift and a form of heavenly life.
- The pope appeals repeatedly to what "appears to outsiders" as a legitimating criterion: actions that give the *appearance* of greed or ownership are forbidden even if technically lawful, because the scandal to neighbours (*scandalum proximorum*) is itself a harm the Rule obliges them to avoid.
- The distinction between *dominium* (ownership) and *usus* (use) underlies the entire section on property: the Holy See holds ownership of everything granted to the friars; the friars hold only bare factual use. This legal fiction is treated as a spiritual reality.
- The final condemnatory clause ("indignationem Omnipotentis Dei … incursurum") is standard canonical formula but reinforces the authoritative, binding character of the declaration.
