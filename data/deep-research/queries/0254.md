# Graves Ac Diuturnae

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Pius IX calls the Swiss bishops, clergy, and faithful to resist the deceptive schism of the Old Catholics — who deny Vatican I and benefit from civil favour — while also condemning civil laws that undermine Church authority over marriage.
- **Summary:** Written in 1875, this letter addresses the Swiss Church's crisis caused by the "Old Catholics," a schismatic group that rejected the dogmatic definitions of Vatican I while falsely claiming to be the authentic Catholic tradition. Pius IX reconfirms his 1873 declaration that adherents of this sect are schismatics, urges the faithful to shun their churches, writings, and clergy entirely, and exhorts pastors to fortify the faithful. He also protests civil laws in Switzerland that violate canonical prescriptions on Christian marriage, framing the whole situation as a coordinated assault — by heretics empowered by civil authority — on both the spiritual unity of the Church and the sanctity of sacramental life.
- **Response to:** The ongoing "plots and efforts" of the Old Catholics in Switzerland, particularly their occupation of parishes and churches with "apostate priests," their appointment to teach theology at the University of Bern, and the promulgation of civil laws opposed to canonical marriage prescriptions; also continues from Pius IX's earlier letter of 21 November 1873 formally condemning the sect.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The Swiss faithful are directed to completely shun Old Catholic religious celebrations, buildings, writings, and clergy; to remain obedient to their legitimate bishops who hold a "legitimate mission from the Apostolic See"; and to conform to canonical laws on marriage. Bishops and clergy are directed to instruct their flocks and strengthen the wavering.
- **Tension:** (1) Church authority vs. civil authority: the document explicitly states that schismatics "enjoy the favor of the civil authority" while "the religious freedom of Catholics remains publicly oppressed by schismatic laws" — a direct clash between state power and ecclesiastical jurisdiction. (2) True vs. false Catholic identity: the Old Catholics claim to uphold "the purity of Catholic doctrine" and call themselves "the only true Catholics" while, as the document argues, rejecting the pope's supreme magisterium and the Vatican Council definitions. (3) Visible unity vs. the allure of deception: the document repeatedly warns that the schismatics use "lies and deception" and appear in "sheep's clothing," making discernment difficult for ordinary faithful.

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument:** The letter opens by naming the enemy — the Old Catholics — and framing them in prophetic language ("sons of darkness") to frame the stakes as spiritual, not merely organisational. It then moves to their specific tactics: occupying parishes, infiltrating universities, and claiming a false Catholic identity. Pius IX references his prior condemnation (1873) and renews it, before issuing direct pastoral instructions: shun them entirely (their buildings, clergy, writings). A separate section then pivots to civil marriage laws, broadening the crisis beyond schism to sacramental jurisdiction. The final sections address the clergy and then the lay faithful directly, in paternal tones, urging perseverance, obedience to legitimate pastors, and prayer. The letter closes with an intercession for the "wretched people who have gone astray" — a note of pastoral mercy amid the stern condemnations.

**Tone:** Predominantly authoritative and declarative — this is a reaffirmation and expansion of a prior juridical condemnation — but it shifts to an explicitly pastoral register in sections 6–8 when addressing clerics and then the lay faithful. The phrase "We Speak to you with paternal affection" marks the deliberate tonal shift. The closing prayer for those who have "gone astray" adds a note of genuine solicitude.

**Notable framing:** The document consistently frames the Old Catholics not as honest dissenters but as deliberate deceivers: "sons of darkness," users of "deception and hypocrisy," "strangers and thieves." This rhetorical strategy serves to justify the instruction to completely shun all contact rather than engage in dialogue. The document also frames the Swiss faithful's predicament as a test of endurance ("patience, constancy, and priestly fortitude") that will be rewarded with "a crown of unfading glory in heaven," anchoring the social-political struggle in an eschatological register.

**Key quotations:**
- "these sons of darkness … love to deceive the unwary and the innocent and to draw them into error by deception and hypocrisy"
- "They should shun their writings and all contact with them … They should avoid them as strangers and thieves who come only to steal, slay, and destroy."
- "the religious freedom of Catholics remains publicly oppressed by schismatic laws"
- "May He look favorable on your country until when the errors and the counsels of the impious have been destroyed, it enjoys the tranquil peace of truth and justice."
