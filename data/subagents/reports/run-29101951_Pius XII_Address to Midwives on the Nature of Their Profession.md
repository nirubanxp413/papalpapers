# Address to Midwives on the Nature of Their Profession

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The professional role of the midwife is inseparable from her spiritual apostolate — she must defend God's ordering of life, marriage, and procreation against an array of named modern threats, from eugenics and sterilization to contraception and hedonism.
- **Summary:** Pius XII addresses a gathering of midwives not primarily as a medical audience but as a corps of "apostles" stationed at the threshold of new life. The address moves through a series of interlocked moral questions — the inviolability of the unborn, baptism of dying newborns, the duties of motherhood, the condemnation of sterilization, the conditional permissibility of natural family planning (the "agenesic periods"), the requirement of heroic continence when pregnancy is truly impossible, and a rebuttal of "personalist" theories of marriage that de-prioritise procreation. Throughout, the midwife is charged with knowing divine law precisely enough to refuse immoral cooperation, counsel mothers, and defend God's order in the intimate space of professional practice.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly names or clearly implies the following as the conditions it is reacting against: (1) the "direct destruction of so-called 'useless lives'" practiced "a few years ago" and condemned by the Church as contrary to natural and divine law — a recent historical episode of state-ordered killing; (2) the spread of eugenic and social "indications" invoked to justify sterilization, which the Church had been forced to condemn publicly "when ten years or so ago sterilization came to be more widely applied"; (3) the rising use of artificial contraception and any interference with the conjugal act; (4) the "new theory" of using natural sterile periods (the agenesic / rhythm method), which required authoritative moral clarification; (5) "personalist" theories of marriage — attributed to "writers over the last twenty years or so" — that locate the primary purpose of conjugal union in the personal and affective bond of the couple rather than in procreation; (6) a literature promoting the "technique of love" and detailed guidance on sexual intimacy under the guise of education; (7) "hedonism" described as "unceasing waves" invading the world and threatening to submerge marital life; (8) the widespread argument that heroic continence is simply "impossible" for married couples.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs midwives to: (a) refuse any cooperation with abortion, contraception, or sterilization — a calm but "unequivocal 'no'"; (b) actively cultivate in mothers and fathers the "esteem, desire, joy, and loving welcome of the newly born"; (c) administer emergency baptism when a newborn is in danger of death and no priest is available; (d) inform couples of the biological and technical aspects of the agenesic-period theory while leaving moral judgment to the Church; (e) counsel that when pregnancy must be absolutely avoided, the only licit path is total abstinence — not contraception or any "technique"; (f) correct the "personalist" inversion of values by insisting that procreation remains the primary end of marriage; (g) resist hedonistic literature and refrain from propagating it; (h) model and transmit an understanding that the generative function, while not diminished, is not to be "exalted beyond measure."
- **Tension:** Several explicit tensions run through the text: (1) **Divine law vs. medical/social "indications"** — the document asserts that no indication whatsoever (medical, eugenic, social, economic, moral) can justify the direct taking of innocent life; professional authority and state authority are explicitly subordinated to divine precept. (2) **The "impossible" demand of continence** — the document directly confronts the objection that long periods of abstinence are humanly impossible, inverting the argument: because God commands it, it must be possible, and grace makes up the deficit. (3) **Personal love vs. procreative primacy** — the text acknowledges that personal and affective values in marriage are "truly genuine," but insists they are "in the second degree of the scale of values," not primary; writers who reverse this order are said to propagate a "grave inversion." (4) **Professional duty vs. patient expectation** — midwives are told they will frequently be called upon to do what patients want but what is morally illicit; their apostolate requires them to refuse while still caring for the person. (5) **Nature's voice vs. cultural pressure against children** — the document notes that the idea of children as a "blessing" provokes "contradiction and even derision" in contemporary culture, while Scripture and reason insist otherwise.

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument**

The address has a distinct movement: it opens with the collaborative mystery of human conception (parents, nature, God) and immediately establishes that anyone who acts at "the cradle of life's origin" must understand the order the Creator wishes maintained. This frames everything that follows — the midwife is not a neutral technician but a participant in a divinely ordered process. From this foundation the text descends through escalating specificities: first the absolute inviolability of innocent human life (the most foundational claim, and the one with the clearest historical urgency given the reference to state-ordered killing "a few years ago"); then the welcoming of the newborn and the apostolate toward mothers; then the supernatural dimension (emergency baptism); then the duties of motherhood framed through Mary's "fiat."

The middle section forms the doctrinal core: it addresses in sequence the conjugal act (citing *Casti Connubii* of 1930), sterilization, birth control (including the extended, careful treatment of the agenesic periods), and heroic continence. The natural family planning section is notably the most legally precise — Pius XII distinguishes between using the sterile periods while the conjugal right remains intact (permitted), limiting the right itself by marriage contract (invalidating the marriage), and using only the sterile periods while still capable of intercourse (morally permissible only with sufficiently grave reasons). This is careful casuistry embedded in an apostolic address.

The final third pivots to the "order of values" debate — a direct engagement with contemporary theological and philosophical writing on marriage. The document characterises these writings as a "grave inversion" and responds by restating the Church's 1944 declaration and a subsequent Holy See decree. It closes with the defense of human dignity against hedonism.

**Tone**

The address alternates between two registers. When addressing doctrine — on the inviolability of life, sterilization, contraception, or the primary end of marriage — the tone is authoritative and categorical ("there is no man, no human authority, no science, no 'indication' at all... that may offer or give a valid judicial title"). When addressing the midwife's pastoral work with mothers — evoking Scripture's images of fruitful vines and olive shoots, or the mother's joy after the pain of birth — the tone becomes genuinely warm and contemplative. This oscillation is deliberate: Pius XII is forming a professional conscience that must be firm at the doctrinal edge and tender in pastoral practice simultaneously.

**Notable framing**

The most distinctive framing device is the word "apostolate," used repeatedly to recast a secular profession as an ecclesial mission. The midwife is not asked to add religious duties to her professional ones — the professional act itself is the apostolate. The refusal to cooperate with immoral requests, the encouragement of a mother, the emergency baptism: all of these are the same mission. This framing makes the midwife a kind of lay religious operative, uniquely positioned (more than the priest, the document implies) to exercise influence in the intimate space of birth.

A second notable framing is the deliberate use of medical and legal vocabulary — "indication," "faculty," "title," "debt," "right" — to conduct a theological argument. The document systematically denies that any category of medical or legal justification can override divine law, but it does so by engaging the vocabulary of those categories rather than dismissing them.

The reference to heroism is also striking: the document invokes the contemporary experience of heroism under duress (likely wartime memory) to argue that married people are capable of the heroism of continence — "It would be a wrong towards men and women of our age to judge them incapable of continuous heroism." The appeal is simultaneously moral and flattering.

Finally, the document is self-aware about its polemical targets. It closes the section on hedonism by explicitly disavowing Manichaeism and Jansenism — preemptively blocking the accusation that its strictness amounts to a condemnation of bodily pleasure as such. This suggests the arguments being rebutted were already being made in the circles Pius XII expected the midwives to encounter.
