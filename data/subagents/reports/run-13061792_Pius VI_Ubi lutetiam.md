# Ubi lutetiam

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document extends and precisely delimits the authority of French bishops to absolve clergy who unlawfully occupied church positions under the schismatic Civil Constitution, while also condemning a forged brief circulated in the Pope's name.
- **Summary:** Pius VI responds to repeated petitions from French bishops who sought clarification on whether their previously granted absolution faculties extended to "intruded" clergy — those who, having sworn the civil oath, illegitimately occupied parishes and dioceses under France's Civil Constitution of the Clergy. The Pope distinguishes between two tiers of offenders: lower clergy (priests and lesser ecclesiastics) who may be absolved by the bishops under strict conditions, and higher clergy (archbishops and bishops) whose absolution is reserved to the Holy See alone due to the greater gravity of their crime as architects of the schism. The document closes by denouncing a fraudulent brief circulating under the Pope's name that praised the constitutional church and discredited the Pope's own authentic letters.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to (1) the petitions of French bishops in Paris who found the prior apostolic indult of 19 March 1792 ambiguous regarding intruded clergy; (2) the ongoing schism caused by the Civil Constitution of the Clergy, which the text calls "in part heretical and in part schismatic" and which the entire French episcopal body had previously condemned; (3) a forged brief ("Il Nostro cuore paterno") circulated by schismatics, falsely dated 2 April 1792, that repudiated the Pope's own letters against the Civil Constitution and urged obedience to constitutional bishops.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates and directs toward: (1) the reconciliation and return to the Church of intruded clergy, through a prescribed written recantation of the civil oath, acknowledgment of the illegitimacy of their ordinations, a sworn promise of obedience to the Holy See and legitimate bishops, and public renunciation of occupied parishes; (2) the eventual end of the schism, which the Pope says he continuously implores God for "in tears"; (3) vigilance among the faithful against fraudulent documents claiming papal authority.
- **Tension:** (1) Mercy vs. canonical rigour: the Pope repeatedly invokes the ancient severity of the Church against schismatic clergy while deliberately choosing to extend indulgence — he cites Nicaea, Innocent I, Jerome, and the Synod of Alexandria as precedents for graduated clemency, but insists conditions must be strictly met. (2) Papal jurisdiction vs. delegated episcopal authority: the Pope draws a precise boundary between what bishops may do (absolve lower intruded clergy) and what remains exclusively reserved to Rome (absolve intruded archbishops and bishops), framing this reservation as a structural necessity to prevent inconsistent application and to preserve the dignity of the apostolic see. (3) Sincere conversion vs. false reconciliation: the document warns penitents that insincere absolution offers no benefit before God and would "transform the remedy into poison," citing the scriptural principle that the Holy Spirit flees from falsehood.

## Unstructured

**Overall argument and flow:**

The letter moves through three distinct phases. The first (§§1–6) is administrative and juridical: the Pope recounts the back-and-forth with French bishops over the scope of the 19 March indult, confirms that its omission of intruded clergy was deliberate and not a clerical oversight, and asserts the Holy See's exclusive right to absolve the more gravely culpable intruders — particularly those who were consecrated or ordained under the schismatic regime and then actively exercised that illegitimate authority, compounding their sin with every subsequent act performed under invalid jurisdiction. The Pope's language here is precise and legalistic, cataloguing the crimes of the intruders in cumulative terms: they profane sacraments, lead the faithful into error, construct a "new constitutional church" distinct from Christ's Church in substance, laws, and name, and "trace with their own hand a wide road to schism."

The second phase (§§7–13) is historical and theological: the Pope anchors his measured clemency in patristic and conciliar precedent. He surveys the Nicene canon 8 (on Novatians), the letters of Innocent I to Rufus of Thessalonica, Jerome at the Synod of Rimini, and the Synod of Alexandria attended by Athanasius, Hilary of Poitiers, and Eusebius of Vercelli. The cumulative argument is that the ancient Church distinguished between those born into heresy and those who deliberately embraced it — and treated the latter far more harshly, especially when they were clergy. The Pope uses this history to justify both the conditions he imposes (written recantation, public renunciation, penitential satisfaction) and his reservation of higher-order cases to Rome. The tone in this section becomes explicitly pastoral toward the penitents: "we desire ardently that their confidence may revive so as to turn toward the common Mother and take prompt refuge in her bosom." He explicitly invokes Pope Leo I and promises a welcoming embrace to those who sincerely repent.

The third phase (§§16–17) pivots abruptly to expose and condemn the forged brief. The Pope identifies specific false details (wrong location: St. Mary Major instead of St. Peter's; impossible dating: fourteen days after his last genuine letter), characterizes the document as "false, fictitious, calumnious, heretical, and schismatic," and calls on bishops and the faithful to redouble their vigilance. This section underscores a running concern in the letter about the instability of authority and legitimacy: just as the intruded clergy simulate legitimate church office, the forged brief simulates legitimate papal speech — both are forms of fraudulent substitution that the Pope treats with parallel severity.

**Tone:**

The document is primarily authoritative and juridical in register, but with recurring pastoral inflections. The Pope positions himself explicitly as a father ("diletti figli Nostri") who is both enforcing canonical order and reaching out to the wayward. The grief and urgency of the schism situation is present throughout — the closing image of the Pope imploring God "in tears" ("ininterrottamente supplichiamo in lacrime") for the end of the schism gives the letter an unusual emotional weight for a document that is otherwise largely administrative in form.

**Notable framing:**

The distinction between the two tiers of guilty clergy is framed not merely as a matter of degree but of kind. The Pope argues that bishops and archbishops who intruded are the "heads" and "authors" of the entire schism, making their culpability categorical rather than merely greater in quantity. This framing justifies the reservation to Rome in ecclesiological as well as disciplinary terms: the authors of a schism against the apostolic see can only be reconciled by that same see. The forged brief is presented as a final act of desperation by schismatics who have "lost hope of defending their authority, despised by all and now annihilated" — suggesting the Pope views the schism as already in a terminal phase, which gives the absolution procedures an urgency of pastoral recovery rather than ongoing punishment.
