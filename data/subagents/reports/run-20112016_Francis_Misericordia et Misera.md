# Misericordia et Misera

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Mercy is not a parenthesis in the Church's life but its very essence — simultaneously a personal spiritual encounter with God's forgiveness and a social imperative that demands concrete, ongoing works of charity in the world.
- **Summary:** Written at the close of the Extraordinary Jubilee of Mercy (2015–2016), this apostolic letter reflects on what the Church has experienced during the Holy Year and charts the path forward. Francis argues that the Jubilee's grace must not end with the closing of the Holy Door but must be celebrated, embodied, and institutionalised in permanent ways — through the liturgy, the Sacrament of Reconciliation, scripture, consolation, care for the family, and the corporal and spiritual works of mercy. The letter issues concrete institutional decisions (extending priests' faculty to absolve abortion, extending the Missionaries of Mercy ministry, extending sacramental validity for SSPX confessions) and announces a new World Day of the Poor. Both the personal transformation of the sinner and the structural transformation of society are treated as expressions of a single mercy.
- **Response to:** The conclusion of the Extraordinary Jubilee of Mercy and the closing of the Holy Door; the richness of grace received during that Holy Year; a contemporary culture the document describes as "often dominated by technology" in which "sadness and loneliness appear to be on the rise, not least among young people"; multiple global crises the text names explicitly — hunger, forced migration, disease, prison conditions, illiteracy, extreme individualism, and people's estrangement from God; the crisis of the family; and the prior provisions Francis had made for the Jubilee year (abortion absolution faculty, SSPX sacramental provision) which the letter now extends.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Continued, joyful living of mercy beyond the Jubilee as a permanent feature of ecclesial life; a "pastoral conversion" shaped daily by mercy; a "culture of mercy" based on encounter and active solidarity; renewed priest commitment to the Sacrament of Confession; a proposed annual "Sunday of the Word of God"; "24 Hours for the Lord" as a recurring pastoral initiative; new and creative works of mercy adapted to contemporary poverty and marginalization; and the new World Day of the Poor to be celebrated on the Thirty-Third Sunday of Ordinary Time.
- **Tension:** The document names and holds in tension: the closure of the Jubilee versus the insistence that mercy must never become a "mere parenthesis"; the priority of God's love over law and legal justice (illustrated through the woman caught in adultery and the theme of the Sacrament of Penance) versus the continued moral weight of sin (e.g., "abortion is a grave sin" even as its forgiveness is fully extended); the spiritual interiority of mercy (personal conversion, joy, prayer) versus its irreducibly social and public character ("mercy becomes visible and tangible in specific and powerful acts"); and the temptation to theorise about mercy versus the demand for concrete, hands-on engagement with the poor.

## Unstructured

**Key themes and their texture in the text:**

The document opens with Augustine's phrase *misericordia et misera* — "mercy with misery" — which functions as a governing icon: the encounter of God's mercy with human sinfulness. This framing recurs throughout. Francis uses Gospel scenes (the woman caught in adultery in Jn 8, the sinful woman at the Pharisee's dinner in Lk 7) not as abstract illustrations but as dense narrative anchors that ground the entire letter in concrete encounter rather than doctrinal abstraction.

**Flow of argument:**

The letter moves in rough phases:
1. *Reflective* (§§1–4): Meditating on the Jubilee's meaning through biblical narrative — forgiveness, joy, the experience of mercy as transformation.
2. *Liturgical and sacramental* (§§5–12): How mercy is celebrated — the Eucharist, the Word of God, Confession, the Missionaries of Mercy, and the specific institutional extensions Francis grants.
3. *Pastoral and consoling* (§§13–15): Mercy as consolation in suffering, silence as pastoral presence, the family in crisis, the moment of death.
4. *Social and missionary* (§§16–21): Mercy as a social value requiring new creativity, works of mercy, the World Day of the Poor, and the establishment of a "culture of mercy."
5. *Marian close* (§22): Mary as the first exemplar of mercy and companion on the journey.

**Tone:**

The tone is distinctly pastoral rather than juridical, though it contains juridical acts (the faculty extensions in §§12). Francis's characteristic register is present throughout: direct address ("let us not lose such occasions"), rhetorical questions that open into invitation, and a preference for narrative over systematic argumentation. The letter describes mercy as "like a gusting but wholesome wind," "like leaven that makes the dough rise," and "handcrafted" — imagery that is organic, non-bureaucratic, and deliberately anti-triumphalist.

**Notable framing:**

The letter is explicitly *transitional*: it marks an ending (the Jubilee) and a beginning (life after the Holy Year). Francis frames this not as a return to normal but as a permanent new orientation. The phrase "This is the time of mercy" — repeated emphatically in §21 — functions as a rhetorical seal: mercy is not periodic or occasional but constitutive and perpetual.

The extension of abortion absolution (§12) is presented entirely within the logic of mercy — stating firmly that abortion is a grave sin while simultaneously insisting that "there is no sin that God's mercy cannot reach and wipe away when it finds a repentant heart." The institutional provision is embedded in the pastoral argument rather than separated from it.

The social dimension culminates in the announced World Day of the Poor (§21), justified through the identification of Christ with the poor in Mt 25. Francis frames poverty not as a welfare issue but as an evangelical and eschatological one: "as long as Lazarus lies at the door of our homes, there can be no justice or social peace."

**Scripture use:**

Scripture is woven densely and continuously throughout. The Old Testament (Isaiah, Micah, Psalms, Ezekiel, Hosea, Genesis) is read typologically as witness to divine mercy across history. The New Testament (especially John, Luke, Paul's letters) provides both narrative (the woman caught in adultery, the sinful woman) and doctrinal grounding (2 Cor 5 on reconciliation, Gal 3 on the law's educational role, Rom 8 on the law of the Spirit). The effect is to present mercy not as a theme *within* scripture but as scripture's unifying thread.
