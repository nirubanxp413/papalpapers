# Omnem Sollicitudinem

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Apostolic See defends the integrity of Greco-Ruthenian Catholic liturgical rites against unauthorized innovations being imposed by an illegitimate administrator seeking to introduce schismatic practices.
- **Summary:** Pius IX writes to the Ruthenian bishops of the Latin-Polish territories to condemn the actions of a "pseudo-administrator" in the diocese of Chelm who has usurped ecclesiastical jurisdiction and issued a circular letter (October 20, 1873) ordering liturgical innovations with the apparent aim of drawing Catholics into schism. The pope reaffirms the long-standing principle that no liturgical changes may be introduced into Oriental rites without consultation with and approval from the Apostolic See, declares all the pseudo-administrator's dispositions null and void, praises the faithful of Chelm for their heroic resistance, and exhorts the legitimate Ruthenian bishops to enforce strict observance of approved liturgical canons—especially those of the Synod of Zamosc—and to suppress disorder from its very beginning.
- **Response to:** The document responds explicitly to: (1) the circular letter of the pseudo-administrator of Chelm (20 October 1873) ordering innovations in divine worship and sacred liturgy; (2) the ongoing "bitter controversy" over liturgical matters among Catholics of the Greco-Ruthenian rite; (3) the prior departure of Chelm's legitimate bishop, leaving the diocese vulnerable; and (4) a historical pattern of earlier controversies in the ecclesiastical province of Lwow over diversity of discipline and rite, previously settled in 1863 by convention between Latin and Greek-rite bishops.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs the Ruthenian bishops to: preserve scrupulously the liturgical practice approved or tacitly permitted by the Apostolic See; reject all innovations outright; instruct pastors and priests to observe the sacred canons (especially of the Synod of Zamosc) under strict penalties if necessary; and act to totally suppress all liturgical disorder "from its very beginning." It also implicitly anticipates continued pressure on the Chelm faithful and calls for vigilance to prevent their being torn from the Church into heresy and schism.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between legitimate ecclesiastical authority (the Apostolic See and its canonical processes) and usurped or self-authorised reform—those who, "desirous of novelty," change rites "to their own tastes" under the pretext of purification or restoration to pristine integrity. A secondary tension runs between the preservation of distinctly Oriental Catholic rites (protected as precious patrimony) and the pressure—whether from within or from schismatic influence—to homogenize or alter those rites. The text also surfaces the tension between the spiritual welfare of "simple and uneducated people" who may be deceived, and the bad-faith actors who fraudulently invoke Holy See documents to justify their innovations.

## Unstructured

**Key themes and flow of argument**

The letter moves in a clear arc: historical foundation → current crisis → juridical condemnation → pastoral praise → episcopal directive.

Pius IX opens by establishing continuity with his own prior commitments and those of his predecessors (Clement VIII, Paul V, Benedict XIV, Gregory XVI) to protect Oriental Catholic liturgies. This is not framed as a new policy but as *maintenance* of an ancient, well-grounded tradition. The repeated citations of predecessor documents (going back to 1595) create an aura of settled, irreversible principle: the Apostolic See has always held Oriental liturgies sacred and has always held sole authority over their modification.

The pivot to crisis comes in §§6–8. The "present wretched conditions" in Lwow and especially Chelm are introduced with language of grief and urgency ("pains Us even more," "wretched state of affairs"). The pseudo-administrator is portrayed not merely as incompetent but as actively malicious—a usurper who "has not feared" to seize jurisdiction and who quotes papal constitutions fraudulently to deceive the uneducated. The scriptural citation from John 10:1 ("has not entered the sheepfold by the door, but by some other way") frames him not just as an ecclesiastical irregular but as a figure of the false shepherd—a theologically loaded condemnation.

The juridical response in §9 is terse and absolute: all the pseudo-administrator's acts are "null and void," declared so by apostolic authority. §§10–11 then articulate the doctrinal rationale: the canons require ancient Oriental rites to be "scrupulously retained," and the pretext of "purifying" or "restoring" rites is unmasked as a deception whose real purpose is to "prepare pitfalls for the faith" and hand the Ruthenians over "to heresy and schism."

**Tone**

The tone shifts noticeably between sections. When addressing the pseudo-administrator and the crisis, it is authoritative, juridical, and morally severe ("usurp," "intruder," "fraudulently interpreted," "deceptive pretext"). When addressing the faithful of Chelm, it becomes genuinely pastoral and even admiring—their resistance is called "truly heroic" and is framed as a public witness "before God, angels and men." The closing sections addressed to the legitimate bishops resume an exhortatory but collegial register ("We exhort you… venerable brothers"), ending with an apostolic blessing.

**Notable framing**

- Liturgical integrity is presented as inseparable from doctrinal integrity: §2 states explicitly that "the closest possible link binds liturgical practice and dogmatic truths," making any unauthorized rite not merely an administrative offense but a potential doctrinal one.
- The Ruthenian rite is consistently framed as a *Catholic* possession to be protected, not as a foreign or inferior tradition to be tolerated. The special benevolence of Roman Pontiffs toward Ruthenians is repeatedly stressed.
- Change introduced through legitimate processes is acceptable (§3 acknowledges rites "borrowed from other Churches" that were welcomed); the problem is *unilateral* or *illicit* innovation, not change per se.
- The reference to 300,000 Ruthenians being "lamentably torn from the bosom of the Church" under Gregory XVI implicitly raises the historical stakes: this has happened before and could happen again.
