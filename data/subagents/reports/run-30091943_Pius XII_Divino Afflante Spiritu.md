# Divino Afflante Spiritu

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** Catholic biblical scholars must embrace modern philological, archaeological, and textual-critical methods — especially the study of original Hebrew and Greek texts and ancient literary forms — in order to reach the genuine literal meaning of Scripture while remaining faithful to Church authority and inerrancy.
- **Summary:** The encyclical, issued on the fiftieth anniversary of Leo XIII's *Providentissimus Deus*, is a programmatic directive for Catholic biblical studies. Pius XII ratifies the doctrinal foundation (Scripture is divinely inspired, inerrant, and authored by God), then calls Catholic exegetes to go further by working from original-language texts, mastering ancient Oriental languages, applying textual criticism, and — most distinctively — studying the "literary forms" and "modes of expression" of ancient Eastern peoples to correctly interpret what the sacred writer actually intended. The document also addresses the institutional apparatus supporting this work (Pontifical Biblical Institute, Biblical Commission, seminaries, academic degrees), calls for pastoral dissemination of Scripture to the faithful, and closes with a brief but pointed appeal to Scripture as the remedy for a world ravaged by war.
- **Response to:** (1) The fiftieth anniversary of Leo XIII's *Providentissimus Deus* (explicitly stated as the occasion); (2) "new and serious difficulties and questions" arising from the "wide-spread prejudices of rationalism and more especially from the discovery and investigation of the antiquities of the East" (§4); (3) critics "outside or hostile to the Church" who attacked the "authenticity, antiquity, integrity and historical value of the Sacred Books" (§42); (4) Catholic writers who sought to restrict scriptural inerrancy only to "matters of faith and morals," treating other content as *obiter dicta* not connected with faith (§1); (5) commentators who neglect theological doctrine and focus only on "historical, archaeological, philological and other auxiliary sciences" to the detriment of pastoral usefulness (§24); (6) those who complain that Catholic biblical commentaries fail to "raise their hearts to God" and demand instead a purely "spiritual or mystical interpretation" (§25); (7) arbitrary early textual criticism that introduced "preconceived ideas" into Scripture (§18).
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) Catholic exegetes, to diligently study Hebrew, Greek, and other Oriental languages and work from original texts rather than relying exclusively on the Vulgate (§§14–16); (2) biblical scholars, to apply textual criticism rigorously and produce new critical editions of the sacred text (§19); (3) exegetes, to determine the "peculiar character," age, sources, and "forms of expression" of each sacred writer — especially the literary forms (*genera litteraria*) of the ancient East — before pronouncing on meaning (§§33–39); (4) commentators, to prioritize the theological exposition of Scripture, not merely its historical or philological dimensions (§24); (5) seminary professors, to form future priests with genuine love for Scripture and to aim at theological, not merely curiosity-satisfying, instruction (§§53–55); (6) bishops and priests, to spread Scripture among the faithful through vernacular translations, biblical associations, periodicals, and preaching (§§50–52); (7) Catholic laity, to engage in archaeology and historical research as a service to Christian truth (§40); (8) all Catholics, to extend charity rather than suspicion toward Catholic scholars who propose new interpretations (§47); (9) all peoples, to find in Scripture — and in Christ whom it reveals — the only possible remedy for the wounds of war (§§56–58).
- **Tension:** (1) **Inerrancy vs. modern scholarship:** the document must simultaneously affirm that Scripture contains no error whatsoever and endorse the use of the same archaeological, philological, and critical methods that non-Catholic scholars have used to challenge that inerrancy — it resolves this by arguing that properly applied science confirms rather than undermines faith; (2) **Vulgate authority vs. original texts:** the Council of Trent declared the Latin Vulgate "authentic," yet the document argues that this authenticity is "not specified primarily as critical, but rather as juridical" (§21), thus permitting — indeed requiring — Catholic scholars to use original Hebrew and Greek; (3) **Ancient tradition vs. modern progress:** the Fathers of the Church are authoritative guides, yet the document insists that modern scholars can and must go beyond them because new discoveries have opened questions the Fathers could not have resolved (§§31–32); (4) **Scholarly freedom vs. doctrinal constraint:** the document simultaneously urges that "many things… of the greatest importance" remain open for scholarly debate (§47) and insists that any interpretation must accord with the Church's "traditional teaching regarding the inerrancy of Sacred Scripture" (§46); (5) **Technical exegesis vs. pastoral spirituality:** the document criticizes commentaries that are merely archaeological or philological, yet also cautions against arbitrary "spiritual or mystical" readings that ignore the literal sense (§§24–27).

## Unstructured

**Core argument and flow.** The document moves in three broad phases. The first (§§1–10) is historical-institutional: it recounts fifty years of papal initiative since *Providentissimus Deus* — the founding of the Biblical Commission, the Pontifical Biblical Institute, academic degree requirements, the monastery of St. Jerome for the Vulgate revision — establishing that the Church has been actively and consistently promoting rigorous Scripture study. This serves as legitimation for what follows.

The second phase (§§11–48) is the technical heart of the document, and its most distinctive contribution. Pius XII argues that archaeological discoveries, papyri, ancient codices, and the recovery of Oriental literature have created new conditions for biblical interpretation that were simply unavailable to previous generations. The key innovation is the extended treatment of *literary forms* (§§33–39): the document argues that ancient Eastern peoples "did not always employ those forms or kinds of speech which we use today," and that correct interpretation requires the exegete to identify what *genre* the sacred writer was employing — poetic description, legal formulation, historical narrative — before judging whether an apparent error is actually an error. The analogy offered is striking: "the words of God, expressed in human language, are made like to human speech in every respect, except error" (§37), mirroring the Incarnation ("the substantial Word of God became like to men in all things, except sin"). This parallel frames philological method as theologically principled, not merely pragmatic.

Within this same phase, the document makes an unusually generous statement about scholarly latitude: most biblical texts have not been defined by Church authority, and "there remain therefore many things, and of the greatest importance, in the discussion and exposition of which the skill and genius of Catholic commentators may and ought to be freely exercised" (§47). The tone here is defensive of scholars against overzealous Catholic critics — "let them abhor that intemperate zeal which imagines that whatever is new should for that very reason be opposed or suspected" (§47).

The third phase (§§49–62) is pastoral and exhortatory: priests, bishops, seminary professors, and the faithful are all called to a greater love of Scripture. The document frames Scripture reading not as academic exercise but as the "food by which the spiritual life is nourished unto perfection."

**Tone.** The document is primarily authoritative — it issues directives, confirms rules, and establishes expectations. But it is also warm and encouraging toward biblical scholars, whose work it describes as "sublime" and whose difficulties it acknowledges with sympathy ("let us not wax impatient," §45). The passage on war (§§56–58) is the most emotionally heightened moment in the document — the only place where the encyclical speaks directly to a world outside the academy, and where it shifts from institutional to pastoral register. There, Scripture is not a scholarly object but the sole source of consolation and peace for a civilization tearing itself apart.

**Notable framing.** The document consistently frames scholarly rigor and Catholic faith as mutually reinforcing rather than in tension. Where rationalist critics used historical-critical methods to undermine Scripture, Catholic scholars use the same methods to confirm it. Science, properly conducted, serves revelation. This apologetic logic runs throughout: every new tool — textual criticism, archaeology, ancient languages — is reframed as a gift of "Divine Providence" (§12) that helps the Church understand God's word more fully. There is no anxiety about modernity here; on the contrary, the document is confident that Catholicism can absorb and master modern scholarship while remaining doctrinally anchored.

**The literary forms passage as the document's center of gravity.** If one paragraph can be said to define the document's significance within its own argument, it is §35: "What is the literal sense of a passage is not always as obvious in the speeches and writings of the ancient authors of the East, as it is in the works of our own time… the interpreter must, as it were, go back wholly in spirit to those remote centuries of the East and with the aid of history, archaeology, ethnology, and other sciences, accurately determine what modes of writing, so to speak, the authors of that ancient period would be likely to use." This is the fulcrum on which the document's reconciliation of inerrancy and historical criticism rests.
