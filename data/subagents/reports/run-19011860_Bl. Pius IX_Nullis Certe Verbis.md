# Nullis Certe Verbis

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Pope publicly defends the necessity of the Church's temporal sovereignty over the Papal States as inseparable from the freedom of the apostolic ministry, while drawing spiritual consolation from the universal Catholic solidarity expressed in response to its threat.
- **Summary:** Written in January 1860, this document addresses the bishops of the Catholic world to report on the crisis over the Papal States. Pius IX recounts the widespread expressions of loyalty and support from clergy and laity, then pivots to the "new cause of sorrow": the published letter of Napoleon III advising the Pope to voluntarily cede the rebellious provinces of Emilia. The document sets out the Pope's firm refusal — grounded in oaths, justice to Catholics, the rights of all sovereigns, and the integrity of the Holy See — and calls on bishops to continue rousing the faithful to defend both the Church and the civil dominion of the papacy, closing with an appeal for prayer through Mary and the saints.
- **Response to:** (1) Attacks by revolution on the legitimate civil dominion of the Holy See in Italy, as described in the Pope's encyclical of June 18 (prior year) and two consistory allocutions. (2) The published letter of Napoleon III in *Le Moniteur* recommending the voluntary surrender of the Emilian provinces. (3) The rebellions in Bologna, Ravenna, and other provinces, described as instigated by specific men and resources rather than reflecting popular will.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) Bishops are to continue inflaming the faithful to defend the Catholic Church, the Holy See, and the patrimony of St. Peter. (2) All parties — explicitly including the Emperor of France — are reminded they must one day give account before the tribunal of Christ, urging prudence and mercy over justice. (3) Universal prayer is solicited, through the intercession of Mary, Peter, Paul, and all the saints, for divine intervention on behalf of the Church.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the Pope's insistence that the temporal power of the papacy is a divine grant essential to the free exercise of the apostolic ministry (and therefore not alienable) and the counsel of a powerful secular monarch (Napoleon III) that surrendering it would be a pragmatic solution to present disorder. A secondary tension exists between the claim that the rebellions in Emilia do not reflect the genuine will of the people and the political reality that foreign and domestic powers are treating those provinces as effectively lost. The document also carries an implicit tension between the Pope's pastoral duty to souls in the disturbed provinces (where "piety, religion, faith, integrity, and morals are weakened daily") and his political inability to exercise effective civil governance there.

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument:** The document moves in three recognisable beats. It opens with gratitude — the Pope acknowledges the "innumerable letters" signed by "hundreds of thousands of Catholics" expressing loyalty, which he frames as divine consolation ("Blessed be God…who consoles Us in all our tribulation"). This establishes the relational bond between the papacy and the universal Church as the emotional and ecclesial foundation of everything that follows.

The second beat is the disclosure and rebuttal of Napoleon III's advice. The Pope is unusually specific: he names the newspaper (*Le Moniteur*), summarises the Emperor's argument, and then quotes his own reply at length. The arguments against ceding the provinces are layered — canonical (solemn oaths), political (it would incentivise future rebellions), moral (what is not his to give, he cannot give), juridical (the patrimony "pertains not to the succession of any royal family but to all Catholics"), and systemic (it would weaken the rights of all princes of the Christian world). The reminder to the Emperor that "all must give an exact account…before the tribunal of Christ" is diplomatically bold and theologically framed as pastoral concern rather than threat.

The third beat is the call to action: bishops are to keep the faithful mobilised, and the document closes with a Marian and apostolic intercession.

**Tone:** The tone is simultaneously authoritative and pastoral, but leans distinctly toward the authoritative-declarative. The Pope is not asking for advice; he is reporting decisions already made and soliciting implementation of a strategy already determined. The pastoral warmth is present (he speaks of his "burning charity," his "paternal benevolence," and his willingness to "lay down Our life"), but it serves to reinforce obedience rather than open deliberation. The document is essentially a communiqué to allied bishops reporting the diplomatic posture adopted with a major secular power.

**Notable framing:** The document frames temporal sovereignty not as a political convenience or historical accident, but as a theological necessity: without civil dominion, the Pope cannot exercise the apostolic ministry "in full liberty and without any impediment." The loss of the Papal States is therefore not primarily a political loss but a threat to the freedom of the universal Church. This framing is stated explicitly ("God gave the civil power to the Roman Pontiff, so that he, never subject to any power, might exercise in full liberty…the supreme task of the apostolic ministry") and is the load-bearing premise of the entire document. The Pope also frames his own suffering in explicitly evangelical terms — quoting Christ's "you will have tribulation in the world, but be confident, I have overcome the world" and the Beatitude on persecution for justice — positioning the papacy's trial as a participation in Christ's own passion, which elevates the political dispute to a spiritual one.

**What the text does not say:** The document does not engage with whether the majority of the Emilian population actually supports the rebellion (it claims they did not, but only briefly and in passing). It does not offer any political pathway to resolution beyond refusal. It does not address internal Church reform or the spiritual condition of the Church outside the crisis zone. All these silences reinforce the document's primary function: to consolidate and maintain a coalition of episcopal support around a fixed position in a rapidly deteriorating political situation.
