# Cum nos superiori

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Social
- **Theme (one sentence):** In the face of violent suppression of the Church's freedom and institutional structures, Pius VI issues emergency canonical legislation to ensure the valid and unimpeded election of a future pope under conditions of persecution and exile.
- **Summary:** Writing from the Carthusian monastery near Florence — where he has been forcibly exiled — Pius VI documents the catastrophic state of the Church: Cardinals expelled, imprisoned, or deported; the patrimony of St. Peter plundered; monasteries dissolved; priestly immunity violated; and the Pope himself driven from Rome. Against this backdrop, he issues a formal apostolic letter (bolla) suspending or relaxing numerous procedural requirements for the conclave, allowing Cardinals gathered in the territory of a Catholic prince to elect a new Pope even without the full College present, and explicitly permitting Cardinals to deliberate on electoral procedures while the Pope still lives. The letter is framed as an urgent necessity — a provisional but legally binding adaptation of centuries of conclave law to conditions of extreme institutional crisis.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to: (1) the expulsion, imprisonment, and deportation of Cardinals from Rome; (2) the plundering of the patrimony of St. Peter; (3) the suppression of religious communities and profanation of monasteries; (4) the violation of priestly immunity and restriction of sacerdotal authority; (5) the Pope's own forced exile from the Roman See; (6) an earlier emergency decree of December 30 of the 23rd year of his Pontificate (the letter "Christi Ecclesiae") which the current document supplements and partially supersedes, judging it insufficient given the worsening crisis of the subsequent nine months.
- **Tension:** The central tension is institutional survival versus external violent force: the Church's canonical procedures for succession (conclave rules, mandatory location, quorum, solemn ceremonies, prohibitions on discussing elections before a pope's death) are in direct conflict with the political and military reality of Cardinals being scattered, imprisoned, and prevented from assembling freely. A secondary tension exists between strict canonical tradition (the elaborate conclave legislation of Gregory X, Clement V, Clement VI, Urban VIII, Pius IV, Gregory XV, Clement XII, etc.) and the pastoral necessity of ensuring the Church does not remain without a head. The document also anticipates the danger of schism if Cardinals in different locations were to elect rival popes.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document is directed toward the next papal election after Pius VI's death. It anticipates: (1) the need for Cardinals to convene outside Rome, in the territory of a Catholic prince; (2) the possibility that not all Cardinals will be able to attend; (3) the risk that Cardinals may be prevented from traveling; (4) the need for the Dean of the Sacred College (or the highest-ranking present Cardinal) to announce the pope's death and summon the conclave; (5) the election being valid with a two-thirds majority of those Cardinals who do assemble; (6) Cardinals being permitted to deliberate on electoral procedures even during Pius VI's lifetime — something previously forbidden under pain of censure.

## Unstructured

The document is above all an act of institutional crisis management cast in the highest register of papal legislative authority. Pius VI writes from a position of profound physical and political weakness — exiled, confined to a Carthusian monastery outside Florence, describing himself as "this Our weak old age" — but the tone is entirely authoritative and juridically precise. There is no pastoral warmth or doctrinal reflection; this is legislation, not exhortation, for the most part.

**Flow of the argument:**

The letter opens by establishing the precedent: an earlier decree (December 30, Year 23) had already relaxed some conclave rules in response to anticipated danger (§1). Paragraphs 2–3 then argue that the anticipated dangers have been exceeded in severity — the crisis is worse than feared — making that earlier decree insufficient. The rhetorical move is diagnostic: "We were not afraid of imaginary dangers; here is what actually happened." This grounds the subsequent legislation in demonstrable necessity rather than mere precaution.

Paragraphs 4–5 contain the core legislative acts: (a) formal derogation from nearly all non-essential conclave ceremonies and procedural requirements, citing and partially superseding a list of historic constitutions by name; and (b) the grant of permission for Cardinals to discuss electoral procedures while the Pope is still alive — explicitly waiving the penalties set by Paul II's constitution "Cum secundum." This is notable: it removes a centuries-old prohibition in the name of practical preparation.

Paragraphs 6–9 work out the mechanics: notification of the Pope's death, the gathering of Cardinals in a Catholic prince's territory, the role of the Dean of the College, the validity of an election by two-thirds of those present after the customary ten-day waiting period, and the requirement that this letter be read aloud at the first session of the conclave.

Paragraph 9 shifts to an exhortatory register — the only passage with sustained rhetorical and spiritual appeal. Pius VI invokes "the blood of the mercy of Our God," the Cardinals' sacramental oath to defend the Church "to the shedding of blood," and the image of Peter's bark tossed by storms without a helmsman. He urges the Cardinals to set aside private interests and elect a successor swiftly and unanimously. The urgency is real: the document fears schism if Cardinals in different locations proceed independently.

Paragraphs 10–13 are standard canonical boilerplate establishing the letter's perpetual validity, its applicability to authenticated copies, and the sanctions (divine and apostolic displeasure) against those who would obstruct it.

**Tone and framing:**

The dominant tone is juridical and legislative — Pius VI speaks in the first-person plural of sovereign authority throughout, issuing decrees "motu proprio," invoking "the fullness of apostolic power," and citing canonical precedents by name. Yet there is a sustained undercurrent of lament: the specific enumeration of harms in §2 (Cardinals expelled, imprisoned, deported; patrimony plundered; monasteries dissolved; priestly immunity violated; the Pope himself exiled) reads as a formal record of grievance as much as a legal predicate. The document seems aware it may serve as historical testimony as well as immediate instruction.

**Notable framing:**

The document consistently frames external political violence against the Church in terms of threats to "liberty" — "libertà apostolica," "libertà della Chiesa," "libertà degli elettori." The loss at stake is not primarily described in theological terms (heresy, schism, loss of faith) but in institutional and juridical terms: freedom of assembly, freedom of election, freedom of movement. This is striking. The enemies of the Church are not named by creed or nation; they are characterized by their actions — those who expel, imprison, plunder, dissolve, violate. The document's political reticence is conspicuous: it legislates around a crisis whose perpetrators it declines to name.

The closing image — the "barca di Pietro" tossed by storms without a helmsman, and the Catholic flock threatened by wolves breaking in from every direction — frames the crisis of succession as both an institutional emergency and a pastoral catastrophe, tying the legal machinery of conclave law to the protection of ordinary believers.
