# In Plurimis

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The abolition of chattel slavery is the culmination of a divine and ecclesial project rooted in the spiritual equality of all persons in Christ, and its completion demands both celebration and ongoing vigilance.
- **Summary:** Addressed to the Bishops of Brazil on the occasion of Leo XIII's priestly golden jubilee (during which Brazilian slaves were legally freed as a gift), the document traces a grand arc from the Fall — which introduced slavery as a consequence of sin — through the liberating message of Christ and the sustained centuries-long effort of the Church to ameliorate and ultimately abolish slavery, culminating in Brazil's emancipation. The Pope praises the Brazilian emperor and bishops, cautions freed slaves to use liberty well, and closes with an urgent appeal regarding the active slave trade still being conducted in Africa, chiefly attributed to Muslim raiders.
- **Response to:** (1) The emancipation of Brazilian slaves offered as a jubilee gift during the fiftieth anniversary of Leo XIII's priesthood; (2) The long historical persistence of slavery across pagan and Christian eras; (3) Ongoing slave-raiding in equatorial Africa, described in detail as the seizure and forced march of some 400,000 Africans per year, many dying en route.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) Complete legal abolition in Brazil — urging bishops to press its smooth, non-disruptive implementation; (2) Proper conduct for newly freed slaves: gratitude, industry, civic obedience, avoidance of "license"; (3) Missionary expansion in Africa (holding up St. Peter Claver as the model) as the means to end the remaining slave trade; (4) A universal appeal to "all who hold high positions in authority and power" to "repress, forbid, and put an end to that kind of traffic."
- **Tension:** Three distinct tensions run through the text: (a) *Patience vs. justice* — the Church explicitly "deprecated any precipitate action" in freeing slaves, fearing tumult, while simultaneously claiming credit for the abolition project; freed slaves were told to endure suffering "following the steps of Christ" even as the document condemns slavery as evil; (b) *Spiritual equality vs. social hierarchy* — Baptism makes all equal in Christ, yet slaves are also instructed to obey their masters faithfully and not to "confound liberty with license"; (c) *Universal human dignity vs. particular condemnation of Islam* — the document asserts the dignity of all humans from creation while specifically blaming Mohammedans for the African trade, framing it as a contrast between Christian civilization and Muslim "perfidy and cruelty."

## Unstructured

**Argument flow.** The document moves through five phases: (1) *Occasion* — Brazil's jubilee emancipation gives the Pope a warrant to write. (2) *Theological grounding* — slavery originates in the Fall; God never intended humans to dominate each other; Christ came to "preach deliverance to the captives," and baptism dissolves the distinction between slave and free. (3) *Historical narrative* — a long review of the Church's record: the Apostles' letters on master-slave relations, the patristic writers (Chrysostom, Ambrose, Lactantius), papal interventions from Gregory I through Gregory XVI, manumission practices, religious orders dedicated to ransoming captives, and the Church's resistance to the colonial slave trade in the Americas (Paul III's declaration of the natural rights of indigenous peoples). (4) *Present concern* — a vivid, emotionally charged account of the contemporary East African slave trade, presented as eyewitness testimony. (5) *Practical exhortations* — to bishops (press the law forward), to the freed (behave well), to missionaries (follow St. Peter Claver), and to all world leaders (act against the remaining trade).

**Tone.** The document is predominantly *authoritative and historical*, marshalling a chain of precedents to establish the Church's claim as "the banisher of slavery and causer of true liberty, fraternity, and equality among men." The tone shifts to *pastoral* in the final sections addressing freed slaves, and to *urgent and emotive* in the passage about African victims — "those traveling through such places see the pathway strewn with the remains of bones." The overall register is self-consciously historical: Leo presents not just a moral argument but an institutional one, curating a legacy.

**Notable framings.**
- Slavery is not natural but penal — "the state of slavery is rightly regarded as a penalty upon the sinner" (quoting Augustine). This frames it as a theological anomaly, not merely a social injustice, but it also means the document does not call slavery intrinsically wrong in an abstract philosophical sense — it is wrong because it perpetuates the distortion introduced by sin and contradicts Christ's redemptive work.
- The Church is cast as the *primary historical agent* of abolition; secular emancipation movements are welcomed but folded into an ecclesial narrative. The document even credits Gregory XVI's 1837 condemnation as having influenced European states to act.
- St. Peter Claver — just canonized by Leo XIII in January 1888 — is presented as the embodied model for the continuing missionary work: a man who called himself "the slave of the Negroes." This canonization, mentioned within the document itself, gives the letter a double function: ecclesiastical program-statement and hagiographic advertisement.
- The African slave trade section distinguishes sharply between the (now-ended) Atlantic trade "by sea" and the ongoing land-based trade. This serves to redirect concern without conceding that the problem is solved, and it implicitly assigns residual guilt to non-Christian actors.
- The cautions to freed slaves — to "never confound liberty with license," to "restrain envy of another's wealth or position," to be "obedient to the laws" — reveal an anxiety about social disruption that runs alongside the emancipatory rhetoric. Liberation is welcomed; disorder is not.
