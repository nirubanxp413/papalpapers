# Mens Nostra

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Spiritual Exercises — particularly the Ignatian method — are presented as the supreme remedy for the spiritual and moral disorders of the age, essential for clergy, religious, laity, and workers alike.
- **Summary:** Issued at the close of Pius XI's jubilee year commemorating his priestly ordination, *Mens Nostra* calls the entire Church to embrace the practice of Spiritual Exercises (formalized retreats of meditation and prayer). The pope traces the practice from Christ's own withdrawal into the desert through the Desert Fathers, St. Jerome, St. Peter Chrysologus, and St. Charles Borromeo, to its definitive codification by St. Ignatius of Loyola. He argues that the Exercises heal what he diagnoses as the core disease of the modern age — "levity and thoughtlessness" driving absorption in external things, materialism, naturalism, and rationalism — by reorienting the soul toward eternal truths. The document extends its exhortation to bishops, secular clergy, religious, Catholic Action laity, and workmen, and recommends monthly or quarterly recollections for those unable to make full retreats.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to (a) the Jubilee Year it is closing, drawing together its spiritual fruits; (b) the "most grave disease" of the age: "levity and thoughtlessness," "constant and passionate absorption in external things," and "insatiable thirst for riches and pleasures"; (c) the spread of Naturalism, which the text says "weakens the firmness of faith and quenches the flames of Christian charity"; (d) Rationalism, described as a "deadly plague"; (e) the aftermath of the First World War — "the cruel carnage of the war, which has so bitterly troubled the human family" — which left many seeking genuine peace; and (f) the extension of material prosperity to workers, seen as requiring a spiritual counterweight lest they fall into materialism.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates and directs action toward (a) the wider extension of Spiritual Exercises to all orders of society, especially the laity and workmen; (b) a flourishing Catholic Action movement, whose lay participants need the Exercises to sustain apostolic zeal; (c) the annual Vatican retreat, already instituted, as a model for the universal Church; (d) bishops gathering at provincial synods combining retreat with deliberation; (e) "Retreats for Workmen" and sodalities of perseverance as a social-spiritual counterpoise to materialism; and (f) a hoped-for "spiritual regeneration" of society producing social peace — "peace will reign in society and in the hearts of all."
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the interior/eternal and the exterior/temporal: souls drawn outward by wealth, pleasure, and the noise of modern life versus the call to recollect themselves in silence and meditate on God. A secondary tension runs between the apostolic need for clergy to be active in the world and the need for periodic withdrawal so they do not "lose themselves" (St. Bernard is cited: "what will it profit, if thou gain all, and lose thyself?"). A third tension appears between Naturalism/Rationalism — philosophies that the text treats as spiritually corrosive and dominant "far and wide" — and the supernatural spirit in which "alone our holy religion consists."

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument.** The document moves in a clear pastoral arc. It opens by recalling the occasion (the jubilee year) and the hope that it would bear lasting spiritual fruit. It then pivots to the question of how to secure those fruits permanently, and finds the answer in the Spiritual Exercises. Sections 4–7 build the case: the Exercises are urgently needed because the age is spiritually sick (§4), they have apostolic roots in Christ himself and the early Church (§5–6), and their modern codification by Ignatius of Loyola has proved uniquely fruitful across four centuries (§6–7). Sections 8–12 apply this to specific audiences in descending order of church office: the Apostolic See itself (§8), bishops (§9), secular priests (§10), Catholic Action laity (§11), and workers (§12). Sections 13–16 turn practical: closed, private retreats are preferred over public ones; adequate duration matters; and the Ignatian method is named as the preeminent method. Section 17 closes the loop by recommending monthly/quarterly recollections for those who cannot make full retreats. The final two paragraphs (§18–19) close with a Christmas blessing, linking the peace of the Nativity to the peace promised by the Exercises.

**Key themes grounded in the text.**
1. *Spiritual medicine for a sick age.* The document's language is explicitly diagnostic — the age suffers from a "most grave disease," namely distraction and external absorption. The Exercises are the cure prescribed.
2. *Contemplation as the root of apostolic action.* A persistent argument is that genuine apostolic fruitfulness — Catholic Action, missionary zeal, priestly holiness — flows necessarily from interior recollection, not from busyness. The image of the upper room at Pentecost is given as the founding type of this principle.
3. *Ignatian primacy.* Pius XI explicitly names Ignatius "the chief and peculiar Master of Spiritual Exercises" and cites his own earlier apostolic constitution *Summorum Pontificum* (1922) confirming this. The method's four-century track record, adaptability to all states of life, and doctrinal safety (explicitly contrasted with "false mysticism") are cited as its distinguishing marks.
4. *Social peace as the fruit of personal holiness.* The document does not treat spiritual renewal as merely individual. It repeatedly insists that ordered interior lives produce social peace, and that the chaos of post-war society reflects interior disorder. Workers' retreats are framed as a counterweight to materialism within the new economic conditions.
5. *Continuity and tradition.* The document is thick with citations — Jerome, Chrysologus, Bernard, Borromeo, Leo XIII, Pius X — constructing a chain of authority that roots the Exercises in the deepest strata of Catholic tradition rather than presenting them as a Counter-Reformation novelty.

**Tone.** Predominantly pastoral and exhortatory rather than dogmatic or legislative, though it does note that Canon Law already binds religious and clergy to periodic retreats (§9–10). The register is warm and reflective throughout — Pius XI speaks in the first person of his own experience of the Exercises and of the consolations they have given him personally. The closing paragraphs are devotional and lyrical, not juridical.

**Notable framing.** The document deploys the contrast between *noise* and *silence*, *movement* and *recollection*, *external* and *internal* as its organizing metaphors. St. Gregory's image of water — gathered upward when contained, dispersed uselessly when left loose — is the most vivid single figure for the argument. The explicit mention of Naturalism and Rationalism as named adversarial forces, and the framing of Catholic Action laity as "pacific and strenuous soldiers of Christ," places the Exercises within an explicitly contested spiritual landscape, even as the tone remains pastoral.
