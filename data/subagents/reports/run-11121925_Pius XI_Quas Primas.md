# Quas Primas

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Christ's universal kingship — established by scripture, tradition, and the hypostatic union — must be publicly recognized by individuals, families, and nations alike, and the institution of the Feast of Christ the King is the Church's liturgical remedy for the modern rejection of that sovereignty.
- **Summary:** Pius XI grounds Christ's kingship in both the Old and New Testaments, patristic theology, and the Council of Nicaea, arguing that this kingship extends beyond the interior life to encompass civil society and political authority. He diagnoses contemporary society's ills — discord between nations, collapse of family, erosion of public morality, and the rise of anti-clericalism — as direct consequences of having "thrust Jesus Christ and his holy law out of their lives." The encyclical's constructive move is the institution of an annual feast (the last Sunday of October) to renew public acknowledgement of Christ's reign, positioning liturgical celebration as more pedagogically powerful than doctrinal pronouncements alone.
- **Response to:** The exclusion of Christ from "private affairs and politics" that Pius XI says he lamented at the start of his pontificate; the growth of anti-clericalism and states placing religion "under the power of the state" or treating it as merely one among many; the rebellion of "individuals and states against the authority of Christ" producing "seeds of discord," national rivalries, greed, and family breakdown (as named in §24); the historical suppression of public mention of Christ's name by nations in "conferences and parliaments" (§25).
- **Prescriptive towards:** Annual universal observance of the Feast of the Kingship of Christ; yearly renewal of the dedication of mankind to the Sacred Heart; Catholic laity actively defending Christ's rights in public life and winning "over to their Lord those hearts that are bitter and estranged from him" (§24); rulers and princes governing "by the mandate and in the place of the Divine King" (§19), making just laws and ensuring sound moral education for the young (§32).
- **Tension:** The document names but does not fully resolve the tension between Christ's kingdom being explicitly "spiritual" and "not of this world" (§15) while simultaneously insisting it is "a grave error" to say Christ has no authority in civil affairs (§17). A second tension runs between the Church's claim to complete independence from state power (§31) and the demand that states positively submit to Christ's rule — a demand that implies the Church has a public ordering function the state cannot contain.

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument.** The encyclical opens by reviewing blessings of the 1925 Holy Year — the Missionary Exhibition, canonizations, the Nicaea centenary — as evidence that Christ's kingdom is living and expanding. This celebratory frame sets up the encyclical's central act: the formal institution of the Feast of Christ the King. The argument then moves in three stages: (1) theological demonstration that Christ is king in the "strict and proper sense" (§7–16), drawing on the Psalms, Isaiah, Daniel, Zechariah, and the New Testament alongside patristic authority (Cyril of Alexandria) and conciliar definition (Nicaea); (2) social and political application, contending that this kingship entails obligations on civil rulers and nations, not just individual souls (§17–25); (3) practical prescription — the feast as instrument of social renewal, supported by historical examples of how liturgical feasts have corrected prior defects of faith and devotion (§22–23).

**Theological grounding.** The document is unusually dense in scriptural citation for an encyclical of this era. Pius XI distinguishes between Christ's "metaphorical" title of king (sovereignty over hearts by reason of intellect, truth, and charity) and the "strict and proper sense" of kingship (received as man from the Father, entailing legislative, judicial, and executive power — §14). The hypostatic union is given as the ultimate foundation: because Christ is both divine and human, his dominion is both natural and acquired through redemption. The phrase "You were not redeemed with corruptible things, but with the precious blood of Christ" (§13) anchors the claim that believers are literally Christ's property — "our very bodies are the members of Christ."

**Social diagnosis.** The encyclical names a specific catalogue of social ills it attributes to the rejection of Christ's authority: "seeds of discord," "bitter enmities and rivalries between nations," "insatiable greed," "blind and immoderate selfishness," breakdown of family unity, and "society… shaken to its foundations" (§24). This catalogue is not framed as speculative but as already observed. The document self-references an earlier encyclical (*Ubi Arcano*) where Pius XI lamented the same trends, signalling that *Quas Primas* is presented as a constructive response to a previously diagnosed crisis.

**Tone.** The tone is predominantly authoritative and declaratory rather than pastoral or hortatory. Pius XI speaks as legislator — he "ordains," "institutes," and "commands." Yet the closing sections shift toward the pastoral, expressing hope that even those "without the fold" may accept "the sweet yoke of Christ," and framing the letter as "a token of Our fatherly love" as Christmas approaches. The metaphor of "plague" (for anti-clericalism, §24) and "remedy" (for the feast) gives the document a medical-diagnostic register: society is sick, the feast is the prescription.

**Notable framing.** The encyclical frames liturgical celebration as superior to doctrinal teaching for popular formation: "feasts reach them all; the former speak but once, the latter speak every year — in fact, forever" (§21). This is an explicit theory of how social transformation happens — not primarily through argument but through repetitive, embodied, communal ritual. The historical survey of feasts (Corpus Christi as remedy for eucharistic coldness, the Sacred Heart as remedy for Jansenist severity) positions the new feast in a tradition of liturgical counter-moves to doctrinal or spiritual crises. The Feast of Christ the King is thus presented as the counter-move to secularism and anti-clericalism.
