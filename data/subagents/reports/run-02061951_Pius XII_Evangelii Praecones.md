# Evangelii Praecones

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** On the 25th anniversary of Rerum Ecclesiae, Pius XII surveys the measurable progress of Catholic missions, exhorts their expansion against atheistic materialism and communism, and lays down principles for building native churches, native clergy, Catholic Action, and socially just institutions.
- **Summary:** Evangelii Praecones opens with a statistical review of missionary growth since 1926 (missions, Catholics, priests, seminaries, schools, hospitals), acknowledges the ongoing martyrdom of missionaries and faithful in China and Korea under persecution, and then provides extensive practical guidance across several domains: proper formation of missionaries, the paramount goal of establishing a native clergy and hierarchy so that missions become self-sustaining, the indispensable role of Catholic laity through Catholic Action, the importance of schools and publications in countering communist and heretical influence, and charitable works in medicine and care of the sick. The document closes with an urgent warning that humanity is dividing into two camps — for or against Christ — and calls all Catholics to support missions through prayer, vocations, and material aid.
- **Response to:** The document states it responds to: (1) the 25th anniversary of Pius XI's encyclical Rerum Ecclesiae; (2) the suffering of missionaries, native clergy, nuns, and faithful in the Far East — specifically China and Korea — through persecution, exile, imprisonment, and death; (3) the rapid spread of "atheistic materialism" and communism, which the document frames as an active global threat deceiving "the simple and untutored"; (4) "heretical teaching" threatening missionary work in Latin America; (5) the political reality that newly developing nations may expel foreign missionaries, making native clergy an urgent necessity.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs attention toward: (1) building a self-sustaining native clergy and hierarchy so that "as soon as possible" the mission can "continue to exist and flourish without the aid of missionary organizations"; (2) expanding Catholic Action by forming lay associations of men, women, students, workers, artists, and athletes as auxiliaries of missionaries; (3) establishing more schools, colleges, and publications to counter communist and heretical error and to evangelise educated future leaders; (4) enacting social reform grounded in justice and charity — not charity alone — to protect workers' dignity against both private exploitation and state absolutism; (5) continuing to evangelize the approximately one billion people the document reckons as not yet having received the Gospel.
- **Tension:** Several tensions are named or clearly implied in the text: (a) the vast field of souls to be reached versus the chronic shortage of missionary laborers ("The harvest indeed is great, but the laborers are few"); (b) the foreign missionary's ongoing presence versus the legitimate aspirations of native peoples for self-governance — the document explicitly quotes its predecessor asking what would happen if a "native population raised to a higher degree of culture… wants to drive out all governors, armed forces and missionaries belonging to the occupying foreign power"; (c) the universality of Christian charity transcending "racial conflicts and national frontiers" versus the geopolitical reality of nations divided by opposing interests; (d) the sufficiency of charity versus the prior necessity of justice — "Charity indeed can remedy to a certain extent many unjust social conditions. But that is not enough. For in the first place there must be justice"; (e) inculturation versus evangelization — the Church must "graft a good scion upon the wild stock" rather than destroy native culture, yet must also free native philosophies "from error and all contamination."

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument.** The encyclical moves through three broad phases. The first (§§1–16) is essentially celebratory: a retrospective of measurable missionary growth over 25 years, expressed in concrete numbers (missions, Catholics, priests, schools, hospitals, seminaries) and culminating in an exhortation drawn from John — "Lift up your eyes and see the countries; for they are white already to harvest." This section acknowledges martyrdom in the Far East as the price of progress, and frames the suffering of imprisoned and exiled missionaries in Korea and China with consolation drawn from scripture (the grain of wheat, Paul's glory in tribulations, St. Cyprian on persecution).

The second phase (§§17–54) is directive and programmatic. It identifies the central threat — communism and atheistic materialism — and lays out principles and norms for the missionary apostolate: the missionary's self-abnegation and cultural love for the land he serves; proper training in languages, medicine, agriculture, and ethnography; the ultimate goal of establishing a native church under a native hierarchy; the role of Catholic Action historically (from early Rome through medieval queens and medieval guilds) and today; the utility of schools and publications; charitable works in medicine; and, crucially, social reform. The passage on social reform (§§49–53) is the most explicitly socio-political: it quotes Pius XII's 1942 Christmas address condemning both "exploitation of private capital" and "state absolutism," insisting workers must have the right to private property as a foundation of dignity.

The third phase (§§55–73) addresses institutional coordination: how mission territories are divided among religious institutes, the principle that these institutes hold territories at the will of the Holy See rather than by "private title in perpetuity," the importance of inculturation (the long passage on the Church "grafting" upon native culture rather than destroying it), the role of the Missionary Union of the Clergy and the Pontifical Societies, and a final urgent appeal framing the moment as a "supreme crisis" in which humanity is dividing for or against Christ.

**Tone.** The document is pastoral-exhortatory rather than purely doctrinal. It uses statistics to inspire confidence, scriptural quotations to console and motivate, and historical examples (St. Boniface, medieval queens, early Church laity) to legitimate Catholic Action as ancient rather than novel. The tone shifts to genuine alarm when discussing communism and the Far East, and to near-prophetic gravity in the final sections. The encyclical cites its own predecessors extensively (Benedict XV's Maximum illud, Pius XI's Rerum Ecclesiae, Pius XII's own earlier statements), framing itself as one link in a continuous chain of missionary tradition rather than a break.

**Notable framing.** The inculturation passage (§§56–60) is remarkably generous by the standards of 1951: the Church is explicitly said not to "destroy or extinguish whatever its people possess that is naturally good, just or beautiful." The metaphor of grafting rather than uprooting is developed at length, and St. Basil is quoted on learning from pagan wisdom before receiving faith. Simultaneously, the document is sharply polemical against communism as a doctrine, insisting it reduces "the dignity of the human person almost to zero" by subjecting everything to state control. This double move — cultural openness combined with ideological anti-communism — is the document's most distinctive rhetorical feature, and reflects the Cold War context that the document itself names only partially (it mentions "the Far East" and Korea and China without naming the Soviet Union directly).

The figure of the estimated one billion people still outside the faith serves as both a motivating statistic and a moral urgency: the document returns to it more than once to insist that the crisis is real and the labor insufficient. The "Communion of Saints" is invoked at the close as the theological rationale for why ordinary Catholics supporting missions at home are genuinely participating in the apostolate, not merely donating to a distant cause.
