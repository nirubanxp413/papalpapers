# Inter praeteritos

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document authoritatively resolves longstanding canonical and theological controversies surrounding the conditions, suspended faculties, and dispensations governing the Holy Year Jubilee indulgence, in order to equip confessors and penitentiaries to exercise their sacramental ministry faithfully during the 1750 Anno Santo.
- **Summary:** Benedict XIV addresses the penitentiaries and confessors appointed for the upcoming 1750 Holy Year, systematically working through three of his own Apostolic Constitutions (Peregrinantes, Cum Nos nuper, and Convocatis) and resolving the principal disputed questions these documents were designed to settle. The letter covers: the requirement for actual (not merely intended) sacramental confession and communion; rules for the basilica visits (all four in a single day, definition of that day's boundaries, who counts as a Roman versus a pilgrim); the scope and rationale of the general suspension of indulgences and confessor faculties during the Holy Year; the powers granted to minor penitentiaries over absolution, vow commutation, and dispensations; and a series of further clarifications about who may gain the Jubilee indulgence, what spiritual state is required, and whether the Jubilee may be gained more than once. The Pope situates his own decisions within a wide tradition of canonical scholarship, citing dozens of earlier theologians, and frames his interventions as both clarifying prior papal intent and decisively settling ambiguities left open by his predecessors.
- **Response to:** Disputes and controversies that had arisen among confessors during previous Holy Years, as the text itself recounts; conflicting opinions of canonists and moral theologians recorded in the scholarly literature; the Pope's own promise (made in his Consistorial Allocution of 5 May 1749) to issue clarifying guidance; the proliferation of treatises on the Holy Year that ignored or misread the actual text of papal constitutions; past inconsistencies between different popes' suspension formulas and the interpretive uncertainty these created; requests from bishops for a general constitution addressing the faculty of confessors to absolve accomplices in sins against the sixth commandment.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Confessors and penitentiaries charged with ministry during the 1750 Holy Year, directing them to exercise only the faculties expressly granted them and within the conditions specified; pilgrims and Roman faithful seeking to gain the Jubilee indulgence through proper confession, communion, and basilica visits; the institutional practice of the Roman Church in future Jubilees, as the Pope's resolutions are framed as authoritative precedents intended to prevent controversies from re-opening.
- **Tension:** (1) Between canonical rigor and pastoral accommodation: the Pope repeatedly expresses a strong desire that the basilica visits and confession be made in a state of grace, yet explicitly accommodates the "human fragility" of those who begin visits in a state of mortal sin, provided grace is restored before the final prescribed act. (2) Between the breadth of formulaic language in suspension bulls and the need to delimit exactly which faculties are suspended, generating repeated conflict between those who read general suspension terms broadly and those who restrict them to faculties granted specifically for the occasion of the indulgence. (3) Between the privileges and autonomy of religious orders (Regulars, including exemptions from confession without superiors' permission) and the centralizing authority of the Holy See during the Jubilee. (4) Between the accumulated weight of competing theological opinions among celebrated authors and the Pope's responsibility to resolve those disputes with a single authoritative word, which the text frames throughout as his principal motive for writing.

## Unstructured

**Key themes and flow of argument**

The document is an exercise in practical, applied canonical governance. Its overarching purpose is not doctrinal declaration or exhortation to piety, but the technical clarification of disputed legal questions about how the Holy Year Jubilee indulgence works. The Pope has produced three major Apostolic Constitutions in preparation for the 1750 Holy Year (Peregrinantes, Cum Nos nuper, Convocatis), and this encyclical letter walks through all three in sequence—ten parts—explaining what each provision was intended to mean and why, with extensive reference to the scholarly controversy each provision was designed to settle.

The argument follows a consistent pattern throughout: (a) identify the controversy as it existed in the prior literature; (b) survey the competing opinions and their principal authorities; (c) explain what the Pope's own constitution says or intends; (d) declare the question resolved. This structure is repeated dozens of times across the document's ten parts, covering topics ranging from whether "confession" means actual sacramental confession or merely a "confession in intention," through the exact boundaries of a canonical "day" for purposes of basilica visits, through the scope of the suspension of indulgences and faculties, through the powers of minor penitentiaries to absolve from censures and dispense from vows, through who qualifies as a Roman or a pilgrim, through whether the Jubilee can be gained more than once in the same year.

**Tone**

The tone is predominantly authoritative and juridical, with occasional pastoral warmth (the opening address to "diletti figli," the closing embrace and apostolic blessing). The Pope writes in the first person plural throughout as the supreme legislator of the church, drawing on his personal experience of having witnessed two previous Holy Years and having studied the scholarly literature himself. He frequently positions himself as above the controversy—not following any particular school, but settling disputes by his own interpreted intent and by the text of his own constitutions. The tone is notably pedagogical: the document is explicitly designed so that confessors can understand not just what to do but why, so they will not stray beyond their authority "to the detriment of their own souls and the souls of their penitents."

**Notable framing**

One of the most distinctive features of the document is the Pope's repeated insistence on reading the words of papal constitutions carefully and precisely, as opposed to relying on what earlier authors said about earlier constitutions. He opens by criticizing theologians who resolve controversies by citing predecessors rather than reading the actual text of the constitution at issue. This textual rigor is the core intellectual method of the letter.

The document also frames the Holy Year not as an automatic spiritual benefit but as a carefully conditioned legal transaction: the Jubilee indulgence is granted only to those who fulfill specific works in a specific manner, with specific dispositions. The Pope devotes considerable energy to boundary cases—the dying pilgrim who cannot complete the visits, the sick Roman who cannot leave home, the child who cannot yet receive communion, the sinner who falls back into mortal sin midway through the prescribed visits—and in each case reaches a considered resolution. This focus on edge cases reinforces the juridical character of the document.

The document also reveals institutional complexity within the church's penitential hierarchy: the distinctions between the Cardinal Major Penitentiary, the minor penitentiaries of the four basilicas, confessors appointed by the Cardinal Vicar, regular clergy with their own superiors' faculties, and ordinary confessors with diocesan approval create a layered system of delegated authority that the document maps with care. The suspension of certain faculties during the Holy Year—designed to draw pilgrims to Rome—is shown to be in tension with the practical need to confess and absolve outside Rome, and the document resolves this by drawing precise geographic and categorical distinctions.

Finally, the document closes with a note of modesty: the Pope acknowledges that he has relied mostly on "modern authors" because the controversies were largely raised by modern authors writing about the Holy Year, and invites the confessors to read also his earlier encyclical of 26 June 1749 on preparation for the Holy Year. The closing is pastoral in register—an embrace, a recommendation, and an apostolic blessing—but the body of the text is unmistakably legislative in character.
