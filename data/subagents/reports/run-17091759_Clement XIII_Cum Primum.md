# Cum Primum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Clergy are corrupted by avarice and worldly business dealings, scandalizing the faithful and degrading holy orders, and bishops must enforce longstanding canonical prohibitions against them.
- **Summary:** Clement XIII, responding to widespread complaints from preachers and pastors, addresses the persistent problem of clerics engaging in commerce, money-changing, managing lay estates for pay, and otherwise subordinating their spiritual vocation to worldly profit. He does not invent new law — he emphasises that existing canons and prior papal constitutions already clearly forbid such conduct — but he renews and reaffirms those penalties, warns bishops against being fooled by clever evasions, and instructs them to investigate, discipline, and refer hard cases to the Apostolic See. He closes by urging bishops to exercise their local judgment, as they are best placed to know the particular circumstances of their territories.
- **Response to:** Reports from "many fervent and zealous churchmen" — preachers travelling cities and provinces — who unanimously complained of clerical avarice; observed practices including clergy using intermediaries or false names to disguise business dealings, monks exceeding ecclesiastical moderation in pursuit of temporal gain for their communities, and clergy obtaining ordination through false or forged documents of title.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Bishops are directed to diligently investigate clergy under their jurisdiction; recognise and counter fraudulent evasion strategies; not permit loose or privately-derived interpretations of canon law; refer questions of contract permissibility to the Apostolic See; grant dispensations only on verified need; and ultimately use "the unsheathed sword of penalties and censures" against persistent offenders.
- **Tension:** The fundamental tension is between the sacred character of holy orders — the cleric who "recognizes only God as the portion of their inheritance" — and the actual conduct of clergy who pursue temporal profit, attend markets "with almost the same splendor and elegance as laymen," and hire themselves out in "servile ministries" to lay households. A secondary tension runs between the letter of existing canon law (which already prohibits these things) and the clergy's persistent, sophisticated evasion of it (using intermediaries, false names, relatives as proxies, or pleading "indigence"). A third tension is reputational/social: laity who should look to clergy for "examples of Christian life and aids to eternal salvation" instead observe and endure them as commercial adversaries or servile household staff, leading laypeople to "despise not only those who do such things, but often even the whole priestly company."

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument**

The document opens with a pastoral report — Clement has been receiving consistent testimony since assuming the pontificate that avarice is endemic among clergy. The tone immediately is one of corroborated, actionable complaint rather than speculative concern. He frames greed theologically (quoting or paraphrasing the Holy Spirit's designation of it as "the root of all evil") before describing its practical consequences: negligence in duty, entanglement in worldly affairs, contentious behavior in civil disputes, and public scandal.

Clement's rhetorical move in paragraph 3 is notable: he insists there is nothing new to legislate. Councils, popes, and holy fathers have already legislated this "more plainly" and "more attentively" than almost anything else. His role is not innovator but enforcer. He is therefore not issuing new canons but *renewing and reaffirming* those of Pius IV, Urban VIII, Clement IX, and most recently Benedict XIV (1741). The document is thus explicitly cumulative and retrospective.

Paragraphs 6–8 constitute a practical manual for bishops: a taxonomy of evasion strategies used by clergy (intermediaries, false names, nominal relatives, claiming pure motives for profitable acts), paired with guidance on how to see through them. Clement is candid that in many cases intent cannot be proven legally, but argues that mere vigilance and announced scrutiny will have a deterrent effect, and that God "who scrutinizes the innermost recesses of man will not be mocked."

Paragraph 9 offers a rare specific ruling: money-changing is, without qualification, a "true and proper act of business" and forbidden to all clergy. This is framed as a clarification of an existing uncertainty, not a new prohibition.

Paragraph 13 widens the frame: the document's concern is not only technical violations of business canons but the broader *appearance* of clerical life. Clergy who manage farms, attend markets, and haggle over agricultural produce may not always cross the precise canonical line, but they "lower themselves below men of the lowest status" and erode lay esteem for the entire order. Clement wants bishops to address this softer dimension through instruction, not just canonical prosecution.

Paragraphs 15–17 identify two further species of the same problem: (a) clergy who hire out their labor as household administrators for lay families, and (b) clergy who obtained ordination fraudulently — by forging title documents — and then, lacking a legitimate income, inevitably slide into commercial activities to survive. The latter is identified as a structural root cause, and the remedy is upstream vigilance at ordination.

**Tone**

Predominantly authoritative-legislative: the Pope is issuing binding directives, renewing penalties, and declaring specific acts (money-changing) categorically forbidden. But sections 7, 13, 14, and 18 are pastoral-deliberative in character — he acknowledges the difficulty of proof, the complexity of individual circumstances, and the need for episcopal prudence and local knowledge. The closing lines explicitly devolve significant authority back to the bishops, noting they are better situated than Rome to assess "the needs of the regions, the customs of the people."

**Notable framing**

- Clerical greed is positioned not merely as a personal moral failure but as an *institutional* scandal: it leads laypeople to despise the entire priesthood, not just offenders.
- The document treats legal evasion as its own category of offense — the clergy's sophistication in hiding commerce is evidence of guilt, not innocence.
- The ordination-fraud pipeline (paragraph 16) reveals an awareness that the problem is partly systemic: irregular candidates who cannot sustain themselves legitimately from their title are predictably driven to commerce.
- The phrase "sons of light" who "outdo the sons of this generation in their concern for worldly greed" (paragraph 13) is a pointed inversion of the gospel parable, used to shame clergy by noting they have surpassed secular people in the very vice most unsuited to their state.
