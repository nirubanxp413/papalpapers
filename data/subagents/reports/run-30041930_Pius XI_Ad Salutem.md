# Ad Salutem (Pius XI, 1930)

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** On the occasion of the 1500th anniversary of Augustine's death, Pius XI presents Augustine's life and doctrine as the authoritative template for defending Catholic truth against heresy, grounding political authority in divine order, and resisting the moral and spiritual erosion of modern material progress.
- **Summary:** *Ad Salutem* is a commemorative encyclical marking fifteen centuries since the death of Augustine of Hippo. It proceeds by narrating Augustine's personal conversion, then surveying his major theological contributions (the Trinity, grace and free will, the two cities), and finally applying Augustinian principles to contemporary conditions — specifically the dangers of materialist civilization, permissive moral culture, and unstable relations between Church and civil rulers. The document is simultaneously a doctrinal argument (Augustine as the Church's ultimate witness against Pelagianism, Donatism, and pride-based errors) and a pastoral exhortation to bishops, clergy, and laity to venerate, study, and imitate the saint.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to: (1) the 1500th anniversary of Augustine's death, which has prompted "Christians the world over" to prepare public commemoration; (2) contemporary civilization's absorption in material progress — "when man's inventive genius is bringing under his sway nature's forces and energies," causing the spirit to "grow too forgetful of its Creator"; (3) "certain writers, shallow and even licentious" who "even in our day" extol the "beauty and fitness and attractiveness" of pagan Greek-Roman religion; (4) the "pernicious error" of the eighteenth century — that "the inborn impulses of the will should neither be feared nor curbed" — whose educational consequences Pius XI says he already condemned in his earlier *Encyclical on the Christian Education of Youth*; and (5) ongoing heresy, schism, and what the text calls the persecuting hostility of earthly rulers toward the Church.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) The forthcoming Eucharistic Congress at Carthage, which Pius XI hopes will also honour Augustine's memory and mission; (2) the evangelisation of Africa — the pope closes by praying that Africans, "whether they are as yet ignorant of Catholic truth or are at a variance with Us, may not spurn the light of the Gospel"; (3) the training of future priests — "spare no pains in giving an exemplary training to those who seem to have a vocation to the priesthood"; (4) a revival of religious and priestly vocations ("men arise all the world over, resembling the holy Doctor, many 'sowers of chaste counsel'"); (5) Christian rulers governing in conscious subordination to divine law rather than "idle ambition."
- **Tension:** The document names or clearly implies several persistent tensions: (a) **Grace vs. free will** — the central Augustinian battlefield, with Pelagianism (denying grace's necessity) and early Protestantism/Jansenism (denying free will's reality) marking the two opposing errors; (b) **Heavenly City vs. Earthly City** — the fundamental Augustinian duality, in which the two cities are "intermingled" throughout history but headed toward entirely opposite ends; (c) **Church authority vs. private judgment / heretical independence** — running through the Donatist, Pelagian, and Manichean controversies and the document's insistence on the Roman Pontiff as the "apostolic fountain-head"; (d) **Spiritual end vs. material progress** — the encyclical explicitly names the risk that technological and industrial advancement causes humanity to "make fleeting goods its goal to the neglect of eternal ones"; (e) **Passion vs. virtue** — the concupiscence left by original sin is described as a war within every person: "sin fights against you; only let it not rule in you."

## Unstructured

**Overall argument and flow**

The document has a tripartite movement: *praise and authority* (§§1–6), *life and doctrine* (§§7–35), *application and exhortation* (§§36–52). The opening sections establish that every major pope from Innocent I onward has endorsed Augustine, making him not just a great individual but effectively the Church's own continuing voice. This is a structural move: by the time Pius XI turns to Augustine's teachings on grace, the two cities, or Church-State relations, Augustine's words already carry near-magisterial weight.

**Augustine's conversion as doctrinal argument**

The account of Augustine's wandering (Manichean sect, carnal pleasures, Platonic philosophy) and eventual conversion is not merely biographical. The pope uses it to demonstrate that: (a) the human will is genuinely weak without grace; (b) Monica's persistent tears exemplify the power of intercessory prayer; (c) the Epistles of Paul — especially the passage "not in rioting and drunkenness... but put ye on the Lord Jesus Christ" — are the instrument of decisive conversion. The conversion narrative is essentially a compressed proof of Augustinian theology.

**Materialism as the contemporary analogue to ancient error**

Paragraph 12 is unusually direct about present conditions. The Pope writes that "today... our spirit, absorbed in creatures, grows too forgetful of its Creator, makes fleeting goods its goal to the neglect of eternal ones." The speed at which "creations of art and industry... are carried to every corner of the earth" is named as the mechanism of this spiritual forgetfulness. Augustine's maxim — "Thou hast created us for Thyself, and our heart is restless till it rest in Thee" — is invoked as the antidote, but the framing implies that the encyclical's deepest contemporary worry is not a named heresy but rather the diffuse, structural effect of industrial modernity on spiritual orientation.

**The two cities as philosophy of history**

The *De Civitate Dei* section (§27) draws the sharpest political edge. The document explicitly rejects fortune, ambition, and "ceaseless efforts of minds and hearts to develop natural forces" as the engine of history, insisting instead that "human events serve only to extend the City of God." The "earthly City" is not merely pagan antiquity — the text implies that any political or cultural project that ends in "empty and fleeting glory" is Babylonian in structure. The description of the ideal Christian emperor (§28) — slow to punish, quick to forgive, governing "not at the bidding of idle ambition, but of love of eternal happiness" — reads as a direct countermodel to rulers who "hamper the action of the Church."

**Moral concerns: the body and concupiscence**

Paragraph 32 is the passage most explicitly aimed at contemporary culture. From the Pelagian-derived error that human impulses need no curbing, the pope traces a chain: permissive educational methods → free mingling of the sexes → uncontrolled adolescent passions → licentious writing, reading, and theatre → "immodest fashions of dress, which Christian women can never be at too great pains to abolish." The argument is genealogical: a theological error (underestimating original sin) generates identifiable social and cultural symptoms. Augustine's account of concupiscence is the diagnostic framework.

**Tone**

The document is substantially *authoritative* rather than pastoral, though it moves between registers. When quoting Augustine directly — especially the Confessions passages on Monica, on the window at Ostia, on "too late have I loved Thee" — it becomes affective and contemplative. When addressing heresy, Church-State conflict, or contemporary moral decline, it becomes legislative. The closing sections (§§49–52), calling for vocations and African missions and commending the Carthage Eucharistic Congress, shift to hopeful exhortation. The Pope explicitly identifies himself with the subject: "We have... commemorate[d] the Saint... as We have most gladly" done — the joy is personalised.

**Notable framing choices**

- Augustine is consistently framed through the lens of his reception by subsequent popes, making him simultaneously historical figure and living institutional instrument.
- The document carefully quarantines Augustine's authority within Church teaching: "the lavish praises... are to be understood in a proper sense, and not... as though the weight of Augustine's word were to be set ahead of the very authority of the teaching Church" (§6). This prevents any Protestant or Jansenist appropriation of the saint.
- The phrase "Rome has spoken, the cause is finished" (*Roma locuta, causa finita est*) is both cited and acknowledged as a condensed popular proverb of Augustine's original words — Pius XI uses this to underline the papal dimension of the Augustinian legacy.
- Newman's conversion is mentioned in a footnote (§15, fn. 25) as a contemporary instance of the persuasive force of Augustine's maxim that "the decision is sure in which the world concurs" — a rare reference to a modern figure, inserted lightly but deliberately.
