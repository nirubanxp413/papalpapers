# In suprema Catholicae

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Amid Europe-wide wars, natural disasters, and pestilence—interpreted as signs of divine wrath—Pope Benedict XIV grants a plenary (Jubilee-level) indulgence to Christians in Rome and throughout Italy as an instrument of collective penitence, calling the faithful back to prayer, fasting, confession, communion, and almsgiving.
- **Summary:** The brief opens with the Pope's admission that his hope for a peaceful pontificate—built on Christian unity among princes—has been shattered by escalating catastrophe: kingdoms fighting kingdoms, earthquakes, floods, plague, famine, and cities emptied of inhabitants. Echoing Gregory the Great, he reads these disasters as "evident signs of divine indignation." In response, he decrees a solemn procession (from Sant'Andrea della Valle to Santa Maria sopra Minerva) and grants a full Jubilee indulgence to those across Rome and Italy who, over two designated weeks, visit appointed churches, fast on Wednesdays, Fridays, and Saturdays, confess, receive the Eucharist, and give alms to the poor. He extends provisions for travellers, the imprisoned, and the infirm through confessors authorised to commute conditions. He also grants confessors the faculty to absolve from otherwise reserved sins and censures, and explicitly overrides any conflicting apostolic privileges or religious-order statutes.
- **Response to:** Wars among Christian kingdoms throughout Europe; earthquakes, floods, pestilence, and famine devastating various regions (explicitly mentioned as already having occurred); the spiritual condition of collective sin understood as the cause of these calamities; the Pope's own personal sense of being overwhelmed ("quasi sommergono") by the gravity of his office.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Active participation in a specified public procession; individual fulfilment of penitential conditions (church visits, fasting, sacramental confession, reception of the Eucharist, almsgiving); universal publication of the brief by bishops and parish clergy throughout Italy and adjacent islands; use of authorised confessors for those unable to fulfil the standard conditions; prayer for the cessation of war and the return of peace.
- **Tension:** The document names a core contradiction: all warring parties "bear the name of Christians" yet wage war against one another—Christian identity and Christian conduct are in open conflict. A second tension runs between divine justice (the calamities as punitive "arm of avenging justice") and divine mercy (the indulgence as the Pope's appeal to God's remembered mercy). A third, more structural tension concerns authority: the brief explicitly overrides existing privileges and statutes of religious orders and other apostolic concessions, asserting papal sovereignty over all competing ecclesiastical jurisdictions in the grant of absolution.

## Unstructured

The document moves in two distinct registers that are deliberately juxtaposed. The first section (unnumbered preamble through paragraph 1) is almost elegiac in tone: Benedict describes, with accumulating grief, a world in collapse—wars, earthquakes, floods, plague, famine, empty cities, unburied dead, and blood "poured like water." The rhetorical climax invokes Gregory the Great's lament about a dying world, lending prophetic gravity to the crisis. This is not background scene-setting but the theological foundation: the calamities justify—and necessitate—the extraordinary spiritual remedy that follows.

The second register (paragraphs 2–8) is juridical and meticulously procedural. The document specifies exact liturgical routes, named churches, precise days and weeks, categories of persons (Romans, Italians, travellers, the imprisoned, the sick, religious, laity), the scope of absolution (including sins normally reserved to the Holy See and those read out in the Holy Thursday letters), the handling of vow commutation, and the conditions for authentication of the document's copies. The contrast between the lament and the legal architecture is intentional: the indulgence is framed as a precisely calibrated instrument of divine mercy deployed against a well-characterised spiritual emergency.

The argument's flow is: (1) the world is suffering catastrophically; (2) this is interpreted as divine punishment for sin; (3) God's mercy can be invoked through penitence; (4) the Pope, as the custodian of the "treasury of heavenly favours," opens this treasury through a structured, time-limited grant; (5) all ecclesiastical intermediaries are obligated to transmit and publicise the grant without delay.

Tone is simultaneously authoritative and pastoral. The authoritative register is clearest in the command language of paragraph 5 ("strictly order and command… in virtue of holy obedience") and the sweeping derogation clause of paragraph 7. The pastoral register is present in the Pope's personal voice of distress at the opening and in the practical accommodations made for those who cannot fulfil standard conditions (the imprisoned, the ill, travellers).

A notable framing choice: the document consistently attributes the calamities not to political or natural causes but to collective sin and divine anger, positioning the Pope's response not as political mediation between warring Christian princes but as spiritual triage—bypassing the political dimension entirely in favour of a sacramental solution. The indulgence thus functions as both a spiritual remedy and an implicit critique: if Christian kingdoms are fighting each other, the appropriate response is not diplomacy but conversion.
