# In Multiplicibus Curis

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document appeals for peace, justice, and international protection of the Holy Places in Palestine amid active armed conflict, grounding the plea in both spiritual devotion to the sacred land and urgent humanitarian concern for its victims.
- **Summary:** Writing in October 1948, Pius XII addresses the ongoing war in Palestine with deep personal sorrow, describing the continued bloodshed, displacement of thousands of refugees, and threat to sacred Christian sites. He recounts the Holy See's efforts — diplomatic appeals before the war, humanitarian aid through representatives in Palestine, Lebanon, and Egypt, and organized Catholic relief in various countries — and calls for prayer as the primary recourse where human means fall short. He advocates for an internationally guaranteed special status for Jerusalem and free access to Holy Places throughout Palestine, urging the Christian world not to contemplate these events with indifference.
- **Response to:** The armed conflict in Palestine (declared war), destruction and damage to sacred buildings and charitable institutions near the Holy Places, displacement of thousands of refugees, and appeals received daily by the Holy See from those affected by the war.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The establishment of an international character for Jerusalem and its outskirts; international guarantees for free access to Holy Places throughout Palestine and freedom of worship; organized Catholic charitable efforts in various countries; continued prayer by bishops and the faithful under the auspices of the Blessed Virgin (as invited in the prior encyclical *Auspicia Quaedam*); and the resumption of Christian pilgrimages to the Holy Land.
- **Tension:** The document names the tension between the ongoing violence and the sanctity of a land held sacred by Christians — "beneath the skies which echoed on that fateful night with the Gospel tidings of peace, men continue to fight." There is also a tension between the Holy See's obligation of apostolic impartiality ("above the conflicts which agitate human society") and its active advocacy for justice, peace, and protection of specific sites. A further tension exists between human inadequacy ("insufficiency of human means") and the faith that prayer and international cooperation can achieve a just resolution.

## Unstructured

*In Multiplicibus Curis* is a brief but emotionally weighted document — more a pastoral letter and public appeal than a doctrinal encyclical. Its tone is simultaneously grieving and active: the Pope opens by locating the Palestine conflict among "multiple preoccupations" that weigh on him, then quickly personalizes his sorrow around the symbolic power of the land where Christ "shed His blood to bring redemption."

The argument follows a loose chronological logic: (1) the Pope feels sorrow; (2) he has not remained passive — he acted before the war, during the war, and through humanitarian channels; (3) recognizing human limits, he turned to prayer; (4) he now calls on bishops and the faithful to join in prayer; (5) he articulates a concrete political outcome he hopes prayers will support — international status for Jerusalem and guaranteed access to Holy Places.

The framing is distinctly spiritual-geopolitical. The Holy Places are not merely historical sites but living centers of Christian devotion: pilgrims "approached with the deepest respect to kiss with most ardent love." Their threatened destruction is presented as a wound to the entire "great human family." Yet the document also names specific political mechanisms: international guarantees, free access, respect for religious customs of "each of the parties at present in conflict" — language that gestures toward neutrality while clearly prioritizing the preservation of Christian sacred sites.

The tone is pastoral but carries an implicit urgency: the Pope says "We do not believe that the Christian world could contemplate indifferently" the situation — a gentle rebuke folded into a call to action. The reference to his earlier encyclical *Auspicia Quaedam* and the address to the Sacred College of Cardinals on June 2nd gives the document an intertextual context within an ongoing campaign of papal concern.

Notable is the document's brevity and restraint: it does not name political parties, does not assign blame for the conflict, and does not specify which "parties at present in conflict" are meant — consistent with the stated posture of impartiality. The focus remains on two concrete goods: the welfare of refugees and the protection of sacred sites, both framed ultimately as matters of justice and peace rather than partisan interest.
