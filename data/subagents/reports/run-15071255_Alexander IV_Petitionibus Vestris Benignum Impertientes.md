# Petitionibus Vestris Benignum Impertientes

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Social
- **Theme (one sentence):** Pope Alexander IV formally grants the University of Salamanca the right to possess and freely use a common seal, responding to a petition made by the university's masters, rectors, and scholars.
- **Summary:** This is a brief administrative papal bull issued at Anagni on 15 July 1255, the first year of Alexander IV's pontificate. In it, the pope benignly assents to the petition of the University of Salamanca — addressed as "dilectis filiis universitati magistrorum, rectorum et scholarium Salamantin." — conferring on them, by apostolic authority, free faculty to have and use a common seal ("commune sigillum"). The document closes with a standard warning formula threatening divine indignation from God and the apostles Peter and Paul against anyone who would dare infringe or rashly oppose this concession.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly states it responds to a petition ("petitionibus vestris") made by the university community — masters, rectors, and scholars — requesting the right to a common seal. No other antecedent events or concerns are named in the text.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The grant directs the University of Salamanca to freely exercise the right to use a common seal ("eo uti libere valeatis"). It also prescribes that no person shall infringe upon this concession, under pain of divine indignation.
- **Tension:** Not stated in text beyond the standard legal/protective formula warning against any who might "infringere vel ei ausu temerario contraire" (infringe or rashly oppose the concession), which implies a latent concern that the grant could be challenged or disputed by unnamed parties.

## Unstructured

The document is exceedingly short — a single-sentence grant sandwiched between an opening address and a closing curse formula. Its tone is purely administrative and authoritative: there is no pastoral dimension, no doctrinal argument, and no rhetorical elaboration. The pope simply receives a petition and grants it by apostolic authority.

The central act is the conferral of the right to a "commune sigillum" (common seal). In the medieval institutional context the text itself implies, a common seal is the marker of a legally recognised corporate entity capable of acting collectively — but the document does not explain this significance; it merely grants the faculty "libere" (freely).

The address formula ("dilectis filiis… salutem et apostolicam benedictionem") establishes the standard hierarchical framing of papal to subordinate, with the affectionate "beloved sons" register. The phrase "benignum impertientes assensum" (benignly granting assent) characterises the pope's disposition as gracious and receptive rather than initiating.

The closing sanction is formulaic but structurally important: it universalises the prohibition ("nulli ergo omnium hominum") and invokes divine authority (God Almighty and the apostles Peter and Paul), elevating a bureaucratic grant to a spiritually protected decree. This is the only moment the spiritual register enters the text, and it functions as legal enforcement rather than theological reflection.

Overall, the document's argument has no real flow — it is a performative speech act: petition received, faculty granted, opposition forbidden. The text offers almost no interpretive content beyond the bare institutional fact of the seal grant, and the source note confirms it is an original parchment bull preserved in multiple manuscript copies, underscoring its function as a durable legal instrument.
