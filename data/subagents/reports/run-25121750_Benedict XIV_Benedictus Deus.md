# Benedictus Deus

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document formally extends the Holy Year Jubilee indulgence beyond Rome to all faithful Catholics throughout the world, prescribing specific acts of piety in their local dioceses as equivalent substitutes for Roman pilgrimage.
- **Summary:** Opening with a joyful reflection on the great success of the Jubilee Year of 1750 in Rome — where pilgrims of every language, people, nation, age, and sex converged to purify their souls, receive the sacraments, and pray for the Church — Benedict XIV formally extends the Jubilee Indulgence to all faithful Christians worldwide. Within six months of the document's publication in each diocese, Catholics must visit their cathedral and three other local churches on fifteen days, receive confession and communion, and pray for the exaltation of the Church, the eradication of heresies, concord among Catholic princes, and the salvation of the Christian people. Extensive provisions accommodate sailors, travellers, cloistered religious, the sick, prisoners, and children; confessors are granted extraordinary powers of absolution and dispensation; and bishops, clergy, and Catholic princes are exhorted to promote the jubilee energetically in their territories.
- **Response to:** The conclusion of the Holy Year of 1750 celebrated in Rome, during which countless faithful arrived from every nation demonstrating "the fervour of their faith and the greatness of the Kingdom of God"; expressed desire from the Emperor, Kings, and Catholic princes that the jubilee be extended to their kingdoms and domains.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Future conduct of all Catholics worldwide: performing specific church visits, sacramental confession and communion, and prescribed prayers within six months; the pastoral responsibility of bishops to promulgate the bull, prepare the faithful, and adapt its requirements to local circumstances; the duty of Catholic princes to support the bishops and foster obedience and piety among their subjects.
- **Tension:** A fundamental spiritual-practical tension runs through the document: the Jubilee Indulgence was originally bound to physical pilgrimage to Rome and its four basilicas, yet the document must make that grace spiritually accessible to those who cannot travel — the sick, imprisoned, cloistered, young, and geographically distant — without diluting the fervour and authenticity of the original observance. A secondary tension exists between the special privileges and constitutions of religious orders (including the exemption from external confession), which the bull explicitly overrides "for this occasion only," and the Church's general discipline.

## Unstructured

**Document type and tone:** This is a papal bull (bolla), not an encyclical in the modern sense. Its tone is simultaneously exultant, pastoral, and juridical. The opening paragraphs are warmly celebratory — Benedict speaks in the first person with evident personal emotion ("quante volte Ci siamo commossi"), recalling the sounds of devout prayer filling the streets, hills, and piazzas of Rome. The remainder of the document shifts into precise canonical language, specifying conditions, exceptions, and dispensations with legal exactitude. The contrast between the personal, pastoral voice of the opening and the technical, legislative structure of the body is striking.

**Flow of the argument:** The document moves through three broad phases. First (opening — §1), a thanksgiving narrative: the Jubilee Year was a visible outpouring of universal Catholic faith, and this itself becomes the warrant for further petition and grace. Second (§2–§6), the juridical core: the extension of the indulgence with specific conditions, detailed accommodations for those unable to fulfil the standard rite, extraordinary faculties granted to confessors, and provisions for those who begin but cannot complete the prescribed works. Third (§7–§11), a pastoral-political exhortation: bishops are urged to act urgently and in unity with Rome; Catholic princes are called to facilitate the bishops; the bull's authority over all contrary constitutions and privileges is asserted; and the document closes with a formal anathema against anyone who would tamper with it.

**Key themes:**

1. *The Jubilee as universal Catholic communion*: Rome's Jubilee is not merely a local Roman event but a manifestation of the universal Church; pilgrims arrived "of every language, people, nation, social class, age, and sex." The extension of the indulgence draws all Catholics into this communion regardless of proximity to Rome.

2. *Penitential purification as the heart of the Jubilee*: The document consistently links the indulgence to genuine contrition, confession, and communion. The faithful are not merely performing external acts — they must "provide for the purification of their souls" (§ opening). Those who sincerely intend to gain the jubilee but cannot complete all required works are still included "if truly penitent, confessed, and communed" (§6).

3. *Hierarchical mediation and pastoral responsibility*: The document places enormous weight on the bishops as mediators of grace. Benedict does not simply declare the indulgence available; he commissions the bishops to make it available, adapt conditions locally, and ensure that the faithful receive proper preparation. The unity of local churches with Rome is made visible through this act of extension.

4. *Prayer for the Church's external enemies and internal unity*: The prayers required as part of the jubilee are not private petitions but ecclesially directed — for "the exaltation of Holy Mother Church, the extirpation of heresies, the concord of Catholic princes, and the salvation and tranquillity of the Christian people." The Jubilee is thus both an internal act of purification and an intercessory movement oriented toward the Church's mission in the world.

5. *Accommodation and universality*: A notable feature is the document's extraordinary care to include those on the margins of standard sacramental practice — sailors, travellers, cloistered nuns, prisoners, the sick, and unbaptised or uncommuned children. This pastoral inclusiveness is not merely juridical convenience; it reflects the document's insistence that the grace of the Jubilee is truly universal ("to all faithful followers of Christ of both sexes, wherever in the world they live").

**Notable framing:** The Catholic princes appear not merely as political figures but as stakeholders in the spiritual welfare of their peoples: Benedict argues that facilitating the jubilee will strengthen their subjects' sincere obedience toward them and bring down divine help, peace, and prosperity upon the princes themselves (§8). This is a distinctly pragmatic-spiritual appeal that links ecclesial grace to political order.
