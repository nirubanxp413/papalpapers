# Instruction on the application of the Apostolic Letter Summorum Pontificum of His Holiness Pope Benedict XVI given Motu Proprio (April 30, 2011)

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Continuity without rupture — the ancient and the reformed Roman liturgy are "two usages of the one Roman Rite" that must coexist peacefully, and this Instruction exists to make that coexistence practically operative.
- **Summary:** Issued by the Pontifical Commission *Ecclesia Dei* four years after *Summorum Pontificum* (2007), this Instruction translates the Motu Proprio into binding canonical norms. It defines who may celebrate the *forma extraordinaria* (the 1962 Missal), under what conditions groups of faithful may request it, what preparation priests must have, and how diocesan bishops and the Commission are to resolve disputes. Theologically, the document insists there is no contradiction or rupture between the two forms of the Roman Rite, anchoring this in the ancient principle *lex orandi, lex credendi* — the Church's rule of prayer corresponds to its rule of belief. Practically, it aims to guarantee access to the older form for those who sincerely desire it while preserving the legitimacy of the ordinary form and the authority of the Roman Pontiff.
- **Response to:** (1) The "lacuna" left when Paul VI introduced the new Missal in 1970 without norms governing continued use of the 1962 Liturgy. (2) The growing number of faithful requesting the *forma extraordinaria*, making new norms "necessary." (3) The need for "proper interpretation and correct application" of *Summorum Pontificum* itself, following inquiry among bishops worldwide. (4) A history stretching back through John Paul II's 1984 indult *Quattuor abhinc annos* and his 1988 Motu Proprio *Ecclesia Dei*, which the document places in continuity with Benedict's policy.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) Diocesan bishops, who are directed to "undertake all necessary measures to ensure respect for the *forma extraordinaria*" and to handle disputes in keeping with the mind of the Holy Father. (2) Seminary formation: "future priests should be given proper formation, including study of Latin and, where pastoral needs suggest it, the opportunity to learn the *forma extraordinaria*." (3) Reconciliation within the Church — the document names this explicitly as one of the Motu Proprio's aims: "promoting reconciliation at the heart of the Church." (4) Faithful themselves, who are barred from requesting the old form if they "support or belong to groups which show themselves to be against the validity or legitimacy of the Holy Mass or the Sacraments celebrated in the *forma ordinaria* or against the Roman Pontiff."
- **Tension:** The text names or clearly implies three related tensions: (a) *Legitimacy*: some groups apparently dispute the validity of the Mass in the ordinary form — the document directly addresses this as a disqualifying condition for accessing the old form. (b) *Continuity vs. rupture*: the document quotes Benedict XVI's insistence that "there is no contradiction between the two editions of the Roman Missal" and that "what was sacred for prior generations, remains sacred and great for us as well, and cannot be suddenly prohibited altogether or even judged harmful" — language that implicitly acknowledges that some have framed the post-conciliar reform as a break. (c) *Access vs. episcopal gatekeeping*: the document acknowledges that the *Summorum Pontificum* was partly needed because bishops had not always been "generous" in granting the older rite to those who requested it; the Instruction establishes the Commission as a hierarchical superior to which the faithful may appeal if an Ordinary acts contrary to the Motu Proprio.

## Unstructured

**Nature of the document.** This is not an encyclical or pastoral letter but a canonical *Instruction* — a juridical implementing act issued under canon 34 of the Code of Canon Law. Its tone is accordingly administrative and precise rather than pastoral or theological in any expansive sense. Almost every paragraph resolves a practical question: who qualifies as a *coetus fidelium*? what Latin proficiency is required of a priest? does the old Confirmation formula still apply? This procedural texture is itself significant: it signals that the theological argument had already been made (in *Summorum Pontificum* and Benedict's accompanying letter to bishops), and what remained was the mechanics of enforcement.

**Flow of the argument.** The Introduction (§§1–8) is the only genuinely theological section. It lays out the doctrinal warrant in three moves: (1) the *lex orandi / lex credendi* principle establishes that liturgical form is inseparable from doctrinal transmission; (2) the history of the Missal from Gregory the Great through John XXIII, Paul VI, and John Paul II establishes continuity of custodianship; (3) Benedict's own framing — "growth and progress, but not rupture" — reframes the 1970 reform not as a replacement but as a parallel development. Having established that both forms are equally legitimate expressions of the one Roman Rite (§6), the rest of the document is simply consequence: if both forms are legitimate, access to the older one must be guaranteed, disputes must be adjudicated, and clerics must be trained.

**Key framing moves.** The document deploys the phrase "precious treasure to be preserved" (§8a) for the *Usus Antiquior*, a framing that positions attachment to the old rite not as nostalgia or resistance but as proper stewardship. Similarly, the *forma extraordinaria* is never framed as a concession to a faction but as something the Church "generously grants" to faithful who are its "principal addressees" (§8b) — language that shifts the burden of proof onto bishops who might withhold it, rather than onto faithful who request it. 

**The reconciliation aim.** Among the three stated aims of *Summorum Pontificum* (§8), "promoting reconciliation at the heart of the Church" is the most politically charged. The document does not elaborate on what division it is healing, but the context of §19 (disqualifying those attached to groups that reject the validity of the ordinary form or the authority of the Pope) makes clear the reference: communities and priests associated with the SSPX or similar traditionalist groups whose break with Rome centred on the liturgical reform. The Instruction attempts a double move — affirming the old rite as fully legitimate while insisting its use cannot become a platform for rejecting the ordinary form or papal authority.

**Tone and authority register.** The Instruction oscillates between two registers: it speaks in the imperative voice of canon law ("is to permit," "are obliged," "must not") while repeatedly framing obligations in pastoral language ("pastoral zeal," "spirit of generous welcome," "good of souls"). Bishops are told to ensure the old form is respected but also that they should approach disputes with the mind of the Holy Father. Priests are presumed qualified if they "present themselves spontaneously" — a notably permissive standard. This blend of juridical firmness and pastoral flexibility reflects the document's underlying purpose: to move access to the old rite from the realm of episcopal discretion (which had been uneven) into the realm of enforceable right, while leaving room for local pastoral judgment on scheduling and logistics.

**What the document does not address.** The Instruction is silent on the theological significance of the differences between the two forms — it does not discuss the theological content of the 1962 Missal versus the Paul VI Missal in any depth, nor does it engage with the debates over the offertory, the orientation of celebration, or the sacramental theology of the prayers. These are conspicuously bracketed; the document's strategy is to assert formal equivalence ("two usages of the one Roman Rite") without adjudicating the substantive differences. This is consistent with the document's genre as a canonical Instruction rather than a theological treatise.
