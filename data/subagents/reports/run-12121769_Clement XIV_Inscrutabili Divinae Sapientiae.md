# Inscrutabili Divinae Sapientiae

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** On the occasion of his papal inauguration, Clement XIV grants a plenary indulgence to all the faithful who fulfill prescribed penitential requirements, following the ancient custom of his predecessors.
- **Summary:** This brief encyclical is primarily a juridical-sacramental proclamation issued at the start of Clement XIV's pontificate. It opens with a humble acknowledgment of the awesome gravity of the papal office and a plea for divine wisdom and strength, then transitions directly into the announcement of a plenary indulgence — offered to all faithful worldwide who visit designated churches, fast on prescribed days, make confession, receive communion, and give alms. Special provisions are made for the cloistered, infirm, imprisoned, and those travelling by sea or land. The document concludes by carefully delimiting what the indulgence does not cover (irregularities, ongoing excommunications without reconciliation) and invoking the intercession of Saints Peter and Paul.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to the occasion of a new pontificate, framing the indulgence as adherence to "the ancient custom of Our predecessors" and as flowing from Clement's elevation to the papacy "by the inscrutable counsel of divine wisdom." The opening prayer also implicitly acknowledges the weight and fear that accompanies assuming supreme pastoral responsibility.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document prescribes specific penitential works for the faithful to perform within a defined window of fifteen continuous days or two weeks, with the hope that this period may fall during Lent. It directs ordinaries, vicars, and confessors in how to administer the indulgence and commute requirements for those unable to fulfill all conditions. It also instructs that printed copies carry full authority when certified by notaries and sealed by persons of ecclesiastical dignity.
- **Tension:** The document holds in tension the breadth of sacramental mercy — absolution from "all sins, excesses, crimes, and delicts, however grave or enormous, even those reserved… to the Apostolic See" — against firm limits on papal dispensation: explicitly excluded are dispensations from irregularities, infamy, or defects, and the indulgence is withheld from any excommunicated person who has not made satisfaction and reconciled with the injured parties.

## Unstructured

This is a formulaic inaugural encyclical whose primary function is legal-canonical rather than doctrinal or exhortatory. Its tone shifts in two distinct phases: the opening paragraph is genuinely personal and pastoral, marked by self-described fear and acknowledged weakness ("Our awareness of the weakness of Our own resources deeply disturbs Us"), relying entirely on divine grace and the intercession of "all Christians." The remaining paragraphs, however, are dense canonical prose — conditional clauses, lists of qualifications, and careful legal exclusions — that read as administrative instruments rather than spiritual reflections.

The document's theological content is largely implicit: indulgences presuppose the treasury of merit, the authority of the keys, the penitential system, and the intercessory role of the Church. None of these are argued or explained; they are assumed as settled ecclesiology and used as the scaffolding for the juridical proclamation.

Notably, the indulgence is deliberately inclusive in its reach — it covers "each and all of the faithful of both sexes," including those in perpetual cloister, those imprisoned, the bodily infirm, and those at sea or on land journeys. This pastoral sensitivity to practical obstacles coexists with careful restrictions that reveal the boundaries of the apostolic power being exercised: the document does not claim to fix infamy, restore competency, or shelter the unreconciled.

The invocation of "the ancient custom of Our predecessors" is the only argument given for the grant itself — authority here rests entirely on tradition and office, not on any particular spiritual or social crisis. This makes it a document almost entirely about the sacramental-institutional life of the Church, with social or political concerns entirely absent from the text.
