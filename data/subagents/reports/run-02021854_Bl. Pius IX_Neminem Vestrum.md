# Neminem Vestrum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Pius IX calls the Armenian Catholic faithful and clergy of Constantinople to abandon internal dissensions, submit to episcopal authority and the Holy See, and pursue peace — while clarifying the correct attitude toward schismatic Armenians.
- **Summary:** Writing to the Armenian Catholic nation in the ecclesiastical province of Constantinople, Pius IX recounts the long history of papal care for the Armenians, then turns to the present crisis: harmful dissensions have taken root, fuelled by polemical published writings. He describes the remedial steps already taken — prohibiting divisive texts, conducting special congregational reviews, establishing five new bishops, and receiving a profession of Catholic faith from the Mechitharist monks of Venice. He then issues a series of exhortations addressed in turn to bishops, priests, the laity, the nobility, and the whole nation, all centring on unity of spirit, obedience to pastors, and the pursuit of peace. He closes by silencing all past controversies, forbidding further polemics, and expressing hope that Armenian schismatics will one day freely return to Catholic unity.
- **Response to:** Internal discord and published polemical writings ("harsh words and pronouncements totally inconsistent with Christian love") dividing factions within the Armenian Catholic community; the condemned "Connational Society"; disputes over doctrine and discipline of the Mechitharist monks; confusion about whether to treat schismatics harshly or leniently; questions about practices distinguishing Armenian Catholics from schismatics.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Lasting harmony and peace among all orders of the Armenian Catholic nation; diligent observance of decrees from the Congregation for the Propagation of the Faith; proper clerical formation in the seminary of Constantinople; faithful obedience of clergy and laity to their bishops; the eventual voluntary return of schismatic Armenians to Catholic unity through persuasion and love rather than coercion.
- **Tension:** Unity vs. discord — the text repeatedly names "dissensions," "contentions," "discord," and "rivalries" as the central threat to the Armenian Catholic community, set against the repeated injunction to "harmony and peace." A second tension runs between zeal for Catholic reunion with schismatics and the temptation to pursue that goal "with harshness and severity" rather than "love and good will." A third tension exists between preserving ancient Eastern rites (which the Holy See values) and eliminating practices introduced precisely to distinguish Catholic Armenians from schismatics — with some parties wanting to erase those distinctions prematurely.

## Unstructured

The document is structured as a comprehensive pastoral letter to a specific national church in crisis. Its rhetorical movement is roughly: historical gratitude → present lament → remedial action taken → layered exhortations → doctrinal clarification → closing mandate for silence on past controversies.

**Tone:** The letter is predominantly authoritative but tempered throughout with an insistent pastoral warmth. Pius IX frames his interventions as expressions of "paternal affection" and "zealous love" for Armenia, and every directive is wrapped in the language of care for souls. The phrase "venerable brothers and dearly beloved sons" recurs as a structural marker, signalling that the pope is simultaneously exercising jurisdiction and expressing affection. There is a notable firmness in the closing paragraphs, where the pope imposes "perpetual and absolute silence on past questions" and warns that future disturbers "will not in any way be able to excuse themselves" — but even here the tone is presented as reluctant severity, not triumphalism.

**Flow of argument:** The first two sections establish historical continuity: the Holy See has always cared for Armenian Catholics, and recent structural improvements (the archiepiscopal primatial see, five new bishops) are fruits of that ongoing commitment. Section 3 introduces the rupture: despite these blessings, "harmful dissensions" have grown. Sections 4–5 describe the curative measures taken (prohibition of writings, examination by cardinals, reception of the Mechitharist profession of faith). Sections 6–12 pivot to exhortation, addressed sequentially to bishops, priests, laity, and nobility — each address calibrated to the responsibilities of that group. Sections 13–15 are the doctrinal centrepiece: the pope distinguishes between (a) those who use harshness against schismatics (disapproved) and (b) those who, under the pretext of promoting unity, want to abolish the canonical practices and disciplinary rules that mark Catholic distinctiveness from schism (also disapproved). This double rejection is characteristic of Pius IX's style: he refuses both extremes and insists on the Church's own middle path. Section 16 closes with the silence decree and apostolic blessing.

**Notable framing:** The document makes repeated and pointed use of scripture to reinforce its calls for unity — Matthew 12:25 ("every city or house divided against itself will not stand"), Galatians 5:15 (biting and tearing leading to mutual destruction), 1 Peter 5:2–3 (shepherds as examples, not lords). The citations from St. Leo the Great and St. Gregory XVI, attributed to predecessor popes on the same theme of Armenian harmony, rhetorically embed the current letter within a tradition of continuous papal solicitude. The Mechitharist monks' profession of faith is quoted at length and approvingly — this inclusion functions both as doctrinal reassurance and as a model of the submission the pope hopes to see from all.

**Key implicit concern:** Though the document does not name any individual faction or political force in detail, the cumulative weight of its prohibitions (against divisive writings, against the "Connational Society," against premature elimination of practices distinguishing Catholics from schismatics) suggests an underlying anxiety about movements within the Armenian Catholic community that would either politicise or theologically dilute Catholic identity. The insistence that episcopal and papal authority "can never be subject to civil powers" (section 12) introduces a structural concern — the potential interference of Ottoman imperial or secular Armenian political forces in Church governance — though the text treats this as a background warning rather than the primary subject.
