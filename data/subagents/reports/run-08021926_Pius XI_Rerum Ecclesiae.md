# Rerum Ecclesiae

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Church must urgently expand foreign missions by recruiting native clergy, founding native religious congregations, mobilizing Catholic faithful in prayer and almsgiving, and reorganizing mission territories — all in order to establish self-sustaining local Churches among still-unevangelized peoples.
- **Summary:** Pius XI addresses the universal episcopate and missionary Vicars and Prefects to press for a thoroughgoing renewal of the Church's missionary effort. He argues that the billions of unbaptized constitute an obligation of charity on every Catholic, and that recent momentum (especially the Vatican Missionary Exhibition of 1925) must be channelled into concrete structures: the Missionary Union of the Clergy, the Society for the Propagation of the Faith, the Association of the Holy Childhood, and the Society of St. Peter the Apostle. Most distinctively, he insists that mission territories will remain fragile unless a native clergy — and native religious orders — are formed to govern local churches from within, rather than depending perpetually on foreign missionaries who face linguistic barriers, political expulsion, and numerical insufficiency.
- **Response to:** (1) The grave losses to missions caused by World War I (missionaries recalled, killed, or expelled; mission fields left unattended); (2) the failure over centuries to build a sufficiently large and empowered native clergy, which Benedict XV's *Maximum Illud* (1919) had already mourned; (3) a perceived underestimation of native peoples' intellectual capacity and priestly potential among some missionaries; (4) the fact that European countries themselves now face a shortage of priests, limiting the pool available for export.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) Every bishop establishing or revitalising the Missionary Union of the Clergy in his diocese; (2) mission superiors opening and funding seminaries for native students and refusing no qualified candidate on grounds of race or diocesan inconvenience; (3) founding native religious congregations suited to local "genius and character"; (4) deploying contemplative orders in mission territories to draw down divine grace; (5) reorganising mission stations so that no territory is left unvisited; (6) the Pope's own reserved authority to transfer, divide, or reassign mission territories to whichever institute best serves the Church's growth.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the missionary institutes' attachment to their own territories and congregations (treated as a "peculiar and perpetual right") and the Holy See's insistence that mission fields belong to the universal Church and may be redistributed at papal discretion. A second tension runs through the native-clergy argument: the document names, but firmly rejects, a latent racism among some missionaries who treat native priests as a "lower grade" fit only for subordinate roles, while asserting their full equality and eventual governance of local churches. A third tension lies between the urgency of evangelising nearly a billion pagans and the practical scarcity of missionaries exacerbated by post-war European priest shortages.

## Unstructured

**Flow of argument.** The letter moves in three broad arcs. The first (§§1–17) addresses all Catholic bishops worldwide: it roots the missionary obligation in charity and the mandate of Christ, invokes the momentum of the 1925 Vatican Missionary Exhibition and its permanent museum, and then works through the practical mechanisms by which the faithful and clergy should support missions — habitual prayer, the Missionary Union of the Clergy, and financial contributions to the three pontifically centralised missionary societies. The second arc (§§18–28) turns directly to Apostolic Vicars and Prefects and constitutes the doctrinal heart of the document: the imperative to build a native clergy ("If you do not work with all your might to attain this purpose, We assert that not only will your apostolate be crippled, but it will become an obstacle"), to found native religious congregations, to train and deploy catechists, and to invite contemplative orders into mission territories. The third arc (§§29–34) addresses mission organisation and governance: distribution of missionaries across territories, pastoral methods modelled on Christ's healing ministry, appropriate building programmes, education of local elites, and — most assertive of all — the Pope's explicit declaration that he will transfer, divide, or reassign mission territories whenever he judges it necessary for the Church's growth.

**Key themes.**

1. *Native clergy as structural necessity, not optional supplement.* The document quotes Benedict XV's *Maximum Illud* at length and goes further: the native priest is argued to be superior in cultural access and linguistic fluency to any foreign missionary, and the absence of a native clergy is presented as the single greatest structural weakness of the missions. The Pope even addresses the prejudice that native peoples are intellectually inferior, calling it "a grievous mistake" and pointing to native students in Rome who "frequently even surpass" their European peers.

2. *Universal charity as the theological ground of mission.* The obligation to evangelize is not framed primarily as institutional or juridical but as an expression of the same charity that binds Christians to God and neighbour. The document repeatedly circles back to the argument that to withhold the faith from those who lack it is a failure of love.

3. *Centralisation under Rome.* The transfer of the Society for the Propagation of the Faith to Rome, the re-organisation of the three pontifical missionary societies, and the explicit papal reservation of the right to reallocate mission territories all point to a deliberate consolidation of missionary governance under direct Holy See authority, away from the autonomy of individual religious institutes.

4. *Post-war repair and renewal.* World War I appears as a rupture that depleted missionary ranks and exposed the fragility of missions dependent on European manpower. The document's urgency is partly fuelled by the need to rebuild lost ground.

5. *Prayer and sacrifice as co-equal with active mission.* The inclusion of contemplative orders and the repeated emphasis on habitual intercessory prayer — especially by children, religious women, and seminarians — reflect a theology in which spiritual means are not secondary to institutional ones.

**Tone.** The document combines an authoritative, directive register (the Pope "commands" that seminaries be established, states he "shall transfer" mission territories, uses "We assert") with a genuinely pastoral warmth — inviting bishops not to "look upon compliance with this Our paternal exhortation as an irksome duty," expressing personal emotion at the sight of nearly a billion unbaptised ("We have no rest in our spirit"), and closing with an invocation of Mary as Queen of Apostles. The combination is characteristic of a document aimed at motivating compliance rather than merely mandating it.

**Notable framing.** The analogy between the missionary army ("officers" and "privates") is used to underscore discipline and coordination; the image of the native clergy spread "like a network" across a territory evokes structural resilience against political disruption; and the repeated citation of Scripture (Matthew, Luke, John, Isaiah, Paul) grounds each practical directive in apostolic precedent.
