# Prodiit jamdudum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** When the Vigil of Saint Matthias falls on the last day of Carnival, the near-certainty of public fasting violations justifies the Pope — and only the Pope — temporarily moving the fast to prevent scandal.
- **Summary:** Benedict XIV addresses a concrete liturgical conflict in 1751: the obligatory fast for the Vigil of Saint Matthias coincides with the last day of Carnival, when popular excess makes observance "almost inevitably" impossible. Bishops across Italy wrote asking whether they could move the fast on their own authority. The Pope surveys canonical precedents and two competing theological opinions, concludes that ordinary episcopal authority cannot override established papal and canonical law on the matter, and then personally grants a one-time dispensation permitting the fast to be anticipated to the preceding Saturday — while urging clergy not to avail themselves of it, since they should not be "immersed in Carnival intemperances as the laity mostly are."
- **Response to:** (1) A 1748 encyclical (XLV, vol. 2 of the *Bollario*) addressing three ongoing abuses during Carnival — festivities overrunning into the first day of Lent and Ash Wednesday Mass, masks permitted on Fridays and holy days of obligation, and street performers allowed during Vespers and Christian Doctrine — which the present letter reconfirms. (2) Letters arriving from bishops and prelates "from various parts, including the most distant places in Italy" before 1751 began, warning that the coincidence of the St. Matthias Vigil with Carnival's last day would produce "inevitable, public, and perhaps general scandals" of fasting violations.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Archbishops and bishops of the Papal Temporal State for the specific 1751 Carnival season; the permission is framed as a one-time concession ("for this time only"), not a permanent rule. Bishops are also directed to exhort secular and regular clergy to fast on the actual vigil day regardless. The document reaffirms as a general principle that ordinary authority cannot derogate from what has been established by Roman Pontiffs and Canon Law.
- **Tension:** Two tensions run through the document. First, an institutional one: the bishops' genuine pastoral concern (preventing public scandal) pulls against the canonical principle that ordinary episcopal authority cannot override provisions established by popes and Canon Law — the Pope resolves this by acknowledging both and then intervening with his own higher authority. Second, a social-spiritual one: the document openly names the incompatibility between Carnival licentiousness ("la baldanza carnevalesca non ha alcun ritegno" — Carnival boldness has no restraint) and the discipline of fasting, and frankly concedes that punishment of violators would fall only on "few miserable people" while the powerful would escape, either because their offenses would not reach superiors' ears or because prudence would discourage action against them.

## Unstructured

**Document type and occasion.** This is a tightly practical administrative letter, not a doctrinal or moralistic treatise. It has a dual occasion: a reconfirmation of existing Carnival regulations from 1748, and a new problem specific to 1751. The document is transparent about its reasoning in an unusually procedural way — it walks through the consultation process, the responses received, the legal opinions considered, and the conclusion reached, almost like a published decree with explanatory notes.

**Argument structure.** The text proceeds methodically through four numbered points of reasoning before arriving at its conclusion. First, the fear of fasting violations is "well-founded, not idle." Second, there is canonical precedent for moving fasts (the Vigil of St. John the Baptist when it falls on Corpus Christi Thursday; feast days of principal patron saints when a fast coincides). Third, if a fast can be moved to allow a feast to be celebrated with "proportionate joy, freed from the sadness of fasting," it can *a fortiori* be moved to avoid exposing it to scandalous, public, nearly general irreverence. Fourth — the crux — can bishops do this on their own authority?

**On authority.** The treatment of episcopal vs. papal authority is the intellectual core of the letter. The Pope engages two schools of canonical opinion (Gobat and La Croix on one side; Raynaud, Merati, Cavalerio, Diana, and others on the other) and explicitly sides with the more restrictive opinion as "more common and better founded." He references his own prior work *De Synodo Dioecesana* (lib. 7, cap. 30) on the limits of ordinary authority. Then, in a neat pivot, he uses the very gap he has just established — papal authority reaching where ordinary authority cannot — to grant the dispensation himself. The logic is: bishops asked, ordinary authority won't cover it, but mine will, so I grant it.

**The Agape analogy.** The most striking argumentative move is the comparison to the *Agapae* (early Christian communal meals), which the Church eventually suppressed despite their apostolic origin because of the disorders they generated. Benedict uses this to make a broader point: the Church has always been willing to suppress or modify ancient customs when disorder makes them untenable. Moving a fast for one year is far less radical than abolishing an apostolic tradition. He also notes that both cases involve "the vice of gluttony" (*il vizio della gola*), a link that grounds the analogy in moral continuity.

**Tone.** The tone is deliberately measured and juridical. The Pope is not scolding; he is adjudicating. He acknowledges that Carnival excess is an existing social fact that no reasonable administrator can pretend away ("the Carnival boldness has no restraint, especially on the last day and night"). He is even frank about the inequality of ecclesiastical enforcement: the powerful will not be punished. This realism is not resignation — it motivates the dispensation — but it is candid in a way that is more administrative than prophetic. The document closes with a pastoral opt-out: bishops who have no reason to fear violations in their territory should simply let things run their natural course.

**Clerical/lay distinction.** A notable framing is the explicit differentiation between clergy and laity in the closing instruction. Clergy (secular and regular) are instructed to fast on the actual vigil day and not take advantage of the dispensation, "not being considered as immersed in Carnival intemperances as the laity mostly are." This two-tier anthropology — lay people as susceptible to Carnival passions, clergy as expected to maintain discipline regardless — runs implicitly through the whole document and underlies the structure of the problem itself.
