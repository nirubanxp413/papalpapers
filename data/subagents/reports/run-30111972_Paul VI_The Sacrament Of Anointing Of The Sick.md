# The Sacrament Of Anointing Of The Sick

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document formally revises the rite of Anointing of the Sick — renaming it, simplifying it, and broadening its scope — to restore the sacrament's full scriptural and ecclesial meaning beyond its late-medieval association with imminent death.
- **Summary:** Paul VI issues an Apostolic Constitution officially reforming the sacrament long known as "Extreme Unction." Drawing on a chain of authority from Scripture (James 5:14–15, Mark 6:13) through the Councils of Florence, Trent, and Vatican II, the document redefines the proper recipients (those dangerously ill, not only the dying), simplifies the anointing rite (forehead and hands, a single new formula), permits vegetable oils other than olive oil where that is unobtainable, and sets a transition timeline for the Latin Rite. The revision is presented as a clarification and recovery of authentic tradition, not a departure from it.
- **Response to:** (1) The accumulated liturgical complexity of the Middle Ages — multiple body-part anointings and elaborate formulas that had accumulated over centuries. (2) The overly narrow interpretation of the sacrament as reserved for the moment of death (its designation as "the sacrament of the dying" at Trent and the popular name "Extreme Unction"). (3) The Second Vatican Council's explicit mandate to revise this rite and to adapt "those elements which were subject to change" to "present-day conditions." (4) Requests from "numerous bishops" regarding the practical unavailability of olive oil in parts of the world.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) The Latin Rite Church going forward: a mandatory adoption of the new Order by January 1, 1974. (2) Episcopal conferences, who are directed to prepare and seek confirmation of vernacular editions. (3) A broader pastoral practice in which the sacrament is offered earlier in serious illness or old age, not withheld until the point of death. (4) The sick themselves, who are encouraged "to contribute to the welfare of the whole People of God by associating themselves freely with the passion and death of Christ."
- **Tension:** The central tension is between two understandings of the sacrament's identity: (a) the centuries-old, narrowed form — "Extreme Unction," for the dying, anointing five senses, named "the sacrament of the dying" — and (b) the broader, scripturally grounded form the document asserts — for "those who are dangerously ill" more generally, administered also in cases of old age, capable of being repeated if the person recovers and falls ill again. The document resolves this tension legislatively by authoritative decree, but its careful citation of Trent (which had itself used the narrower formula) alongside Vatican II signals awareness that the revision requires doctrinal justification, not merely administrative convenience.

## Unstructured

**Nature of the document.** This is a brief Apostolic Constitution — a formal legislative act, not a pastoral letter or theological treatise. Its tone is almost entirely authoritative: Paul VI does not invite reflection but "lay[s] down" and "approve[s]" and "revoke[s]" and "abrogate[s]." The pastoral dimension is present but thin; the document's primary register is canonical and liturgical.

**Argument structure.** The document moves in a clear arc: (1) scriptural grounding — James 5:14–15 as the original locus; (2) historical survey of tradition — East and West, Innocent I, the Roman Pontifical, medieval customs; (3) conciliar authority — Florence, Trent, and especially Vatican II; (4) specific rationale for each change (formula revision, oil flexibility, rite simplification); (5) the formal decree itself, in capitals; (6) implementation timeline and abrogation of prior law.

**Key conceptual move: from death-preparation to illness-care.** The most theologically significant step is the shift in scope quoted from Vatican II's *Sacrosanctum Concilium*: "not a sacrament for those only who are at the point of death." This reframing also affects the name: the document notes the sacrament "may also and more fittingly be called 'Anointing of the Sick'" — implicitly retiring "Extreme Unction" as a misleading label, though it does not explicitly forbid it. The communal dimension added by *Lumen Gentium* (the "whole Church commends those who are ill") extends the sacrament beyond a private dying rite into an ecclesial act of solidarity.

**The effects of the sacrament as cited from Trent.** Even while broadening the sacrament's scope, the document preserves and re-cites Trent's rich description of effects: the grace of the Holy Spirit, taking away remaining sins, relieving and strengthening the soul, arousing confidence in divine mercy, helping the sick person bear illness and resist temptation, and — notably — "sometimes regains bodily health, if this is expedient for the health of the soul." This conditional bodily healing is cited without commentary, integrated into the theological account without being foregrounded.

**Practical accommodation as a theological signal.** The permission for non-olive vegetable oils is introduced almost parenthetically — "at the request of numerous bishops" in parts of the world where olive oil is "unobtainable or difficult to obtain." This small provision is significant: it grounds liturgical matter in pastoral reality and implicitly acknowledges the Church's global geographic expansion beyond Mediterranean culture.

**The new formula.** The document reproduces the new Latin formula in full (capitals): *"Per istam Sanctam Unctionem et suam piissimam misericordiam adiuvet te Dominus gratia Spiritus Sancti, ut a peccatis liberatum te salvet atque propitius alleviet."* ("Through this holy anointing may the Lord in his love and mercy help you with the grace of the Holy Spirit. May the Lord who frees you from sin save you and raise you up.") Compared to the old formula — which named each sense individually and asked forgiveness for sins committed through that sense — the new formula is unified, active in voice, and orientated toward salvation and relief rather than penitential enumeration.

**Overall framing.** The document frames reform as recovery, not innovation. Every change is anchored in a prior authority — scripture, councils, patristic tradition. The word "restore" is not used, but the logic is restorative: the revisions correct medieval accretion and reactivate the sacrament's original, fuller scope. This is characteristic of the post-Vatican II reform program more broadly, and the document fits squarely within it.
