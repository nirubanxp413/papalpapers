# Nostis Et Nobiscum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document calls the bishops of Italy to defend the Catholic faith and social order against the twin threats of revolutionary anti-Catholic agitation and the spread of Socialism and Communism, prescribing pastoral, doctrinal, and disciplinary remedies.
- **Summary:** Written from Naples in December 1849, after Pius IX's forced exile from Rome and the temporary overthrow of papal civil authority, this encyclical addresses the bishops of Italy. It identifies a coordinated campaign by enemies of the Church to detach Italians from Catholicism by promoting Protestantism, Socialism, and Communism under false slogans of liberty and equality. The Pope rebuts the claim that Catholicism has harmed Italy, argues that the Church is the foundation of Italy's true glory, and issues detailed directives on preaching, sacramental practice, clerical formation, religious orders, Catholic schools, control of printed materials, obedience to civil authority, and care for the poor — all aimed at fortifying the faithful against ideological subversion.
- **Response to:** The forced withdrawal of the Pope from Rome; the brief revolutionary seizure of Rome and the Papal States; the actions of "enemies of God and men" who used the period of disorder to undermine Catholic institutions, disrupt the clergy, and promote anti-Catholic and revolutionary ideologies; the spread of Socialism and Communism among the Italian people; Protestant Bible Societies distributing vernacular scriptures; the defection of some clergy to the Church's enemies; the proliferation of impious pamphlets, newspapers, and leaflets.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Strengthening episcopal cooperation and shared counsel; intensifying doctrinal instruction of the laity; promoting reception of the sacraments of Confirmation, Penance, and Eucharist; combating bad books and Protestant biblical interpretations; reforming and disciplining the regular and secular clergy; careful selection and formation of seminarians; Catholic oversight of schools and youth education; encouraging the faithful to venerate the Apostolic See and obey lawful civil authority; counseling the poor to accept their condition with patience; enlisting political leaders to protect the Church.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the Catholic social and spiritual order — grounded in obedience to the Church, legitimate authority, and providential acceptance of social inequality — and the revolutionary movements of Socialism and Communism that promise equality and liberty while, the document argues, actually threatening plunder, disorder, and eternal ruin. A secondary tension exists between the Church's custodianship of scripture and tradition versus the Protestant principle of individual scriptural interpretation, which the document presents as the ideological gateway exploited by revolutionary agitators. A further tension is internal to Italian Catholic society: some clergy and laypeople have already gone over to the Church's enemies, creating scandal and weakening the faithful.

## Unstructured

**Key themes and flow of argument:**

The encyclical opens with a vivid account of the revolutionary disorders that accompanied Pius IX's exile from Rome — framed not merely as political events but as diabolical attacks on the Church. The document then pivots from crisis-narration to exhortation, structured as a sustained pastoral directive to the Italian episcopate.

The first major argumentative move is a rebuttal of the claim that Catholicism has harmed Italian national greatness. Pius IX inverts this: the Church preserved and elevated Italy after the fall of Rome and the barbarian invasions; Italy's true glory is inseparable from its Catholic heritage. This is not mere apologetics — it is a response to a specific propaganda line attributed to the Church's enemies, who urged adoption of Protestantism as a path to national revival.

The document then identifies the deeper agenda behind the pro-Protestant campaign: Socialism and Communism. The text names both terms explicitly and repeatedly (paragraphs 6, 18, 25, 32), characterizing them as the real goal of those who promote religious liberalism — a deliberate misuse of "liberty" and "equality" to incite the lower classes to plunder and overthrow civil and divine order. This ideological diagnosis is unusually explicit for a mid-nineteenth-century papal text.

The doctrinal and pastoral remedies follow in sequence: faithful instruction in Catholic doctrine; sacramental practice as spiritual armor; suppression of bad books and Protestant Bible distribution; promotion of Catholic publishing; episcopal cooperation and shared intelligence about local causes of disorder; careful clerical formation; reform of religious orders; Catholic control of schools at all levels; and cultivation of devotion to the Apostolic See.

The social teaching sections (paragraphs 19–25) are notably systematic. The document grounds civil obedience in scripture, asserts that social inequality is natural and immutable, warns that redistributive violence leads only to oligarchy, and argues that Catholic charity — not revolutionary redistribution — is the proper remedy for the condition of the poor.

**Tone:** Predominantly authoritative and urgent, with pastoral warmth directed toward the bishops and, through them, to the faithful. The rhetoric against the "enemies" is sharp and at times lurid (references to "diabolical design," "foaming waves of a savage sea," dying "in the arms of a wanton prostitute"). The closing sections adopt a more consoling and prayerful register, invoking the Virgin Mary Immaculate, Peter, Paul, and the saints.

**Notable framing:** The document consistently conflates theological and political threats — Protestantism, Socialism, and Communism are presented as a unified front against Catholic civilization, not as distinct phenomena. The Pope frames his own forced exile and the revolutionary interlude in Rome not as political defeat but as a providential trial requiring pastoral response. The extensive quotation from St. Augustine on the Catholic Church's social function (paragraph 33) serves as the encyclical's positive counter-image to the disordered society threatened by revolution: a hierarchically ordered community bound by charity at every level, from slave to king.
