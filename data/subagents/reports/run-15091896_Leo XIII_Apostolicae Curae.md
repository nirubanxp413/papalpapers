# Apostolicae Curae

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** Anglican ordinations performed according to the Edwardine Ordinal are declared absolutely null and void due to defects in both sacramental form and intention, meaning the Anglican church lacks a valid priesthood and hierarchy.
- **Summary:** Leo XIII sets out to definitively resolve a longstanding controversy over whether Holy Orders conferred under the Edwardine Ordinal (introduced under Edward VI) are valid. After commissioning a thorough scholarly investigation — examining Vatican archives, prior papal decrees, and historical cases — he affirms the centuries-old practice of the Apostolic See: Anglican ordinations are and have always been null and void. The argument rests on two inseparable grounds: (1) defect of form — the Ordinal's formulae omit any clear expression of the sacrificial priesthood and the power to consecrate the Eucharist; and (2) defect of intention — the deliberate removal of Catholic sacrificial language from the rite signals an intention contrary to what the Church performs in ordination. The document closes with a pastoral appeal for Anglicans, especially their clergy, to return to the Roman Church.
- **Response to:** The document responds to a recently revived controversy in which certain Anglican writers and "some few Catholics, chiefly non-English" had argued for the absolute or doubtful validity of Anglican orders — motivated respectively by Anglican desire for a valid priesthood and by Catholic desire to smooth the path to reunion. It also responds to earlier unresolved historical cases (e.g., the 1684 French Calvinist case and the 1704 Gordon case) and to the prior but apparently not universally known rulings of Julius III and Paul IV.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates and directs toward the reconciliation of Anglicans with Rome: it explicitly invites Anglican ministers and laypeople to "return to His one only fold," promising that they will receive valid sacraments, true remission of sins, and "the most powerful aids for their eternal salvation." It also forecloses further theological debate on the question by declaring the judgment final and legally binding.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between Anglican claims to possess a valid apostolic hierarchy and priesthood versus the Roman Catholic insistence that the Edwardine reform deliberately severed sacramental continuity. A secondary tension exists between the ecumenical desire (acknowledged by both Anglican writers and well-meaning Catholic writers) to find a bridge toward reunion, and the doctrinal necessity — as Leo frames it — of not allowing "a pernicious error" to be fostered in the minds of those who "might suppose that they possessed the Sacrament and effects of Orders, where these are nowise to be found." The document also implicitly acknowledges a tension between pastoral charity and juridical declaration: Leo emphasizes that "nothing should be left untried that might in any way tend to preserve souls from injury" before issuing his final ruling.

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument**

The document moves through four broadly sequential phases:

1. *Historical and procedural justification (§§1–22):* Leo begins by situating the question within his broader pastoral concern for England and its return to unity. He then narrates the process: a commission of scholars with divergent views was assembled, archives searched, prior cases examined, and sessions convened. The historical anchor is the 16th-century papal response to the Edwardine Ordinal — the faculties granted by Julius III to Cardinal Pole, Paul IV's Bull *Praeclara Charissimi* (1555), and subsequent Holy Office decisions (1684, 1704). Leo argues these precedents already definitively resolved the question; the recent revival of the controversy stems merely from "insufficient knowledge of these documents."

2. *Doctrinal analysis of form (§§24–32):* The Ordinal is examined on its own terms. The document explains that in any sacrament, the "form" must signify what it effects. The Edwardine priestly ordination formula — "Receive the Holy Ghost" — fails to specify the sacred order of priesthood, and specifically omits reference to the power of consecrating and offering the Eucharist (citing the Council of Trent). Later attempts to supply the deficiency by adding "for the office and work of a priest" are dismissed: they came too late (a century after the original rite, by which time the hierarchy had already lapsed), and in any case, a corrupted rite cannot be rehabilitated by amendment once its essential character has been fixed. The broader context — the deliberate removal from the Ordinal of all language referencing sacrifice, consecration, and the sacrificial priesthood — is treated as dispositive: "every trace of these things … was deliberately removed and struck out."

3. *Doctrinal analysis of intention (§33):* The document adds a complementary argument on defect of intention. The Church judges intention as externally manifested in the rite. Where the rite is changed with the "manifest intention of introducing another rite not approved by the Church and of rejecting what the Church does," the intention is not merely absent but actively contrary to the Sacrament.

4. *Declaration and pastoral appeal (§§34–41):* The definitive judgment is issued — Anglican ordinations "have been, and are, absolutely null and utterly void" — then immediately followed by a warm pastoral appeal to Anglicans to return to Rome, with special exhortation to their ministers. The closing paragraphs are notably tender in tone, promising a welcoming reception by the Church and holding out the prospect of the sacraments as "fountains of the Savior."

**Tone**

The tone is predominantly authoritative and juridical: Leo is issuing a definitive doctrinal ruling, grounding it in historical precedent, formal sacramental theology, and the authority of the Apostolic See. However, the document opens and closes with pastoral warmth — explicit care for England's "eternal salvation," acknowledgment of Anglican sincerity ("those who desire and seek with a sincere heart"), and personal expressions of desire for reconciliation. The juridical sections (particularly §§24–33) are technical and scholastic in register, quoting Trent and invoking canonical principles. The framing device — that nothing was left untried before the judgment was issued — serves to present the declaration not as hostile but as the unavoidable conclusion of rigorous charity.

**Notable framing**

Leo frames the entire exercise as an act of *apostolic charity*, not juridical aggression. The commission, the archival search, the postponement for prayer — all are presented as evidence that the Church exhausted every avenue before closing the question. This framing positions the final declaration as the most loving possible response: to leave Anglicans thinking they possess valid orders would be to allow "a pernicious error" to endanger their souls. The document thus makes pastoral care the justification for doctrinal finality. Notably, Leo explicitly credits Anglican writers with sincerity ("desirous as they were that their own people should not lack the twofold power over the Body of Christ") and credits Catholic advocates for Anglican validity with goodwill ("a wish to smooth the way for the return of Anglicans to holy unity"), while nonetheless concluding both were mistaken.
