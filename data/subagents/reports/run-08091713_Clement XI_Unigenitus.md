# Unigenitus

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** God's grace is omnipotent, irresistible, and the sole source of every good work, faith, charity, and salvation — without it, the human will can do nothing but sin.
- **Summary:** The document is a condemnation of 101 propositions drawn from the Jansenist writer Paschasius Quesnel's *Réflexions morales*. The propositions themselves articulate a theology in which divine grace is absolutely sovereign and irresistible, human nature without grace is wholly corrupt and incapable of any good, charity is the only valid motive for obedience and prayer, all graces flow exclusively through the Church, Scripture should be open to all the faithful, and persecution of those who hold these truths is itself an act against God. The closing sentence of the document declares all 101 propositions condemned under a wide range of canonical censures, ranging from "false" and "rash" to "heretical" and "smacking of Jansenism."
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to the theological writings of Paschasius Quesnel — named in the footnote as the author of *Réflexions morales* — whose propositions are reproduced and then condemned. The text implicitly responds to Jansenist teaching on grace, predestination, and the relationship between fear and charity in moral life. It also responds to disputes over access to Scripture for the laity and women (propositions 79–85), and to questions about the legitimacy of excommunication and ecclesiastical authority (propositions 90–94).
- **Prescriptive towards:** The condemnation is directed toward the entire Catholic Church; the document anticipates that Catholics must reject these propositions. Propositions 79–85 point forward to questions about lay and female access to Scripture. Propositions 90–94 anticipate ongoing struggles over authority, excommunication, and the rights of conscience against unjust ecclesiastical censure. The final condemnatory clause signals continued vigilance against Jansenism and schism.
- **Tension:** The core tension is between **grace as omnipotent and irresistible divine will** versus any account of human freedom or cooperation with grace. A second major tension runs through propositions 59–68: **charity versus fear** as the legitimate motive for penance, prayer, and obedience — with the condemned propositions insisting that servile fear alone produces only hypocrisy and sin. A third tension concerns **ecclesiastical authority versus the rights of the faithful**: propositions 79–85 claim Scripture must be open to all (including women and the laity), while propositions 90–94 defend the right to endure unjust excommunication rather than betray truth, and proposition 94 warns that absolute rule over faith fosters division and drives enemies away from the Church.

## Unstructured

The document as it survives is unusual in structure: it consists almost entirely of the condemned propositions themselves (numbered 1–101), followed by a single brief condemnatory sentence. The reader encounters the Jansenist theology directly, before any refutation, which gives the text an unusual quality — the condemned ideas are fully stated and in some cases compellingly argued.

**Flow of argument within the propositions (as extracted):**

The propositions move through a coherent theological arc:

1. **Propositions 1–25: The omnipotence of grace.** Grace is not merely helpful but constitutive of every good act. Nothing good can be willed or done without it. God's grace operates like his creative and resurrection power — it effects what it wills without resistance. Propositions 10–16 are especially uncompromising: grace is "the omnipotent Will of God," and "no human will resists Him." Proposition 30 draws the predestinarian implication: "All whom God wishes to save through Christ are infallibly saved."

2. **Propositions 26–42: Faith, grace, and the contrast between Adam and Christ.** Faith is the first grace; no grace exists outside the Church (proposition 29). The grace of Adam was merely proportionate to his created nature; Christian grace is omnipotent, flowing from union with the risen Christ. Without grace the will has "no light except for straying" (proposition 39) and "can love nothing except to our own condemnation" (proposition 40).

3. **Propositions 43–68: Charity vs. fear as the animating principle of Christian life.** Baptismal grace kills sin; all action springs from either love of God or self-love. Charity alone is what God hears, rewards, and crowns. Fear of punishment is insufficient for genuine penance — it leads to despair (proposition 60) and produces only slaves (proposition 65). Servile fear misrepresents God as "a stern imperious, unjust, unyielding master" (proposition 67). This section has a pastoral urgency: the condemned propositions press for interior transformation over external conformity.

4. **Propositions 69–78: The nature and scope of the Church.** The Church is defined mystically — it "embraces all the elect and the just of all ages" (proposition 72, 76). Christ is its head, life, and substance; the Church is "one single man" (proposition 75). Membership is not merely formal: "He who does not lead a life worthy of a son of God ... ceases interiorly to have God as a Father" (proposition 77).

5. **Propositions 79–86: Scripture must be open to all.** This cluster is striking. Proposition 80: "The reading of Sacred Scripture is for all." Proposition 83: heresies arise not from women reading Scripture but from "the proud knowledge of men." Proposition 84: withholding the New Testament is "to close for them the mouth of Christ." Proposition 85: forbidding Scripture to Christians is "a kind of excommunication." These propositions directly challenge clerical gatekeeping of the sacred text.

6. **Propositions 87–101: Penance, excommunication, persecution, and truth.** The final cluster addresses ecclesial discipline and the situation of those under unjust censure. Propositions 91–92 assert that a person remains in the Church as long as attached to God by charity, even when "by the wickedness of men we seem to be expelled." Proposition 93 suggests Jesus heals wounds inflicted by "the precipitous haste of the first pastors." Propositions 96–100 describe the condition of those who preach truth under persecution: they are opposed by all powers, treated as heretics, excluded from the sacraments — yet this is "generally the final trial and especially meritorious" (proposition 98). Proposition 100 is the most charged: it speaks of a time that "has come" when servants of God are "sacrificed to the devil" by those who believe they are honoring God.

**Tone:**

The propositions themselves (as reproduced in the condemnation) carry a tone that is both intensely theological and, in the later numbers, apologetic and persecuted — speaking on behalf of those who suffer unjust ecclesiastical condemnation. The condemnatory conclusion, by contrast, is terse, official, and comprehensive, applying a long list of canonical censures in a single sentence. The juxtaposition is striking: the condemned text is rhetorically rich and morally urgent; the papal condemnation is procedurally blunt.

**Notable framing:**

The document does not offer counter-arguments — it simply lists and condemns. This is characteristic of a formal condemnatory constitution rather than a polemical or pastoral encyclical. The theological substance lies entirely within the condemned propositions, which are Jansenist in character: grace as irresistible, human nature as radically incapacitated, charity as the only valid motive, and Scripture as the common inheritance of all the faithful. The Church condemned these propositions as, among other things, "heretical, clearly renewing many heresies respectively and most especially those which are contained in the infamous propositions of Jansen."
