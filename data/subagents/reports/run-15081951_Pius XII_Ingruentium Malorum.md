# Ingruentium Malorum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Faced with pressing calamities — war threats, persecution of the Church, and the corruption of children — Pius XII urges the faithful to seek refuge and remedy through fervent recitation of the Rosary, especially within the family.
- **Summary:** The encyclical opens by cataloguing the "calamitous conditions of our times": fractured harmony among nations, threats of new armed conflicts, violent persecution of the Church in many parts of the world, and a campaign to corrupt the souls of children. Against these evils, Pius XII prescribes not force or human power but the Marian Rosary — particularly its practice within the Christian family — as the most efficacious spiritual weapon. The letter concludes with a special plea to pray for those imprisoned in camps and jails, including bishops removed from their sees for defending the Church's rights.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to: (1) the failure to re-establish fraternal harmony among nations and ongoing threats of "new bloody conflicts"; (2) "a violent storm of persecution" unleashed against the Church in many parts of the world, including martyrdom, calumnies, and deprivation of liberty; (3) a "new crime" — an organised campaign to corrupt the souls of children and detach them from the Faith; (4) the imprisonment of bishops, clergy, fathers, and mothers in "prison camps, jails, and concentration camps."
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs towards: (1) a more fervent and universal practice of the Rosary during the month of October, understood as an annual collective act of intercession; (2) restoration of the Rosary as a daily custom within the Christian family, with the family home becoming "like that of Nazareth, an earthly abode of sanctity"; (3) peace among individuals, families, peoples, and nations; (4) renewed rights and freedom for the Church in public life; and (5) the return of those who have "wandered from the path of truth and virtue."
- **Tension:** The core tension is between the Church's apparent worldly weakness — subjected to persecution, martyrdom, and political oppression — and its claimed access to divine power through prayer; the document frames this as a David-and-Goliath dynamic ("Not with force, not with arms, not with human power, but with Divine help"). A secondary tension exists between the dissolution of family and social bonds (the root cause of civil unravelling in Pius's diagnosis) and the family Rosary as the regenerative remedy.

## Unstructured

**Flow of argument:** The encyclical is brief and tightly organised. It opens with a personal reflection — Pius XII's practice, since his elevation to the papacy, of entrusting humanity's destiny to Mary, noting the magnificent response of the faithful and the 1950 proclamation of the Assumption as evidence of this devotion's vitality. It then pivots sharply to sorrow: a three-fold inventory of contemporary evils (geopolitical, ecclesial, and moral/generational). The prescription is immediate and singular: resort to the Rosary. The middle sections elaborate *why* the Rosary is effective (its heavenly origin, its accessibility to the uneducated, its ability to draw virtue through meditative repetition) and *where* it should be practised (above all in the family home). The letter closes with a pastoral and humanitarian note: pray specifically for the imprisoned, including bishops.

**Key themes in the text's own terms:**
- *"Approaching evils" / "calamitous conditions"* — The document's animating motive is an acute sense of imminent danger; Pius uses the phrase "ingruentium malorum" (pressing/encroaching evils) to describe the situation demanding a response.
- *Mary as chief refuge* — The theological logic is Marian intercessory: Mary is the "cause of salvation for the whole human race" (quoting St. Irenaeus), and the Rosary is the "most convenient and most fruitful means" of reaching her.
- *The Rosary as accessible to all* — Notably inclusive language: "all, even the most simple and least educated, have in this a prompt and easy way to nourish and preserve their own faith." The document stresses universal accessibility as a positive feature.
- *Family as foundation of civil society* — "In vain is a remedy sought for the wavering fate of civil life, if the family, the principle and foundation of the human community, is not fashioned after the pattern of the Gospel." The family Rosary is thus not merely a pious practice but a sociopolitical remedy.
- *Persecution and martyrdom* — The document names real, ongoing suffering: martyrs' blood flowing, bishops removed from their sees, people held in concentration camps. These are named as present facts, not hypothetical dangers.
- *Children under threat* — Described as "the most beautiful flowers" of the Church being stolen from "the mystical garden," this passage conveys a specific alarm about an organised anti-religious campaign targeting youth.

**Tone:** Predominantly pastoral-exhortatory, with moments of genuine paternal sorrow. The David and Goliath quotation (1 Kings 17:45–47) introduces a combative, confident register, asserting that the Church's spiritual resources exceed any earthly threat. The overall tone is urgent but not despairing — Pius consistently redirects from lamentation toward confident trust in Mary's intercession.

**Notable framing:** The document does not name specific nations, political regimes, or ideologies by name. The evils are catalogued in moral-theological language ("hatred and rivalry," "the impious," "those who have miserably wandered from the path of truth") rather than geopolitical terms. This universalises the problem while preserving a stance of spiritual rather than political authority.
