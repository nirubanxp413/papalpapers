# Editae Saepe

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The life and example of St. Charles Borromeo furnishes a model of true Catholic reformation — rooted in obedience to Rome, doctrinal fidelity, and sacramental renewal — as a direct counterweight to the "modern apostasy" of Modernism, which Pius X treats as a more dangerous recurrence of the sixteenth-century Protestant revolt.
- **Summary:** Issued on the third centenary of Borromeo's canonization (1910), the document moves from hagiography into polemic. Pius X holds up Charles as the archetype of the "true reformer" — one who begins reform within himself and the clergy, adheres strictly to Council and Roman authority, and uses prayer, sacraments, and catechesis rather than political noise or doctrinal novelty. Against this model the pope contrasts two intertwined threats: the Protestant reformers of Borromeo's era, who are said to have destroyed discipline and fomented Europe's subsequent wars, and the present-day Modernists, who are accused of an even deeper subversion by infiltrating the Church from within. The pastoral remedies proposed are catechetical instruction (referencing the encyclical *Acerbo Nimis*), sound preaching, frequent and reverent reception of the Sacraments (especially Confession and Eucharist, echoing *Tridentina Synodus*), and organized Catholic Action.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to (a) the centenary occasion of Borromeo's canonization; (b) the ongoing threat of Modernism, which the pope has already "denounced and condemned" in prior acts; (c) what the text calls a "universal apostasy" that "stealthily creeps into the very veins of the Church"; (d) secular/lay schools that Pius X says are "the stronghold of the powers of darkness," stripping religion from public education; (e) suppression of Church rights by hostile civil governments in certain countries.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (a) Bishops and clergy: vigilant defense of integral Catholic faith, expulsion of heresy, catechetical schools, sound preachers; (b) Pastors: promotion of frequent Holy Communion with proper reverence; (c) Lay Catholics and their leaders: engagement in "Catholic Action" — organized apostolate of charity and social restoration; (d) All the faithful: imitation of Borromeo's interior spirituality (contemplation, obedience, humility) as the ground of any outward reform.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between *true reformation* (interior, sacramental, obedient to Rome, gradual) and *false reformation* (externally activist, rationalist, doctrinally experimental, autonomous). This is both a spiritual tension — faith vs. mere natural virtue; grace vs. human self-sufficiency — and a social one: the Church's authority to govern and sanctify society vs. the modern secular state's claim to monopolize education and public order. A secondary tension runs between patient, hidden reform (Borromeo's model, "God is not in the earthquake") and the loud, revolutionary tactics the text condemns as vainglory and party spirit.

## Unstructured

**Shape of the argument.** The document is structured as a centenary commemoration that doubles as a polemical manifesto. Paragraphs 1–5 establish the hagiographic occasion and Pius X's signature programme of *instaurare omnia in Christo*. Paragraphs 6–8 offer a theology of the Church's providential resilience — persecution and error never destroy her but purify her — setting up the historical parallel that follows. Paragraphs 9–14 narrate the sixteenth-century crisis: the Protestant revolt, described in sharply negative terms ("they were corrupters, not reformers"), and God's providential response in Charles. Paragraphs 15–35 turn this historical narrative into a sustained typological argument: Borromeo's virtues and methods become a template, while their negative mirror-image (false reformers, past and present) is built up point by point. Paragraphs 36–44 move to practical applications: sacramental life, catechesis, preaching, Catholic Action, fidelity to just civil authority while resisting unjust laws.

**Tone.** The tone is primarily authoritative and didactic but is inflected by pastoral warmth in hagiographical passages and by unmistakable urgency in the anti-Modernist sections. Phrases like "a universal apostasy even worse than the one that threatened the age of Charles" and "it stealthily creeps into the very veins of the Church" signal alarm. The document is self-referential: Pius X cites his own prior encyclicals (*E Supremi*, *Acerbo Nimis*, *Tridentina Synodus*, *Ad diem illum*) to situate *Editae Saepe* within a coherent pontifical programme.

**Key framing moves.**
1. *Typological parallelism.* The Protestant Reformation and Modernism are explicitly equated as two waves of the same revolt: "Both these heresies are fathered by the 'enemy' who 'sowed weeds among the wheat.'" This framing lets Borromeo's historical victory serve as a proof of concept for the present struggle.
2. *Inversion of "reform."* The text insists on wresting the language of reform away from the innovators: true reform is attributed to Borromeo and the Council of Trent; the Protestants and Modernists are re-labelled "corrupters." This is a rhetoric of reclamation.
3. *Inside/outside distinction.* The document acknowledges that the present danger is partly internal — "waged and sometimes supported by those who should be the first to come to Our aid" — which heightens the sense of urgency beyond that of mere external persecution.
4. *Hierarchy of means.* The document constructs an explicit hierarchy: supernatural means (prayer, sacraments, grace) > doctrinal instruction > natural and civic means. False reformers invert or bypass this hierarchy; true reformers honour it.

**Notable vocabulary.** "Restore all things in Christ" (*instaurare omnia in Christo*) functions as a leitmotif across the whole document. "True reformer" vs. "false reformer" is the primary structural opposition. "Modern apostasy," "Modernism," and "innovators" are the terms for the present enemy. "Catholic Action" appears as the organized lay apostolate, already a term of art in Pius X's pontificate.
