# Ecclesiae Sanctae (Motu Proprio Implementing Four Council Decrees) — Paul VI, 1966

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Church, having concluded the Second Vatican Council, must now translate its decrees into binding experimental norms that reform governance, renew religious life, and mobilise the whole People of God for missionary activity in the modern world.
- **Summary:** Ecclesiae Sanctae is a legislative Motu Proprio that promulgates interim norms for implementing four Vatican II decrees — Christus Dominus, Presbyterorum Ordinis, Perfectae Caritatis, and Ad Gentes Divinitus. Paul VI frames the document as a necessary and urgent response to the "new goals and areas of the apostolate which through the Council have been opened up to the Church in the modern world." The norms are explicitly provisional — to remain in force only until the revised Code of Canon Law is promulgated — and episcopal conferences are invited to report back their observations. The document covers: the redistribution and remuneration of clergy; the authority and structures of bishops; the adaptation and renewal of religious institutes; and the intensification of global missionary activity. Its method is structural reform in service of spiritual renewal.
- **Response to:** The document directly states it responds to the conclusion of the Second Ecumenical Vatican Council, specifically the four decrees named above. It also responds to "great changes" in the world, which it describes as being "in need of a shining light" and longing for "the supernatural flame of charity." Internally, it acknowledges the existing Code of Canon Law as a framework that must be respected but is already under revision to meet new circumstances.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates the promulgation of a new Code of Canon Law, towards which these norms are explicitly transitional. It directs episcopal conferences to gather observations and submit them to the Apostolic See. It directs all Religious institutes to convene a special general chapter within two to three years to promote adaptation and renewal. It anticipates a coming meeting of the Synod of Bishops, inviting episcopal conferences to submit missionary questions in advance. It directs the Church toward becoming "truly missionary" so that "the entire People of God become aware of its missionary obligation."
- **Tension:** Several tensions are named or clearly implied: (1) Central authority vs. local governance — the document repeatedly affirms the primacy of the Roman Pontiff while simultaneously decentralising powers to episcopal conferences and diocesan bishops; (2) Continuity vs. renewal in religious life — institutes must preserve "the spirit of the founders and their specific aims and healthy traditions" while purging "alien elements" and those that are "obsolete"; (3) Exemption vs. episcopal oversight — Religious institutes (especially exempt ones) are brought under greater local Ordinary authority, creating jurisdictional tension explicitly managed throughout Part I; (4) Urgency of mission vs. insufficiency of resources — the document frankly notes that "voluntary contributions of the faithful for the missions are not at all sufficient" and calls for mandatory diocesan contributions.

## Unstructured

**Structure and scope.** The document is unusually long and multi-part for a Motu Proprio — essentially a legislative codebook in four sections corresponding to the four decrees. It moves from governance (Christus Dominus / Presbyterorum Ordinis) through religious renewal (Perfectae Caritatis) to missionary expansion (Ad Gentes Divinitus). Each section consists of numbered norms, many of which are highly practical (e.g., clergy incardination after five years, bishops to retire by age 75, bishops' resignations to be tendered "not later than at the completion of their 75th year"). This procedural density is the document's most striking formal feature.

**Tone.** The tone is predominantly legislative and administrative — dry, precise, conditional. Paul VI writes in the imperative and subjunctive moods of canon law. Yet the Preamble and the sections on missionary activity and religious renewal carry a pastoral register. The opening line — "the governing of holy Church … demands indeed that new norms be established" — frames the entire document as pastoral necessity, not bureaucratic exercise. Similarly, the Perfectae Caritatis section insists that "the task of the chapters is not completed by merely making laws, but especially by promoting spiritual and apostolic vitality."

**Key arguments and flow.** The document's argument is sequential: (1) The Council has opened new goals for the Church; (2) Norms are now needed to make those goals operative; (3) Because canon law revision is pending, these norms are experimental and correctable; (4) Episcopal conferences are the primary instruments of implementation and reform. This last point is significant: throughout all four sections, episcopal conferences are repeatedly empowered — to establish norms, propose reforms, coordinate clergy, and report to Rome. This represents a structural decentralisation rooted in the Council's ecclesiology of collegial governance (Lumen Gentium).

**Notable framings.** 
- The experimental character of the norms is stated explicitly and revisited multiple times ("to be observed by way of experiment"; "these experiments can be prolonged until the next Ordinary general chapter"). This is a rare admission of institutional tentativeness in a papal legislative document.
- The section on religious renewal (Perfectae Caritatis) uses the language of "genuine knowledge of their original spirit" to argue for reform — innovation is authorised precisely by return to origins, not departure from them.
- The missionary section opens with the striking claim that "the whole Church may become truly missionary and the entire People of God become aware of its missionary obligation" — making mission a universal ecclesiological category, not merely the task of specialist institutes.
- Laypeople appear explicitly in several provisions: they may join personal prelatures, serve on pastoral councils, participate in missionary work with professional training, and exercise consultation in religious governance. This is notable given the document's otherwise clerical structure.
- The document ends on the practical challenge of funding missions, noting that "voluntary contributions … are not at all sufficient" and mandating a "fitting contribution … made annually by the diocese itself." Spiritual urgency is directly linked to material obligation.

**Overall character.** Ecclesiae Sanctae reads as the Church's administrative engine turning over after the Council — important less for theological novelty than for the mechanics of translation from conciliar vision into institutional practice. Its key contribution is procedural: it establishes the habits, structures, and feedback loops (episcopal conferences → Apostolic See) through which post-conciliar Catholicism was to govern itself in the years before the 1983 Code of Canon Law.
