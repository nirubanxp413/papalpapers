# Peregrinantes (Benedict XIV, 1749)

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The proclamation of the 1750 Holy Year (Jubilee) calls all Catholics to sincere penitence, pilgrimage, and spiritual renewal, framed as a "new kind of war" against sin and the enemies of salvation.
- **Summary:** Benedict XIV announces a universal Jubilee for the year 1750, beginning at the winter solstice. The document establishes its theological foundation (universal sinfulness, the Church as merciful mediator, the treasury of merits), specifies the practical requirements for gaining indulgence (confession, communion, church visitations in Rome), and calls bishops, secular rulers, and all faithful into coordinated action. The tone escalates from penitential exhortation to military metaphor — pilgrims are "soldiers," the journey a "spiritual war" against pride, impurity, and cupidity — while closing with a diplomatic appeal to Catholic princes to protect and provision pilgrims.
- **Response to:** The document responds to (a) general human sinfulness and indifference to the Church's repeated warnings; (b) the close of a "long war" among nations, whose temporal peace the Pope hopes will be channelled into spiritual renewal; (c) the Church's own tradition of centennial then twenty-five-year jubilees established by "Our predecessors."
- **Prescriptive towards:** (a) The imminent Holy Year of 1750 and the pilgrimage it requires; (b) the hoped-for return of those who "long ago, deceived by lies of the devil, left the Church" — i.e., schismatics and heretics — through the example of Catholic unity; (c) future Judgment Day, invoked repeatedly as the ultimate horizon demanding present action.
- **Tension:** Two interlocking tensions run through the text. First, a spiritual tension: the Church "continually looks after its members" and "generously offers indulgences at all times," yet "few listen" and "many are entangled with evil affections" — the tension between grace freely offered and habitually refused. Second, a social/institutional tension: those who have left the Church "do not deny that the foundations of each individual sect are weak," yet refuse to return — the tension between intellectual recognition of Catholic claims and willful schismatic persistence. A secondary practical tension appears around physical limitation: pilgrims who cannot complete all visitation requirements are still granted the indulgence "as if they had actually fulfilled all the conditions."

## Unstructured

**Opening frame — the pilgrim condition:** The document opens by naming its own title: "we are pilgrims from the Lord and seekers after our future homeland." This is not merely rhetorical; the entire structure rests on the double meaning of pilgrimage — literal (travel to Rome) and eschatological (movement toward eternal life). The opening paragraph is notably stark in its universalism of guilt: "we all have sinned," "our conscience bears witness," "we must endure His scourges." The Pope includes himself in this "we," which gives the document an unusual confessional quality before it shifts to authoritative proclamation.

**Argument structure:** The document moves through a clear arc: (1) universal sinfulness and Church mercy ignored → (2) historical rationale for jubilees (centennial → 25-year cycle) → (3) proclamation of the 1750 Jubilee and its theological grounding → (4) specific practical requirements for the indulgence → (5) exhortation to pilgrims not to let "comforts of home" or "labor of the journey" deter them → (6) description of Rome as transformed from pagan Babylon into the citadel of Christianity → (7) appeal to lapsed Christians to return → (8) militarized call to bishops → (9) appeal to secular rulers.

**The "New Rome" passage as rhetorical pivot:** Section 9 is the document's most elaborate set-piece. Rome is presented as a city whose pagan identity has been wholly converted: "impious games and mad spectacles of the circus were obliterated," "shrines of false deities overturned," pagan temples "converted into Christian churches." This serves dual purposes: it argues for Rome as the appropriate destination of pilgrimage (the physical evidence of Christianity's victory) and implicitly argues for the Catholic Church's superior institutional continuity against those who have "left" it.

**Military metaphor as structural escalation:** Beginning in section 11, the document shifts register sharply. "A new kind of war against the enemies of our salvation must now be waged." Bishops are addressed as generals ("sound the trumpets," "declare a spiritual war"), pilgrims as soldiers carrying arms (vigils, prayers, fasting, almsgiving). The enemies are internal: "the license of thinking and acting," "luxury and pride," "cupidity for gain," "impurity," "enmity," "hatreds." The military framing is explicitly spiritualised — "What are the arms that Satan fears if not the vigils of the pious?" — but the institutional demand is clear: the jubilee is not a private devotional act but a mobilisation of the whole Church body.

**Tone:** Authoritative but with pastoral registers embedded. The document oscillates between papal decree (sections 5–6, 15–16, with formal binding language) and direct pastoral appeal ("Rouse your enthusiasm," "Let not the comforts of home hold you back"). The closing appeal to Catholic princes is diplomatic — deferential in address, but functionally directive, assigning them the role of providing pilgrims with safe passage, hospitality, and food.

**Notable framing — eschatological urgency as motivator:** The approaching Judgment Day is invoked not as threat alone but as clarifying lens. Those who have left the Church "will have no excuse on the Judgment Day." Pilgrims are asked to weigh "spiritual gift by the standards of the Christian faith" against worldly eagerness for "earthly treasure." Time is scarce: "our days are running out, and we shall be poured out like water on the earth." The jubilee is thus positioned as a rare and perhaps final opportunity for collective and individual spiritual settlement before the ultimate accounting.
