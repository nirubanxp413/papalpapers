# Ubi communis

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Social | Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Pope extends to the Bishop of Corsica and his colleagues the formal canonical warnings already issued to the French clergy regarding the Civil Constitution of the Clergy, condemning the intrusion of illegitimate bishops and calling the faithful to remain steadfast.
- **Summary:** Written as a brief (breve) to the Bishop of Aleria in Corsica, this document extends to Corsica the second monitorial letter already directed to France, which warned clergy involved in the Civil Constitution of the Clergy to recant within a defined period or face canonical penalties. Pius VI catalogues the specific offenses of the intruded bishop in Corsica — including an illicit consecration, exercise of jurisdiction without authority, dispensations, ordinations, and the administration of sacraments — while also praising those clergy and laity who have resisted, suffered imprisonment, or gone into exile. The document closes by granting the bishops of Corsica the same special faculties already extended to French bishops to assist their populations in this spiritual crisis.
- **Response to:** The introduction of the Civil Constitution of the Clergy of France into Corsica; the illicit election and consecration of an intruded bishop (Ignazio Francesco Guasco) in violation of ecclesiastical law; the suppression and obstruction of the Pope's first monitorial letter (of 13 April 1791) through violence and coercion; the continued exercise of episcopal functions by the intruded bishop and his delegates despite suspension; and the exile or harassment of legitimate bishops and their flocks.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The intruded bishop, his vicars, and clergy approved or delegated by him are warned that continued abuse of papal clemency will result in the formal declaration of the ultimate canonical penalty; the legitimate bishops of Corsica are directed to distribute this second warning letter throughout their dioceses and to unite their own pastoral efforts with the Pope's; all the faithful who have wavered are urged to recognize the gravity of the offenses and return; those who have remained firm are encouraged to persevere.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between legitimate ecclesiastical authority (rooted in communion with the Holy See) and the authority claimed by clergy who accepted the Civil Constitution — characterized in the text as intrusion, sacrilege, and nullity. A secondary tension exists between pastoral clemency (the repeated monitorial warnings allowing time for recantation) and the necessity of canonical discipline (the threatened declaration of ultimate penalties). A further tension is visible between official coercion and spiritual resistance: the text acknowledges that violence and injustice were used to suppress the papal letters, yet praises those who nonetheless held firm, suffered imprisonment, or accepted exile.

## Unstructured

**Key themes and flow of argument:**

The document is tightly structured around one operative act: the extension to Corsica of the canonical warnings already issued to France. The argument proceeds in a logical sequence — from the principle that shared error requires shared warning (§1), to the specific transmission of the letters (§2), to a detailed account of what the Roman See already knows has happened in Corsica (§§3–5), to a recognition of those who have resisted (§§6–7), to the purposes of the present letter (§8), and finally to the grant of special faculties and apostolic blessing (§9).

**Tone — authoritative with pastoral framing:**

The tone is decisively authoritative rather than pastoral or hortatory in the primary sense. The Pope speaks as a juridical authority issuing formal warnings with legal consequences (canonical censures, declared nullities, the threat of ultimate penalty). Yet the document carefully frames this authority in paternal and pastoral language: "paterne voci" (paternal voice), "Apostolica clemenza" (apostolic clemency), "amorevolmente" (lovingly). The warnings are cast not as punitive acts but as expressions of charity ("la carità dell'Apostolica Sede") that precede penalties. This dual register — juridical precision alongside paternal concern — is a consistent feature throughout.

**Notable framing:**

- **Nullity as a recurring legal concept:** The document explicitly states that acts performed by those lacking jurisdiction were declared null, and that the intruded bishop remains suspended. The language of nullity (nullità degli atti) is central to the document's authority claims.
- **Catalogue of specific offenses:** Rather than speaking in generalities, the text enumerates concrete acts: sacrilegious imposition of hands in Aix, election of vicars, granting of matrimonial dispensations, ordinations in December, administration of Confirmation. This specificity functions rhetorically to demonstrate that the Roman See is fully informed and that the offenses are not abstract.
- **Praise of the resistant as counter-narrative:** Sections 6 and 7 shift register to praise — those who recanted, the priests and laity who endured imprisonment, the five bishops who remained firm or were forced into exile. This serves to construct a counter-community of faithful Catholics in contrast to the intruded clergy, and implicitly to shame the latter by comparison.
- **Corsica as an extension of France:** The document explicitly equates the condition of Corsica's clergy and people with that of France's, granting the same faculties and applying the same warnings. This framing positions Corsica not as a unique case but as a subsidiary theatre of the same broader ecclesiastical crisis.
- **Time-bound warnings:** The references to 60-day intervals for the second and third warnings situate the document within a formal canonical process, not a spontaneous intervention. The document communicates that procedure is being followed and that the intruded clergy have been and are being given opportunities to repent before the final penalty falls.
