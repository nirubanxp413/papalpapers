# Quam graviter

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Pope protests three Royal Council decrees that subordinate Church authority to secular power, urgently calling French bishops to petition the King for redress.
- **Summary:** Writing to French archbishops and bishops assembled in Paris, Clement XIII expresses deep shock and distress at three decrees ("Arrêts") issued by the Royal Council of France on 24 May 1766. He argues that if the Church must defer entirely to secular authority to enforce obedience among the faithful, the divine power of the Church is fatally undermined. He is equally alarmed that the secular power has claimed the right to re-examine and reform the Constitutions of Religious Orders without consulting the Holy See. Believing the Most Christian King was not fully aware of the abuses these edicts would generate, the Pope charges the assembled bishops with presenting the evidence of those abuses to the King directly, and announces he is writing separately to the King to express his grief and urge a receptive hearing.
- **Response to:** Three Royal Council decrees of 24 May 1766 that the Pope describes as subjecting the Church's exercise of its rights and its ability to demand obedience from the faithful to the approval of secular authority, and that claim secular jurisdiction over the Constitutions of Religious Orders without reference to the Holy See.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The French bishops are directed to appear before the King, present vivid evidence of the abuses the edicts would cause, and solicit the King's royal protection of Church authority; the Pope also anticipates a parallel direct appeal from himself to the King.
- **Tension:** The central tension is jurisdictional: the Pope insists that a clear line of demarcation must separate ecclesiastical from secular power, and that no secular authority may annul Church decrees on faith, discipline, or moral norms. A secondary tension concerns governance of Religious Orders — whether reform of their Constitutions belongs to the Holy See alone or may be undertaken by the Crown.

## Unstructured

*Quam graviter* is an extremely brief and urgent diplomatic intervention rather than a doctrinal exposition. Its entire argument rests on one ecclesiological premise stated as a rhetorical question: "what will remain of the divine power of the Church" if its authority is entirely subject to secular approval? The document names no theological sources and cites no scripture; its authority derives from the Petrine office itself ("questa Santa Sede del beato Pietro").

The tone moves quickly from personal anguish ("colpiti e sconcertati" — struck and bewildered) to political strategy. The Pope does not denounce the King personally; he expressly attributes the harmful decrees to a failure of information rather than ill will, noting the King's "great rectitude and singular respect towards the Church." This framing is a tactical move: it preserves the relationship and opens a path to remedy without forcing a rupture.

The document has two interlocking concerns. The first is general: if secular authority can override any Church decree touching faith, discipline, or conduct, the Church's independence is illusory. The second is particular: the reform of Religious Orders has historically required consultation with Rome, and the new decrees bypass that requirement entirely. The Pope cites prior precedents ("esempi, non così rari, in codesto Regno" — examples not so rare in that Kingdom) as evidence that France itself has acknowledged Rome's role in the past.

The rhetorical strategy is notably diplomatic rather than confrontational. Clement does not issue a condemnation or threaten ecclesiastical sanctions; instead, he mobilises the assembled bishops as intercessors, reminds them of their pastoral duty ("a voi compete il dovere"), praises their zeal effusively, and coordinates his own parallel letter to the King. The document closes with a warm apostolic blessing, underscoring that this is a letter of crisis management within an ongoing relationship, not a declaration of conflict.
