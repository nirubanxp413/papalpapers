# IN EMINENTI

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document formally condemns and prohibits Freemasonry and all analogous secret societies, forbidding Catholics from joining or aiding them under pain of automatic excommunication, on grounds of both spiritual danger and harm to civil order.
- **Summary:** Issued from Rome in 1738, this papal constitution declares that certain secret societies — variously known as "liberi Muratori," "Francs Massons," or similar names — have spread widely and are condemned. The document charges that these gatherings unite men of every religion under oaths of secrecy sworn on Scripture, threatening both the stability of civil governments (many of which have already banned them) and the spiritual salvation of souls. All Catholics, lay and clerical, are strictly prohibited from joining, supporting, sheltering, or promoting these societies; transgressors incur immediate excommunication reserved to the Pope himself. Bishops, local ordinaries, and inquisitors are empowered to prosecute violators as strongly suspected of heresy.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to the public rumor ("rumore publico") and actual spread of these secret societies across many regions, noting that their secretive oaths and multi-religious membership have already aroused widespread suspicion among the prudent and that various secular powers had already banned them as threats to the security of their kingdoms.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs future conduct by commanding universal Catholic abstention from these societies; it empowers bishops, ordinaries, and inquisitors to continue prosecuting any transgressors, and declares the constitution perpetually valid, anticipating ongoing attempts to join or propagate such societies.
- **Tension:** The primary tension is between the Church's demand for full doctrinal and institutional loyalty — membership in any one religion with no secret cross-religious fraternal bonds — and the appeal of these societies, which the document frames as offering only a superficial appearance of natural honesty ("affectata quadam contenti honestatis naturalis specie") while concealing morally suspect, possibly heretical, and politically dangerous activities behind oaths of secrecy. A secondary tension exists between ecclesiastical authority and the secular sphere: the document acknowledges that civil powers have already acted against these societies, but asserts the Church's independent right and duty to condemn them on canonical and spiritual grounds.

## Unstructured

**Theme and argument flow**

The document opens with a statement of the Pope's pastoral duty, set at a moment of difficulty ("difficillimis hisce temporibus perturbationum"), to guard orthodoxy and repel dangers from the Catholic world. This framing positions the condemnation not as reactive policing but as proactive shepherding.

The argument then unfolds in two main moves. The first (§1) is diagnostic and sociological: these societies have spread widely; they admit men of every religion; they operate under solemn oaths of secrecy on Scripture, enforced by threat of severe penalties; because of these features they have already generated strong suspicion ("vehementem... suspicionem") among prudent and upright people — the document treats this popular suspicion as its own evidence, citing the maxim that those who hate the light must be doing evil. The further fact that secular rulers have already banned them is cited as corroboration.

The second move (§2–4) is juridical and prescriptive. The Pope declares that these societies cause "gravissima damna" — grave harms — to both temporal civil peace and the spiritual salvation of souls, and on that dual basis condemns and prohibits them by full apostolic authority. The specific harm to souls is not spelled out in detail; the condemnation rests on the societies' structural features (secrecy, cross-religious membership, oaths) rather than on documented doctrinal errors.

**Tone**

The tone is markedly authoritative and juridical throughout: the vocabulary is that of formal legal condemnation ("damnamus et prohibemus," "districte... praecipimus," "sub poena excommunicationis... ipso facto"). There is no pastoral softening, no invitation to dialogue, and no appeal to the conscience of potential members. The document's rhetorical logic is declaratory: these things are evil by their nature, the Church has acted, and no further deliberation is invited. The only nod to reasons not fully stated is the phrase "aliisque de iustis, ac rationabilibus causis nobis notis" — "and for other just and reasonable causes known to us" — which reserves unexpressed justifications to the papal office.

**Notable framing**

The document deploys two striking biblical images: those who join such societies are like "thieves breaking into the house" ("fures Domum perfodiant") and like "foxes trying to destroy the vineyard" ("Vulpium vineam demoliri nitantur"). These metaphors frame Freemasons not merely as dissenters or seekers of natural virtue, but as covert adversaries of the Church acting under a pleasing exterior. The phrase "affectata honestatis naturalis species" — a contrived outward appearance of natural honesty — is key: the document implicitly contests Enlightenment-style natural morality clubs by insisting their ethical veneer conceals moral danger.
