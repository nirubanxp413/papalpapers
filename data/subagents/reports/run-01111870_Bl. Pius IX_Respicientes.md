# Respicientes

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document protests the forcible seizure of papal temporal territories by the Piedmontese government, arguing that temporal sovereignty is indispensable to the Church's spiritual freedom, and pronounces excommunication on all who participated in the usurpation.
- **Summary:** Issued on November 1, 1870, *Respicientes* is Pius IX's formal protest against the final military occupation of Rome and the Papal States by the Kingdom of Piedmont-Sardinia, culminating in the fall of Rome on September 20, 1870. The pope rehearses a long history of Piedmontese aggressions — legislative attacks, military invasions, manipulation of plebiscites, imprisonment of bishops — and frames each episode as a violation of both divine and human law. He formally rejects every proposal that he voluntarily surrender temporal power, invokes the precedent of Pius VII against Napoleon, declares all usurpations null and void, and excommunicates those responsible. He closes by calling bishops and the faithful to prayer and expressing confidence that God will ultimately deliver the Church.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to (a) decades of Piedmontese legislative and military aggression against the Papal States beginning in the 1850s; (b) the September 8, 1870 letter from the King of Piedmont asking Pius to surrender temporal power voluntarily; (c) the military occupation of Rome on September 20, 1870; and (d) subsequent acts of the occupying government — suppression of Church rights, harassment of clergy, publication of anti-Catholic literature, violation of papal residences, and a plebiscite the pope characterises as fraudulent and illegitimate.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates (a) future acts by the occupying government to "confirm these usurpations" and declares them condemned and invalid in advance; (b) the possibility that the usurpers may repent and return ("the wandering sons, returning to Us"); (c) the need for universal episcopal and lay prayer to obtain divine assistance; and (d) the ongoing duty of Pius and his successors to transmit the rights and dominions of the Holy See undiminished.
- **Tension:** Two central tensions permeate the text. First, a political-ecclesial tension between the Church's claim that temporal sovereignty over Rome is divinely ordained and necessary for the free exercise of spiritual authority, and the Piedmontese state's claim to have legitimate grounds (national unity, public order) for absorbing those territories. Second, a pastoral-juridical tension between Pius's stated desire to show paternal charity to those who have wronged the Church — even comparing them to "the rebellious Absalom" whom a father still loves — and his juridical obligation to pronounce excommunication and refuse any compromise that would diminish the Church's rights.

## Unstructured

**Flow of argument.** The document is structured as a formal legal-cum-pastoral protest. It opens with lament (quoting Lamentations), then proceeds historiographically: Pius narrates the entire sequence of Piedmontese aggressions from 1850 to 1870 as a single continuous campaign, citing his own prior encyclicals and addresses as a documentary record. This narrative move is important — it positions the 1870 occupation not as a sudden event but as the culmination of a twenty-year conspiracy, lending cumulative moral weight to the protest. The middle sections describe the occupation of Rome and its immediate consequences in vivid, almost journalistic terms (wicked books sold openly, shameful pictures, cardinal forced to flee), before moving to the formal declarations of protest and excommunication. The close shifts register entirely, becoming pastoral and prayerful.

**Argument about temporal power.** The most developed theological argument concerns why the pope cannot voluntarily surrender temporal authority. The text makes this claim through two moves: (1) citation of Pius VII's 1809 apostolic letter, using the Naboth parable — Naboth refused to surrender his ancestral vineyard to the king and defended it with his blood; the pope cannot surrender an inheritance held "for many centuries." (2) A functional argument: temporal rule is the necessary condition for free, safe exercise of spiritual authority; without it, "the highest Ruler of the Church would be unable to offer help to the Catholics spread all over the earth." The document presents temporal sovereignty not as a perk of power but as a structural requirement of the universal pastorate.

**Tone.** The document is predominantly authoritative and juridical, but with significant pastoral registers. The juridical voice dominates: Pius formally declares, protests, renews, confirms, and pronounces excommunication. The pastoral voice surfaces in lament (opening quotation from Lamentations, the analogy to weeping over children), in the description of the king's proposals as deserving pity rather than anger, and most clearly in section 14 where Pius says he "desires nothing more than that the wandering sons, returning to Us, may be embraced with paternal charity." The tension between these two registers — legal condemnation and parental longing — is emotionally and rhetorically central to the document.

**Notable framing.** Pius frames the Piedmontese government's actions through a rhetoric of hypocrisy and pretence throughout: the king's September 8 letter used "fallacious sophistries," professed "the images of a loving son and of a loyal Catholic," and offered "empty promises." The plebiscites are described as "feigned," "ludicrous," and a form of vote-stealing. This consistent framing — not merely that the government acted wrongly, but that it acted fraudulently while presenting a false face of legitimacy — elevates the moral charge from aggression to apostasy. The text also invokes a providential framework: enemies "prevailed" temporarily (quoting Lamentations and Psalm 129), but the Lord's hand is "not shortened" and He will deliver His Church as He has before. St. Bernard's letter to King Conrad is quoted in closing to ground this confidence historically and scripturally.

**Scriptural and patristic texture.** The document is heavily interlaced with biblical and patristic citations: Lamentations 1:16 (opening lament), Wisdom 6:8–9 (no partiality with the powerful), the Naboth story via St. Ambrose, 2 Corinthians 6:14–15 (no fellowship between light and darkness), Psalm 129 (enemies have not prevailed), and St. Bernard. This intertextual density positions the current crisis within the long scriptural tradition of persecution and deliverance, and gives the excommunication the weight of apostolic and divine authority rather than mere institutional self-interest.
