# Meeting with the Representatives of Science (Regensburg Address)

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Faith and reason are not opponents but intrinsically unified through the Greek-Christian synthesis grounded in *Logos*, and modern reason's self-imposed limitation to the empirically falsifiable impoverishes both science and humanity.
- **Summary:** Delivered as a university lecture at Regensburg, Benedict XVI argues that the encounter between Biblical faith and Greek philosophical inquiry — particularly the identification of God with *Logos* (reason/word) — was historically decisive and remains binding. He traces three stages of "dehellenization" (Reformation, liberal theology of Harnack, and contemporary cultural pluralism) as misguided attempts to strip Christianity of its rational heritage. Against these, he calls for a *broadening* of reason — not a retreat from science, but an enlargement of its scope to include the questions of God, ethics, and religion — so that genuine interreligious and intercultural dialogue becomes possible and theology retakes its rightful place within the university.
- **Response to:** The document responds to (1) the modern reduction of reason to positivistic, empirically falsifiable science (traced to Kant and the natural sciences), (2) the Harnackian liberal theological programme of dehellenizing Christianity to make it compatible with modern reason, (3) the contemporary claim that the Greek-Christian synthesis was only a contingent "inculturation" that other cultures need not accept, and (4) the question of violence in religion, introduced through the 1391 dialogue of Manuel II Paleologus with a Persian Muslim on the incompatibility of coercion with faith.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The text anticipates and directs toward (1) a renewed dialogue of cultures and religions premised on a shared, enlarged rationality, (2) the re-inclusion of theology as a full scientific partner within the modern university, (3) overcoming the self-imposed limitation of reason by recovering its "vast horizons" so that ethics and religion regain their power to create community rather than becoming purely private matters, and (4) recognizing that "not to act reasonably, not to act with *logos*, is contrary to the nature of God" as the common ground for that dialogue.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between a *narrow* modern reason (mathematical-empirical, excluding God and ethics as unscientific) and a *broad* classical reason (Greek-Christian *Logos*, encompassing faith, ethics, and metaphysics). Secondary tensions include: the voluntarist image of an arbitrary God unbound by reason (found in both late medieval Islamic thought, per Ibn Hazm, and in certain Christian scholastic strands after Duns Scotus) versus the Church's insistence on an analogy between divine reason and human reason; and the claim that the Greek heritage of Christianity is culturally contingent versus the text's assertion that the fundamental decisions relating faith to reason are intrinsic to faith itself.

## Unstructured

**Flow of argument:** The lecture opens with a nostalgic evocation of the *universitas* — the integrated community of reason across disciplines — that Benedict experienced as a young professor. This is immediately the normative ideal against which the rest of the lecture measures the fragmentation of modern thought. The Manuel II Paleologus quotation serves as a rhetorical entry point: the emperor's statement that "not to act with *logos* is contrary to God's nature" encapsulates the thesis. Benedict then traces this *logos*-theology back through John's Gospel prologue, the Septuagint, and the Old Testament's progressive monotheism, arguing that the Greek-Biblical encounter was neither accidental nor merely historical but theologically necessary.

The middle section surveys three waves of "dehellenization": (1) the Reformation's *sola scriptura*, which tried to free faith from philosophical accretions; (2) Harnack's reduction of Christianity to a humanitarian moral message and the consequent restriction of theology to historical-critical method; (3) contemporary cultural-pluralist demands that other cultures be free to re-inculturate the Gospel without the Greek synthesis. Benedict grants partial validity to each impulse while rejecting each as ultimately self-defeating: they all collude with the modern reduction of reason that they claim to resist.

**Tone:** The tone is distinctly academic and professorial — this is a lecture, not a homily. Benedict situates himself as a former Bonn professor returning to his intellectual home, and the style is reflective and argument-driven rather than magisterial or pastoral. There is scholarly citation (Khoury's edition of the Byzantine dialogue, Arnaldez on Ibn Hazm, references to his own 1959 inaugural lecture and his book *Introduction to Christianity*). The tone is neither triumphalist nor polemical, but the argument is pointed: it is a "critique of modern reason from within," as Benedict explicitly names it.

**Notable framing:** Benedict frames the broadening of reason not as a religious imposition on secular culture but as what reason itself demands once it honestly confronts the limits of positivism. The question "why does matter have a rational structure that our minds can grasp?" cannot be answered by natural science and must be "remanded to philosophy and theology." This is presented as an internal scientific necessity, not an external religious claim. Similarly, the West's cultural identity — "Europe" itself — is defined as the product of the Greek-Biblical synthesis, making dehellenization not just a theological error but a civilizational one. The warning about "pathologies of religion and reason" when ethics is privatized signals the social stakes Benedict sees in the epistemological argument.

**On the Manuel II quotation and violence:** The text uses the emperor's blunt accusation against Mohammed primarily as a vehicle to introduce the rationality-of-God argument, not as a statement about Islam per se. Benedict explicitly notes in footnote 3 (added, it seems, after the controversy) that the quotation does not represent his personal view of the Qur'an and that he quoted it solely to introduce the theme of faith and reason. The deeper point is that coercion is incompatible with the nature of faith because "faith is born of the soul, not the body" — a statement applicable to any religious tradition.
