# Miranda Prorsus

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Church must guide and regulate motion pictures, radio, and television so that these powerful new means of communication serve truth, virtue, and the salvation of souls rather than becoming instruments of moral harm.
- **Summary:** Pius XII addresses the bishops of the world to set forth comprehensive norms governing the Catholic Church's engagement with the three dominant mass-communication technologies of the mid-twentieth century: motion pictures, radio, and television. The document affirms that these technologies are gifts of God capable of enlightening and ennobling humanity, yet warns that they can equally disfigure minds and enslave souls when abused. The encyclical lays down obligations for every actor in the chain — spectators, producers, directors, distributors, actors, clergy, national offices, civil authorities — and calls for organized national and international Catholic offices to classify, coordinate, and direct content in accordance with Christian morality. Special sections treat each medium's distinctive features and dangers, with television receiving particular attention for its penetration into the domestic sphere and its potential to corrupt children within the family home.
- **Response to:** (1) The two-decade track record of *Vigilanti cura* (1936) on motion pictures, which the document explicitly revisits as the model and occasion for extending norms to radio and television; (2) the petitions of bishops and Catholic laypeople engaged in these fields who requested updated guidance; (3) the recognized spread of television as a new phenomenon that had "taken tremendous steps forward in some nations" during Pius XII's own pontificate; (4) the stated conduct of those who "neither understand nor acknowledge the teaching authority of the Church" in media matters, those driven by profit, those holding "incorrect philosophies of art," and the explicit use of radio and television by "atheistic communism" to eradicate religious ideas from the minds of children.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) The establishment of national Catholic offices for motion pictures, radio, and television in every country, unified under a single committee or in close coordination; (2) the formation of an international association of those national offices, subject to Holy See approval; (3) the Christian formation of spectators — especially children and youth — so they can evaluate media with a "correct conscience"; (4) increased production and quality of Catholic radio programs and religious broadcasts; (5) the training of priests and laypeople to use these media competently for the apostolate; (6) civil authorities who are urged to regulate content in the public interest, protect youth, and support quality educational productions.
- **Tension:** (1) Between the God-given gift of technological communication and the human capacity to abuse it for evil — "from God . . . only good; from free will, the potential for darkness"; (2) between genuine artistic and creative freedom and the Church's insistence that art cannot be exempt from moral law ("technical and artistic norms" do not justify immoral content); (3) between the home as a protected sphere of family and religious life and television's power to "penetrate" that sphere with harmful material; (4) between the Church's claimed right to communicate the Gospel through all available media and the political suppression of religious content (the "aerial iron curtain" of atheistic states jamming Catholic broadcasts); (5) between industry economic pressures (profit, programming schedules, competitive demands) and moral obligations incumbent on producers, distributors, and theater managers.

## Unstructured

**Key themes and flow of argument**

The encyclical opens with a theological grounding: technology itself is a gift of God, and all human communication ultimately participates in the divine mission of spreading truth. This framework — that communicating truth is itself a quasi-priestly act — establishes the Church's claim to a special stake in mass media. The document then moves through three layers: (a) general principles applicable to all three media, (b) medium-specific norms for cinema, radio, and television in turn, and (c) a closing exhortation to clergy on pastoral responsibility.

The general-principles section (§§21–74) is philosophically the most substantive. It invokes Aquinas on sense knowledge as the pathway to intellectual truth, argues that the sight faculty is "nobler" than other senses and therefore uniquely capable of elevating the spirit, and concludes that cinema, radio, and television should primarily serve truth and virtue — in that order. The document is careful to distinguish legitimate freedom from "unchecked license," insisting that public authorities may regulate media without infringing civil liberty because media are shared social goods, not purely private expression.

The cinema section (§§75–114) is the most detailed and operationally specific, drawing heavily on *Vigilanti cura*. It assigns moral responsibility up and down the production chain: authors, producers, distributors, theater managers, and actors each receive their own paragraph of obligation. The film-classification office is treated as the centrepiece institutional mechanism. Notably, commercial posters advertising films are also explicitly condemned if they arouse scandal — an unusual level of granularity. Catholic film critics are told they may not omit moral appraisal from reviews.

The radio section (§§115–144) shifts in tone to be somewhat more encouraging, acknowledging radio's unique power to cross "all frontiers" and reach people in their homes. The metaphor of a household guest is used for radio programs: listeners should "give the same thoughtful and prudent invitation that you would extend to a friend." The section explicitly names the Cold War context: jamming by atheistic states to prevent the Gospel from reaching their populations is condemned as a form of persecution, and listeners are urged to support Catholic programs as an act of solidarity with the oppressed.

The television section (§§145–166) is the most theologically anxious. The document's language noticeably escalates: television is said to be capable of bringing "poisoned air" into the home itself, and the metaphor of an infectious disease penetrating the domestic sphere is used twice. The home is cast as the final sanctuary, and television's unique danger is precisely its intimacy — that parents cannot easily supervise what enters through "the small white screen." The document acknowledges that industry representatives claim their scheduling contracts make comprehensive moral programming impossible, but refuses to accept that as justification for delay.

**Tone**

The document combines an authoritative-regulatory voice (issuing specific institutional mandates for national offices, film-rating committees, clerical advisers) with a pastoral-exhortatory voice (direct appeals to "dear young," to parents, to priests). The authoritative register dominates — this is a document that issues directives and assigns duties — but Pius XII consistently frames those duties within a providential narrative: technology is humanity's gift from God, the Church's role is stewardship, and harm arises solely from human misuse of divine gifts.

**Notable framing**

- The patron saint designation (St. Gabriel the Archangel as patron of electronic communication) is invoked as an institutional act by the Pope, linking the apostolic mission of the herald-angel directly to modern telecommunications workers.
- The analogy of industrial machinery enslaving workers (§18) is used as a historical precedent: just as the Industrial Revolution reduced men to "serfdom," unchecked communication technology risks enslaving not just physical but spiritual capacities.
- The classification of cinema theaters as "schools" (quoting Pius XI, §54) frames entertainment not as leisure but as pedagogy, which then justifies the Church's claim to regulate it as she would any educational institution.
- Throughout, the document treats the audience — especially youth — as essentially passive and susceptible, in need of formation before exposure. The remedy is consistently institutional: offices, committees, classifications, and trained advisers, rather than individual discernment alone.
