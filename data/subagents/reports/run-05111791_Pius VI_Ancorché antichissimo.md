# Ancorché antichissimo

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Social
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Holy See formally and perpetually protests the French National Assembly's decree of 14 September 1791 annexing Avignon and the Comtat Venaissin, declaring it null, unjust, and violent, and reaffirming the Pope's sovereign rights over those territories.
- **Summary:** This chirografo (a formal document signed by the Pope's own hand) approves and confirms a solemn protest submitted by Giacomo Borsari, General Commissioner of the Apostolic Chamber, against France's annexation of Avignon and the Comtat Venaissin. Pius VI narrates a long sequence of protests, diplomatic notifications, and legal declarations already made since 1790, recounts the history of legitimate papal sovereignty over these territories (established by treaty in 1228 and purchase in 1348, confirmed by emperors and thirteen French kings), and argues that both the annexation decree and the alleged popular consent of the subjects are null — the former violating the law of nations, the latter having been extracted through terror, assassination, and manipulation. The Pope explicitly forbids any future negotiation over "compensation," invoking a solemn papal oath never to alienate this patrimony, and directs the document to be preserved in perpetuity in the Secret Archive of the Apostolic Chamber.
- **Response to:** The French National Assembly's decree of 14 September 1791 declaring Avignon and the Comtat Venaissin incorporated into France; the rebellion of Avignonese subjects beginning in late 1789; the expulsion of the papal vice-legate Monsignor Casoni in June 1790; repeated French military incursions framed as "peacekeeping"; and a series of violent acts (assassinations, sieges, arson, looting) carried out in the territories by armed factions.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The preservation of all prior protests and declarations in the Apostolic Chamber's Secret Archive "in perpetual memory"; the instruction that the Cardinal Camerlengo register this chirografo with the same formalities as previous approvals; the firm direction that no future negotiation over compensation or indemnity be entertained ("in no way could the alienation of legitimate sovereignty over those provinces ever be justified"); and a standing instruction that the protest be renewed each time circumstances require it, embedded in every relevant document.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the Holy See's claim of legitimate, ancient, and legally unimpeachable sovereign authority (confirmed by five centuries of possession, treaties, and oaths) and the French National Assembly's claim of authority derived from popular consent and national rights. The document explicitly frames this as a conflict between lawful sovereignty and revolutionary usurpation. A secondary tension exists between Pius VI's stated "paternal clemency" toward the Avignonese — he is described as having offered general pardon to the rebellious subjects — and the subjects' continued defiance, which the document characterises as ingratitude and contempt. A third tension, implied throughout, is between the principle that subjects may not change rulers at their own caprice and the emerging revolutionary doctrine that popular will is sovereign.

## Unstructured

**Nature of the document.** This is not an encyclical or a pastoral letter — it is a legal-diplomatic instrument: a chirografo (a document authenticated by the Pope's personal signature) formally approving and confirming a series of earlier protests. Much of the body text is a direct quotation of Borsari's protest of 27 October 1791, which Pius VI then endorses in §4 in full. The document's tone is juridical and authoritative throughout: there is no pastoral register, no theological argument, and almost no appeal to Scripture or tradition. It is the voice of a temporal sovereign asserting rights under the law of nations.

**Flow of the argument.** The document proceeds in five numbered sections. §1 introduces the context: a brief history of the rebellion and of the protests already lodged, culminating in the unexpected September 1791 decree. §§2–3 reproduce Borsari's extended protest, which builds a detailed legal case: (a) historical title — the Comtat acquired by treaty in 1228, Avignon purchased in 1348; (b) European recognition — emperors and thirteen consecutive French kings acknowledged the sovereignty; (c) the invalidity of France's claimed "rights" — the National Assembly itself twice voted against annexation before reversing course; (d) the invalidity of the alleged "free and solemn" consent — shown to have been extracted from a terrorised minority, not freely given by the general population. §4 is Pius VI's formal act of approval. §5 concerns the legal force of the chirografo itself, pre-empting any challenge to its validity.

**Key argumentative moves.** The protest makes heavy use of historical specificity — dates, names, vote counts, treaty references — to establish that the annexation has no basis in law or fact. The invocation of the National Assembly's own prior votes (May 4 and May 14, 1791) against annexation is rhetorically striking: the Assembly is shown to have contradicted itself. The characterisation of the "free vote" of Avignon as the expression of "a thousand citizens — or perhaps brigands, foreigners, children or scoundrels hoping to escape the gallows" directly contests the revolutionary vocabulary of popular sovereignty on its own terms. The reminder that Pius VI had already sworn, following Pius II, never to alienate these territories elevates the issue beyond mere political interest to a matter of papal oath and ecclesiastical patrimony.

**What the document does not do.** It does not engage with the theological or spiritual reasons why the Church should hold temporal territories; it does not address the French Civil Constitution of the Clergy or any wider religious controversy with the Revolution; it does not appeal to Catholic princes to take military action. The recourse to "all Catholic princes" is diplomatic notification, not a call to arms. The document is entirely focussed on the narrow legal-diplomatic question of sovereignty over Avignon and the Comtat Venaissin.

**Tone.** Highly formal and legalistic. The Pope writes in the first person plural of sovereign authority ("Our motu proprio, in full awareness, in the fullness of Our supreme power"). The emotional register occasionally breaks through — the annexation decree is received "with great surprise and bitterness of soul" — but these moments are brief and subordinated to the juridical argument. The overarching posture is one of firm, non-negotiable refusal: the document systematically closes every procedural avenue through which the annexation might be legitimised.
