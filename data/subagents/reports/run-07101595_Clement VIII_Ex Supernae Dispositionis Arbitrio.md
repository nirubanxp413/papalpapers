# Ex Supernae Dispositionis Arbitrio

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Pope Clement VIII formally approves and canonically confirms the University of Mexico, granting it the same privileges, immunities, and statutes as the universities of Salamanca, Alcalá (Complutensis), and Lima, at the petition of King Philip II of Spain.
- **Summary:** This papal bull, issued from Frascati on 7 October 1595, responds to a petition by King Philip II ("Philippus Hispaniarum Rex Catholicus") to formally ratify the University of Mexico, which had already been operating in "Civitate Mexicana Novae Hispaniae" for several years. The document confirms all degrees previously conferred in theology, canon law, and other faculties, authorizes future conferral of degrees in those fields plus philosophy and civil law, and explicitly places the Mexican university on equal footing ("pariformiter et aeque principaliter, hac absque ulla prorsus differentia") with the universities of Salamanca, Alcalá, and Lima. The pope also prohibits any ecclesiastical or secular judge from disturbing the university's faculty, students, or officials, and declares any contrary action null and void.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to (1) the de facto existence of a general studium in Mexico City that had been operating "a pluribus annis" (for several years) conferring degrees in good faith but without formal apostolic authority, and (2) the humble supplication of King Philip II, who sought apostolic confirmation to ensure the university could proceed "cum maiori Dei gloria, ac eius sanctae fidei exaltatione" (with greater glory of God and exaltation of the holy faith).
- **Prescriptive towards:** The bull anticipates and directs: (1) future conferral of degrees at the University of Mexico under the same rigorous examination requirements as Salamanca and Alcalá ("praevio, ac diligenti, ac rigoroso examine, ac servatis servandis"); (2) governance and administration of the university according to the statutes, customs, and privileges of Salamanca, Alcalá, and Lima; (3) protection of the university's community from interference by any ecclesiastical or secular authority; and (4) the ongoing cultivation of Catholic faith among the "novi fidei Catholicae cultores" (new adherents to the Catholic faith) in the Indies.
- **Tension:** The text implicitly acknowledges a tension between the university's long-standing de facto operation (degrees already conferred and acted upon) and the lack of formal apostolic legitimacy — resolved by the bull's retroactive confirmation. A secondary tension underlies the document: the growing importance of the New World ("Indiae occidentales") as a frontier of Catholic mission requiring institutional intellectual infrastructure, set against the existing Old World university model (Salamanca, Alcalá) used as the normative benchmark.

## Unstructured

The document is structured as a formal papal bull in the standard chancery style: it opens by asserting the pope's pastoral office ("gregi Dominico praesidentes"), frames the act as flowing from that office's duty to care for the state and peace of universities, and then moves through a lengthy narrative clause explaining the circumstances before arriving at the operative grant.

**Key themes in the text:**

1. **Apostolic authority as the source of legitimacy.** The core rhetorical move is that the university's pre-existing activity (degrees, privileges, faculty) gains stability and lawfulness only through the pope's formal apostolic authority ("apostolica authoritate tenore praesentium concedimus"). The document does not question what was done before, but secures it retroactively and authorizes what comes next.

2. **The New World as a Catholic mission field.** King Philip's stated motivation, as relayed in the bull, is "quanto universae Reipublicae Christianae praesertim apud illos novos fidei Catholicae cultores talis universitas … utilitatis afferat" — how useful such a university is for the whole Christian commonwealth, especially among those new adherents of the Catholic faith. The university is framed not merely as an educational institution but as an instrument of evangelization and church consolidation in the Indies.

3. **Equivalence with Iberian universities.** A structurally significant feature is the explicit, repeated emphasis on full and equal parity — "pariformiter et aeque principaliter, hac absque ulla prorsus differentia" — with Salamanca, Alcalá (Complutensis), and Lima. The University of Mexico is not given lesser or derivative status; it is placed in the same category. This suggests awareness that such a claim needed to be stated emphatically, perhaps precisely because the colonial context might otherwise imply inferiority.

4. **Royal and papal coordination.** Philip II is both the temporal lord of the territories ("earum partium etiam dominus temporalis existit") and the petitioner. The document presents the grant as the pope acting "dum id Catholicorum Regum vota exposcunt" — when the wishes of Catholic kings require it. This frames the act as a harmonious exercise of spiritual and temporal authority, with the pope as the ultimate source of canonical validity.

5. **Legal comprehensiveness and non-obstante clauses.** A significant portion of the bull is devoted to overriding any contrary privileges, constitutions, apostolic letters, or local statutes — including the Apostolic Chancery's rules against conceding graces "ad instar" (in the likeness of other grants). This suggests the lawyers drafting the document were aware of potential canonical objections to granting equivalence with Salamanca by this mechanism, and were careful to preempt them.

**Tone:** The tone is authoritative and juridical throughout — this is a formal grant of privilege, not a pastoral letter. There is no hortatory or devotional language beyond the brief framing of the pastoral office at the opening and the closing invocation against violators ("indignationem Omnipotentis Dei, ac Beatorum Petri, et Pauli Apostolorum eius se noverit incursurum"). The document reads as institutional and administrative rather than spiritually exhortatory.

**Flow of argument:** Opening assertion of pastoral duty → summary of the existing situation (university operating de facto) → statement of Philip II's pious motivation and petition → operative grant (confirming past degrees, authorizing future ones, granting full parity with Salamanca/Alcalá/Lima) → protection clause (no one may disturb the university) → nullity clause (any contrary act is void) → non-obstante clauses overriding all contrary provisions → final warning against violation.
