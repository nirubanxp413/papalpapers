# The 350 Years of the Union of Uzhorod

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The 350th anniversary of the Union of Uzhorod is commemorated as both a spiritual milestone of restored communion with Rome and a call to ongoing ecumenical reconciliation between Eastern and Western Christianity.
- **Summary:** Writing on the occasion of the 350th anniversary of the 1646 Union of Uzhorod — by which 63 Byzantine-rite priests of the Eparchy of Mukacheve entered full communion with the See of Peter — John Paul II traces the historical lineage of this union from the Council of Florence (1439) through the Union of Brest (1596) and the subsequent growth of Greek Catholic eparchies in Europe and America. He honours the martyrdom of those who suffered under persecution (notably Bishop Theodore Romzha), celebrates the survival of Eastern liturgical tradition within Catholic unity, and pivots to an urgent ecumenical appeal: that the approaching Great Jubilee of 2000 be marked by forgiveness, the dialogue of truth and charity, and a renewed commitment to visible unity among all Christians.
- **Response to:** (1) The historical act of the Union of Uzhorod on 24 April 1646; (2) the tradition of Cyril and Methodius as heralds of unity between Constantinople and Rome; (3) decades of severe persecution and martyrdom suffered by the Greek Catholic Church, explicitly including the death of Bishop Theodore Romzha; (4) the prior teaching of Vatican II on ecumenism (*Unitatis redintegratio*, *Orientalium Ecclesiarum*, *Lumen gentium*) and John Paul II's own encyclical *Ut unum sint* (1995).
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) The forthcoming Great Jubilee of the Year 2000 and the examination of conscience it demands; (2) accelerated pursuit of "full reconciliation between the Churches"; (3) Eastern Catholic Churches fulfilling their "special duty" of fostering unity with Eastern Christians through fidelity to Eastern traditions, prayer, and example; (4) renewed formation of future pastors, liturgical vitality, catechesis, and practical charity within the Greek Catholic communities; (5) the forgiveness of "misunderstandings of centuries past" as an "indispensable condition" for Christian unity.
- **Tension:** (1) The ongoing division between Catholic and Orthodox Christians, described as openly contradicting Christ's will and damaging the proclamation of the Gospel; (2) the tension between Eastern Catholic Churches maintaining their own organisational structures and traditions while being fully in communion with Rome — a balance the document insists must be respected as a contribution to, not an obstacle in, ecumenical dialogue; (3) the legacy of violent persecution under which Greek Catholics suffered, set against the present call to forgiveness and reconciliation.

## Unstructured

The document is simultaneously a commemorative letter and a forward-looking ecumenical manifesto. Its argument moves in a clear arc: historical gratitude → martyrological honour → present opportunity → future imperative.

**Historical scaffolding.** The first two sections carefully construct a chain of ecclesial continuity: Council of Florence (1439) → Metropolitan Isidore of Kyiv → Union of Brest (1596) → Union of Uzhorod (1646) → growth of eparchies in Europe and the Americas. The effect is to frame the Uzhorod union not as an isolated or politically coerced event but as the natural fruition of a centuries-long ecumenical impulse rooted in the apostolic mission of Cyril and Methodius. The language is deliberately genealogical — "mustard seed," "flourishing offshoots," "vigorous tree" — giving the Greek Catholic tradition an organic, providential character.

**The martyrological turn.** Section 2 pivots abruptly from institutional history to suffering: "The events which followed the Union were filled with suffering and sorrow." Bishop Theodore Romzha is named explicitly as one who "did not hesitate to offer his life for the sheep of his flock." This is treated not as a historical tragedy but as a "precious patrimony" — a "marvellous lesson of faithfulness." The tone here is veneration rather than lament, and the rhetoric frames persecution as a form of witness that enriches the whole Catholic Church, not just the Greek Catholic communities.

**Ecclesiological argument: unity through diversity.** Section 3 contains the letter's central ecclesiological claim: that the survival of Eastern liturgical and disciplinary traditions within full communion with Rome is itself a demonstration of Catholicity. The document quotes Leo XIII to the effect that Eastern variety "adorns the whole Church" and "clearly shows the divine unity of the Catholic faith." This moves against any reading of union as absorption or Latinisation; the document is explicit that the Eastern churches' preservation of their traditions is a gift to universal Catholicity.

**Ecumenical urgency.** Sections 4 and 5 shift into explicit ecumenical exhortation, leaning heavily on Vatican II (*Unitatis redintegratio*) and *Ut unum sint*. The argument is that Christian division "provides a stumbling block to the world" and impedes the Church's sacramental function as "a sign of intimate union with God and of the unity of all mankind." Eastern Catholic Churches are assigned a specific ecumenical vocation: their dual fidelity to Eastern tradition and Roman communion makes them uniquely positioned as bridges to the Orthodox. The document is careful to note that this requires genuine respect for Eastern structures, not a one-sided demand.

**Tone.** The letter is pastoral but with a clear authoritative register — it issues directives ("there must be devised a new quality of evangelization") while wrapping them in affective language (gratitude, emotion, joy). The closing invocation of Mary as "Mother of unity and peace" and "Virgin of Pentecost" ties the ecumenical aspiration to Marian intercession, a characteristically Johannine-Pauline rhetorical gesture. The overall tone is celebratory but not triumphal: the suffering of the past is held alongside the hope for the future without allowing either to overshadow the other.

**Notable framing.** The Great Jubilee of 2000 functions as an implicit deadline or horizon throughout the letter's second half — creating urgency without despair. The phrase "purification of past memories" (drawn from *Ut unum sint*) is introduced as the spiritual mechanism by which forgiveness becomes possible, placing the ecumenical task firmly in the register of conversion and prayer rather than institutional negotiation alone.
