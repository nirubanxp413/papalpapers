# Communium Rerum — On St. Anselm of Aosta

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document commemorates St. Anselm as a model of doctrinal defense, obedience to the Apostolic See, and steadfast resistance against both external enemies of the Church and internal corruptions of faith — held up as a living mirror for the Church's present struggles.
- **Summary:** Pope Pius X issues this letter on the occasion of the eighth centenary of St. Anselm's death, praising Anselm's holiness, intellectual rigor, and unwavering defense of Church liberty and doctrine. The document weaves together a hagiographic portrait of Anselm with an extended exhortation to bishops of the present day, drawing explicit parallels between Anselm's conflicts with princes and heretics and the Church's current battles against hostile governments, secularism, and Modernism. The pontiff uses Anselm's example to press three intertwined calls: defense of Church liberty against secular encroachment, safeguarding orthodox doctrine against Modernist novelty, and deep unity between bishops and the Apostolic See.
- **Response to:** (1) Recent natural disasters in Italy and the outpouring of Catholic charity they occasioned; (2) the Pope's own sacerdotal jubilee and associated displays of Catholic devotion worldwide; (3) the ongoing persecution and legal suppression of the Church in various countries, including the expulsion of religious orders and restriction of Catholic public life; (4) the internal spread of Modernism ("this hot-bed of error and perdition"), previously condemned in the 1907 encyclical *Pascendi dominici gregis*; (5) secular governments claiming Church property and liberty in the name of patriotism and progress.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) Bishops are directed to hold firm against both secular hostility and internal doctrinal corruption, never seeking a "cowardly neutrality"; (2) the clergy and especially young clerics are urged back to the scholastic method of theology rooted in Anselm and Aquinas; (3) princes and rulers are addressed indirectly via Anselm's own letters, urging them to be protectors rather than masters of the Church; (4) pastoral leaders are warned against both excessive indulgence and excessive rigidity, with Anselm's long exile held as the model of prudent, obedient suffering.
- **Tension:** Three main tensions are explicit in the text: (a) the Church's sacred liberty vs. the encroachment of secular states claiming to act in the name of liberty and civilization; (b) reasoned faith (the scholastic tradition Anselm inaugurated) vs. both arrogant philosophical rationalism and timid fideist anti-intellectualism; (c) the need for pastoral firmness vs. the danger of overstepping into either excessive rigor or excessive indulgence toward error.

## Unstructured

**Central argument and flow:**

The letter opens with a note of consolation — the recent disasters have called forth a great wave of Catholic charity, which Pius takes as a sign of the enduring vitality of the Church. He then transitions to thanksgiving for demonstrations of Catholic devotion globally (North America centenary, Eucharistic Congress in Britain, Lourdes jubilee), reading all of these as confirming his pontifical program: "the return of the erring to God through Christ, and to Christ through the Church" (§4). This introductory movement is genuinely pastoral and warm in tone.

The occasion proper — the 800th anniversary of Anselm's death — is introduced in §5, where Anselm is framed as a "most vigorous exponent of Catholic truth and defender of the rights of the Church." The portrait that follows (§§6–11) is hagiographic in character: Anselm's harmony of humility and greatness, his capacity to calm enemies, his blending of the contemplative and active life, and his suffering of exile without bitterness. Every quality praised is implicitly offered as a template for the bishops being addressed.

Beginning at §12, the document pivots decisively into exhortation. The Pope names "how evil are the days on which we have fallen" (§13) and catalogues current persecutions: contempt shown to the Church during disaster relief, anti-clerical legislation, expulsion of religious orders, restriction of Catholic education, mockery of the faith, and the broader effort to exclude the Church from public life. The language here grows notably severe — the agents of this persecution are likened to their "father, who was a murderer from the beginning" (§13, citing John 8:44). The tone shifts from consolatory to combative.

Section §15 introduces the second front: the "intestine and domestic" war of Modernism, described as more dangerous precisely because it operates from within, trying to "corrupt the springs of Christian life and teaching." Modernism is called a "hot-bed of error and perdition" and a "cancer among the young generations" (§16). The diagnosis is familiar from *Pascendi* (referenced explicitly in §43): intellectual pride, the pestilential atmosphere of irreligion, and the "blind frenzy for novelty."

From §18, Anselm's own historical context is introduced as a structural parallel: the Church in his day faced similar internal laxity, secular overreach by kings and princes, and doctrinal confusion. This parallelism is the document's central rhetorical move — Anselm's age mirrors our own, and therefore Anselm's virtues prescribe our own response.

Sections §§21–35 draw heavily on Anselm's actual letters, quoting his exhortations to princes, his defiance of exile, his anguish at the weight of episcopal office, and his unwavering recourse to the Apostolic See. The accumulated quotations function less as historical illustration than as a series of moral imperatives addressed to present-day bishops. Particularly striking is the insistence on obedience to the Holy See as both the means and the guarantee of victory: "the obedient man will sing victory" (§34, citing Prov. 21:28).

Sections §§44–56 address Anselm's intellectual legacy more directly. The document identifies two opposing errors of his time — rationalist overreach (philosophy dissolving faith) and fideist cowardice (refusing rational engagement with doctrine) — and presents Anselm as the Catholic middle path. His formula — "I believe in order that I may understand" (*fides quaerens intellectum*) — is described as the foundation of scholastic theology, and Anselm is credited as the "precursor" of Thomas Aquinas and the whole scholastic tradition. Pius uses this to reinforce the program of Leo XIII's *Aeterni Patris* and his own *Pascendi*: a return to the scholastic method as the antidote to Modernist intellectualism.

The final sections (§§57–58) close with a direct invocation of Anselm's intercession and a reiteration of the call to vigilance, particularly against allowing Modernist teachers access to youth.

**Tone:** The document is predominantly authoritative — Pius speaks as a superior directing a hierarchy — but it is laced throughout with a pastoral register: expressions of gratitude, consolation in shared suffering, and genuine pathos in the passages recounting Anselm's own anguish. The extensive use of direct quotation from Anselm's letters gives the exhortations an indirect, almost ventriloquistic quality: Anselm speaks, and his words happen to say exactly what Pius wants to say to his bishops.

**Notable framing:**

- The letter uses Anselm's centenary not merely to honor a saint but to construct a typology: Anselm as the Church's response to a prior age of crisis becomes the model for the Church's current response. The parallelism is asserted rather than argued.
- The twin threats — external secular persecution and internal Modernist subversion — are presented as two faces of the same demonic war ("one of the many forms of the eternal war waged against divine truth," §17).
- Obedience to the Apostolic See is treated not just as a canonical obligation but as the spiritual source of perseverance and ultimate victory.
- The document's most quoted phrase, put forward as Anselm's own: "In this world God loves nothing more than the liberty of His Church" (§24), is used twice and offered as the summation of the entire letter's argument about the relationship between temporal authority and the Church.
