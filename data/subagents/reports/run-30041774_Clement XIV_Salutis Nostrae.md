# Salutis Nostrae

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The proclamation of a Holy Year of Jubilee for 1775, offering all the faithful a formal, institutionally structured occasion to seek full remission of sins through confession, communion, pilgrimage, and prayer.
- **Summary:** Clement XIV formally announces the Jubilee Holy Year beginning Christmas Eve 1774 and lasting through 1775. Drawing on the inherited theology of redemption — Christ's passion, the power of the keys entrusted to Peter, and the "treasury" of merits accumulated by Christ, Mary, the martyrs, and the saints — the document presents the Jubilee as a mechanism through which sinners may recover divine grace. It specifies precise conditions for indulgence (number of basilica visits, confession, communion, prescribed intentions), provides dispensations for pilgrims who fall ill or die en route, exhorts bishops to promulgate the year among their flocks and bring sections of them to Rome, and calls on Catholic rulers to ensure safe passage for pilgrims. The tone is simultaneously theological, pastoral, and administrative — equal parts spiritual invitation and canonical decree.
- **Response to:** The document situates itself within the long-standing apostolic tradition ("a constant concern of their Apostolic ministry") of calling sinners to repentance, and specifically to the 25-year cycle of Holy Years established by earlier popes as a regular mechanism for distributing indulgences. It explicitly responds to the permanent condition of human sinfulness — the faithful who "by the weakness of their nature and their own wickedness wretchedly abandon their excellent state as heirs of God" — and to the need to renew the Church's offer of mercy for each new generation ("so as not to deprive any generation of the great benefit of propitiation").
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document is directed toward the coming Jubilee year itself and anticipates: (1) a large assembly of pilgrims from many nations converging on Rome; (2) the participation of bishops in promulgating and shepherding their flocks; (3) the co-operation of Catholic emperors, kings, and princes in providing "safe roads and inns for the pilgrims"; and (4) collective prayer for the "exaltation of Holy Church, the destruction of heresies, peace among Catholic rulers, and the safety and quiet of the Christian people." It frames this universal gathering as a foreshadowing of "the triumph of the Cross and religion."
- **Tension:** The central spiritual tension is between human weakness and divine mercy — the inherited condition of sin ("ancient slavery to sin") set against the "infinite force of His passion and merits" and the institutional capacity of the Church to distribute that mercy. A secondary, implicit tension exists between private piety and public, structured religious observance: the document insists that difficulties and "labors" of pilgrimage are themselves spiritually meritorious, not obstacles, and urges the faithful to be no less zealous for the Jubilee than merchants are "greedy for profit or zealous for travel." A third, faint tension is named in passing: the pope's awareness of the burden of his office ("your striving in love and piety promises great aid and assistance to Ourselves... when you assist Us to support the weight of Our office"), suggesting an underlying appeal for ecclesial solidarity around papal authority.

## Unstructured

**Core argument and flow**

The document opens with a compressed theology of salvation: Christ's passion redeemed humanity from sin; He gave Peter the power of the keys; the popes, as successors, have exercised this power by calling sinners to repentance; the 25-year Jubilee cycle is the formalized expression of this perennial apostolic duty. This theological prologue (§1–2) is brief — more of a legitimating frame than a developed argument — and quickly gives way to the formal proclamation (§2–3): the Jubilee is announced for 1775, the conditions are stated with canonical precision (number of visits, qualifications for pilgrims, the required intentions when praying).

**Tone: authoritative-pastoral hybrid**

The document oscillates between a declarative, juridical register ("We proclaim and promulgate," "We grant and impart," "We decree") and a genuinely pastoral, almost homiletic voice. §5 is the most rhetorically rich passage: it deploys sensory imagery of Rome — the sight of the apostles' tombs, blood-stained earth, the majesty of churches, the "tears of others who bewail their sins" — as a spiritual argument for pilgrimage. The exhortation "go up to the city of Sion and be filled with the riches of the Lord's house" reads more like preaching than canon law. This is unusual: the document is formally a bull-like proclamation but contains a sustained pastoral appeal.

**The "treasury of the Church" as central mechanism**

The document's implicit ecclesiology rests on the idea of a spiritual treasury — Christ's merits, Mary's merits, those of the martyrs and "all good men" — that the pope holds "in dispensing" and can "unlock" and "open" to the faithful. This is stated explicitly: "We shall unlock all the riches of mercy... We shall open for you the rich store of satisfaction." The Jubilee is, mechanically, the opening of this treasury on a grand scale. The communion of saints is invoked not as a devotional sentiment but as a structural argument: because we are "gathered together in the one body of the Church," the merits of the saints can benefit ordinary sinners, and "we can benefit each other."

**Notable framing: the Jubilee as spectacle and foreshadowing**

The document frames the gathering of pilgrims in Rome as itself theologically meaningful — "the most delightful and beautiful sight, foreshadowing in public view the triumph of the Cross and religion" (§5). The Jubilee is not merely pastoral administration; it is eschatological theatre, a visible anticipation of ultimate Christian triumph. This is the document's most ambitious rhetorical move, elevating a logistical religious event into a prefigurement of the end-times vindication of the faith.

**Secular authority as instrument**

§7 is notable for its direct address to "the emperor-elect and all Catholic kings and princes," asking them to "act in unison with the bishops" and provide infrastructure for pilgrims. The document offers a transactional rationale: this "will benefit the peace of the realm and... God Himself will be appeased and favorable towards them." The relationship between Church and Catholic states is assumed to be cooperative; secular rulers are cast as instruments of pastoral implementation, with temporal benefits promised in return for their co-operation.

**What is absent**

The document contains no reference to specific heresies by name, no explicit theological controversy, no named political events or conflicts, and no discussion of internal Church reform. The phrase "destruction of heresies" appears once, in the list of prayer intentions pilgrims must hold, but it is formulaic rather than targeted. The document is entirely forward-looking toward the Jubilee event itself; it does not retrospect on Clement's pontificate or address any crisis.
