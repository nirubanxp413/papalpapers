# Cum Religiosi

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** Widespread ignorance of the fundamental mysteries of the faith among laypeople renders them unfit to receive the sacraments, and bishops must redouble efforts to ensure effective catechetical instruction throughout their dioceses.
- **Summary:** Benedict XIV writes to Italian bishops after learning that many laypeople travelling to Rome to obtain marriage dispensations are found to be ignorant even of the core mysteries of faith required for salvation ("those matters which must be known by necessity of means"), and therefore cannot worthily receive the Sacraments of Penance and the Altar. The Pope exonerates the bishops of personal fault, attributing the ignorance to the obstinacy, sluggishness, or neglect of the laity themselves. He nonetheless urges bishops to renew their zeal, invoking the example of St. Charles Borromeo who persisted despite limited fruit, and provides a detailed programme of catechetical remedies drawing on the Council of Trent, synodal statutes, and the pedagogical method of St. Augustine.
- **Response to:** Reports from "religious men devoted to the improvement of divine worship" that pilgrims arriving in Rome for marriage dispensations are ignorant of basic articles of faith; prior papal encyclicals (including the letter of January 16th to the cardinals-archpriests and an earlier encyclical at the start of Benedict's pontificate) that attempted to address the same deficit.
- **Prescriptive towards:** A renewed and systematic programme of catechesis: schoolmasters and mistresses teaching Christian doctrine on fixed days; confessors instructing penitents who lack necessary knowledge; priests instructing couples before marriage; heads of households ensuring instruction of their children and servants; public recitation of acts of Faith, Hope and Charity at parish Mass; parish priests explaining the Gospel and chief mysteries on feast days; preachers joining doctrinal instruction to moral exhortation; use of the Augustinian method of friendly questioning to verify and consolidate understanding.
- **Tension:** The document sits between pastoral patience and institutional urgency: the Pope acknowledges that bishops have already done their duty (synods are filled with correct instructions), yet the problem persists—located in the resistance and passivity of the laity. This creates a tension between episcopal accountability and lay responsibility, and between the demands of sacramental preparation (which require doctrinal knowledge) and the practical hardship of the poor pilgrims who are eager to return home and marry. A secondary tension exists between the ideal of thorough catechesis and the reality of slow or obstinate learners, resolved partially by Augustine's counsel to be "merciful" and to "briefly impart the most necessary doctrines" to those who are very slow.

## Unstructured

**Core concern and occasion.** The encyclical is tightly occasional: it arises from a concrete administrative discovery—pilgrims coming to Rome for marriage dispensations cannot receive the required sacraments because they do not know the faith. The document moves from this observed problem outward to a general directive on catechesis across all Italian dioceses.

**Argument flow.** The letter proceeds in four recognisable steps. First, Benedict notes the good news: special ministers have been appointed in the three patriarchal basilicas and are working with great zeal (§1, opening). Second, he introduces the problem: many of these pilgrims are found to be ignorant of even the most essential mysteries of faith, delaying their dispensation and adding to their poverty and distress (§1, second paragraph). Third, he carefully exculpates the bishops—the fault lies with the subjects, not their pastors—while still using this moment to "stir up their zeal again" (§2). Fourth, he draws on the model of St. Charles Borromeo (and the typological parallel of the Assyrian king in 4 Kings 17) to argue that renewed effort, even after apparent failure, is the only proper response (§3), and then issues a detailed practical programme (§4).

**Tone.** The tone is pastoral-administrative rather than polemical or doctrinal in any controversial sense. Benedict does not condemn heresy or respond to theological dispute; his concern is practical piety and sacramental fitness. The register shifts from warm encouragement ("we heartily assert," "We encourage you and implore you by the mercy of Jesus Christ") to precise bureaucratic specification (listing schoolmasters, confessors, priests, fathers of families, preachers, and the recitation of acts of Faith, Hope and Charity). The closing Apostolic Blessing and the affectionate "Venerable Brothers" frame the whole in familial episcopal collegiality.

**Notable framing devices.**
- *Necessity of means*: the technical theological phrase "what must be known by necessity of means" (necessary for salvation as opposed to merely precept) is invoked twice, anchoring the urgency—ignorance of these truths is not a minor pastoral gap but a barrier to eternal salvation.
- *St. Charles Borromeo as paradigm*: the invocation of Borromeo's fifth Milanese synod is striking because it is quoted to legitimise persistence in the face of disappointing results, not just as an example of zeal in itself. The rhetorical move is: even a great saint found his efforts bore little fruit and pressed on—so must you.
- *Augustinian pedagogy*: the explicit citation of Augustine's *De Catechizandis Rudibus* (ch. 10) on friendly questioning after instruction is unusual in its practical specificity; it signals that the Pope expects not merely formal catechism sessions but genuine dialogic verification of learning.
- *Social texture of the pilgrims*: the brief but vivid sketch of the pilgrims—"oppressed by poverty and begging for their food with their own hands," eager to leave Rome—grounds an otherwise administrative document in human reality and gives the catechetical programme a note of pastoral urgency rather than mere juridical compliance.
