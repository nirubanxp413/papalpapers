# Urbanitatis Veteris

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Leo XIII expresses deep admiration for Greek civilization and Catholic heritage while urging reunion of the Eastern and Western Churches under the Roman Pontiff, backed by concrete educational initiatives for the Greek Catholic clergy.
- **Summary:** Written to the Latin-rite bishops in Greece, this letter blends personal affection for Greek culture and philosophy with a pastoral and ecclesiastical agenda. Leo recalls Greece's glories — its philosophy (Aristotle), its Church Fathers (Athanasius, Chrysostom, Cyril and Methodius), and even the Greek-born popes of antiquity — to argue that reunion with Rome would cost Greece nothing and would in fact restore former greatness. He announces his approval of a seminary for young Greek-born clerics at Athens and frames this as a continuation of the long-standing papal benevolence toward Greece. The letter closes with an appeal for the gradual dissolution of "prejudiced opinions" that stand in the way of reunion.
- **Response to:** The historical schism between Eastern and Western Christianity; Greek bishops' joint letter of 9 September requesting a clerical seminary at Athens; the long memory of Greek suffering under "a foreign master" and subsequent refuge offered by the Roman states; papal predecessors' (Pius V, Pius VII) acts of solidarity with Greece; the lingering "prejudiced opinions" born of "lamentable occurrences in the distant past."
- **Prescriptive towards:** Reunion of the Greek/Eastern churches with Rome; the education of Latin-rite but Greek-born clerics in humane letters and their ancestral language before proceeding to theology; continued episcopal diligence in making Catholic faith demands clear to the faithful; cultivation of love for country united with love and zeal for the Catholic faith.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between Greek national/ecclesiastical identity and reunion with Rome — the document acknowledges that "prejudiced opinions" have taken deep root, and implicitly addresses Greek fears that reunion would mean the loss of distinctive rites, dignity, and cultural heritage. A secondary tension exists between Greek philosophical and patristic pride and the claim that this very inheritance is best honored within Catholic communion (e.g., Aristotle honored through Thomas Aquinas).

## Unstructured

**Key themes and flow of argument:**

The letter is structured as an extended act of *captatio benevolentiae* — a rhetorical winning of goodwill — before arriving at its practical and ecclesiological request. Leo opens by professing his personal love for Greek literature and philosophy, rooted from his youth. He anchors this admiration in his own promotion of Thomism: to honor Aquinas, he says, is to honor Aristotle, "your Aristotle." This is a clever move that positions the papacy as the guardian and promoter of Greek intellectual heritage rather than its rival.

The second movement (§§2–3) shifts to Christian history: Leo praises Greek liturgical and sacramental tradition, the Greek Church Fathers, and the Greek-born popes of the early Church. The grief he expresses over the schism ("We scarcely ever recall the Popes of Greek origin without grief and longing") is genuine in tone and functions rhetorically to frame the division as a shared loss rather than a Latin triumph.

The third movement (§4) pivots to the practical: establishment of a seminary at Athens for Greek-born clerics of the Latin rite, who are to master their ancestral tongue and literature before advancing to theology. This institutional proposal is presented as organically flowing from the preceding affection and esteem.

The fourth movement (§5) deploys historical precedent — Pius V's alliance and campaign to "free all of Greece," Pius VII's hospitality to Greek refugees — to demonstrate a consistent pattern of papal benevolence. The culminating argument is that reunion would not cost Greece "its dignity, its fame, and all its adornments" but would add to its glory, citing the age of Constantine, Athanasius, and Chrysostom as evidence that Roman papal authority and Greek flourishing coexisted.

**Tone:** The tone is warm and personally engaged — unusually so for a papal document — but it is ultimately authoritative. Leo speaks as the holder of the supreme Catholic office, and the pastoral warmth serves the overriding ecclesiological claim: that Rome is the legitimate center of Christian unity.

**Notable framing:** Leo does not frame reunion as Greek submission but as restoration of a lost mutuality ("the sharing of man and great blessings would have remained if concord had remained"). The document consistently appeals to what Greece already is and has been, rather than asking it to become something foreign. The educational initiative functions as a concrete embodiment of this respect, giving the abstract appeal for reunion a visible, institutional form.
