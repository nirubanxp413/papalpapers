# In gravissimis

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Amid the crisis of schism and revolutionary disruption in France, Pius VI grants expanded delegated faculties to bishops in communion with Rome so they can continue to govern, absolve, dispense, and pastor their flocks.
- **Summary:** Written on 19 March 1792, the letter responds directly to requests from bishops in Paris and Rome for broader powers to function in extraordinary circumstances. After affirming the bishops' fidelity to the Apostolic See, the Pope convokes a special Cardinal congregation and then issues a detailed indult granting fourteen categories of expanded faculty — covering absolution of censures (including those arising from the civil oath), dispensations from canonical impediments, control over religious orders whose superiors have been displaced, transfer of indulgences from occupied churches, and the right to sub-delegate most of these powers to priests. All faculties are limited to one year and to territories under the "Most Christian King," and their exercise must be explicitly attributed to Apostolic delegation.
- **Response to:** The document responds explicitly to (a) the civil oath (*giuramento civile*) imposed on clergy, which the Pope's earlier apostolic letter of 13 April 1791 had penalised with suspension *a divinis*; (b) the occupation of churches by "pseudo-pastors" (schismatic clergy who accepted the constitutional settlement); (c) the displacement of religious from their convents and the suppression or incapacitation of regular superiors; and (d) formal requests of 16 December 1791 (from bishops in Paris) and 8 January 1792 (from bishops in Rome) for broader ordinary and delegated powers.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs bishops toward (a) continuing active pastoral governance under delegation from Rome, explicitly naming themselves as acting *in virtù del potere delegato dalla Sede Apostolica*; (b) reconciling clergy and laity who took the civil oath, provided they publicly retract it and repair scandal; (c) maintaining canonical discipline (marriage registers, reserved conditions on dispensations) even under emergency conditions; and (d) sustaining loyalty to Rome and to "sound doctrine" in the face of what the text frames as an ongoing calamity.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the Church's ordinary canonical order (jurisdiction residing in Rome, regular hierarchy, observance of canons and constitutions) and the emergency pastoral reality of schism, occupied churches, dispersed religious, and coerced clergy. A secondary tension is named between bishops who are "valorosi militi di Cristo" striving to maintain the faith and an unnamed mass of people who, in the words the Pope attributes to the Apostle, "no longer endure sound doctrine and surround themselves with teachers who suit their desires." The document tries to resolve the first tension by expanding delegated authority downward while insisting all acts be formally attributed to Rome, thereby preserving the fiction of ordinary canonical order even while acknowledging the emergency.

## Unstructured

The document is tightly functional: the opening pastoral consolation (paragraphs 1–2) and closing exhortation (paragraph 5) are brief; the bulk is the fourteen-point indult (paragraphs 6–19) followed by a temporal and territorial limitation clause (paragraph 20). This structure mirrors the epistolary genre of an apostolic indult — the theological framing is minimal, the legal-canonical detail is extensive.

**Tone:** Predominantly authoritative-administrative rather than pastoral or doctrinal. Pius VI is not teaching; he is legislating for an emergency. The emotional register shifts briefly in paragraphs 1 and 5 — "greater and sweeter comfort," "arm yourselves with the armour of God," "valiant soldiers of Christ" — borrowing Pauline martial imagery to encourage beleaguered bishops. Otherwise the prose is the dry precision of canon law.

**Key framing devices:**

1. *Delegation as continuity with Rome.* Every faculty is framed as an extension of Apostolic authority, not a concession of autonomy. The two declarations of fidelity quoted from the bishops (paragraph 3) — that all acts will cite the Apostolic mandate and that Roman customs and decrees will be observed — are placed prominently before the grant itself, as if to establish the terms of the contract.

2. *The civil oath as the precipitating event.* The oath is not discussed theologically here; it is treated as a canonical fait accompli — it generated censures (suspension *a divinis*), and the document now provides the machinery for lifting those censures, provided public retraction and scandal repair. The April 1791 letter is cited twice (paragraphs 6 and 7) as the baseline penalty instrument; this letter is essentially its pastoral sequel.

3. *"The calamity of these times."* The phrase appears at paragraph 20 as the conditional limiting clause: the faculties last "for one year … if the calamity of these times lasts that long." This is the document's single overt acknowledgment that the situation may resolve — or, more grimly, that it may not.

4. *Scope and gradation of faculties.* The fourteen items move roughly from the most urgent (absolution of schismatics, paragraph 6) to the most routine (sub-delegation powers, paragraphs 13–14), passing through marriage dispensations (paragraph 9, the most detailed section) and care for displaced religious (paragraphs 14–15). The marriage section's insistence on secret registers "preserved carefully and occultly" (paragraph 9) reveals an expectation that normal diocesan record-keeping has broken down.

5. *Implicit ecclesiology.* The document implies that the Church's visible unity is constituted precisely by episcopal communion with Rome and the formal attribution of acts to Apostolic authority. "Pseudo-pastors" are defined by their absence from this communion (they "invaded and occupied" churches), not by theological error per se. The bishops addressed are distinguished by the clause "who have communion and grace with the Apostolic See" (paragraph 6 heading).

**Flow of argument:** Statement of distress → receipt of episcopal petitions → recognition of their justice → consultation of a Cardinal congregation → grant of specific faculties → exhortation to courage → detailed enumeration of faculties → duration and territorial limits → mandatory attribution clause. The structure enacts the logic it describes: Rome is consulted, Rome deliberates, Rome delegates, and every downstream act must point back to Rome.
