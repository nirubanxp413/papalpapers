# Etsi Nos

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Catholic Church in Italy faces a coordinated assault by anti-religious political sects that threatens both the faith of the Italian people and the temporal freedom of the Roman Pontiff, and bishops must mobilize clergy, associations, and public writing to mount a vigorous defense.
- **Summary:** Leo XIII addresses the bishops of Italy to warn that a "pernicious sect" has declared open war on the Church — suppressing religious houses, confiscating Church property, undermining Catholic education, and reducing the Pope to a prisoner of another civil power. He argues that Italy's historical greatness is inseparable from Catholic faith, so the ongoing campaign against the Church harms both religion and the nation. He calls bishops to action on three fronts: renewing lay courage and Catholic associations, flooding the public sphere with sound Catholic writing to counter hostile publications, and rigorously forming priests in both virtue and advanced learning, while also asking for financial generosity toward seminaries stripped of resources by hostile legislation. The letter closes with an appeal for prayer to Mary, Joseph, and the Apostles Peter and Paul.
- **Response to:** The document itself names the following as the present situation it addresses: suppression of religious houses; confiscation of Church goods; civil-only marriages contracted "in despite of the laws" of the Church; the Church's exclusion from the education of youth; the Pope being "despoiled of his Civil Princedom" and placed "in the hands of another Power"; heretical temples and schools appearing in Rome; an imminent assembly of anti-Catholic sectarians planned for Rome; a flood of hostile books and "turbulent and wicked journals"; anti-clerical laws reducing the number of seminary candidates; and the failure of many Catholics to respond with adequate courage so far.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates and directs toward: bishops awakening the faithful and encouraging "daring effort" against cowardly inertia; the founding and growth of Catholic lay associations (youth groups, workers' associations, relief institutions, committees meeting periodically); a campaign of Catholic writing in every province to counter hostile publications, written with "gravity and moderation" in plain language; the formal restoration of the Pope's temporal liberty and rights through petitions and lawful means; generous financial support from Italian Catholics for seminaries; better formation of priests in philosophy, natural sciences, Scriptural studies, and virtue; and intercessory prayer to the Virgin Mary, St. Joseph, SS. Peter and Paul.
- **Tension:** Two closely linked tensions run through the document: (1) *Church liberty vs. civil power* — the State has stripped the Church of property, personnel, and the Pope's civil sovereignty, and religious freedom is being destroyed by political actors who call themselves defenders of Italy's honor; (2) *Catholic courage vs. inertia* — many Catholics from whom much was expected have shown insufficient "activity and vigor," and Leo insists that passive endurance of evil is now more dangerous than open confrontation.

## Unstructured

**Overall character.** *Etsi Nos* is addressed exclusively to the bishops of Italy (not to the universal Church), giving it a sharper, more urgent tone than many of Leo's broader encyclicals. The argument moves from diagnosis of crisis to historical apologia to practical mobilisation orders. It is authoritative in register — issuing directives — but also pastoral in that it seeks to explain *why* the situation demands action rather than simply commanding obedience.

**Flow of argument.** The letter proceeds in three recognisable movements:

1. *Paragraphs 1–9: The diagnosis.* Italy is under attack from a "pernicious sect" that has declared war on Christ and is systematically dismantling Catholic institutions. The document catalogues concrete injuries (property confiscation, civil marriage, exclusion from education, Pope deprived of temporal sovereignty, anti-Catholic gatherings in Rome). Leo then argues the inverse: Italy owes its historical greatness — unity, law, the arts, freedom from foreign domination — to the Church. To strip away the Church is thus not only impious but self-destructive, and Leo invokes the Pauline image of land that yields thorns being "very near unto a curse."

2. *Paragraphs 10–17: The call to engagement.* Having established the stakes, Leo turns the indictment into an imperative. Catholics have been too passive. He calls for: renewal of courage, formation of associations, public agitation by lawful means for restoration of papal temporal rights, and above all a counter-offensive in publishing. The writing campaign is discussed with unusual specificity — it must appear "as far as possible daily," must be plain in language, moderate in tone toward persons even while firm on errors, and must be funded by the laity. The implicit model is that the enemy has won through organisation and media, and the Church must respond in kind.

3. *Paragraphs 18–22: Seminary formation and prayer.* The final movement shifts to the Church's internal resources: the quality of priests. Leo stresses that defending faith in this environment requires *advanced* learning — philosophy, natural sciences, Scripture scholarship — not merely conventional piety, because adversaries are "well prepared" and "pertinacious." He notes that hostile laws have shrunk seminary resources and asks Italian Catholics to emulate French and Belgian generosity. The letter ends liturgically, entrusting Italy to the intercession of Mary, Joseph, Peter, and Paul.

**Key framing devices.** The document repeatedly frames the conflict as a war ("declared war against Jesus Christ," "cruel and deplorable war without limit," "war to the death against the Church"). This martial vocabulary coexists with an insistence that Catholics must act strictly "within the limits of the law" — a deliberate tension that signals Leo's awareness of political constraints on the Italian Catholic community following the loss of the Papal States. The call to action is thus forceful but carefully bounded.

**Tone on social/political matters.** Leo makes a pointed civic argument: the enemies of the Church "pretend to be friends of the honor of Italy," but history refutes them. The Church preserved Italian unity, liberty, science, and art. This is not merely religious apologetics; it is a claim about political legitimacy — the Church (and specifically the temporal sovereignty of the Pope) is presented as a structural precondition for Italy's stability and prosperity. The line "Say that the State in Italy can never prosper nor become stable and tranquil unless provision be made for the dignity of the Roman See" is quoted as if from an earlier statement, suggesting Leo is restating a public position he has taken elsewhere.

**Notable omissions.** The document does not name the specific "sect" (though the context makes Freemasonry the clear referent), does not engage any of the theological or philosophical arguments of the adversaries in detail, and does not address the legitimacy of Italian unification directly — it treats the loss of papal temporal power as a plain injustice without debating the political question as such.
