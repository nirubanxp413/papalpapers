# Regnans in Excelsis

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The fullness of apostolic authority is invoked to excommunicate and depose Elizabeth I of England as a heretic who has usurped church governance, destroyed Catholic practice, and persecuted the faithful — thereby releasing her subjects from all oaths and duties of obedience.
- **Summary:** The bull opens by affirming that Christ entrusted the one holy Catholic Church solely to Peter and his successors, who hold the plenary power to plant and destroy among all nations. The Pope then catalogues Elizabeth I's offenses: seizing the supreme headship of the Church in England, dismantling Catholic worship (Mass, fasting, celibacy, rites), filling the royal council with heretics, ejecting Catholic bishops and priests, forbidding acknowledgment of Roman authority, coercing subjects into abjuring papal obedience, and imprisoning clergy (many of whom died in captivity). Having exhausted all peaceful remedies — entreaties from Catholic princes, attempted papal legations blocked by Elizabeth — the Pope turns "of necessity to the arms of justice" (*ad arma iustitiae de necessitate conversi*), formally declaring Elizabeth a heretic and excommunicant, stripping her of her pretended right to the English kingdom, and commanding all nobles, subjects, and peoples to refuse obedience to her under pain of the same excommunication.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to Elizabeth's self-styled usurpation of the "place of supreme head of the Church" (*supremi ecclesiae capitis locum… monstrose sibi usurpans*) in England; her reversal of the Catholic restoration achieved under Mary I; her abolition of the Mass, Catholic rites, and clerical celibacy; her mandate that Calvinist practices be observed throughout the realm; her violent suppression of Catholics and imprisonment of clergy; and her refusal to receive papal nuncios or heed the entreaties of Catholic princes urging her conversion.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The bull directs all nobles, subjects, and peoples of England — and "all others who have in any way sworn to her" — to cease obeying Elizabeth's commands, laws, and directives, under penalty of the same anathema. It also provides practical instruction that authenticated notarial copies of the bull should carry the same legal force as the original wherever it is needed, anticipating the need to enforce the sentence across territories.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the Pope's claim to plenary authority over all nations and kingdoms — including the power to depose rulers — and Elizabeth's exercise of both temporal and spiritual supremacy over England, which the text portrays as a monstrous usurpation. A secondary tension runs through the bull between the duty of Catholic subjects to obey their crowned sovereign and their duty to obey the Church: the bull resolves this by declaring the oath of obedience to Elizabeth void. The text also implies a tension between mercy (multiple failed attempts at peaceful persuasion) and the necessity of a punitive canonical sentence.

## Unstructured

**Genre and tone.** This is a formal Papal Bull, not an encyclical or pastoral letter. Its tone is strictly juridical and declaratory throughout — there is no pastoral warmth, no appeal to Elizabeth personally, no invitation to repent. The Pope writes as judge pronouncing sentence (*de apostolicae potestatis plenitudine declaramus*), and the genre matches: indictment, verdict, sentence, and directive to enforce.

**Argument flow.** The document's logic is tightly sequential:

1. *Theological preamble* — Christ gave plenary power to Peter and his successors to govern the universal Church over all nations; this is the foundation of papal authority to act.
2. *Papal duty* — the Pope is bound to preserve Catholic unity regardless of labor or difficulty.
3. *Indictment* — Elizabeth's offenses are enumerated with specificity: usurpation of church headship, dismantling of Catholic worship, appointment of heretics to offices, persecution of Catholics, blocking of papal communications.
4. *Exhaustion of remedies* — the bull explicitly notes that prayer, warnings from Catholic princes, and papal legates (all blocked from entering England) have failed; Elizabeth's heart is described as "so hardened" (*ita obfirmatum atque induratum*) that peaceful persuasion is closed.
5. *Formal sentence* — excommunication, deprivation of royal dignity, release of subjects from oaths.
6. *Enforcement directive* — universal prohibition on obeying her, with identical anathema threatened against violators.
7. *Authentication clause* — notarized copies to carry full legal force.

**Notable framing.** Elizabeth is never acknowledged as Queen without qualification: she is consistently "praetensa Angliae regina" (the *pretended* Queen of England). This rhetorical move strips her legitimacy even before the formal sentence. The document also anchors Catholic legitimacy historically: Mary I had restored Catholicism with the support of the Roman See after Henry VIII's rupture, and Elizabeth has "recalled [England] to miserable ruin" the kingdom that had been returned to the faith. The progression Henry VIII → Mary I (restoration) → Elizabeth I (re-destruction) frames Elizabeth's reign as a relapse rather than a legitimate reform.

**Spiritual and political dimensions.** Both are substantial and inseparable in the text, justifying the "Mixed" classification. The spiritual offense (heresy, usurpation of church authority, destruction of sacraments) is the cause; the political consequence (deprivation of the kingdom, release of subjects) flows directly from it. The bull does not treat these as independent claims but as a single canonical act: a heretical ruler forfeits the right to govern, and the Pope's spiritual authority entails the power to pronounce that forfeiture.

**Scope of authority claimed.** The opening lines assert the broadest possible papal mandate — power given to rule over "all nations and kingdoms, to uproot, destroy, dissipate, scatter, plant, and build" (*qui evellat, destruat, dissipet, disperdat, plantet, et aedificet*), language drawn from Jeremiah 1:10. This citation provides the scriptural warrant for the extraordinary political act that follows.

**What is not stated.** The text makes no offer of reconciliation, no conditions under which the sentence could be lifted, and no appeal to Elizabeth's conscience — the bull treats her conversion as foreclosed. It does not address the practical difficulties of enforcing excommunication against a reigning Protestant monarch, nor does it engage with the theological arguments of Protestant reformers.
