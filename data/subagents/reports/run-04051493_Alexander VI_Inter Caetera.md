# Inter Caetera

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed (Spiritual mission framed as justification for political/territorial grant)
- **Theme (one sentence):** The pope grants Spain sovereignty over newly discovered western lands in perpetuity, grounding the grant in the duty to spread the Catholic faith and convert the peoples found there.
- **Summary:** Inter Caetera is addressed to Ferdinand and Isabella of Spain and commends their completion of the Reconquista before dispatching Columbus westward. It describes the peoples of the discovered islands as peaceful, unclothed, and disposed toward Christian belief, and uses that receptivity as a spiritual mandate. The document then formally grants all lands discovered or to be discovered west and south of a line drawn 100 leagues from the Azores and Cape Verde Islands to Spain and its heirs forever, prohibits all other parties from entering those territories without Spanish permission under penalty of excommunication, and commands Spain to send learned and God-fearing men to instruct the inhabitants in the Catholic faith and good morals.
- **Response to:** The completion of Columbus's first voyage and his report of discovered islands; the successful conclusion of the reconquest of Granada from the Saracens; Spain's prior long-standing intention to seek unknown lands for the purpose of bringing their inhabitants to Christian faith.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Future voyages of discovery and trade in the western and southern Atlantic; the religious instruction and conversion of the peoples already found and yet to be found; the exclusion of all other Christian rulers and persons from these territories without Spanish license; the appointment of qualified clergy and educators to the new territories.
- **Tension:** The document presents a tension between the spiritual rationale (saving souls, spreading faith) and the material reality of territorial and commercial monopoly (gold, spices, dominion, excommunication of trade rivals). It also implicitly positions the pope's temporal authority to grant sovereignty over lands that neither he nor Spain yet possesses, treating apostolic power as the legitimating source of both spiritual mission and political title.

## Unstructured

**Flow of argument:** The document opens with a statement of the pope's highest pastoral priority — the exaltation of the Catholic faith and the overthrow of "barbarous nations" — and positions Ferdinand and Isabella as ideal instruments of that priority, citing their long record of Catholic zeal and specifically the recent recovery of Granada. It then narrates the Columbus voyage as the direct fruit of that same royal zeal: an enterprise long intended but delayed by Granada. Columbus's discovery is presented as already spiritually promising because the peoples he found appear monotheistic and "sufficiently disposed" to embrace Catholicism. The document pivots from this spiritual description to the legal act at its core: the formal papal donation of all western and southern lands to Spain and its successors in perpetuity. The donation is carefully bounded (the line, the proviso about prior Christian possession), then reinforced with a command to send qualified missionaries, and locked in with a blanket prohibition backed by excommunication against any unauthorized entry by any person of any rank.

**Tone:** Decidedly authoritative rather than pastoral. The rhetorical register is that of a legal instrument — "give, grant, and assign," "make, appoint, and depute," "strictly forbid" — bolstered by appeals to apostolic fullness of power and the vicarship of Christ. The exhortatory language toward Ferdinand and Isabella ("we exhort you very earnestly," "nor do we doubt your compliance") is formal flattery that underscores rather than softens the document's commanding posture. The closing invocation of divine wrath against any who would "infringe or contravene" completes the juridical-theological frame.

**Notable framing:** The peoples of the discovered lands are described almost entirely in terms of their convertibility: they live in peace, go unclothed, do not eat flesh, believe in one God, and "seem sufficiently disposed to embrace the Catholic faith." This framing renders them recipients of a spiritual gift rather than agents with existing claims. Their disposability for conversion is the very feature that triggers and justifies the papal grant. The document does not name them, individuate them, or attribute any juridical standing to them. The grant of "full and free power, authority, and jurisdiction of every kind" over their islands and mainlands is issued without any stated limit derived from their own prior habitation. The only protected parties are "Christian kings or princes" in prior actual possession — a proviso that explicitly protects existing Christian interests while leaving non-Christian inhabitants unprotected by the same logic.

**Gold and spices:** The mention of "gold, spices, and very many other precious things" is brief but structurally significant — it appears immediately before the pivot to the formal grant, subtly acknowledging the material stakes that motivate the undertaking even as the document's explicit justification remains spiritual.

**Papal authority:** The document repeatedly stresses that the donation is made "of our own accord, not at your instance nor the request of anyone else" and "out of the fullness of our apostolic power, by the authority of Almighty God conferred upon us in blessed Peter." This insistence on unsolicited, self-generated authority reinforces the pope's role as the ultimate temporal arbiter of newly encountered territories, framing the grant as a divine act mediated through the papacy rather than a political transaction between Spain and Rome.
