# Si Diligis – Canonisation Of St Pius X

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document asserts the exclusive, hierarchically-bounded teaching authority of the Church against emerging trends of independent lay theology and doctrinal innovation.
- **Summary:** Delivered as an allocution at the canonisation of St. Pius X, Pius XII uses the occasion to invoke St. Pius X's example of obedient pastoral fidelity in order to warn bishops worldwide about a "spiritual contagion": teachers who disregard conformity with the Church's living Magisterium, and a new class of "lay theologians" who claim a teaching authority independent of episcopal oversight. The document rehearses the divinely-instituted hierarchy of teaching offices — Pope, Bishops, delegated teachers, laity — and insists that no legitimate teaching in the Church can exist outside that chain of authority and vigilance.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly states it responds to: (1) "certain teachers" who "care little for conformity with the living Teaching Authority of the Church" and who "follow their own bent too much," giving excessive weight to "the intellectual temper of more recent writers"; (2) the emergence of what the text calls "lay theology" — an organised, institutionalised movement claiming its own *sui juris* teaching authority, with chairs, courses, notes, and seminars, and appealing to charismatic gifts and historical precedent to justify independence from episcopal supervision; (3) the errors previously treated in the encyclical *Humani generis*, which are here said to have "their direct origin in a neglect of conformity with the living Teaching Authority."
- **Prescriptive towards:** Bishops are directed to intensify personal engagement with "the sublimity and profundity of supernatural truth" and to preach urgently to people "being engulfed by the darkness of error in matters of both mind and heart." They are called to exercise active supervision over all delegated teachers — in seminaries, colleges, universities, institutions of higher learning — and to ensure lay apostolic workers remain "under the authority, leadership, and watchfulness" of legitimate teachers. The document anticipates an ongoing battle against doctrinal drift and calls for time ("if God so permits") to address the remaining two offices of bishop (priest and ruler) on a future occasion.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between hierarchically-authorised teaching and independent intellectual or lay initiative. The document names this directly: lay theologians who "distinguish their teaching authority from, and in a certain way set it up against, the public Teaching Authority of the Church." A secondary tension runs between legitimate scholarly progress (the Church is said to "foster the study of human branches of learning") and the claim that "matters of religion and morals… pertain solely to the office and authority of the Church." There is also an implicit tension between trust and surveillance: Pius XII insists that canonical oversight of what professors teach "does not at all imply distrust or suspicion," yet the entire address is structured around the need for such oversight precisely because of observed deviation.

## Unstructured

**Occasion and framing.** This is an allocution — a direct, oral address — not an encyclical. Its immediate occasion is the canonisation of Pius X, and the figure of that saint functions throughout as a model: someone who "loved and fed," who "strenuously fulfilled" the dominical command, and who "urgently stressed the need for union with the mind and teaching of the Church." The canonisation is thus not merely celebratory; it is deployed rhetorically as proof that obedient, orthodox pastoral service is the path to sanctity.

**Argument structure.** The allocution moves in a clear arc: (1) pastoral invocation — the text opens with "Si diligis… pasce" ("If you love… feed"), framing the entire document as a meditation on pastoral duty; (2) collegial address — Pius XII positions himself as "an ancient among the ancients," citing 1 Peter 5, to speak as a fellow bishop; (3) diagnosis — he names the "spiritual contagion" of non-conformity with the Magisterium; (4) doctrinal clarification — a detailed taxonomy of teaching authority (Pope → Bishops → delegated teachers → lay helpers, never lay theologians *sui juris*); (5) historical anchoring — Benedict XV and Pius X are both quoted to show this is not a new concern; (6) call to action — bishops are urged to preach and supervise with renewed energy; (7) blessing.

**Tone.** The tone is pastoral in register but juridical in substance. Phrases like "it is necessary to maintain to the contrary that there never has been, there is not now, and there never will be in the Church a legitimate teaching authority of the laity withdrawn… from the sacred Teaching Authority" are absolute and declarative. Yet the document is careful to soften potential offense: it disclaims any intent to suppress scholarly zeal, and frames episcopal supervision as an expression of confidence rather than suspicion. This double move — reassurance alongside firm prohibition — is a consistent rhetorical pattern throughout.

**The "lay theology" phenomenon.** The document's most specific and evidently urgent target is the emergence of organised lay theological instruction: "professors… occupying established chairs, courses are given, notes published, seminars held." This is described not as informal catechesis (which is explicitly praised) but as a rival institutional structure claiming doctrinal standing. The two justifications the lay theologians are said to offer — charismatic gifts (citing Romans 12, 1 Corinthians 12) and historical precedent (laymen who taught without episcopal commission) — are each refuted: the very claim to independence, the text argues, is itself evidence of not being guided by the Spirit of God.

**St. Paul's warning as prophetic framing.** The document closes its critique of lay theology with a quotation from 2 Timothy 4:3-4 — the "itching ears" passage about people accumulating teachers to suit their own preferences. This frames the contemporary situation as a fulfillment of apostolic prophecy, lending the document an eschatological urgency that goes beyond mere administrative concern.

**Relationship to *Humani generis*.** The text explicitly cross-references *Humani generis* (1950), Pius XII's earlier encyclical against theological novelties. This allocution functions partly as an enforcement memo: it identifies non-conformity with the Magisterium as the *cause* of the errors condemned in that document, and calls bishops to active institutional vigilance rather than waiting for Rome to intervene after the fact.

**What is not said.** The document does not address the specific doctrinal content of the errors it fears — it is entirely procedural and jurisdictional in focus. It says nothing about the laity's positive theological vocation beyond catechesis and lay apostolate. Social, political, or economic matters are entirely absent; the "darkness of error" described is framed as intellectual and spiritual, not societal.
