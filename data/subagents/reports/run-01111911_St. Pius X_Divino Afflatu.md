# Divino Afflatu

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document reforms the order of the Roman Psalter in the Divine Office to restore weekly recitation of the full Psalter, which had been eroded by the proliferation of feasts of saints, so that clerics may better praise God and nourish their piety.
- **Summary:** Pius X begins by extolling the Psalms as divinely inspired compositions of unmatched power to foster piety, stir virtue, and give voice to the Church's prayer. He explains that the ancient and conciliar tradition required the full Psalter to be recited each week, but that over time the growth of saints' feasts had increasingly displaced ferial and Sunday Offices, causing many Psalms to be omitted. Responding to petitions from bishops (including at the First Vatican Council) and other pious persons, the Pope commissioned a revision of the Psalter's arrangement to restore full weekly recitation without increasing the burden on clergy or diminishing the cult of saints. He formally decrees the new order of the Psalter, to be observed from 1 January 1913 by all bound to the Roman Breviary, and signals this as a first step toward broader reform of the Breviary and Missal.
- **Response to:** (1) The historical erosion of full weekly Psalter recitation caused by the multiplication of saints' feasts displacing ferial Offices; (2) repeated complaints ("graves querimoniae") from prudent and pious men that so many Psalms were being omitted; (3) petitions submitted to the Apostolic See by bishops from many parts of the world, especially at the Vatican Council, requesting restoration of the old custom of weekly Psalter recitation.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) All patriarchs, archbishops, bishops, abbots, and other church prelates, cardinals included, to implement the new Psalter order in their dioceses, churches, and monasteries by the appointed date; (2) all individuals obliged to recite or chant the canonical Hours to observe the new Psalter arrangement inviolably from 1 January 1913; (3) a forthcoming dedicated commission of scholars to undertake the broader reform of the Roman Breviary and Missal.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the ancient duty to recite the complete Psalter weekly — seen as essential for clerical devotion and attentiveness in prayer — and the legitimate growth of the sanctoral calendar, whose proliferating feasts had gradually supplanted the ferial Office and displaced the very Psalms the tradition valued most. A secondary tension concerns the burden on clergy already stretched by reduced numbers: the reform must restore devotional fullness without making the Divine Office more onerous.

## Unstructured

**Key themes:**

The document is dominated by a single coherent theme: the spiritual primacy of the Psalms and their proper place in the Church's liturgical life. Pius X opens with a dense testimonial passage — drawing on Basil, Athanasius, Ambrose, and Augustine — that functions almost as a theological prologue, establishing the Psalms as simultaneously the voice of God and the voice of the Church, as a mirror for the soul, and as the supreme school of virtue and praise. The cumulative effect of these patristic citations is to make the reform feel not like an administrative decision but like a recovery of something sacred that had been lost.

**Flow of argument:**

The argument moves in a clear sequence: (1) praise of the Psalms' spiritual value, grounded in patristic authority; (2) the ancient law requiring full weekly recitation, upheld by earlier popes and councils; (3) the problem — the growth of saints' feasts had displaced ferial Offices and caused many Psalms to be skipped, to the spiritual detriment of clergy; (4) the petitions and complaints that prompted action; (5) the Pope's response — commissioning a scholarly committee, obtaining curial approval, and decreeing the new arrangement; (6) the prospective scope — this is explicitly called "the first step" toward a full Breviary and Missal reform; (7) the formal decree with canonical force and penalties for non-compliance.

**Tone:**

The tone is authoritative throughout, marked by the formal language of papal legislation (edicimus, declaramus, sancimus), but the opening sections are pastoral and almost devotional in character. The Pope does not simply issue a mandate; he first builds a case from tradition and spiritual need. The pastoral register gives way to unambiguous jurisdictional command in the final paragraphs, where penalties in law are invoked against those who fail their obligation.

**Notable framing:**

The document frames the reform as a restoration rather than an innovation — returning to a "vetus consuetudo" (old custom) endorsed by Trent and earlier popes. The language of burden is carefully managed: the reform explicitly promises not to increase the load on clergy ("ne…molestius divini Officii onus clericis, immo temperatius evaderet"). The prospective mention of a commission for the full Breviary and Missal reform is notable: it positions this Psalter revision as preparatory, not final, and signals awareness that the issue is systemic. The document also takes care not to demote the cult of saints ("ne quicquam de Sanctorum cultu decederet"), suggesting the reform is framed as a balance between two legitimate goods rather than a correction of excess.
