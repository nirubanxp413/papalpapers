# Inscrutabile

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** A newly elected pope calls his bishops to defend the Church against a corrosive philosophical movement that denies God, subverts authority, and threatens both spiritual and civil order.
- **Summary:** Written on Christmas Day 1775 at the opening of his pontificate, Pius VI addresses the entire Catholic episcopate with a dual concern: internal church governance (clergy formation, liturgical dignity) and an urgent external threat posed by a "philosophy full of deceit" that promotes atheism, rejects revelation, and encourages rebellion against both priestly and royal authority. The pope frames his election as a providential but humbling call to leadership in dangerous times, appeals for united episcopal resistance to irreligion, and closes with an exhortation to preach, correct, and build charity among the faithful.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to the spread of a deceitful philosophy that leads men to deny God's existence or relevance, to mock religion as born of "empty fear," and to proclaim that mankind is born free and subject to no one — with direct attacks on the authority of both priests and kings. It also responds to internal neglect: lax standards for clergy admission and inadequate care for church buildings and sacred objects.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs bishops to: (1) rigorously screen and educate candidates for the clergy; (2) maintain the beauty and dignity of churches; (3) actively combat "poisonous books" and quarantine those infected by false philosophy; (4) preach publicly, invoke civil order and obedience to rulers, and build fraternal charity among the faithful; (5) pray and remain courageous rather than retreat into "ill-judged silence."
- **Tension:** Two interlocking tensions run through the text. First, a spiritual tension between the authentic, divinely-grounded faith of the Church and a rising intellectual movement that denies God and ridicules religion as superstition. Second, a social-political tension between the divinely ordered hierarchy of church and state and the philosophical claim that human beings owe obedience to no ruler — framed as an "innate liberty" that the document condemns as a pretext for sedition. Internally, there is also a tension between the ideal of a holy, well-formed clergy and the reality of poorly qualified priests entering church service.

## Unstructured

**Tone and voice.** The document opens in a markedly humble and personal register — Pius VI recounts his own unworthiness, tears of gratitude, and fear at the weight of leadership. This pastoral humility quickly gives way to an authoritative, almost martial tone ("arise and gird on your sword, which is the word of God") as the central diagnosis of crisis takes hold. The closing returns to warmth, ending with an apostolic blessing and announcement of a concurrent jubilee. The oscillation between vulnerability and commanding urgency is deliberate: the pope establishes his own humanity before issuing strong directives.

**Flow of argument.** The document has a clear four-part movement:
1. *Personal preamble* (§§1–3): Pius reflects on his election as a divine gift he does not deserve, invokes fear at the Church's present condition, and asks the bishops to pray for him.
2. *Practical governance* (§§4–5): Attention to two concrete matters — the quality of clergy admitted and the material dignity of worship spaces — framed as the bishops' baseline duty.
3. *The central crisis* (§§6–7): A sustained and highly charged analysis of the "philosophy full of deceit." This section is the emotional and argumentative center of the letter. It identifies specific doctrinal errors (denial of God, rejection of revelation, assertion of absolute natural freedom) and traces how this philosophy spreads — through academies, noble houses, royal courts, and even into "the sanctuary." The serpent/poison metaphor recurs.
4. *Call to action* (§§8–9): Bishops are urged to expel bad books, isolate the infected, preach Scripture boldly, teach obedience to lawful authority, and maintain unity. Biblical precedents (Josaphat's campaign of public reading, the priest Helcias and King Josiah) ground the action plan.

**Key framing choices.** The document never names specific philosophers, countries, or movements — a significant choice. The threat is rendered as a diffuse, almost supernatural contagion ("like a cancer," "plague of epidemic proportions," armed by the devil with "the poison of his deceitfulness"). This framing widens the addressable audience and avoids narrowing the critique to any single tradition while rhetorically intensifying the danger. The alignment of philosophical irreligion with social insubordination (priests and kings together as targets of this "monstrous conspiracy against innate liberty") means the Church's pastoral battle and the state's political interest are consciously presented as unified. Obedience to civil rulers is explicitly part of the episcopal preaching mandate. The figure of the cowardly hireling-shepherd (from Gregory) is wielded against episcopal silence, making inaction a moral failure, not a neutral posture.

**Notable quotations that anchor the document's self-understanding:**
- "men who are always learning but never arriving at the knowledge of truth" (2 Tim 3, applied to the philosophical movement)
- "They say they are wise and they have become fools" (echoing Romans 1)
- "man is born free and subject to no one" — cited as the subversive core claim
- Bernard's lament: "in peace is my sorrow most sorrowful" — the Church's deepest grief now comes not from pagans or heretics but from within
