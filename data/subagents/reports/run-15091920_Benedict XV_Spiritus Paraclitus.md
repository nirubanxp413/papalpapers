# Spiritus Paraclitus

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The encyclical calls all Catholics — especially clergy — to assiduous, reverent study of Holy Scripture, grounded in the example and doctrine of St. Jerome, affirming Scripture's divine inspiration, absolute inerrancy, and its supreme role as the path to knowledge and love of Christ.
- **Summary:** Issued on the 1500th anniversary of St. Jerome's death, *Spiritus Paraclitus* presents Jerome's life and writings as the model for Catholic biblical scholarship. Benedict XV traces Jerome's doctrine that Scripture is entirely God-authored, wholly inerrant, and indispensable to salvation and priestly ministry. The encyclical rebukes contemporary critics who limit inspiration to "religious" elements only or deny the historical truth of Scripture, aligning their errors against the teaching of Leo XIII's *Providentissimus Deus*. It then prescribes practical formation: daily reading of Scripture by laity and clergy alike, humble submission to the Church's interpretive tradition, attention first to the literal sense before the spiritual, and preaching rooted in the Bible's own simplicity rather than rhetorical display.
- **Response to:** The encyclical explicitly responds to (a) "modern innovators" who distinguish a primary/religious and secondary/profane element in Scripture and argue that only the former is protected from error; (b) critics who deny historical truth in Scripture and claim the sacred writers narrated "in accordance with general opinion or even on baseless evidence"; (c) those who undermine trust in the Gospels by attributing passages to anonymous later compilers rather than eyewitnesses; (d) clerics and professors of sacred learning who "in their own conceit either openly repudiate or at least attack in secret the Church's teaching" on inspiration; and (e) those who disregard the decisions of the Pontifical Biblical Commission. The encyclical also responds positively to the anniversary occasion, building explicitly on Leo XIII's *Providentissimus Deus* and Pius X's establishment of the Biblical Institute.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs bishops to promote: daily Bible reading by the faithful (citing Jerome's specific programmes for women, monks, and clergy); formation of local societies modelled on the Society of St. Jerome to distribute the Gospels and Acts; use of the Biblical Institute in Rome for clerical formation; an amended and restored edition of the Vulgate (already entrusted by Pius X to the Benedictines); and a style of preaching that draws substance from Scripture rather than "declamatory eloquence." More broadly, it summons Eastern churches to return to communion with the See of Peter, and "those Christian nations which have unhappily fallen away from Mother Church" to return to her.
- **Tension:** Two interlocking tensions run through the text. First, an epistemic tension between the Church's tradition of plenary inerrancy and the claims of modern critical scholars who want to exempt "profane" or historical elements of Scripture from that guarantee — a tension Benedict frames as a battle for the integrity of faith itself, not merely an academic dispute. Second, an ecclesial tension between submission to Church authority (the Roman See, the Pontifical Biblical Commission, the Fathers) and the "insane freedom in ventilating opinions" of critics and innovators within Catholic institutions — named explicitly as clerics and professors — who treat their own scholarly judgment as superior to magisterial teaching.

## Unstructured

**Overall shape of the argument.** The encyclical is constructed in four distinct movements: (1) a narrative biography of St. Jerome as exemplary scholar (§§1–7); (2) a doctrinal exposition of Jerome's — and therefore the Church's — teaching on inspiration, inerrancy, and the authority of Scripture (§§8–30); (3) a practical guide to biblical formation drawn from Jerome's own instructions to disciples, clergy, and laity (§§31–57); and (4) a lyrical conclusion on the spiritual fruits of Scripture study — love of Christ, love of the Church, interior peace — with a final appeal for unity (§§58–69).

**Tone.** The document is emphatically authoritative in its doctrinal sections (§§13–27 in particular), deploying a dense accumulation of citations from Jerome, Augustine, and the Council texts to foreclose debate on inerrancy. Yet the sections on formation and spiritual fruit are genuinely pastoral and even warm: Benedict quotes Jerome's personal letters at length and dwells on Jerome's affective attachment to Bethlehem, to Christ, and to Scripture as a source of "interior peace." The encyclical presents Jerome not merely as a polemical weapon but as a beloved model.

**Key framing devices.**

- *Jerome as proxy.* Benedict does not argue directly for his positions but routes them through Jerome: "Jerome's teaching on the superexcellence and truth of Scripture is Christ's teaching" (§30). This allows him to claim patriarchal and even Christological authority for positions that are really responses to 1920-era critical scholarship.

- *"Ignorance of the Bible means ignorance of Christ."* Quoted from Jerome (§63), this formula is the affective centre of the encyclical. It converts what might seem a technical dispute about inspiration into a matter of personal relationship with Christ.

- *The flood metaphor.* Near the close (§68), Benedict warns that "this flood seems on the verge of sweeping away all human institutions — unless God steps in to prevent it," explicitly linking intellectual departure from Scripture and Church authority to the civilisational crisis visible in 1920. Jerome, who spoke of the "Ark of Noe," is invoked as a guide back to safety.

- *Women as exemplars of biblical formation.* Unusually for a papal document, the encyclical devotes sustained attention to Jerome's female disciples — Paula, Eustochium, Demetrias, Marcella, Laeta. Their Hebrew learning and scriptural memorisation are held up without qualification as models. This is not framed as a feminist argument but as evidence that no one, regardless of state of life, is exempt from the call to Scripture study.

**Notable doctrinal moves.**

- The encyclical carefully distinguishes what Leo XIII actually said from what his critics claim he said, refuting the argument that *Providentissimus Deus* permitted exempting "profane" content from inerrancy (§§19–23). Benedict treats this as a misreading, not merely a disputed interpretation.

- On method, the encyclical endorses Jerome's rule: literal sense first, then the spiritual, in proper order — and rejects both naive allegorism and the modern tendency to flatten the text into "pseudo-historical narratives" or genre-based exemptions from truth-claims (§§50–53).

- The ecclesial argument culminates in Jerome's formula: "Any man who is joined to Peter's Chair, he is my man" (§38). Scriptural authority and Roman authority are presented as inseparable: to depart from one is to depart from the other.
