# Laetamur Admodum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** In the face of ongoing crises in Poland, Hungary, and the Near East, Pius XII calls on Catholics worldwide to join in a "crusade of prayer" trusting that divine providence — not armed force — is the only true path to a just and lasting peace.
- **Summary:** Written on 1 November 1956, the document opens with gratitude that previous prayers have borne fruit: Cardinals Wyszyński and Mindszenty have been freed and restored to their sees, and a "new dawn of peace" appears to be emerging in Poland and Hungary. The Pope then turns to a second, still-active crisis — the "flames of another war" threatening the Near East — and urges all bishops and faithful to continue and intensify their public prayers. He addresses national leaders directly, insisting that war yields only "misfortunes and disasters" and that genuine differences among peoples can only be resolved through "reason, law, prudence, and justice." The document closes with a prayer that God, through Mary's intercession, will end the threat of war and protect the rights of the Church everywhere.
- **Response to:** (1) The Pope's own prior Encyclical Letter calling for public prayers for Poland and Hungary; (2) the unjust expulsion and imprisonment of Cardinals Wyszyński and Mindszenty from their sees; (3) the emerging military conflict in the Near East described as "flames of another war being fanned menacingly."
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) Continued and renewed public prayer by Catholics globally; (2) a "crusade of prayer" directed at ending the Near East crisis; (3) Catholics in Poland and Hungary uniting around their restored lawful shepherds to advance "this holy cause"; (4) heads of governments choosing the "ways of justice" over "the rash road of violence."
- **Tension:** Two intertwined tensions run through the text: (a) the tension between the spiritual weapons the Church wields ("the weapons of our warfare are not carnal, but powerful before God") and the earthly forces — political imprisonment, military conflict — that threaten both the Church and peace; (b) the tension between cautious hope (the partial resolution in Poland and Hungary) and ongoing fear ("Our heart still fears," "the threat of another frightening crisis"), reflecting a moment suspended between relief and new danger.

## Unstructured

The document is notably brief — thirteen numbered paragraphs — and functions as a follow-up communiqué rather than a developed doctrinal argument. Its tone is pastoral but urgent, almost telegraphic, with Pius XII moving swiftly from thanksgiving to renewed alarm to direct appeal.

**Structure and flow:** The argument has a simple two-beat structure. The first beat (§§1–4) is one of qualified relief: prayers were answered, the Cardinals were freed, Catholics should rally around their shepherds. The second beat (§§5–11) pivots sharply to fresh crisis: the Near East. This pivot is made explicit with "But even while Our heart still fears on this account, We behold the threat of another frightening crisis" (§5). The document then widens its appeal — from the Church's faithful to "the heads of governments" — before closing with a renewed exhortation to prayer.

**Framing of prayer as power:** A recurring and distinctive move in the text is the elevation of prayer to the status of a geopolitical instrument. The phrase "the weapons of our warfare are not carnal, but powerful before God" (quoting 2 Cor. 10:4) frames intercession not as passive resignation but as an active force capable of illuminating the minds and inclining the wills of world leaders. This framing allows the Pope to speak simultaneously as a spiritual guide and as a stakeholder in international affairs without claiming direct political authority.

**Direct address to governments:** The appeal in §§8–10 to "those who hold the destinies of nations in their hands" is unusually direct. The Pope does not merely invoke general principles; he insists that "no other interest motivates Us but the common good," effectively positioning the papacy as a disinterested moral witness to the conflict. The argument against war is pragmatic as well as moral: war "brings no lasting benefit, but a host of misfortunes and disasters," and a conflict can start as "a tiny spark" and "burst into an enormous conflagration."

**Christological and Marian anchoring:** The Near East crisis is given a specifically Christian geography — the threatened region is identified as "not far from that holy land where angels descended from Heaven and hovered over the crib of the Divine Child, announcing peace to men of good will" (Luke 2:14). This grounds the geopolitical in the sacred. Similarly, the closing prayer invokes Mary's intercession and the feast of Christ the King, closing the document on a note of eschatological hope: the entire human family reunited "under the sway of His most sweet rule."

**Overall tone:** The document is authoritative in register (addressed to bishops, employing the papal "We") but strongly pastoral and affective in content — the Pope repeatedly speaks of fear, joy, and hope as personal experiences. The brevity and directness suggest a document written in response to fast-moving events rather than as a carefully elaborated theological treatise.
