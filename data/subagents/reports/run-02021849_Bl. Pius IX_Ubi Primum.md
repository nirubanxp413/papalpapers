# Ubi Primum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** Pius IX initiates a formal consultation of the world's bishops on whether to define the Immaculate Conception of Mary as dogma, framing it as an urgent spiritual act for the protection and consolation of a Church afflicted by widespread evils.
- **Summary:** Written from Gaeta on 2 February 1849, this letter addresses the Catholic bishops worldwide to report that petitions from bishops, cathedral chapters, and religious orders — including the Dominicans — have accumulated requesting a solemn dogmatic definition that Mary was conceived without original sin. Pius IX declares his own profound personal devotion to Mary, announces that he has commissioned theologians and cardinals to examine the question, asks the bishops to promote public prayers for divine guidance, grants permission for the Roman canonical hours of the Conception to be used in their dioceses, and requests that the bishops respond with their own judgment and an account of devotion among their clergy and faithful on the matter.
- **Response to:** The document responds to the sustained accumulation of formal petitions from bishops, canonical chapters, and religious congregations requesting both liturgical permission to use the word "Immaculate" and a dogmatic definition; it also responds to "the violent hurricane of evils which… everywhere afflict the Church" and the "tribulations, anxiety, difficulties, and punishments of God's anger which afflict the world because of the sins of men."
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs bishops to (1) arrange public prayers in their dioceses for God's enlightenment of the Pope on this matter, (2) report back on the state of their clergy's and people's devotion to the Immaculate Conception, and (3) share their own personal judgment on whether the definition should be made. It also grants liturgical faculty for use of the Roman canonical hours of the Conception without further authorization.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the widespread and fervent popular and episcopal desire for a dogmatic definition and the institutional requirement for deliberate, authoritative discernment before such a solemn act — the Pope notes that "many persons are now wondering why this honor has not already been accorded." A secondary tension is implied between a Church besieged by worldly evils and sin, and the hoped-for Marian intercession that can "transform Our sadness into joy."

## Unstructured

**Key themes and flow of argument**

The document is brief but carefully structured around a single pastoral-administrative purpose: gathering information and building ecclesiastical consensus as a precondition to a dogmatic definition. Pius IX opens by recalling the existing momentum — a "most ardent and wondrous revival" of desire for the definition that predates his own pontificate, extending back to his predecessor Gregory XVI. The petitions cited are notably institutional (bishops, chapters, the Order of Preachers), lending them canonical weight.

The middle section pivots to the Pope's own interiority: his lifelong devotion to Mary, described with warmth ("nothing has ever been closer to Our heart"), and his recognition that divine guidance is required before so momentous a step. This personal register is immediately elevated into a doctrinal claim about Mary's universal protective role — "God has committed to Mary the treasury of all good things" — drawing on Bernard of Clairvaux and Gregory the Great, the only citations in the text. The invocation of current afflictions ("violent hurricane of evils") is notably vague in content but urgent in tone; no specific crisis is named, which keeps the letter focused on the Marian theme rather than on political circumstances.

The document then shifts to its functional requests: prayers, diocesan reports, liturgical faculty. The tone throughout is collegial and consultative rather than unilaterally declarative — the Pope positions himself as seeking guidance from bishops and from God, not issuing a decision. This consultative stance is itself strategically significant: the letter is as much a preparation for consensus as it is a communication.

**Tone**

Predominantly pastoral and devotional, with formal-authoritative inflections when conferring liturgical faculty. The Pope writes as a fellow devotee of Mary ("From our earliest years…") before writing as a legislator ("We likewise, by this present Letter, grant to you… the faculty"). The double register — personal piety and institutional authority — is maintained throughout.

**Notable framing**

Mary is framed not only as an object of devotion but as an active intercessor and protector of the Church in a time of present crisis. The phrase "she desires to transform Our sadness into joy" personifies Mary's will in a way that makes the dogmatic question feel urgent and timely rather than merely antiquarian. The document also frames the Pope's own uncertainty and prayer as a model of humility — he asks for light "to enlighten Our mind with the light of His grace," placing himself under divine judgment before acting.
