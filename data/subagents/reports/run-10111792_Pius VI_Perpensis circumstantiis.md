# Perpensis circumstantiis

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Facing the worsening calamity in France and the dispersal of its clergy, Pius VI expands and extends the faculties granted to French bishops to absolve repentant "sworn" and "intruded" priests regardless of diocesan boundaries.
- **Summary:** This brief declaration responds to the deteriorating ecclesiastical situation in revolutionary France, where archbishops, bishops, and other administrators have fled or are absent from their dioceses. Earlier indults (March 19 and June 13, 1792) had granted limited faculties to absolve priests who took revolutionary oaths or were installed outside canonical process, but restricted their use to within each bishop's own diocese and within territories subject to the French King. The Pope now removes those geographic restrictions and extends all such faculties for a further year from the date of their expiry, conditional on the calamity continuing and on the absolutions following the conditions set in the prior indults.
- **Response to:** The flight and absence of bishops, vicars, and diocesan administrators from France; the practical failure of previously granted faculties due to those geographic and jurisdictional limitations; the ongoing "grave calamities" afflicting the Kingdom of France.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Archbishops, bishops, and diocesan administrators of France (including those in exile), directing them to use these expanded faculties—personally or through delegates approved by the local Ordinary—to receive repentant priests back into the Church wherever those priests now reside.
- **Tension:** The canonical requirement that bishops exercise authority within their own diocese and under the authority of the French Crown clashes with the reality of mass clerical displacement; the document resolves this tension by explicitly suspending the territorial and jurisdictional restrictions "in forza del presente indulto."

## Unstructured

The document is almost purely administrative in form—a canonical declaration adjusting the terms of earlier papal indults—yet its brevity contains a significant ecclesial and social situation. The phrase *"calamità di questi tempi"* ("calamity of these times") is the document's only allusion to the revolutionary upheaval; Pius VI does not name the French Revolution directly, but the context is unmistakable from the details: a kingdom whose clergy have fled, priests who swore revolutionary oaths (*giurati*) or were installed by non-canonical means (*intrusi*), and bishops unable to function within their own jurisdictions.

The pastoral logic is one of mercy under emergency conditions. The document consistently invokes *pentimento* (repentance) as the gate through which absolution may pass: only priests who are *pentiti* and seek to return to the Catholic Church are eligible. The tone is juridical and measured rather than prophetic or condemning; there is no polemic against the Revolution here, only a quiet administrative repair of the mechanisms by which the Church might reclaim its dispersed clergy.

The argument's flow is tightly procedural: (1) state the worsening circumstances and the absence of bishops; (2) observe that existing faculties have proven void and without effect; (3) declare new, broader faculties removing the geographic restriction; (4) impose conditions (the prior indults' requirements still apply; delegates must be approved by the Ordinary of the place of residence); (5) extend the entire package for one additional year contingent on the calamity persisting.

Notable framing: the extension is explicitly conditional—"*se continuerà ancora la calamità di questi tempi*" ("if the calamity of these times continues")—which frames the whole measure as emergency and temporary rather than a permanent structural change to canon law. This keeps the document firmly within the logic of dispensation rather than reform.
