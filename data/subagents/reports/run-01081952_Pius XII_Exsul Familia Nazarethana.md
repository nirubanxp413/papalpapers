# Exsul Familia Nazarethana

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Church has a sacred duty to provide continuous spiritual care to migrants, refugees, and exiles of every nationality, grounded in the archetype of the Holy Family's flight into Egypt, and must organize this care through systematic, canonical structures.
- **Summary:** Promulgated on August 1, 1952 by Pius XII, this Apostolic Constitution begins by establishing the Holy Family of Nazareth—fleeing into Egypt to escape Herod—as the eternal archetype and protector of all migrants and refugees. The document then surveys the Church's historical record of caring for migrants from the early Church through the pontificates of Leo XIII, Pius X, Benedict XV, Pius XI, and Pius XII himself, cataloguing institutions, societies, colleges, and regulations created to serve emigrants. The second half transitions into normative canonical legislation, establishing the authority of the Consistorial Congregation over migration affairs, creating the Office of Delegate for Migration Affairs, and regulating the duties of missionary priests, ship chaplains, local ordinaries, and the Pontifical College of Priests serving Italian migrants. The document thus combines a theological and historical rationale with binding ecclesiastical law.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to: the massive post-WWII displacement of millions of refugees and displaced persons; forced deportations and compulsory migrations caused by totalitarian and imperialistic states ("unrestrained desire for extending national boundaries… cruel and shameless deportation of entire nations"); economic emigration driven by poverty and overpopulation; the 1948 Palestine war which created new streams of Arab refugees; the inadequacy of existing canonical structures to meet these needs; and requests from bishops and cardinals (through Cardinal Piazza) for updated regulations to organize pastoral care of immigrants.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs future action towards: the Consistorial Congregation as the central authority for all Latin-rite migrant spiritual care; local ordinaries to assign same-language or same-nationality priests to immigrant communities; Italian bishops to establish committees for departing migrants and provide catechetical preparation before emigration; an annual "Migrant Day" to be celebrated on the first Sunday of Advent throughout the Catholic world; the training of future missionary priests through the Pontifical College before assignment to migrant work; and a push for receiving-nation governments to liberalize immigration laws (the document records the Pope's repeated appeals to US Senators and Congressmen).
- **Tension:** Multiple tensions are named or clearly implied: (1) the spiritual danger facing migrants who, once separated from their parishes and priests, are targeted by unnamed "perverse men" who offer material aid while aiming to destroy faith; (2) the tension between national sovereignty ("the sovereignty of the State… must be respected") and the natural right of persons to migrate, which the document insists cannot be blocked "for inadequate or unjustified reasons"; (3) the tension between the urgency of material relief and the primacy of spiritual care—"While we do not neglect whatever material assistance is permitted, we seek primarily to aid them with spiritual consolation"; (4) canonical jurisdictional tensions between the Consistorial Congregation, the Congregation for the Oriental Church, and the Congregation for the Propagation of the Faith over migrant priests of different rites; (5) the tension between the need for priests who speak migrants' languages and the chronic shortage of such priests, requiring episcopal sacrifices to send priests abroad.

## Unstructured

**Structure and flow of argument:**

The document moves in two large arcs. The first (Title I) is historical-theological: it opens with the image of the Holy Family in Egypt as the archetype of all refugee families, then unfolds a survey of the Church's care for migrants across centuries—from St. Ambrose ransoming captives and medieval pilgrims' halls in Rome, through colonial-era missionary priests, to the modern migrant crises produced by 19th-century industrialization and 20th-century war. The historical survey is organized by pontificate (Leo XIII, Pius X, Benedict XV, Pius XI, Pius XII), each section cataloguing institutions founded, societies approved, or letters written. This accumulation of evidence functions rhetorically to demonstrate the Church's unbroken priority on migrant care and to rebuff what the text calls the enemies of the Church who assail her "provident enterprises… so unjustly."

The second arc (Title II) is legislative. Having established the historical and theological warrant, Pius XII promulgates binding canonical norms across six chapters: the Consistorial Congregation's competency; the Delegate for Migration Affairs; Directors and missionary priests; local ordinaries' duties toward aliens; Italian bishops' special obligations; and the Pontifical College. The tone shifts from pastoral-historical to formally juridical, listing numbered regulations with explicit canonical cross-references.

**Tone:**

The document moves between at least three registers. In the introduction and historical survey, it is pastoral and grandly narrative—describing the Church's charitable work with evident pride and emotion ("with an aching heart we recall the great masses of refugees"). In Pius XII's first-person accounts of wartime efforts ("We tried everything possible, striving, urging, entreating, pleading"), the tone is almost personally confessional. In the canonical Title II, the register becomes precisely juridical. The closing lines assert full papal authority ("we reject as null and void every contrary measure").

**Key themes from the text itself:**

1. *The Holy Family as archetype*: The opening image of Jesus, Mary, and Joseph fleeing Herod is not ornamental—it theologically grounds the entire document. The Church's care for migrants is an imitation of and participation in the condition of the Son of God himself. This framing elevates migrant care from a charitable option to an ecclesial obligation of the highest order.

2. *The right to migrate as natural law*: The document explicitly grounds the right of migration in natural law and in the theology of creation: "the Creator of the universe made all good things primarily for the good of all." Sovereignty of states is acknowledged but subordinated to this prior right when people are driven by genuine need.

3. *The spiritual danger as primary concern*: Throughout, the document is alert to what it calls the danger to the faith of migrants—attacks by unnamed adversaries who "associated with migrants under the pretext of bringing material aid, but with the intent of damaging their souls." The Church's response is always primarily spiritual (sacraments, preaching, pastors), with material aid presented as secondary.

4. *Language and nationality as pastoral categories*: The document repeatedly insists that effective pastoral care requires priests who share the language and if possible the nationality of their immigrant flock—citing the Fourth Lateran Council (1215) and building an entire institutional architecture (nationality parishes, missionary priests, the Pontifical College) on this premise. This is not merely cultural sensitivity but a canonical principle.

5. *Institutional memory and continuity*: The long historical survey functions to demonstrate that the Church has always done this work and is its originator—the document explicitly complains that the Church's charitable pioneer role is "unjustly assailed by her enemies and scorned and overlooked." The document is partly a record and partly a defence.

6. *Post-WWII crisis as occasion*: While the theological rationale is eternal, the immediate urgency is the post-war world: millions of displaced persons, totalitarian-imposed deportations, the Palestine refugee crisis of 1948, overpopulated European nations, and closed immigration laws in receiving countries. The document does not dwell on political analysis but names these realities directly as the context for legislation.

**Notable framing:**

The document's opening move—identifying the Holy Family as "the archetype of every refugee family"—is a striking theological claim that runs through the entire text as an implicit warrant. Every institutional arrangement that follows (the Consistorial Congregation, the Delegate for Migration Affairs, the ship chaplains) is presented as an extension of the Church's imitation of God's own choice to share the condition of the displaced. The document thus frames bureaucratic canonical legislation as participation in the mystery of the Incarnation.
