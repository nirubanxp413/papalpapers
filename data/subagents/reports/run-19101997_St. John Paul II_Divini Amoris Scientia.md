# Divini Amoris Scientia

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** This apostolic letter proclaims St. Thérèse of the Child Jesus a Doctor of the Universal Church on the grounds that her "science of divine love" — the "little way" of spiritual childhood, trust, and merciful love — constitutes an eminent and universally fruitful spiritual doctrine worthy of that title.
- **Summary:** Pope John Paul II formally declares Thérèse of Lisieux a Doctor of the Universal Church, arguing that despite lacking formal theological training she received a special charism of wisdom from the Holy Spirit. The document surveys her life, her autobiographical writings, and the century-long reception of her message by popes, bishops, theologians, and the faithful worldwide. Its central doctrinal claim is that Thérèse's "little way" — grounded in Scripture, shaped by Carmelite tradition, and centred on the merciful Trinitarian love of God and the experience of divine filiation — represents a mature, original synthesis of Christian spirituality accessible to all the faithful and beneficial to the Church's missionary and contemplative vocation.
- **Response to:** (1) Widespread petitions from Episcopal Conferences and the faithful requesting that Thérèse be named a Doctor; (2) The centenary of Thérèse's death in 1897, which the document treats as a providential moment for recognition; (3) The long record of papal acknowledgement of her holiness (from Pius X through John Paul II himself); (4) The legacy of Jansenism, which the document says Thérèse's doctrine helped heal by stressing divine mercy over divine justice.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) The forthcoming Great Jubilee of the Year 2000, framing Thérèse as a teacher for the threshold of a new era; (2) Young people, identified as those whom Thérèse especially illumines as "leaders and witnesses of the Gospel to new generations"; (3) The Church's ongoing missionary work, noting her patronage of the missions alongside Francis Xavier; (4) A broader invitation to all the faithful — regardless of state or education — to embrace the "little way" as the universal path of holiness.
- **Tension:** The document names and resolves a tension between learned theological wisdom and the humble, "little" wisdom given to simple souls by the Holy Spirit: Thérèse had no formal theological training yet is held to surpass many scholars in doctrinal depth. A secondary tension concerns Jansenist severity (emphasising divine justice and fear) versus the document's insistence on divine mercy and fatherly love as the proper heart of the Gospel.

## Unstructured

**Key themes and flow of argument**

The letter opens with a scriptural anchor — God reveals the secrets of the kingdom to the "little and humble" rather than the learned (Luke 10:21–22) — which functions as the theological thesis for everything that follows. The document then moves through three broad movements.

First, a biographical and historical survey (§§2–5): Thérèse's life is traced from her birth in 1873, through her entry to the Carmel of Lisieux, to her death in 1897. The pace of her Church recognition — beatification in 1923, canonisation in 1925, declaration as patron of the missions in 1927 — is presented as itself evidence of extraordinary spiritual fruitfulness.

Second, an assessment of her writings and doctrine (§§6–10): John Paul II catalogues her literary corpus (three autobiographical manuscripts, 266 letters, 54 poems, prayers, theatrical pieces, last sayings) and then argues for the "eminent doctrine" required of a Doctor. The argument rests on several claims: (a) her teaching conforms to and illumines Scripture and Catholic tradition; (b) she synthesises theology and spiritual life with rare coherence; (c) the core of her doctrine — Trinitarian merciful love, divine filiation, the "little way" — is doctrinally rich despite its apparent simplicity; (d) she corrected a pastoral distortion (Jansenism) by restoring a proper understanding of mercy; and (e) the Catechism of the Catholic Church itself cites her.

Third, the formal proclamation and its significance (§§11–12): The letter argues for the particular timeliness of this designation — Thérèse is a woman, a contemplative, and a young person, each quality framed as a sign for the contemporary Church. The formal words of proclamation are quoted verbatim, and the document closes with a decree affirming the letter's perpetual force.

**Tone**

The tone is predominantly celebratory and pastoral rather than polemical or regulatory. John Paul II writes with evident personal warmth; he quotes his own 1980 address at Lisieux and describes his announcement of the proclamation at World Youth Day in Paris as a personal joy. At the same time, the document adopts the formal juridical register required of an apostolic letter effecting a canonical act — it closes with a standard nullification clause. The combination gives the text a dual register: liturgical-festive in its narrative sections, authoritative-legal at its conclusion.

**Notable framing**

The letter repeatedly positions Thérèse's doctrine as simultaneously very simple and very deep, using this paradox to reinforce its opening scriptural theme. Her lack of scholarly training is not a liability but a credential — evidence that her wisdom is genuinely infused rather than acquired. The document also places her in explicit continuity with the Doctors who formed her (Teresa of Ávila, John of the Cross) and with the broader Magisterium (Second Vatican Council, *Dei Verbum*, *Lumen gentium*), carefully locating her originality within, not outside, the tradition. The choice of World Mission Sunday for the proclamation — noted explicitly — aligns her contemplative "little way" with the Church's outward apostolic vocation, a connection the text reinforces by recalling her co-patronage of the missions.
