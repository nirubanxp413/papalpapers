# Exultavit Cor Nostrum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Amid widespread spiritual and social crisis driven by the enemies of God and civil society, the Pope calls all bishops and faithful to unite in fervent prayer, penance, and renewed devotion—anchored in the Jubilee—so that God's mercy might dispel error and restore peace.
- **Summary:** Pius IX opens by rejoicing at the spiritual fruits of the recent Jubilee (conversions, sacramental participation, prayer) before pivoting to a stark catalogue of evils threatening both religion and civil society: philosophical indifferentism, unbridled liberty, contempt for authority, corrupt literature, and conspiratorial movements. His prescription is collective, humble prayer addressed to God through the intercession of Mary, Peter, Paul, and the saints. He then announces an extension of the Jubilee and orders public prayers throughout all dioceses, expressing hope that unified faith, piety, and love will expand Christ's kingdom.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to (1) the encouraging spiritual fruits of the recently concluded Jubilee observed across many dioceses, and (2) the ongoing "wicked times" characterised by anti-Catholic hostility, indifferentism, irreligion, moral corruption, seditious literature, and movements aiming to "eliminate religion and overturn civil society from the ground up."
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs bishops to order public prayers and a renewed Jubilee in their dioceses; it anticipates that unified, humble prayer will move God to dispel error, restore truth, grant peace, and extend Christ's kingdom across all nations.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the consolation of visible spiritual renewal (jubilee fruits, sacramental fervour) and the deep alarm at accelerating forces—intellectual, moral, and political—that the text frames as actively attacking both the Church and the foundations of ordered society. A secondary tension exists between confidence in God's mercy and the urgency of communal intercession required to avert "the scourge of His anger which we deserve for our sins."

## Unstructured

**Tone and register:** The letter is notably pastoral in its opening and closing benediction yet sharpens into an authoritative, even alarmed, diagnostic voice in paragraph 2. Pius IX is not issuing a doctrinal definition but rather a rallying cry—the tone is urgent and apostolic rather than juridical.

**Structural flow:** The document moves in a deliberate arc: gratitude → lamentation → prescription → exhortation → hope. Paragraph 1 celebrates the Jubilee's harvest; paragraph 2 catalogues the opposing darkness with considerable rhetorical heat (a long list of evils piled up in a single extended sentence); paragraph 3 turns to the remedy—prayer through God and the saints; paragraph 4 is the practical directive ordering diocesan prayers and a new Jubilee; paragraph 5 closes with an image of angelic intercession bearing the Church's prayers to God and a vision of unity.

**Notable framing:** The enemies of God are described as enemies of "the human race" as well—linking religious and social destruction as a single project. This conflation of anti-Catholic and anti-social forces is rhetorically significant: the defence of the Church and the defence of civil order are presented as inseparable. The phrase "monstrous opinions and evil contrivances" signals that the threat is primarily ideological; the document names no specific movement or individual, keeping the diagnosis general and trans-regional.

**Role of the Jubilee:** The Jubilee functions as both a reward (a response to existing fervour) and a tool (a vehicle to generate more prayer). Announcing its extension immediately after the catalogue of evils positions it as the Church's chief spiritual counter-measure.

**Marian and saintly intercession:** The appeal to Mary as "more prompt and efficacious than any other," followed by Peter, Paul, local patrons, and "the heavenly company," reflects a fully integrated theology of intercession deployed as practical pastoral strategy rather than as a doctrinal argument.

**What the text does not say:** The document does not name specific nations, political regimes, or heresies by name (unlike, for example, some other Pius IX documents). It does not threaten censure or condemnation; it invites and encourages. The remedies are entirely spiritual—prayer, the sacraments, the Jubilee—with no call for political or legal action.
