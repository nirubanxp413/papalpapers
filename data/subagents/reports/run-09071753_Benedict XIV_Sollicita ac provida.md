# Sollicita ac provida

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** This constitution codifies fair and rigorous procedural rules for the Church's two book-examination congregations, balancing institutional authority over doctrinal censorship with protections for Catholic authors and requirements for charity in theological disputation.
- **Summary:** Benedict XIV issues this constitution to establish binding procedural norms for the Congregation of the Holy Office and the Congregation of the Index in their work of reviewing, correcting, and prohibiting books. Prompted by public complaints that condemnations had been handled carelessly or unjustly, the document details a multi-reviewer process with progressively broader deliberation, special protections for Catholic authors of good reputation (including the right to correct their work before condemnation), duties of impartiality and subject-matter competence among reviewers, strict confidentiality obligations, and an extended argument—grounded in Augustine and Aquinas—for Christian moderation and charity in the written defense of theological opinions. The constitution concludes with a sweeping derogation clause affirming the perpetual validity of its rules.
- **Response to:** Public complaints ("publicis aliquando injustisque quaerelis") that book proscriptions, especially of Catholic authors, were being handled rashly and without adequate procedure; the prior Decree of July 1750 (confirmed by Benedict XIV) which had already begun addressing procedural reform; the broader tradition of Index regulation stretching from Gelasius I through Trent, Pius IV, Sixtus V, and Clement VIII; and the practice of Catholic disputants attacking one another's opinions in print with insults and contempt, giving scandal to the faithful and comfort to heretics.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Future operation of both Congregations in all book examinations; Relators and Consultors in performing their reviewing duties with competence, impartiality, and freedom from national, school, or family partisanship; Catholic authors of repute, who are to be given opportunity to correct their works before condemnation; theological and scholarly writers generally, who are directed to defend their opinions with Christian charity and moderation following the example of Thomas Aquinas; and the practice of presenting condemned opinions "historically" without refutation, which is identified as a deceptive and harmful stratagem to be censured.
- **Tension:** (1) Between institutional necessity—protecting the faithful from doctrinally dangerous books without needing to hear authors—and equity toward Catholic authors of established reputation, who deserve at minimum notification and opportunity to correct before a condemnation decree is published. (2) Between the legitimate diversity of theological schools and opinions (explicitly acknowledged as allowed by the Apostolic See) and the need to suppress books that undermine Catholic dogma or morals. (3) Between the zeal for truth that motivates sharp theological disputation and the demands of Christian charity and "Evangelicae mansuetudinis," with the text insisting these are not in conflict but that writers who wound and mock opponents show neither true love of truth nor charity. (4) Between the secrecy governing the Congregations' deliberations (protecting informers, reviewers, and the institution) and transparency sufficient to allow Catholic authors to learn what must be corrected in their works.

## Unstructured

**Key themes and argument flow:**

The document opens by situating itself within a long tradition of papal vigilance over harmful books, citing Gelasius I, Gregory IX, Pius IV, Pius V, and Clement VIII, and the Council of Trent's mandate to compile an Index. This genealogy establishes Benedict XIV's authority and frames the constitution as consolidation rather than innovation. He adds personal credibility by recounting his own career involvement with both Congregations at every level—Consultor, Inquisitor General, and now Pope presiding at weekly sessions.

The central occasion for the document is stated plainly: public complaints, described as unjust but acknowledged as existing, that book condemnations were arbitrary or hasty. The response is procedural reform articulated in exhaustive institutional detail: for the Holy Office, a sequence of single-reviewer censure → Consultors' vote → Cardinals' deliberation → papal confirmation; for the Index, an analogous structure with a "Preparatory Congregation" of six consultors and the Master of the Sacred Palace serving as an intermediate filter. For Catholic authors of good repute, a second and potentially third independent reviewer is required before a condemnation can proceed, and the clause "donec corrigatur" suspends public decree pending negotiation with the author.

The document then turns to rules of conduct for Reviewers and Consultors (§§14–24), which form the longest and most ethically substantive section. The rules are:
1. Reviewers are not advocates for condemnation—they supply evidence for the Congregation's judgment.
2. Reviewers must be competent in the subject matter of the book and must recuse themselves if not.
3. Judgment must be free of prejudice—national, familial, scholastic.
4. Passages must be read in full context, not extracted in isolation.
5. Ambiguous statements by an otherwise orthodox Catholic author are to be interpreted charitably.

Two further rules address recurring abuses: (i) books that present condemned opinions "historically" without refuting them are to be treated as just as dangerous as those that assert error directly—the text identifies this as a "most subtle invention of human malice"; (ii) authors who defend opinions must not insult or ridicule those who disagree, invoking Innocent XI's 1679 decree requiring abstention from censure of still-controverted opinions, Augustine on love of truth vs. love of one's own opinion, and Thomas Aquinas as the model of how to refute opponents without contempt.

**Tone:** The document is primarily regulatory and institutional—detailed, procedural, authoritative in register. However, the extended theological argument on charity in disputation (§§22–24) shifts into something more exhortatory and pastoral, drawing on patristic and scholastic sources with genuine warmth. The Pope's first-person autobiographical account of his involvement with the Congregations is unusual and lends the text a credibility-establishing rather than purely commanding quality. The document is neither primarily pastoral nor purely legislative; it occupies a distinct register of institutional self-constitution, defining how a major Church institution should operate.

**Notable framing:** Benedict XIV frames institutional rigor and procedural fairness not as limiting papal authority but as expressing it—the rules are described as "Apostolica auctoritate" established. The charitable treatment of Catholic authors and the requirement for subject-matter competence in reviewers are presented as what the Congregations have always practiced ("constanter actum fuisse"), with this constitution merely making explicit what was already implicit. This rhetorical move defends the institution against past criticism while claiming credit for systematizing it.
