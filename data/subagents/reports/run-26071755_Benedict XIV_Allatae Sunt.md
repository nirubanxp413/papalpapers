# Allatae Sunt

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Social
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Apostolic See has always protected the ancient rites of Oriental Catholics and missionaries must not attempt to impose the Latin rite on Orientals returning to Catholic unity.
- **Summary:** Prompted by a missionary priest in Balsera who asked whether Armenian and Syrian Catholics sharing a Latin church should adopt the Latin calendar and be dispensed from their fasting rules, Benedict XIV issues a sweeping historical and canonical defense of Oriental rites. Drawing on decrees, councils, and papal precedents from the ninth through eighteenth centuries, he demonstrates that Rome has consistently demanded doctrinal conformity (rejection of schismatic errors) while explicitly forbidding any interference with approved Oriental liturgical practices. The encyclical reaffirms the standing decree "no innovations are to be made" and commands missionaries to convert Orientals from heresy, not from their rite.
- **Response to:** A concrete pastoral inquiry from a missionary in Balsera about calendar observance and fasting dispensations for Armenian and Syrian Catholics; the broader documented tendency of Latin missionaries to "destroy or at least weaken the Oriental rite" when converting schismatics; earlier decrees of the Congregation for the Propagation of the Faith (notably the 1702 decree forbidding missionaries from dispensing Orientals from their rite) that apparently had not been widely known or observed.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Missionaries working in the Orient, commanding them to concentrate solely on correcting doctrinal error and not on altering the rite of converts; Oriental Catholics themselves, requiring strict observance of their own approved rites (including fasting rules) without seeking to adopt Latin customs on grounds of convenience; Latin missionaries sharing spaces with Orientals, requiring that services of different rites be held at separate times and that Latins not obstruct Oriental worship.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between missionary zeal for uniformity (Latin missionaries who believe unifying all Catholics under the Latin rite would simplify and strengthen the faith) and the Apostolic See's consistent policy of separating doctrinal unity from ritual diversity. A secondary tension exists between Oriental Catholics who, living among Latins, are gradually drawn to abandon their own disciplines (fasting, calendar, communion practices) and Rome's insistence that each community preserve its ancient approved customs. A third tension surfaces in the Filioque question: where doctrinal orthodoxy demands explicit profession that the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son, Rome has pragmatically varied whether it requires the phrase to be inserted into the Creed, depending on whether the omission risks giving scandal or masking residual heresy.

## Unstructured

**Opening and occasion.** The letter opens with a specific, practical problem: a missionary in Balsera reports that Armenian and Syrian Catholics, sharing the Latin church because they have no building of their own, are being observed not keeping their own fasting rules (abstaining from fish), and asks whether they should simply adopt the Gregorian calendar to avoid confusion. The Congregation of General Inquisition answers: "no innovations are to be made." Benedict uses this reply as a springboard for a comprehensive exposition of papal policy toward Eastern rites.

**Argument structure.** The encyclical is essentially an extended historical brief. Benedict marshals century-by-century evidence — Leo IX, Innocent III, Innocent IV, Alexander IV, Gregory X, Eugene IV, Nicholas V, Gregory XIII, Clement VIII, Paul V, and many of his own constitutions — to establish a single consistent principle: popes have always insisted that Orientals returning to Catholic unity abandon their heresies, but have never asked them to abandon their rites. The phrase "no innovations are to be made" is the through-line, repeated and re-confirmed from 1702 onward.

**Tone.** The document is authoritative and declaratory rather than pastoral or exhortatory. It reads more as a juridical treatise than a pastoral letter: it cites canon law, conciliar decrees, archival documents, and a wide range of secondary theological and liturgical scholars (Leo Allatius, Arcudius, Schelestratus, Thomas of Jesus, Cardinal Bona, Assemanus, and others). The pope writes partly in the first person — noting that he himself supervised the revision of the Greek Euchologion and read all the discussions — which lends personal authority to the conclusions.

**Key framing.** The most striking framing device is the explicit statement in paragraph 48: "The Church does not require schismatics to abandon their rites when they return to Catholic unity, but only that they forswear and detest heresy. Its great desire is for the preservation, not the destruction, of different peoples — in short, that all may be Catholic rather than all become Latin." This is the thesis sentence of the whole document, placed near the end as a summary. The distinction between the Catholic faith (non-negotiable, universal) and the Latin rite (one approved rite among several) is treated as historically demonstrated and canonically binding.

**Notable content sections.** Beyond the historical survey, the document addresses several specific practical questions in detail: whether Oriental priests may celebrate their rite in a Latin church (permitted when Orientals have no church of their own); what constitutes forbidden "mixing of rites" (e.g., a Latin consecrating leavened bread, or a priest alternating between rites at will) versus permitted coexistence; the question of the Gregorian calendar (Orientals generally not bound unless explicitly named, but the Apostolic See may require it in specific circumstances); fasting rules (missionaries may not grant general dispensations from Oriental fasting disciplines simply because Latins eat fish); the Filioque addition to the Creed (handled pragmatically — required or waived depending on whether omission would give scandal or mask heresy); and the Greek practice of a single altar with only one Mass per day (to be preserved, with practical accommodation for Latin priests).

**Flow of argument.** The encyclical moves from (1) the specific Balsera inquiry → (2) the standing decree "no innovations" → (3) a historical survey proving Rome has always preserved Oriental rites → (4) the rules for missionaries converting schismatics → (5) the prohibition on transferring from Latin to Oriental rite and on missionaries inducing Orientals to transfer to Latin rite → (6) a taxonomy of how specific Latin/Oriental ritual crossovers have occurred lawfully → (7) detailed treatment of Eucharistic practices, the Creed, Good Friday ceremonies, calendar, and fasting → (8) the practical answer to the Balsera situation → (9) the concluding principle.

**What is absent.** The document does not engage with the question of why the schism happened or with contemporary geopolitics. It explicitly disclaims use of outside historical knowledge about the world ("We shall touch on several main incidents... briefly as possible"). It is entirely focused on ecclesial and canonical questions of rite and jurisdiction.
