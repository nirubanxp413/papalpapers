# Quae causa

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** In the face of escalating threats from impious enemies against the Church and papal sovereignty, the Pope mobilizes the faithful through prayer, penance, and a Jubilee indulgence, combining spiritual arms with material defense.
- **Summary:** Issued on 24 November 1792, this Brief extends to the dioceses of the Papal States and neighboring territories a Jubilee indulgence already opened to Rome. Pius VI cites worsening hostility from "those impious men" who attack religion, the Apostolic See, and his temporal sovereignty. Prayers ordered earlier (June 1790) went unanswered; the crisis has intensified. The Pope's response is twofold: military preparations to defend the coasts, and an intensification of communal prayer, fasting, confession, communion, and almsgiving. Theaters and profane entertainments are banned for the coming year. The Jubilee indulgence is made accessible to all categories of the faithful — including prisoners, the sick, monks, and shepherds — and may be applied to the souls in purgatory. The document closes by insisting that prayer must not cease when the Jubilee ends.
- **Response to:** The escalating attacks of "those impious men" (*quegli uomini empi*) who direct their ferocity and cruelty against the Church, the Apostolic See, and papal sovereignty; a prior letter of 8 June 1790 in which the same threats were first addressed; unanswered earlier prayers that were ordered in Rome before and during the feast of All Saints; and a general climate in which "hostile threats press upon us and every day greater dangers loom."
- **Prescriptive towards:** Continued prayer and pious exercises beyond the Jubilee period, throughout the entire following year; the permanent suppression of theaters and profane entertainments ("baccanali") across all dioceses for the next year; fidelity to divine worship as an ongoing duty; and the moral reform of the people as the precondition for God's mercy.
- **Tension:** The document holds two tensions in productive balance. First, between spiritual arms and material arms: the Pope explicitly combines "prayers and arms" after the model of St. Pius V, yet insists that "what would our forces avail if the God of armies did not cover us with his protection?" — prayer is primary, military preparation is secondary. Second, between divine punishment and divine mercy: God's anger is "aroused by our sins," yet God "does not wish to punish us" and "seeks someone by whom he might be held back" (Ez 22:30); tribulations are thus reframed as invitations to contrition rather than signs of abandonment.

## Unstructured

**Argument and flow:** The Brief opens by recalling the 1790 letter as its direct precedent, establishing continuity of crisis. The situation has deteriorated: enemies are now described as directing the "darts of their ferocity and cruelty" against the Church, the Apostolic See, and papal sovereignty. The Pope recounts the sequence of prayers already ordered — intercessions in Rome during and after the feast of All Saints — and candidly admits they did not produce the desired result ("But not even then were we heard"). This candor is unusual and serves to intensify urgency rather than undermine confidence.

**Spiritual logic:** The core theological argument, drawn from multiple biblical citations (Psalms, Isaiah, Ezekiel, and patristic sources including Bernard of Clairvaux), is that tribulation itself is a gift: it drives the faithful closer to God, as a child clings more tightly to a mother when afraid. This reframes political crisis as spiritual opportunity. The document does not offer a political analysis of the enemy; it names them only as "impious" and motivated by hatred of religion.

**Model of St. Pius V:** A pivotal section invokes St. Pius V (pope during the Battle of Lepanto, 1571) as the direct model for the current moment. His Brief of 9 March 1566 is quoted: "Although the forces of our enemies are great, nevertheless He who is with us is stronger than he who is against us." The parallel drawn is explicit — similar enemies, similar circumstances, same remedy. This framing positions the current threat as a replay of a situation the Church has already survived through prayer.

**Social breadth of the Jubilee:** The indulgence is carefully structured to reach every sector of society — the laity, regular and secular clergy, enclosed religious, prisoners, the sick, and even shepherds who cannot leave their flocks. This inclusivity signals that the mobilization is total: the entire Church is meant to participate in unified supplication.

**Tone:** The document is simultaneously authoritative (it issues juridical decrees — banning theaters, designating churches, specifying the conditions of the indulgence) and pastoral (it narrates the Pope's own emotional response to seeing the Roman crowds spontaneously processing through the streets, and admits he "could not hold back tears of joy"). The pastoral voice is used to motivate; the authoritative voice is used to direct. Bernard of Clairvaux functions as the chief theological interlocutor throughout.

**Notable framing:** The Pope says explicitly that the goal of military preparations "is not to attack, but to defend our goods, our lives, our faith." This defensive framing is significant: the document does not call for a crusade or aggressive counter-action but for a posture of prayerful resistance. The enemy's identity is left theologically rather than politically defined — they are enemies of religion — which keeps the Brief focused on spiritual mobilization rather than political commentary.
