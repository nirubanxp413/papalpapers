# Fausto Appetente Die

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** On the occasion of the 700th anniversary of St. Dominic's death, Benedict XV celebrates the three defining qualities of Dominican preaching—solid doctrine, fidelity to the Apostolic See, and Marian piety—and urges Dominicans to renew these charisms to meet contemporary spiritual need.
- **Summary:** The document commemorates the approaching seventh centenary of St. Dominic's death by tracing his legacy through three interwoven qualities: an uncompromising commitment to doctrinal learning and preaching (exemplified in confounding the Albigensian heretics and producing luminaries such as Thomas Aquinas), supreme devotion to the Roman Pontiff (symbolised by Dominic's vision of sustaining the Lateran Basilica on his shoulder), and zealous Marian piety expressed chiefly through the Rosary. Benedict XV argues that these same qualities are "not less opportune at present than in the time of their Founder," pointing to widespread doctrinal error, spiritual starvation, and estrangement from the papacy as the evils requiring a revived Dominican apostolate.
- **Response to:** The approaching 700th centenary of St. Dominic's death (explicitly named in §1); the pope's personal devotion cultivated during his governance of the Church of Bologna (§1); contemporary spiritual destitution—"many today, destitute of the bread of life, that is, celestial doctrine, are, as it were, in a state of starvation" (§12); a "variety of errors" drawing people from the Faith (§12); Catholics "turned away from the Vicar of Jesus Christ by ignorance of facts or by a perverse will" (§12); and "these troublous times" (§13).
- **Prescriptive towards:** Dominicans of the First Order renewing zeal in preaching the Divine Word so as to foster "reverence for the successor of St. Peter and a devotion to the Virgin Mother" (§13); Dominican Tertiaries instructing the ignorant in "Christian doctrine and morality" (§13); all Dominicans spreading and promoting frequent use of the Rosary among the Christian people (§13); the entire Order making itself "daily more worthy of such a father" through these centenary celebrations (§13).
- **Tension:** Between doctrinal truth and the spread of heresy or "variety of errors" (§5, §12)—a tension the text frames as perennial, linking the Albigensian crisis to unnamed contemporary errors. A second tension runs between loyalty to the Roman Pontiff and forces—whether "popular movements or the tyranny of princes" (§9)—that estrange Catholics from the papacy. A subtler tension exists between the need for rigorous scholarly formation and the urgency of active preaching: Dominic only undertook preaching "after he had worked hard in the Palentine Athenaeum" (§4), a model the document holds up against superficial evangelism.

## Unstructured

The document is primarily hagiographic and commemorative in purpose, but its internal logic moves steadily from past example toward present obligation. Benedict XV organises praise of Dominic around three pillars—doctrine, papal loyalty, Marian devotion—and uses each pillar to diagnose a corresponding contemporary ill: doctrinal ignorance, estrangement from Rome, and insufficient recourse to Mary's intercession. The structure is thus implicitly diagnostic-prescriptive even while its surface register is celebratory.

The tone is pastoral-authoritative: warm toward the Dominican Order and its heritage, but firm in identifying present failures ("many, ungrateful and forgetful children of the Church," §12) and explicit in directing specific remedies to each branch of the Order (First Order preachers, Tertiaries, and all Dominicans regarding the Rosary).

The Rosary receives the document's longest sustained treatment (§11), including a theological argument for Mary's role as the distributrix of all graces and her unfailing responsiveness to petitioners. This elevation of the Rosary beyond a mere devotional practice—framing it as the instrument by which the Virgin "teach[es] the Most Holy Rosary to the Church, the Spouse of her Son"—suggests the document aims not only to honour Dominic but to re-energise Rosary promotion as an active pastoral strategy.

Notable framing choices: the Albigensian heresy is invoked not merely as historical background but as a typological parallel to contemporary error, implying that the same remedy (Dominican preaching grounded in doctrine and Marian piety) applies now. The miraculous preservation of Dominic's book from fire (§5) and the Lepanto victory achieved during the Rosary's recitation (§10–11) are presented as divine confirmations of the Dominican charism, lending the exhortation a providential warrant rather than merely institutional authority.

The document does not engage social or political questions directly; its horizon is entirely ecclesial and spiritual. "These troublous times" (§13)—the only explicit acknowledgment of contemporary difficulty—is left unspecified, functioning as an open signifier that the reader must supply with content, while the remedy (Dominican mission renewed) is given with precision.
