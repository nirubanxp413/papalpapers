# Saepe Nos

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Pope reaffirms a Church decree condemning the "Boycotting" and "Plan of Campaign" tactics used in Ireland's agrarian struggle, calling Catholics to pursue just causes only through lawful and morally sanctioned means.
- **Summary:** Addressed to the bishops of Ireland, *Saepe Nos* defends the Sacred Congregation's decree forbidding the use of Boycotting and the Plan of Campaign. Leo XIII insists the decree was issued after thorough and careful investigation, expresses genuine sympathy for the suffering of the Irish people, but draws a firm line: no cause, however just, permits recourse to violence or injustice. He appeals to the bishops to communicate this ruling clearly and to ensure no ambiguity remains about the full unlawfulness of the condemned practices.
- **Response to:** The document responds to a "untoward excitement" among Irish Catholics following the decree of the Sacred Congregation; specifically to the circulation of "inconsiderate and dangerous opinions" at public meetings, to interpretations that distort the decree's scope, and to open challenges to the Church's authority to rule on the morality of human actions.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs the bishops to convey these instructions to the Irish people, to leave no room for doubt about the binding force of the decree, and to ensure that Catholics advance their interests "by lawful means" and "without prejudice to justice or to obedience to the Apostolic See."
- **Tension:** The core tension is between legitimate political grievance (the genuine suffering and just aspirations of the Irish people, which the Pope explicitly acknowledges) and the moral constraints the Church places on the methods used to pursue those aspirations. A secondary tension runs between popular/nationalist sentiment and ecclesiastical authority — the document addresses directly those who publicly challenged the decree and summoned people to "excited meetings."

## Unstructured

*Saepe Nos* is a short, tightly argued pastoral-disciplinary letter, occupying a space between a doctrinal statement and a direct intervention in a live political crisis. Its tone is distinctly paternal but firm: Leo opens by cataloguing his prior demonstrations of care for Ireland (supporting the church of St. Patrick, sending a trusted legate, corresponding with Cardinal M'Cabe) before pivoting to express grief at the current "untoward excitement." This rhetorical move — establishing affection before issuing correction — is characteristic of the document throughout.

The argument is structured to pre-empt three specific objections the Pope appears to have encountered:

1. **That the decree was issued without adequate knowledge of Irish conditions.** Leo counters by detailing the extent of his investigation: personal interviews with bishops, a specially sent legate, and reliance on "trustworthy sources."
2. **That the Pope is indifferent to Ireland's cause.** Leo denies this forcefully: "We yield to no one in the intensity of Our feeling for the condition of the Irish people."
3. **That obedience to the decree is not required.** This receives the sharpest reply: to deny the Church's authority to determine what is right and wrong in human actions is said to be "but little in harmony with the profession of the Christian religion."

The document explicitly frames the condemned tactics — Boycotting and the Plan of Campaign — not as politically wrong but as *morally* impermissible, invoking "natural right" and "Divine law" as the standards. It connects this ruling to a prior 1881 pastoral from the Irish bishops themselves, which had already cautioned against "refusing to discharge just obligations" and "preventing others from discharging theirs," lending the current decree historical continuity rather than novelty.

The closing instruction to the bishops is pointed: they are to ensure no room remains for doubt and to secure regret from those who "urged on the people to excitement." The document does not engage the specific political or economic details of the Irish land question; it consistently abstains from adjudicating on the justice of Irish grievances as such, focusing instead on the means of redress. The phrase "nothing is so harmful to a cause, however just, as recourse to violence and injustice in its defense" (§8) serves as the letter's moral pivot and most memorable formulation.
