# Address During The Last General Meeting Of Vatican II

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The council's deepest religious purpose was a renewed affirmation of God's primacy, expressed through a deliberate and loving turn toward humanity — discovering that love of man is the first step toward love of God.
- **Summary:** Paul VI closes Vatican II by reflecting on its ultimate religious significance: not primarily a doctrinal or disciplinary exercise, but an act of spiritual self-renewal in which the Church, guided by charity, turned toward the modern world with affection and service. He argues that the council's intense focus on humanity is not a departure from its theocentric mission but its fulfillment — because knowing and loving man as made in God's image is inseparable from knowing and loving God. The council is thus summarized as "a pressing and friendly invitation to mankind of today to rediscover in fraternal love the God" from whom all life flows.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly names the context it responds to: a contemporary age marked by "forgetfulness of God," the conquest of earthly kingdoms over heavenly ones, secularism presented as the "highest wisdom," the human tendency toward "absolute autonomy" from transcendent law, "upheavals and a hitherto unknown decline even in the great world religions," and the distancing between Church and secular society over "recent centuries." It also responds to critics within the council who suspected excessive responsiveness to "passing events, cultural fashions, temporary needs, an alien way of thinking."
- **Prescriptive towards:** The address anticipates a "post-conciliar period" in which the vitality awakened by the council will continue studying unresolved questions. It directs the Church toward ongoing service to humanity, evangelization through pastoral dialogue rather than condemnation, and the renewal of human and social life oriented toward God. It calls modern humanists to recognize the council's "new type of humanism" that honors mankind more deeply by grounding it in the transcendent.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the Church's theocentric mission (God as the "raison d'etre" of all she does) and her pastoral engagement with a secular, anthropocentric world. Paul VI names this tension directly — the concern that the council may have been "swayed" by the world's cultural fashions at the expense of fidelity to tradition — and resolves it by arguing that charity toward man and love of God are not in opposition but are the same movement viewed from different angles. A secondary tension exists between the Church's authoritative teaching role and the pastoral, conversational, "accommodating friendly voice" she chose to adopt during the council.

## Unstructured

The address is structured as a meditative closing reflection rather than a doctrinal pronouncement. Paul VI explicitly sets aside a full synthesis of the council's accomplishments in favor of one "single thought": the council's religious value. This rhetorical choice is itself meaningful — by foregrounding the question of religious value, he is preemptively defending the council against the charge that its human-centered focus was a theological dilution.

The argumentative flow moves in three main arcs:

1. **Affirmation of the theocentric foundation.** Paul VI begins by anchoring the council in John XXIII's original charge — to guard and teach the "sacred deposit of Christian doctrine" more effectively, seeking "first the kingdom of God." He acknowledges the context of a world trending toward earthly conquest, secularism, and the deification of human autonomy. The council's reassertion of a "theocentric and theological concept of man and the universe" is presented as bold, almost countercultural, but ultimately "truly human, wise and salutary."

2. **The council's turn toward humanity as an act of charity.** The document's intellectual center is the claim that the council's deep engagement with modern humanity — described vividly as "man as the tragic actor of his own plays," "the superman of yesterday and today, ever frail, unreal, selfish, and savage," "man the sinner and man the saint" — is not a concession to secular humanism but a fulfillment of the Gospel command to love one's brother. The encounter between "the religion of the God who became man" and "the religion of man who makes himself God" (secular humanism) ended not in condemnation but in a "feeling of boundless sympathy." This is framed as the council's distinctive spiritual character: "charity has been the principal religious feature of this council."

3. **The convergence: humanism becomes Christianity, Christianity becomes centered on God.** The closing movement resolves the tension through a theological chiasmus — "a knowledge of man is a prerequisite for a knowledge of God" — which mirrors the earlier "a knowledge of God is a prerequisite for a knowledge of man." Paul VI draws on St. Catherine of Siena and St. Augustine to ground this mutual implication. The council's final religious meaning is thus: love man in order to love God; in loving man as bearing the face of Christ, "our humanism becomes Christianity, our Christianity becomes centered on God."

**Tone:** The tone is simultaneously authoritative and deeply pastoral — even affectionate. Paul VI writes with rhetorical warmth unusual for a formal closing address, dwelling on the "wave of affection and admiration" that flowed from the council toward the modern world. He is clearly aware of critics on both sides (those who fear the council was too worldly; those who see the Church as irrelevant) and addresses both through the logic of charity. The repeated invocations of Augustine ("Song is the expression of a lover"; "to turn away from whom is to fall") give the address a contemplative, even lyrical register.

**Notable framing:** The address frames the council's optimistic anthropology — "its attitude was very much and deliberately optimistic" — not as naivety but as a theological choice: "instead of depressing diagnoses, encouraging remedies." This is a conscious contrast with a more condemnatory ecclesial posture, presented as the fruit of charity rather than of doctrinal laxity.
