# Iampridem

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Leo XIII demands the restoration of ecclesiastical freedom in Prussia — especially the right of bishops to govern their own seminaries — while framing that freedom as beneficial not only to the Church but to the civil order, the worker question, and German colonial missions.
- **Summary:** Written to the Archbishops and Bishops of Prussia on January 6, 1886, this letter addresses the ongoing damage caused by laws that disrupted the longstanding agreement between the Apostolic See and the kingdom of Prussia. Leo praises German Catholics and their bishops for enduring these trials with fidelity and courage. He then identifies the irreducible demands the Church must insist on before any lasting peace can be reached: the repeal of laws contrary to Catholic teaching, and above all the full freedom of bishops to govern seminaries and form priests according to canon law. He argues these demands are not threats to civil authority but are in fact advantageous for public order — citing the priest's unique capacity to calm social unrest among the working classes, and the Church's missionary role in civilizing colonized peoples.
- **Response to:** Laws ("these laws") that disrupted the mutual understanding between the Apostolic See and Prussia, diminished the number of priests among the Prussian faithful, left parish churches without pastors, and enabled "treacherous men who call themselves 'old Catholics'" to spread "new and perverse teachings." The document also alludes to previous negotiations and goodwill gestures toward Prussian rulers that have not yet produced a satisfactory settlement.
- **Prescriptive towards:** A lasting, "solid" peace between the Church and the Prussian state — contingent on the state granting bishops full and unobstructed freedom to run seminaries, select seminary faculty, and assign priests to parishes. Secondarily, the document looks toward the expansion of priestly numbers as a remedy for social disorder ("the worker question") and as a resource for German colonial missions among "uncivilized nations."
- **Tension:** The central tension is between Church sovereignty over its own internal governance (especially clerical formation) and the claims of state law to regulate or override that governance. Leo frames this as an absolute: the Church's constitution was given by Christ and cannot be subtracted from by any earthly power, yet he simultaneously insists he is prepared to accommodate state desires "insofar as the divine laws and the duty of conscience permit." A secondary tension runs between the suffering of German Catholics (depleted clergy, parishes without pastors, exposure to schismatic groups) and the document's consolatory tone, which converts that suffering into proof of faith and loyalty.

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument:**

The letter opens with extended praise — Leo has long wanted to write this, he admires the bishops, he admires the German Catholic faithful — which functions as a diplomatic preamble before the harder claims that follow. The praise is not merely rhetorical: Leo uses the demonstrated fidelity of German Catholics under duress as evidence that the Church's cause is just and that Catholics are model civil subjects ("they acted not for political considerations but solely from religious duty").

The document then pivots to the unresolved grievance: these laws remain in force and must be abolished. Leo is explicit that he has already shown flexibility and goodwill toward the Prussian government; the remaining non-negotiable is episcopal control of seminaries. Sections 6–9 form the doctrinal core: the Church is a "supernatural society, perfect in its order" (citing his own encyclical *Immortale Dei*), Christ directly established episcopal authority, and from that authority flows the exclusive right to form priests. The seminary sections are notably detailed — Leo draws on the Council of Trent, the apostolic letter *De salute animarum*, and a military analogy ("Would the heads of government permit young men placed in military institutions to have any other teachers than those who excel in this art?") to press the point that specialized formation requires specialized, internally-controlled institutions.

Sections 12–14 then perform a strategic turn: Leo argues that yielding to the Church on this point is in the state's own interest. Priests calm the working poor ("restraining minds hurtling toward seditious plots"), and missionaries — trained by the Church's own methods — are essential to Germany's colonial project ("to conciliate the minds and to win the confidence of these uncivilized nations"). This is not a concession but an argument: the Church's freedom produces civil goods that the state cannot generate by force alone.

The letter closes with calls for unity among the bishops, orientation toward Rome, and prayer.

**Tone:**

The document is formally authoritative but carefully calibrated. Leo consistently softens assertions of absolute Church right with expressions of willingness to negotiate and pastoral warmth toward the German bishops. The phrase "insofar as the divine laws and the duty of conscience permit" appears as the hinge — it sounds like flexibility but marks the limits beyond which he will not move. The overall register is diplomatic-firmness: this is a negotiating document as much as a doctrinal one.

**Notable framings:**

- German Catholic suffering under the laws is reframed as spiritual heroism: "they attached themselves to the Church with a love so much the greater as they saw it the object of harsher trials."
- The "old Catholics" schismatic movement is treated with disdain but briefly — Leo is more interested in the structural legal question than in theological combat with dissenters.
- The worker question (§13) and colonialism (§14) appear as supporting arguments rather than primary concerns, but their presence signals Leo's awareness of a broader social-political context in which the Church must demonstrate its utility to civil governments.
- The military analogy for seminaries (§8) is striking: it concedes the state's logic of institutional control while insisting the Church's "army" must be trained by its own officers.
