# In Hac Tanta

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The life and mission of St. Boniface exemplifies perfect obedience and devotion to the Apostolic See, and his commemoration is presented as a model and call for the restoration of unity between Germany and Rome in the aftermath of war and social disorder.
- **Summary:** Written to the German archbishops in 1919, this letter marks the twelve-hundredth anniversary of St. Boniface's mission to Germany. Benedict XV narrates at length Boniface's close relationship with successive popes — Gregory II, Gregory III, Zachary, and Stephen — demonstrating through historical correspondence and biography how the saint's entire apostolate was grounded in obedience to and union with the Holy See. The pope draws an explicit line from Boniface's example to the present: Germany, now experiencing "disorder and anarchy," is invited to find renewal by returning to that same ancient unity with Rome, which the pope presents as the source of Christian civilization, charity, and true peace.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly names "unexpected events, those manifestations of disorder and of anarchy which have recently occurred among you and among neighboring countries" (§1) as the immediate context. It also responds to the historical fracture implied by past "discords" and separations from Roman unity (§20), referencing "old hatreds, rivalries, and prejudices" (§23) without specifying them further.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document calls for active commemorations of the Boniface centenary across Germany (§3), anticipates a restored "religious unity and peace" (§4), and explicitly invites "those who have separated from unity to return to the Church" (§23). It looks forward to a world where "the rights of Almighty God and of the Church, their laws, their worship and their authority will be restored" (§26), and where "Christian charity will end wars and furious hatreds, dissensions, schisms, and the errors which crawl everywhere" (§26).
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the present state of Germany — marked by social upheaval, anarchy, schism, and separation from the Church — and the ancient ideal of German unity with the Apostolic See embodied by Boniface. A secondary tension exists between the pope's desire to recall and lament past schisms and his stated preference to leave those evils "in eternal oblivion" (§20), choosing consolation and hope over accusation.

## Unstructured

The letter is essentially a hagiographic argument: Benedict XV retells Boniface's story in close detail, drawing extensively on period sources (Willibald's *Vita*, Boniface's own epistles, papal letters), and the narrative is unmistakably shaped to make a single cumulative point — that Boniface's greatness was inseparable from his submission to Rome. The structure is almost forensic: paragraph after paragraph adds another piece of documentary evidence (a letter, a visit, an exchange of gifts, an oath) to demonstrate the unbroken bond between Boniface and four consecutive popes.

The tone is pastoral-historical rather than doctrinal-polemical. Benedict never names Protestantism, the Reformation, or any specific modern political movement, yet the invitation in §23 for "those who have separated from unity to return to the Church after abandoning the old hatreds, rivalries, and prejudices" is unmistakably directed beyond the immediate German Catholic audience. The framing is gently inclusive: the centenary celebrations are proposed as a moment of restoration, not condemnation.

The letter's rhetorical flow moves in three phases:
1. **Framing the present crisis** (§1): brief acknowledgment of "disorder and anarchy" in Germany.
2. **Historical meditation on Boniface** (§§2–21): extended retelling of the saint's mission as a model of apostolic unity with Rome, relying heavily on primary sources and direct quotation.
3. **Application to the present and future** (§§21–28): the memory of Boniface becomes a living "invitation" and "exhortation" to unity, followed by a closing appeal for charity, peace, and the restoration of Church authority, and practical liturgical grants for the centenary observance (§29).

The document is notably warm and consolatory rather than disciplinary. The pope describes Boniface's light as a "ray of light and a messenger of hope and joy" in "dark times" (§2), and explicitly says he recalls these memories "to gather consolation to bear the present more courageously" (§21). This pastoral tone is reinforced by the extensive use of Boniface's own affective language — his "joys and sorrows," his gratitude, his humility — which Benedict mirrors in his own voice.

The Church-State or unity-schism axis is the dominant theological frame. Quotes from Cyprian, Ambrose, and Jerome (§19) are marshalled to ground papal primacy in patristic authority, subtly elevating the historical narrative into a doctrinal statement without making it the explicit centrepiece. The document's political dimension — addressed to German archbishops in the immediate aftermath of WWI and the revolutionary disorders of 1918–19 — is present but handled with restraint, subordinated entirely to the spiritual and historical argument.
