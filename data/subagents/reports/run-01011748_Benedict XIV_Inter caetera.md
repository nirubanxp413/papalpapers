# Inter caetera

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document disciplines two concrete abuses of Carnival in the Papal State — the profanation of Ash Wednesday and the use of masks on holy days — while asserting that Carnival itself is only tolerated by the Church, not sanctioned, and calling bishops to counter festive disorder with visible piety and indulgences.
- **Summary:** Benedict XIV writes to the bishops and archbishops of the Papal State to address two pastoral complaints: first, that Carnival festivities routinely spill over into the first hours of Lent (people attending Mass and receiving ashes still wearing their masquerade dress); second, that masks and public entertainers (charlatans, acrobats) are increasingly present on feast days. The Pope frames Carnival as something the Church "tolerates" rather than approves, traces its disorder back to pagan antecedents, cites Fathers, Councils, and predecessor popes as authorities, and issues concrete norms — no masks on feast days or Fridays, no entertainers in public squares in the mornings of feast days. He closes by granting a plenary indulgence for those who visit the Blessed Sacrament on the tridua of the three Sundays before Lent, and urges bishops to lead by personal example in pious works during Carnival.
- **Response to:** Specific complaints from "zealous bishops" of the Papal State — conveyed to the Pope in person or in writing — about (1) Carnival merging into Ash Wednesday with people receiving ashes in masquerade dress, and (2) masking and public entertainment spreading into feast days. The document also implicitly responds to the argument that prior bishops tolerated these abuses and therefore they are acceptable.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Future conduct of bishops and their flocks during Carnival: strict enforcement of the boundary between Carnival and Lent; prohibition of masks on feast days and Fridays throughout the Papal State; restriction or elimination of public entertainers on feast days (especially mornings); promotion of Eucharistic exposition tridua and personal episcopal example of piety; extension of the indulgence policy to any diocese that requests it.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between a popular festive culture that the Church cannot simply abolish ("one cannot always obtain from people at once what one would wish, and what would be just to obtain") and the Church's duty to protect the sanctity of liturgical time, especially the transition into Lent. A secondary tension exists between civic/temporal authority (which permits Carnival) and episcopal authority (which must enforce its limits). The document also names the rhetorical tension created by the argument from custom ("our predecessors, who were good men, knew of this and said nothing"), which it condemns as a "diabolical" form of reasoning.

## Unstructured

**Central argument and flow**

The letter opens with two concrete pastoral problems reported by bishops, moves through theological and canonical justification, sets explicit norms, grounds those norms in patristic and conciliar authority, traces the historical roots of Carnival disorder to pre-Christian paganism, describes the Church's prior positive responses (pious devotions, indulgences), and closes with a direct pastoral exhortation and the grant of a new plenary indulgence. The movement is essentially: problem → principle (Carnival is merely tolerated) → norm → history → pastoral remedy → exhortation.

**The doctrine of "mere tolerance"**

The most theologically load-bearing idea in the text is that Carnival is "simply permitted and tolerated" (*semplicemente permesso e tollerato*) by the Church, not commanded or approved. The Pope cites the theologian Thomas Stapleton and Augustine's *Against Faustus* ("what we teach is one thing; what we endure is another; what we are forced to punish, and while punishing are driven to tolerate, is still another") to establish that things merely tolerated cannot be extended by analogy or discretion. This principle provides the juridical foundation for restricting masking to non-feast days: there is no right to expand what is only grudgingly permitted.

**Authority and precedent**

Benedict XIV grounds every norm in a dense layering of authority: St. Basil's *Homily on Fasting*, the University of Paris's condemnation of the Feast of Fools, St. Charles Borromeo's *Memorial* and Provincial Councils, Pope Clement XI's two circular letters (1719, 1721), the Council of Trent (Session 22, Chapter 1), and the prophets Isaiah and Malachi. The effect is to present the current letter not as innovation but as re-affirmation of a long, unbroken disciplinary tradition. The tone is authoritative throughout, but the Pope tempers commands with pastoral realism, acknowledging what cannot immediately be achieved.

**Pagan origins as rhetorical frame**

The text devotes significant space to situating Carnival within a history of pagan excess: the January 1st festivals of Janus and the goddess Strena (with cross-dressing), and the ancient Bacchanalia. The quoted anecdote from Busbecq — a Turkish ambassador reportedly concluding that Christians went mad each year and were cured by a special powder (ashes) sprinkled on their heads — functions as satirical evidence of how scandalous Carnival appears to non-Christian observers. This framing reinforces the document's core argument that current abuses are not innocently festive but a regression to pre-Christian disorder.

**Pastoral constructive response**

Uniquely, the document does not restrict itself to prohibition. Benedict XIV encourages bishops to provide an alternative pious program during Carnival: celebration of public Mass, visits to hospitals, Eucharistic exposition on tridua, distribution of indulgences. The grant of a plenary indulgence (for confession, communion, and devout prayer during any of the three tridua before Lent) is the document's positive pastoral instrument, offered as a counterweight to festive excess.

**Tone**

The tone shifts between authoritative directive and collegial appeal. The opening is measured and sorrowful ("tra le molte cose che Ci conturbano"). The condemnation of the first abuse is sharp ("basta averla riferita, perché ognuno ne concepisca orrore"). The historical excursus is erudite and somewhat detached. The closing exhortation is warmly pastoral ("con pienezza di cuore abbracciandovi"). The overall register is that of a learned bishop-pope addressing peers he trusts to exercise the same zeal, not a punitive edict directed downward.
