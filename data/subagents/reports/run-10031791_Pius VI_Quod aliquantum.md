# Quod aliquantum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Social
- **Theme (one sentence):** The French National Assembly's Civil Constitution of the Clergy unlawfully usurps ecclesiastical authority, violates Catholic dogma and discipline, and must be rejected—especially by bishops refusing the oath it demands.
- **Summary:** Addressed to Cardinal De La Rochefoucauld, the Archbishop of Aix, and other French bishops who had signed an exposition against the Civil Constitution of the Clergy, this Brief by Pius VI systematically condemns the National Assembly's revolutionary decrees. The Pope argues that the Assembly's claim to a natural right of equality and liberty is philosophically false and historically condemned as heretical, that its reorganization of dioceses, its new method of episcopal elections by lay departments, its suppression of religious orders, its confiscation of Church property, and its oath requirement all violate both Catholic dogma and established ecclesiastical discipline. He draws sustained comparisons to the actions of Henry II and Henry VIII of England, condemns by name the Bishop of Autun (Talleyrand) for swearing the oath without reservation, and exhorts the faithful bishops to maintain unity and firmness while awaiting fuller guidance from Rome.
- **Response to:** The French National Assembly's Civil Constitution of the Clergy (decree of mid-July 1790 and the subsequent executive oath decree of 27 November); a letter from King Louis XVI requesting provisional approval of several articles; the oath publicly sworn by the Bishop of Autun; and the written Exposition on the Principles of the Constitution of the Clergy sent to the Pope by the French bishops who opposed the decrees.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The French bishops are directed to refuse the oath; to maintain concordance and unity among themselves; to encourage and support the nuns and religious who resist removal from their cloisters; and to communicate back to Rome their collective judgment on how to proceed toward reconciliation. The Pope withholds formal condemnation for the moment but warns the Bishop of Autun and those who followed him that they must retract or face ecclesiastical consequences analogous to those imposed after the Council of Rimini.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the sovereign authority of the Church (papal primacy, episcopal jurisdiction, canonical discipline) and the sovereignty claimed by the civil power (the National Assembly's right to reorganize the Church, elect bishops through lay departments, confiscate ecclesiastical property, and require clerical oaths to the civil constitution). A secondary tension runs through the document between the revolutionary principle of natural liberty and equality—which the Pope identifies as philosophically incoherent and historically identified with condemned heresies (Waldensians, Beguards, Wycliffites, Luther)—and the Christian obligation of obedience to legitimate authority, both ecclesiastical and royal. A third, more personal tension concerns the bishops themselves: the contrast between those who maintained firm resistance and the Bishop of Autun, who swore the oath, is elaborated at length and framed as a choice between martyrdom-worthy fidelity and scandalous apostasy.

## Unstructured

**Text and argument**

The document is written in Italian and structured as a long formal brief (Breve) rather than an encyclical. Its primary rhetorical form is a legal-theological refutation: the Pope moves article by article through the National Assembly's decrees, citing Scripture, patristic authorities (Augustine, Jerome, Gregory the Great, Chrysostom, Innocent I, Leo IX, Bernard, Thomas Aquinas), conciliar canons (Trent, Nicaea, Chalcedon, Tours, Toledo, Sens, Laodicea, the Trullan Council), and historical precedents to demonstrate that each element of the Civil Constitution is either dogmatically erroneous, schismatic, or at minimum illegitimately introduced by laypeople.

**Opening movement (delay and pain)**

The document opens by explaining the Pope's long silence: he had first withheld response in order not to provoke worse excesses, drawing on Gregory the Great's maxim about the prudent use of silence. He then recounts the sequence of events—the Assembly's July 1790 decrees, the King's requests for provisional approval, the December oath decree—establishing his own careful, patient conduct before shifting to direct condemnation.

**The liberty argument**

A major philosophical section argues that the Assembly's foundational principle—that men living in society have an absolute natural right to think, write, and publish as they wish in matters of religion—is self-defeating. God set limits on Adam's freedom from the beginning; authority and subjection are constitutive of human social life; Paul's letter to the Romans (ch. 13) is quoted at length. The Pope identifies this doctrine of absolute liberty explicitly with condemned heresies: the Waldensians, the Beguards condemned at the Council of Vienne, the Wycliffites, and Luther ("We are entirely free"). This framing converts a political dispute into a doctrinal one.

**Ecclesiastical authority vs. civil power**

The document's longest sustained argument concerns the illegitimacy of any lay or civil authority over the governance of the Church. The Pope cites the Council of Lyon (1527) against Marsilius of Padua, Benedict XIV's condemnation of a French pamphlet subordinating Church to state, Louis XV's 1731 declaration, and the patristic maxim attributed to Hosius and Athanasius ("God gave you the Empire; to us he gave ecclesiastical things"). The Pope insists that the National Assembly has erected exactly the system Marsilius had proposed and which had been condemned as heretical.

**Episcopal elections**

A detailed historical survey argues that episcopal election by the people was never the universal practice of the Church and was introduced only under pressure from Arian emperors; the Assembly's plan to have French departmental assemblies—including Jews, heretics, and heterodox persons—elect bishops is presented as categorically worse than anything previously attempted. Gregory the Great's rejection of non-Catholic electors is quoted directly.

**Suppression of religious orders and confiscation of property**

The abolition of religious orders is linked to Guglielmo di Santo Amore (condemned by Alexander IV), to Luther (condemned by Leo X), and to the Council of Sens. The confiscation of Church property is placed in the tradition of condemned sacrilege, with the story of Heliodorus (2 Maccabees) adduced as a divine warning, and an extended quotation from a Frankish petition to Charlemagne (803 AD) demonstrating that even the Frankish nobility recognized Church property as sacred.

**The Bishop of Autun**

The condemnation of Talleyrand (Bishop of Autun) functions as the document's sharpest personal moment. The Pope describes reading the news of the oath as causing him to pause writing, weeping day and night. He refutes Talleyrand's attempted justification about population transfers and diocesan changes as "the most absurd comparison imaginable." The Bossuet comparison between Thomas Becket and Thomas Cranmer—quoted at length—is deployed as the climax of this section, positioning Talleyrand with Cranmer and the faithful French bishops with Becket.

**Historical parallel: Henry II and Henry VIII**

The final movement draws an elaborate parallel between the National Assembly's decrees and the actions of both Henry II (Constitutions of Clarendon, Becket's martyrdom) and Henry VIII (usurpation of the ecclesiastical primacy, suppression of monasteries, Cromwell and Cranmer). The Pope states explicitly that the Assembly's decrees "are somewhat worse than the ancient ones." The parallel is used to predict the likely outcome and to frame the choice facing the French bishops.

**Tone**

The document is relentlessly authoritative in register; there is almost no pastoral softening. Even the tender passages about the nuns are brief. The Pope frames himself as the heir of Peter whose dignity is at stake, not merely as a concerned father. The closest approach to an open question is the closing request that the bishops communicate their collective judgment on how reconciliation might be achieved—an unusual admission of the limits of papal knowledge at a distance from events. The final benediction is warm but brief.

**Notable framings**

- The Assembly's actions are consistently presented not as political innovations but as repetitions of condemned medieval and Protestant heresies.
- The natural rights language of the Revolution is given a theological genealogy that links it to Waldensians and Luther, effectively delegitimizing it by association.
- The King is treated throughout as a sympathetic but constrained figure, not as an adversary—the adversary is the "larger part" of the Assembly.
- The document explicitly disclaims any intention of restoring the pre-revolutionary civil order: "In exposing what we have reported, we have not intended that the former civil state be re-established."
