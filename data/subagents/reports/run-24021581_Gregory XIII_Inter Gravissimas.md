# Inter Gravissimas

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Social
- **Theme (one sentence):** The reform and standardization of the ecclesiastical calendar to ensure correct calculation and uniform celebration of Easter and holy days across the Christian world.
- **Summary:** Pope Gregory XIII issues this decree to fulfil a task reserved to the Apostolic See by the Council of Trent: the correction of the calendar. The document describes how previous attempts failed due to insurmountable technical difficulties, how a new system of epacts devised by Aloysius Lilius (presented by his brother Antonio) was selected by a panel of experts, and how it is now mandated. Ten days are to be suppressed in October 1582 to restore the vernal equinox to its position as fixed at Nicaea, a new leap-year rule is established to prevent future drift, and the golden number in the calendar is replaced by the cycle of epacts. All patriarchs, archbishops, bishops, abbots, clergy, and faithful are commanded to adopt the new calendar exclusively.
- **Response to:** The drift of the vernal equinox from its position established at the Council of Nicaea (approximately ten days by this time); the failure of previous Roman Pontiffs' attempts to correct the calendar; the mandate left unfinished by the Council of Trent, which had reserved calendar reform to the Apostolic See.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Universal adoption of the corrected calendar by all Christian nations, rulers, and clergy; the proper and perpetually accurate celebration of Easter and feasts; prevention of any future divergence of the equinox or the paschal full moon from their correct positions.
- **Tension:** The tension between long-standing ecclesiastical tradition (the ancient calendar, the golden number, the 28-year cycle of dominical letters) and the need for technical correction to preserve that very tradition's integrity; the tension between the authority of the Roman Pontiff and the practical difficulty of reaching all distant Christian territories in time for uniform implementation.

## Unstructured

*Inter Gravissimas* is an administrative and juridical document rather than a doctrinal or pastoral letter. Its tone is authoritative and legislative throughout — it issues commands ("praecipimus et mandamus"), abolishes the old calendar outright ("tollimus et abolemus omnino vetus kalendarium"), threatens penalties including excommunication, and invokes full apostolic authority.

The central argument runs as follows: the Council of Trent reserved certain matters, including calendar reform, to the Holy See; the reform of the breviary was completed by Pius V; the calendar portion remained unfinished because no technically adequate solution had been found; the Lilian system of epacts finally provided a durable solution; a commission of experts validated it; now the Pope promulgates it as binding law.

The document frames the calendar correction in explicitly theological and ecclesial terms: the purpose is the "right celebration of the Paschal feast according to the sanctions of the holy Fathers, ancient Roman Pontiffs — especially Pius and Victor I — and the great ecumenical Council of Nicaea." The technical apparatus (epacts, bissextile years, dominical letters) is consistently subordinated to this religious rationale.

The text also shows pastoral awareness: it includes a provision for merchants and debtors (judges must account for the ten omitted days in financial obligations), and it provides a supplementary decree for those regions too distant to comply by October 1582, extending the transition to February 1583. This supplementary declaration — issued separately on 7 November 1582 — specifies the exact day of transition (from 10 February to 21 February in the new calendar) and adjusts the liturgical cycle accordingly.

Notable framing choices: the reform is presented not as an innovation but as a restoration — "restituendum" (to be restored) appears repeatedly. The equinox and the paschal moon are described as needing to return to their "proper seats" ("propriis sedibus"). The new calendar is said to be "corrected and completed by the immense kindness of God toward His Church." This language of restoration rather than innovation is characteristic of Counter-Reformation ecclesiastical rhetoric.

The involvement of Christian princes is framed as a collegial appeal: Gregory "exhorts and requests" (hortamur et rogamus) the Emperor-elect Rudolf II and other kings and princes to adopt the calendar and ensure their subjects observe it — invoking the unity ("concordia") of Christian nations in the celebration of feasts as the primary motive. The earlier consultation of "Christian princes and the more celebrated universities" before finalizing the reform is also noted, giving the decree a consultative legitimacy.
