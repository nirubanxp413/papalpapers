# Catholicae Ecclesiae

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Church calls Catholics worldwide to fund and support missionary efforts in Africa to simultaneously abolish the slave trade and spread the Gospel, framing both liberation and evangelisation as inseparable duties.
- **Summary:** Leo XIII addresses Catholic missionaries and bishops, invoking a long papal tradition of opposing slavery to justify two interlocked initiatives: the active suppression of the African slave trade (described as the forced removal and sale of nearly 400,000 Africans each year) and the intensification of Gospel missions in Africa. He announces that Cardinal Lavigerie has been dispatched across Europe to raise support, praises recent diplomatic gatherings in Brussels and Paris, establishes an annual Epiphany collection in all dioceses channelled through the Sacred Congregation for the Propagation of the Faith, and asks bishops and the faithful to participate generously, however modestly.
- **Response to:** The document responds to ongoing African chattel slavery — eyewitness reports and accounts from recent explorers confirming the scale of the trade — and to Leo XIII's own prior letter to Brazilian bishops (1888). It also responds to recent international diplomatic activity: a Brussels meeting of European state representatives and a Paris gathering of private citizens to discuss slave-trade suppression.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates and directs toward (1) the eradication of the African slave trade through coordinated European political will and Catholic charitable funding; (2) the expansion of Catholic missionary infrastructure in Africa — building churches, training missionaries, funding long journeys — so that the Gospel can reach "those living in darkness and blind superstition"; and (3) an institutionalised annual Epiphany collection as a permanent mechanism to sustain these missions.
- **Tension:** The text holds two tensions in productive unity. First, a tension between the urgency of physical liberation (ending slavery as a humanitarian and religious imperative) and spiritual liberation (evangelisation as the "more serious apostolic concern") — the document insists these are mutually reinforcing rather than competing. Second, a tension between the already-accomplished (the Church's historic record of abolition, the Brussels and Paris meetings, existing missions) and the still-insufficient (too few missionaries, too little funding, the trade persisting at massive scale), which drives the appeal for new resources.

## Unstructured

**Key themes and flow of argument**

The document opens with an appeal to institutional continuity: Leo XIII grounds his intervention in a lineage of twelve named predecessors stretching from Gregory the Great to Gregory XVI, all of whom "applied every effort to eliminate the institution of slavery." This genealogical framing is rhetorical strategy — it positions the current appeal not as novelty but as inheritance, and insulates the Pope against any charge of overreach.

The argument then moves from history to present emergency. The quantitative detail — "almost four hundred thousand Africans of every age and sex are forcefully taken away each year" — is striking for a document of this era and signals that Leo is drawing on contemporary explorer accounts and diplomatic intelligence rather than speaking in abstraction. Cardinal Lavigerie's European tour is named explicitly, and recent diplomatic meetings (Brussels, Paris) are acknowledged as signs that secular leaders share the Church's concern. This alignment of Church and secular actors is deliberate: it lends credibility and signals the broad coalition Leo wishes to mobilise.

The pivot in paragraph 3 is revealing: Leo introduces evangelisation as "another more serious apostolic concern" than mere physical freedom. The logic offered is that Christian conversion is the durable guarantee of civil liberty — "wherever Christian customs and laws are in force … neither slavery nor savage barbarism can exist." This is both a theological claim and a civilisational argument. The document does not treat spiritual and material liberation as alternatives; rather, the Gospel is presented as the root cause from which earthly freedom flows.

**Tone**

The tone is primarily authoritative — this is a papal directive establishing an annual collection, allocating funds through a named congregation, and commanding bishops to act. Yet it is also pastoral and even pleading in places: Leo admits the missionaries face danger and hardship, invokes "the harvest is great, the laborers are few," and asks even modest contributions from ordinary faithful so that "the burden spread among many people will make it lighter for all." The closing apostolic blessing reinforces the pastoral register.

**Notable framing**

- The choice of Epiphany for the collection day is carefully explained: the Magi are glossed (via Leo the Great) as "the first fruits of our vocation and faith" — i.e., the first Gentiles called by Christ. The African missions are thus framed as the ongoing fulfilment of the Epiphany narrative, giving the annual collection a liturgical and typological resonance beyond mere fundraising.
- Slavery is consistently described in the language of commerce and degradation: slaves are "put on display and sold like cattle," the trade is "inhuman commerce," and the abolition effort aims to "abolish the blemish of human commerce." The commercial vocabulary intensifies the moral charge.
- The document addresses "venerable brother" (a bishop) throughout, making it a communiqué within the episcopal network rather than a broad encyclical to all the faithful — its audience is the missionary and administrative infrastructure of the Church.
