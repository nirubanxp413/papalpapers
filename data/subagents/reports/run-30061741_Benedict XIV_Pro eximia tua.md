# Pro eximia tua

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Pope demands that the Archbishop of Turin root out the "detestable trade" — clergy and laity profiting with "great greed for shameful gain" from the trafficking of Mass celebrations — from the dioceses of Piedmont.
- **Summary:** This is a short papal brief addressed personally to the Archbishop of Turin, accompanying a companion letter sent simultaneously to all bishops of the Catholic world. Benedict XIV writes that he has been recently informed that certain Piedmontese clergy and laypeople have been conducting a "detestable traffic" (mercatura) in the celebration of Masses to a degree of avidità di turpe guadagno ("great greed for shameful gain") exceeding what he had previously known elsewhere. The document directs the Archbishop to act with urgency to eradicate this abuse completely, instructs him to invoke the secular arm (civil authority) if obstinate offenders resist ecclesiastical penalties, and expresses confidence that King Carlo Emmanuele of Sardinia will cooperate. The tone is urgent and corrective; the Pope frames his intervention as both an act of "particular charity" toward the bishops of Piedmont and a fulfillment of apostolic duty.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to recent reports ("fummo informati recentemente") of clergy and laypersons in Piedmont conducting a "detestable trade" in Mass celebrations on a scale and with a degree of avarice the Pope had not previously known to exist there. It is also a follow-on to a companion encyclical letter ("altra lettera") issued the same day to all Catholic bishops on the same subject.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The Archbishop of Turin is directed to: (1) exert every effort to achieve "complete extinction of the contagion of trade" in his diocese; (2) invoke the "secular arm" (civil/royal authority) against any obstinate offenders who resist canonical penalties; (3) rely on the expected cooperation of King Carlo Emmanuele of Sardinia, whose loyalty to the Holy See the Pope characterizes as "so great, and so often demonstrated by facts themselves."
- **Tension:** The document names two tensions: (a) **ecclesiastical discipline vs. clerical corruption** — priests and lay persons exploiting the sacred act of Mass celebration for financial gain, described as a "sordid business" and an "infamy" that must be erased from Piedmont's dioceses; (b) **pastoral reputation vs. pastoral reality** — the Pope explicitly states he does not wish to "damage the opinion everyone has of the decorum of ecclesiastical discipline" in Piedmont, yet he must intervene because the abuse is real and documented, creating a tension between institutional face-saving and the need for public correction.

## Unstructured

**Form and occasion.** *Pro eximia tua* is not a standalone teaching document but a covering brief — a short, personal transmission letter addressed specifically to the Archbishop of Turin, accompanying a general encyclical ("altra lettera") sent the same day to all Catholic bishops worldwide. This dual-track approach (general + particular) is noted in the text itself and shapes the document's character: it is intensely practical and administrative rather than doctrinal.

**The abuse: "mercatura" in the celebration of Masses.** The central object of concern is described as *la detestabile mercatura nella celebrazione delle Messe* — a "detestable trade in the celebration of Masses." The text does not explain the mechanics of the abuse in detail (those details are presumably in the companion letter), but characterizes it as being driven by *avidità di turpe guadagno* ("greed for shameful gain") and involving both Ecclesiastici Pedemontani (Piedmontese clergy) and laypeople. The Pope uses the language of commerce and contagion — "sordid business," "infamy," "contagion" — to frame the abuse as a corruption of the sacred that spreads and must be "extirpated from the roots."

**Flow of argument.** The brief moves quickly through three beats: (1) disclosure — the Pope reveals what he has learned and acknowledges the grief this causes both him and the Archbishop; (2) mandate — the Archbishop is charged with total eradication, framed as both pastoral duty and an opportunity to honor the Church's reputation; (3) assurance — the Pope assures the Archbishop that secular royal power (the King of Sardinia) will support him, anticipating any concern that ecclesiastical penalties alone might not be sufficient.

**Tone: authoritative, but not purely punitive.** The opening address appeals to the Archbishop's "singular religious spirit and respect for canonical discipline" — flattery that sets up the expectation of compliance. The Pope expresses not anger but *tristezza* ("great sadness"), and frames his writing as an act of carità (charity) toward the Piedmontese bishops. At the same time, the language escalates as it moves forward: the word *pervicacia* ("obstinacy/perversity") is used for those who would resist, and the invocation of the secular arm against them is presented as a routine expectation, not a last resort. The combination is pastoral in framing but coercive in substance.

**Notable framing — reputation and reform.** The Pope explicitly balances two concerns: correcting the abuse AND protecting Piedmont's ecclesiastical reputation. He writes that he did not want to "give the impression of damaging the opinion everyone has about the decorum of ecclesiastical discipline" in those dioceses — yet he writes anyway, because the "apostolic duty of solicitude" demands it. This tension between institutional image management and genuine reform is unusually transparent for a papal document and suggests the problem was either well known or the Archbishop had been slow to act.

**Church-state relationship.** The brief takes for granted an operational alliance between Church and Crown: the Pope instructs the Archbishop to use the "secular arm" without hesitation, and he guarantees cooperation from King Carlo Emmanuele of Sardinia on the basis of the King's demonstrated "reverence" for the Holy See. The relationship is framed as mutual and reliable — the King is expected to act "more willingly than you might think." This reflects the document's assumption that ecclesiastical enforcement and state enforcement are complementary and interoperable.
