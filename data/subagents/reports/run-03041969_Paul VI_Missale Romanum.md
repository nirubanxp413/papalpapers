# Missale Romanum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The promulgation of the revised Roman Missal enacts the liturgical reforms mandated by the Second Vatican Council, aiming to clarify the meaning of the Mass, enrich its texts, and foster the active participation and unity of the faithful.
- **Summary:** Paul VI announces the completion and promulgation of the new Roman Missal, tracing liturgical reform from the Council of Trent through Pius XII's restoration of the Easter Vigil to the Second Vatican Council's Constitution *Sacrosanctum Concilium*. The document outlines the specific changes made — to the eucharistic prayer (new canons and prefaces), the Order of Mass (simplified rites restored to patristic tradition), and the lectionary (a three-year Sunday cycle with Old Testament readings) — while insisting the reform represents organic development, not rupture. The overarching aim is that the faithful and priests will be drawn more deeply into Scripture, more devoutly to the Lord's Supper, and more firmly united with one another through a shared prayer offered in diverse languages.
- **Response to:** The document responds directly to the decrees of the Second Vatican Ecumenical Council, specifically *Sacrosanctum Concilium* (1963), which mandated that rites and texts express the holy things they signify more clearly, that active participation of the faithful be facilitated, that Scripture be opened more lavishly, and that a new rite for concelebration be drawn up. It also responds to the centuries-long tradition of liturgical scholarship — the study of ancient manuscripts, patristic sources, and Eastern liturgical forms — which the text says "prepared the way" for this revision.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates the faithful receiving the new Missal as an instrument of unity — "witnessing and strengthening their unity with one another" — and as a means by which "one and the same prayer in a great diversity of languages will ascend … to our heavenly Father." It also directs that sacred Scripture be regarded by all as "the abiding source of spiritual life, the foundation for Christian instruction, and the core of all theological study," and sets an effective date of the First Sunday of Advent 1969 for implementation.
- **Tension:** The principal tension acknowledged in the text is between continuity and reform: Paul VI explicitly addresses the concern that "this revision of the Roman Missal has come out of nowhere," insisting it follows four centuries of liturgical scholarship and represents legitimate development rather than novelty. A secondary tension concerns the relation between a unified Latin rite and the diversity of languages and adaptations now permitted — the document resolves this by asserting that shared prayer "in a great diversity of languages" remains one prayer ascending to the Father.

## Unstructured

The document is brief and formal — an apostolic constitution rather than a pastoral letter — and its tone is predominantly authoritative and declarative. It reads as a promulgation instrument rather than a reflective theological essay, yet it is careful to justify each major change with a citation from *Sacrosanctum Concilium* or patristic precedent.

The flow of argument is historical before it is prescriptive. Paul VI opens by anchoring the new Missal in the Tridentine Missal of Pius V (1570), immediately establishing that liturgical reform is not novel but characteristic of the Church's life. He then traces a line: Trent → Pius XII's 1951/1955 Holy Week reforms → Vatican II's *Sacrosanctum Concilium* → the present act. This historical framing is deliberate; the document pre-empts objections to change by making continuity the master narrative.

The three key changes receive the most detailed treatment:
1. **The eucharistic prayer**: The addition of new prefaces and three new canons is described as enriching the expression of "the mystery of salvation" and addressing pastoral needs. The standardisation of the words of institution across all canons ("Accipite et manducate…") and the relocation of *Mysterium fidei* to a congregational acclamation are noted without extended theological justification — the act is presented as self-evidently appropriate.
2. **The Order of Mass**: Simplification is framed as a recovery of authenticity — elements "added with but little advantage" are removed; elements "injured through accident of history" (the homily, the prayer of the faithful, the penitential rite) are restored "to the tradition of the Fathers." The rhetorical move is to present both pruning and addition as acts of fidelity.
3. **The lectionary**: The three-year Sunday cycle with Old Testament readings is framed in strongly pneumatological and ecclesiological terms: this hunger for the word of God, "under the guidance of the Holy Spirit," will "impel the people of the New Covenant toward the perfect unity of the Church." Scripture is not merely edifying but constitutive of ecclesial unity.

The concluding paragraph is the document's most explicitly ecclesiological moment, invoking the image of incense rising — "one and the same prayer in a great diversity of languages will ascend, more fragrant than any incense, to our heavenly Father." This image holds the reform's central tension in equilibrium: diversity of language, unity of prayer.

The document does not address resistance to the reform or the existence of those attached to the Tridentine rite — a significant silence given subsequent history, though strictly outside what the text itself states.
