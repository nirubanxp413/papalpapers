# The Syllabus Of Errors

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document catalogues eighty condemned propositions—drawn from prior papal allocutions and encyclicals—spanning rationalism, indifferentism, Church-State relations, marriage, and modern liberalism, asserting that the Church possesses divinely-instituted rights no civil power may legitimately override.
- **Summary:** The Syllabus of Errors is a numbered catalogue of propositions that Bl. Pius IX considers false and already condemned in prior papal documents. Organised into ten thematic sections, it targets errors ranging from pantheism and rationalism through to indifferentism, socialism, secret societies, and the claims of modern liberalism. The document insists on the Church's divine constitution, the inalienable authority of the Roman Pontiff, the sacramental nature of marriage, and the illegitimacy of a fully secular civil order that would exclude or subordinate the Church. An appended narrative passage accuses Masonic sects of orchestrating the assault on the Church across Europe and America, and protests Prussian anti-Church legislation as null and void.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to a series of prior papal allocutions and encyclicals (cited by name and date throughout, e.g. "Qui pluribus" 1846, "Maxima quidem" 1862, "Ad Apostolicae" 1851, and others). It also responds directly to the advance of rationalist philosophy, the claims of secular liberal governments to control education, marriage law, episcopal appointments, and religious orders, the spread of Masonic secret societies, and Prussian governmental persecution of the Catholic Church.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document is prescriptive towards Catholic bishops and teachers, directing them to uphold the condemned propositions as errors and not to accommodate them; it instructs bishops to warn the faithful against Masonic sects and similar associations. It also implicitly addresses civil rulers, asserting they have no legitimate power to override the Church's divine constitution or to absorb ecclesiastical jurisdiction.
- **Tension:** The central and pervasive tension is between the divinely-constituted authority of the Church (and the Roman Pontiff in particular) and the expanding claims of secular civil power—over education, marriage, episcopal appointments, Church property, religious orders, and freedom of communication with Rome. Secondary tensions include: reason versus faith and divine revelation; religious indifferentism (the equal validity of all religions) versus the exclusive truth-claims of Catholic Christianity; and the compatibility or incompatibility of the papacy's temporal power with the Church's spiritual liberty.

## Unstructured

The Syllabus of Errors is structurally distinctive: it does not argue or reason from premises in the manner of an encyclical; it lists. Each of the eighty numbered propositions is a statement presented as an error—meaning the reader is to understand that the opposite of each proposition is what the Church holds. The document is therefore almost entirely negative in its rhetorical mode: it defines orthodoxy by exclusion and condemnation rather than by positive exposition.

The ten sections move from the most fundamental metaphysical and epistemological errors (Pantheism, Rationalism) through to the most immediately political and institutional ones (Modern Liberalism, the temporal power of the Pope). This ordering gives the document a logical architecture: the later, more political errors are implicitly grounded in the earlier metaphysical ones—once human reason is declared sovereign and religion merely optional, the rest of the liberal programme (secular schools, civil marriage, separation of Church and State) follows. The Syllabus presents these as a connected system of error rather than isolated mistakes.

The tone throughout the list itself is terse and impersonal—each error stated plainly with a citation to the prior document in which it was already condemned. The affective register shifts sharply in the appended narrative passages. The prose there becomes urgent and combative: the condition of Catholics is described as "sad and full of perils," a "ferocious war on the Church" is said to be raging in America, and Masonic sects are characterised as a "deadly scourge" winding through "sinuous caverns" and triumphing "as a powerful master." The final passage, concerning Prussia, explicitly declares the offending laws "null and void" on the grounds of divine law—an assertion of spiritual authority over civil legislation in the strongest possible terms.

The document's authority rests heavily on cross-referencing: almost every condemned proposition is anchored to a prior papal pronouncement, giving the Syllabus the character of a consolidated index of already-existing condemnations. This implies that these positions are not newly determined but are being systematically gathered and reaffirmed.

The framing of the relationship between Church and State is not a simple rejection of the political order. The appended text quotes "render to Caesar what is Caesar's," and acknowledges a "twofold power" of natural and supernatural origin. The objection is not to civil authority as such, but to civil authority absorbing or abolishing the jurisdictional sphere properly belonging to the Church. The document insists on the Church as a "true and perfect society" with its own proper rights—not subordinate to, but coordinate with (or in certain respects above) the civil order.

The final section on Modern Liberalism culminates in the famous proposition 80: the claim that the Roman Pontiff "can, and ought to, reconcile himself, and come to terms with progress, liberalism and modern civilization" is listed as an error. This is the document's sharpest formulation of its overall stance: accommodation to modernity on modernity's own terms is itself condemned. The Syllabus does not simply resist individual liberal policies; it rejects the foundational premise that the Church must adjust itself to the spirit of the age.
