# Spesse Volte

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Italian state's suppression of Catholic charitable associations is both unjust and self-defeating, because it strips society of its principal bulwark against socialism, anarchy, and moral disorder — while Catholics themselves remain loyal, peaceable, and inseparable from the Church.
- **Summary:** Written in August 1898 in the wake of Italian riots and subsequent government crackdowns on Catholic institutions, Leo XIII protests the dissolution of hundreds of Catholic charitable societies — rural banks, night shelters, orphan-protection groups, catechism classes — as an arbitrary and unjust act that punishes peaceable citizens for disorders they did not cause. He traces the root cause of Italy's social instability not to Catholicism but to systematic anti-religious policy: the confiscation of Church property, the closure of monasteries, the promotion of Masonic sects, and the spread of "irreligion and immorality" through schools, press, and theatre. He defends Catholic associations as both spiritually necessary and socially conservative — a "powerful conservative force" against socialism and anarchy — and warns that suppressing them aggravates the very instability the government claims to address. He closes with an exhortation to Italian Catholics to persevere in charitable work, obedience to pastors, and fidelity to the Holy See.
- **Response to:** The suppression "recently decreed in various districts of the Peninsula" of Catholic institutions; the suspension of Catholic journals; the dissolution of diocesan and parochial committees; the accusations — called a "ridiculous and ill concealed pretext" — that Catholic associations fomented the riots of 1898; the long preceding history, described as "a progressive series of systematized oppressions," of monastery closures, confiscation of ecclesiastical property, imposition of military service on clergy, and favouring of dissident religions and Masonic sects.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Continued Catholic social and charitable action "keeping outside politics" — in associations, committees, press, congresses, confraternities; maintaining obedience to civil authority while refusing active support for any arrangement that undermines the Papacy's "necessary independence and full and effective freedom"; preserving religious fervour among the faithful; resisting socialism and anarchy through organised Christian charity; and ultimately seeking restoration of the Holy See's temporal independence.
- **Tension:** (1) *Church vs. Italian state*: The document frames an unresolved, sharpening conflict between the anti-religious governing class ("sectarian prejudice," Masonic sects, naturalism, political materialism) and the Catholic Church, which has been progressively stripped of property, institutions, and influence. (2) *Civic loyalty vs. ecclesial loyalty*: Catholics are simultaneously asked to render "obedience which is due" to civil authorities and to refuse any active cooperation that would legitimise the current order, since the current order is "at the instigation of anti-religious sectaries" aimed at the Papacy's downfall. (3) *Social order vs. anti-religious policy*: The document insists that irreligion inevitably produces "bitterness, dissension, depravity, strife," and that suppressing the very organisations that counteract socialism and anarchy only deepens Italy's social crisis — a contradiction the text attributes to the government's own "paltry sectarian prejudice."

## Unstructured

**Flow of argument.** The letter moves through a clear prosecutorial structure. Leo opens by establishing a long record of protest ("Oftentimes during the course of Our Pontificate"), then traces the history of anticlerical legislation against the Church. He pivots to what he presents as the Church's constructive response: the network of charitable associations built up across Italy over the preceding decade, praised even by political opponents. The 1898 riots then enter as a turning-point: rather than identifying their true causes in irreligion and anti-Catholic agitation, the government blamed Catholics and used the unrest as a pretext for suppression. Leo systematically dismantles the accusation — Catholics are, by doctrine, the "staunchest supporters of order" — and then restates the one genuine point of irresolution: Catholics cannot positively endorse a political arrangement that diminishes the Papacy. This is not disloyalty to Italy, he argues, but fidelity to a higher institution that *is* Italy's "special and incomparable glory." He closes with encouragement to bishops and laity to persist.

**Tone.** The document oscillates between two registers. It is authoritative and juridical in its protest — Leo marshals the language of law, justice, evidence, and civic rights ("without any proof of their guilt, without any previous examination, and without any documentary evidence"). It is pastoral and consoling toward the end, assuring Catholics that suffering persecution increases "merit in the eyes of all good men, and what is much more important, before God." A third register emerges occasionally: prophetic warning, as Leo claims he had already predicted — and was not listened to — that the suppression of religion would produce revolution and disorder.

**Notable framing.** Leo explicitly refuses the label of political subversive for Catholic action: "Catholic action, keeping outside politics, concentrates itself upon social and religious work." This is a key move — he separates the ecclesial demand for the Pope's temporal independence (a spiritual and hierarchical obligation) from political insurgency, arguing that Catholics can coexist with the current state while refusing to actively legitimise it. The threat of socialism and anarchy runs throughout as a foil: Catholic associations are "a bulwark against the subversive theories of socialism and anarchy," and suppressing them removes the only reliable barrier against social disintegration. This frames the Church not as an opponent of the Italian state but as its indispensable foundation — a rhetorical inversion of the anti-clerical argument that the Church is an obstacle to national unity. The document's final theological anchor is the patristic tag from St. Ambrose — "Ubi Petrus, ibi Ecclesia" — which grounds the Church's endurance in divine providence rather than political calculation, lifting the document's conclusion above the immediate conflict.

**Audience.** Though addressed formally to "Bishops, Clergy and People of Italy," the document also signals awareness of a broader Catholic world ("Our children in Italy and in other nations"), positioning Italy's situation as an object lesson for the universal Church about the fragility of papal liberty when temporal guarantees are absent.
