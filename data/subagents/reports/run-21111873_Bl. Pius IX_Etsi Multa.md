# Etsi Multa

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Church's institutional freedom and hierarchical authority are under coordinated attack by civil governments and heretical sects across Europe, and faithful Catholics must resist unjust civil laws while remaining loyal subjects of legitimate secular power.
- **Summary:** Issued in 1873, *Etsi Multa* catalogs a broad and worsening persecution of the Catholic Church across multiple jurisdictions — the Papal States, Switzerland (Geneva, Basel, Jura), Prussia and the German Empire, and briefly America. Pius IX details specific civil laws that subordinate episcopal authority to secular power, expel clergy, dissolve religious congregations, and install schismatic "Old Catholic" pseudo-bishops with state backing. He condemns the new laws and excommunicates the schismatic bishop Joseph Hubert Reinkens and his supporters. The encyclical frames all these attacks as the coordinated work of Masonic and secret-society networks whose ultimate aim is the total destruction of the Catholic Church. Against this, the Pope calls bishops to shield their flocks, urges perseverance and prayer, and holds up persecuted Catholics in Switzerland and Germany as contemporary martyrs.
- **Response to:** (1) The capture of Rome and subjugation of the Papal States to civil rule; (2) Swiss cantonal laws (Geneva, Solothurn, Bern, Basel, Aargau, Zurich) rewriting Church governance, subordinating bishops to civil authority, allowing laypeople to elect and dismiss clergy, and forcing schismatic oaths on priests; (3) Prussian *Kulturkampf* laws placing clerical education, appointments, and benefices under lay state power and establishing a royal ecclesiastical tribunal; (4) State recognition and promotion of the "Old Catholic" movement and the consecration of Joseph Hubert Reinkens as a pseudo-bishop with government endorsement; (5) Government persecution of clergy and laity who refuse to comply; (6) Masonic and secret-society activity described as the engine behind all these assaults.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) Bishops are directed to protect their faithful against Masonic sects, correct those deceived into joining them, and repeatedly explain the applicable pontifical decrees; (2) All Catholics are directed to avoid excommunicated Old Catholic clergy "as aliens and thieves"; (3) The faithful are exhorted to perseverance, prayer, and courage in the face of ongoing persecution; (4) Pius signals he will address the situation in America more fully at a future time.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the legitimate authority of the secular state in civil affairs and the Church's claim to complete freedom and exclusive jurisdiction over its own governance, clergy formation, sacramental practice, and hierarchical appointments — a claim the state governments explicitly deny through the contested laws. A secondary tension runs between visible, institutional ecclesial unity (communion with Rome and the pope as its guarantor) and the fragmentation of that unity by schismatic bodies backed by state power. A third, implicit tension is between obedience to civil law and obedience to conscience and divine law: Pius frames Catholic resistance to the Prussian laws not as treason but as the same fidelity shown by the early martyrs.

## Unstructured

*Etsi Multa* is one of Pius IX's most comprehensive crisis dispatches — less a doctrinal essay than an urgent situation report addressed to the world's bishops. Its tone is simultaneously mournful, combative, and exhortatory. The Pope opens in a deeply personal register ("death itself would seem preferable to life tossed about by so many waves"), establishing the cumulative weight of suffering before moving into a near-journalistic account of region-by-region developments.

**Structure of the argument**

The document moves geographically: Rome → Switzerland (Geneva, then Basel and the five cantons, then the Jura territory) → Germany and Prussia → a brief reference to America → a theological synthesis identifying Freemasonry as the common cause → a closing call to resistance and hope. This geography serves a rhetorical purpose: it demonstrates that the assault is not local but pan-European and systematic.

**Ecclesiology as the core**

Beneath the political complaints, the document is a sustained defense of a specific ecclesiological claim: that the Church is a divinely constituted society with its own jurisdiction, distinct from and not derivable from civil authority. The laws Pius condemns are objectionable precisely because they invert this order — treating episcopal jurisdiction as delegated by, and revocable by, the state. The long excursus on the Old Catholics (sections 22–25) reinforces this by defending papal primacy and the indefectibility of the Church against the schismatics' claim that the Vatican Council had caused the Church to apostatize.

**The "two orders" argument**

Section 16 provides the doctrinal hinge: there is "a twofold order of things" — the natural order (civil authority) and the supernatural order (ecclesiastical authority). The document insists these are coordinated, not fused or subordinated. Civil authority is owed obedience "insofar as they are secular." This framing simultaneously defends Catholic civic loyalty (the persecuted clergy are *not* traitors) and sets a principled limit on that loyalty (laws requiring schism or apostasy cannot bind conscience).

**Tone**

The tone shifts noticeably across sections. Passages describing Swiss and German Catholics who endure persecution are warmly admiring — almost hagiographic ("a wonder to the world, to the angels, and to men"). The treatment of the Old Catholics is sharply polemical and juridical, culminating in the formal excommunication formula of section 26. The closing sections (31–32) shift to pastoral encouragement, quoting Chrysostom at length to assert that the Church has outlasted every persecution and will outlast this one.

**Masonic framing**

Section 28 identifies Masonic and related secret societies as the coordinating force behind all the specific persecutions described. This is presented not as conspiracy speculation but as a self-evident inference: the scale and consistency of attacks across different nations and governments cannot, in the Pope's view, be explained without positing a common organizer. The "synagogue of Satan" language is strong and deliberate, drawing on earlier pontifical condemnations of Masonry and positioning them as prophetic.

**Notable absence**

The document does not engage theologically with the arguments made by the Old Catholics or the liberal-Catholic critics of Vatican I; it simply asserts their errors and excommunicates their leaders. The rhetorical strategy is to position the Old Catholics as recyclers of ancient heresies ("draw forth from the ancient store of heretics") rather than as genuine theological challengers requiring substantive response.
