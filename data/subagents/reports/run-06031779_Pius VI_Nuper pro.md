# Nuper pro (Pius VI, 6 March 1779)

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Apostolic See delegates authority to Portuguese bishops to adjudicate cases where inherited estates burdened with pious testamentary obligations cannot satisfy those obligations.
- **Summary:** In response to a petition from Queen Maria Francisca of Portugal, Pius VI grants the bishops (and apostolic delegates) of Portugal and the Algarves the faculty to evaluate, on a case-by-case basis, whether the pious obligations ("pii oneri") attached to majorates and primogenitures are so onerous that the inherited property cannot meet them. Having examined the situation, the bishops are to determine "what can serve to greater honour of God and utility of the churches." The brief is framed as a legal instrument, overriding contrary provisions in testaments, council decrees and prior apostolic constitutions, and its authority is declared final and irrevocable.
- **Response to:** The petition of Queen Maria Francisca of Portugal, who reported that many majorates and primogenitures in her kingdoms were burdened with such heavy pious obligations that "a stento, e neppure a stento, i beni ereditati bastano al pagamento" (the inherited goods barely, and not even barely, suffice for payment). The document responds directly to this named practical impossibility.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The bishops and apostolic delegates of Portugal and the Algarves, who are directed to: (1) examine each case of over-burdened majorates; (2) determine the extent of the burden; (3) establish, "according to conscience," what redress will best serve the honour of God and the utility of the churches. All judges at every level are further directed to interpret the matter solely in light of this brief.
- **Tension:** The document names an implicit conflict between the legally binding last wills of testators (who imposed pious obligations on inheritances) and the practical impossibility of honouring those obligations from the estate itself. A secondary tension is between accumulated prior law—Tridentine decrees, apostolic constitutions, council ordinances, local customs—and the need for a new, overriding papal dispensation. The brief resolves both by asserting the Pope's authority to derogate from all existing contrary norms.

## Unstructured

**Nature of the document.** This is a brief (breve), not an encyclical or doctrinal letter. It is a short, tightly legal instrument. Its tone is authoritative and formal throughout; there is no pastoral warmth or theological elaboration. The language is procedural ("stabiliamo," "concediamo, attribuiamo e diamo la facoltà"), and the closing formula explicitly dates and seals the letter with the Ring of the Fisherman.

**Flow of the argument.** The document follows a classic Roman canonical structure:
1. *Narratio* (§1): the queen's petition, stating the factual problem—majorates and primogenitures so burdened with pious obligations that the estates cannot pay.
2. *Dispositio* (§2): the papal grant of faculty to the bishops to investigate and decide each case, with a conscience-based standard ("secondo coscienza") and a teleological criterion ("maggior onore di Dio e ad utilità delle chiese").
3. *Sanctio* (§§3–5): the enforcement clauses—the letter is perpetually valid; all judges must follow it; all contrary provisions (testaments, councils, apostolic constitutions, customs, privileges) are expressly overridden.

**Key substantive concept: "pii oneri" (pious obligations).** The entire document turns on this term. Testators attached religious or charitable duties—masses, alms, memorial services, or donations to churches—as conditions on inheritable estates (majorates/primogenitures). The document implies that over time these accumulated to an impossible level. The remedy granted is not wholesale abolition of such obligations but a delegated episcopal power to assess and presumably reduce or re-schedule them.

**Notable framing: the queen as petitioner.** The initiative is attributed entirely to the queen ("fu denunciato a Noi" and "umilmente Ci supplicò"). This is a conventional canonical framing—secular rulers petitioning the Holy See for practical ecclesiastical relief—but it is notable that the document makes no independent doctrinal claim. Pius VI acts in response to a concrete reported problem, not as a proactive teacher.

**Scope of the derogation.** Section 5 is the most striking part of the document: the list of instruments overridden is exhaustive—individual testaments, the Council of Trent, all general and provincial councils, apostolic constitutions, oaths, customs, privileges, prior apostolic letters, and "any other contrary concession." This maximal derogation clause suggests the Holy See anticipated legal resistance and wanted the brief to be unassailable. The document does not explain why such resistance was expected, but the breadth of the override implies a complex prior legal landscape in Portugal concerning ecclesiastical inheritances and pious legacies.

**Tone.** Entirely authoritative, zero pastoral or hortatory register. No biblical citations, no theological argument, no moral exhortation. It reads as a pure legal instrument—closer to a rescript or indult than to an encyclical or apostolic constitution in substance, even if issued as a "breve."
