# Au Milieu Des Sollicitudes

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** French Catholics must accept the legitimacy of the Republic as constituted authority, unify across political divisions, and resist hostile legislation — because religion is the irreplaceable foundation of social order, not a political rival to the State.
- **Summary:** Leo XIII addresses the Catholics of France amid what he describes as an organised campaign to annihilate Christianity there. He argues, first, that religion alone can create and sustain the social bond and morality of any nation; second, that all constituted governments — including the French Republic — derive their authority from God and must be accepted by Catholics regardless of political preference; and third, that accepting a government's legitimacy is entirely distinct from approving its legislation, which Catholics are both permitted and obligated to contest when it is hostile to religion. He closes by opposing the separation of Church and State, urging Catholics not to pre-empt the Holy See's handling of the Concordat, and calling for a unified, non-partisan Catholic effort for the good of France.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to: (1) an organised "vast conspiracy" by certain men to annihilate Christianity in France, trampling liberty, justice, and the Church's rights; (2) a series of anti-religious legislative acts in France that the Pope says he had already personally protested to the head of the Republic; (3) deep political divisions among French Catholics over whether to accept the Republic, which he says have weakened their collective action; (4) the "calumny" that Catholic defence of religion is secretly a bid for political domination — a charge he traces back to accusations against Christ himself.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs French Catholics toward: (1) accepting the Republic as the legitimate, constituted government, while distinguishing that acceptance from approval of its laws; (2) abandoning partisan political quarrels ("germs of political strife") and uniting solely for the pacification and moral elevation of France; (3) actively combating — by "lawful and honest means" — specific legislative measures hostile to religion; (4) leaving the question of the Concordat entirely to the Holy See; (5) resisting any push for the formal separation of Church and State.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between civic loyalty to constituted authority (which Leo insists Catholics owe) and resistance to laws that violate divine and Christian principles (which he equally insists Catholics must exercise). A secondary, structural tension runs throughout: the Republic is the legitimate government and must be accepted, yet its actual legislation is described as deeply hostile to religion and to France's own moral good — leaving Catholics required simultaneously to honour the State and oppose much of what the State enacts. A third tension concerns Catholic political unity: the Pope diagnoses division among Catholics as their greatest practical weakness, but that division is itself caused by disagreement over precisely the question he is trying to resolve.

## Unstructured

**Core argument and flow**

The letter opens with a dual register — grief at the "vast conspiracy" against Christianity in France, consolation at French Catholics' increased devotion to the Holy See — and immediately declares its practical aim: urging "pacification" and unity across all French citizens. From this opening, Leo builds a philosophical foundation (§§5–8) before descending to specific political guidance (§§13–29).

The philosophical foundation is essentially a syllogism: morality requires God; God requires religion; religion requires the Catholic Church; therefore a France that drives out the Church destroys itself morally and politically. This is not argued abstractly — Leo grounds it in French national history, treating France's past Christian greatness as direct evidence. The tone in these sections is confident and somewhat triumphal, presenting Catholic France as the natural France.

The political argument is more carefully constructed. Leo distinguishes three things that most French Catholics (in his reading) were conflating: (a) the *form* of government — republic, monarchy, empire — which is morally neutral and determined by historical circumstance; (b) *constituted authority* — the actual government in power — which derives from God regardless of its form and must be obeyed; and (c) *legislation* — which depends on the character of legislators and may be resisted when it violates God's law. This tripartite distinction is the intellectual core of the document and is clearly aimed at persuading French monarchist Catholics to stop treating acceptance of the Republic as a betrayal of Catholic principles. He calls the conflation of these three things the root cause of Catholic political disunity.

**Tone**

The letter is primarily **authoritative and directive**, not pastoral. Leo speaks as a father, but one who expects docility ("We delight in the hope that those who are in power will appreciate Our words…"). He frames his own voice as clarifying confusion, not opening debate. Passages on the anti-Christian conspiracy are emotionally charged ("Poor France! God alone can measure the abyss of evil…"); passages on political theory are deliberately calm and scholastic. There is an apologetic strand — the extended historical analogy between current accusations against Catholics and ancient accusations against Christ — that suggests he is conscious of defending the Church's reputation before a wider French public, not only its own faithful.

**Notable framing**

- The document frames French Catholic political divisions as strategically dangerous, not merely spiritually regrettable. Unity is prescribed as a quasi-military necessity against an organised enemy.
- The separation of Church and State is treated not as a theoretical possibility but as a known weapon: Leo quotes the enemies themselves ("this separation means that political legislation be entirely independent of religious legislation") and predicts it would ultimately end in the State persecuting the Church the moment she became too visible.
- Atheism is described as "monstrous error" but the document is careful to address "all upright and intelligent Frenchmen," not Catholics alone, when it appeals for pacification — suggesting an attempt to claim moral common ground beyond the confessional boundary.
- The Concordat section (§27) is notably cautious: Leo refuses to express a preference for its continuation or abolition, instructing Catholics simply to leave the matter to the Holy See. This restraint contrasts with the assertive tone elsewhere and signals a live institutional negotiation.
- The closing image — "Christ, who loves the Franks" — is an unusually national-historical flourish, tying the universal Church to France's particular identity in a way that underscores the entire letter's attempt to present Catholicism and Frenchness as inseparable.
