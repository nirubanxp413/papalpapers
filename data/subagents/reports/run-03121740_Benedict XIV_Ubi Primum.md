# UBI PRIMUM

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual | Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Newly elected Benedict XIV instructs all bishops in their pastoral duties — clerical formation, residency, visitation, preaching, and exemplary conduct — as essential obligations of episcopal office.
- **Summary:** Written on the very first day of Benedict XIV's pontificate (3 December 1740), this encyclical letter calls all bishops to vigorous fulfilment of their office. It addresses six interlocking duties: maintaining clerical discipline and careful selection of clergy; founding and nurturing seminaries; appointing only worthy pastors to parishes and ensuring regular catechesis; observing personal residency in their dioceses; conducting thorough and repeated pastoral visitations; and setting a personal example of holiness in all spiritual and charitable works. Throughout, bishops are reminded that they will answer before God for any neglect, and are invited to seek support from the Holy See whenever needed.
- **Response to:** The document responds to the occasion of the pope's own election to the chair of Peter ("Ubi primum placuit Deo… humilitatem nostram in suprema Beati Petri Sede collocare") and to existing, named failures in episcopal practice: collapse of ecclesiastical discipline, absence of seminaries in some places, negligent preaching and catechesis (especially of children), unlawful or excessive absence of bishops from their dioceses, and pastoral visitations that are decreed but never carried out.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates and directs towards: rigorous screening of candidates for holy orders; establishment or expansion of seminaries where they do not yet exist; annual spiritual exercises for clergy; faithful personal residency and regular visitation of all parts of each diocese; enforcement of visitation decrees; and the bishops' own exemplary conduct as "form of the flock" in liturgy, charitable works, and personal holiness.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the ideal of the bishop as ever-present, personally vigilant shepherd and the documented reality of absent, negligent, or insufficiently formed bishops. The text also names the tension between external social pressures on bishops (favoritism, lobbying by candidates' patrons, personal requests — "humano respectu, vel importuniis ambientium suggestionibus, vel fautorum precibus") and the canonical standards that must not yield to those pressures. A further tension exists between the sufficiency of delegated administrators and the irreplaceable personal authority and grace of the bishop himself: the text insists that deputies, however capable, cannot substitute for the bishop's own presence and voice.

## Unstructured

**Key themes and flow of argument**

The letter is organized as a programmatic inaugural statement. Having just been placed "in the supreme seat of Blessed Peter," Benedict XIV immediately reaches out to bishops as co-sharers of pastoral responsibility — explicitly invoking the Johannine commission "Feed my lambs; feed my sheep" and interpreting bishops as the "mothers of the lambs," i.e., those who generate the faithful in Christ. This framing is significant: the pope positions himself as the shepherd of shepherds, and the entire letter flows from that relational premise.

The argument proceeds in ascending order of scope: from the individual cleric (ordination standards, seminary formation), to the parish (worthy pastors, preaching, catechesis), to the diocese as a whole (residency, visitation, follow-through on decrees), and finally to the bishop's own personal holiness as the capstone and source of everything else. Each section builds on the previous: you cannot have good parishes without well-formed clergy; you cannot have well-governed dioceses without resident bishops; and you cannot have resident bishops who lead authentically without personal sanctity.

**Tone**

The tone is firmly authoritative yet simultaneously fraternal and pastoral. Benedict consistently addresses the bishops as "Fratres" (Brothers) and speaks of "paternal charity" toward them, but the register is one of instruction and obligation, not mere encouragement. The text twice invokes divine judgment as a motivating frame — the image of the Supreme Judge before whom no excuse of ignorance will stand if wolves have devoured the flock in a bishop's absence. This judicial framing sits alongside an affective, even warm invitation in the final paragraph: "Come to us, who love you all as Brothers and helpers… in the bowels of Jesus Christ."

**Notable framing**

- The bishop as "form of the flock" (forma gregis vestri): bishops are not merely administrators but living models whose personal conduct determines the spiritual temperature of clergy and laity alike.
- The insistence that "good and vigorous workers are not born, but made" (non nascuntur, sed fiunt): formation, not natural talent, is the decisive variable in clerical quality — and this is firmly placed as an episcopal responsibility.
- The analogy of the paterfamilias who must inspect everything himself, rather than delegating: episcopal presence is cast as structurally necessary, not merely preferable.
- The repeated return to visitation as both a diagnostic and a remedial act: it is not enough to visit once; the follow-up — ensuring that what was decreed is actually carried out — is presented as equally obligatory.
- The closing eschatological horizon: the "unfading crown of glory" awaiting faithful shepherds is offered not as pious consolation but as the ultimate rationale for present labor.
