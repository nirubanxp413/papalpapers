# Permoti Nos

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Leo XIII calls the bishops of Belgium to restore unity among Catholics divided by the social question, directing them to act together against Socialism while grounding social reform in Christian religion and morals.
- **Summary:** Written to the Bishops of Belgium in 1895, this letter responds to damaging discord among Belgian Catholics over the social question. Leo XIII diagnoses the disagreement as stemming from Catholics "consulting others" and following personal opinions rather than unified Catholic teaching. He urges the bishops to convene a congress, restore concord, and direct Catholic social action — touching on workers, employers, industry, schools, and civic life — according to evangelical principles of justice and charity. The letter closes with a sharp warning against Socialism, framing it as a direct threat to both religion and the state.
- **Response to:** The letter explicitly responds to "burning discussions" of the social question among Belgian Catholics that have produced "discord" rather than the fruits expected. It cites prior unity (notably in the schools debate) as a model now being lost. It also situates itself as a follow-up to the earlier encyclical *Rerum Novarum*, referencing it as the source of prior commands and warnings on the social calamity.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates a bishops' congress to coordinate a national Catholic social response. It prescribes that all Catholic action should start from common principles, pursue the common good over personal interests, and be "honest, vigorous, and productive." It directs clergy to lead in calming disputes, warns against public controversy in meetings or newspapers, and calls all Catholics to united opposition against Socialism.
- **Tension:** The central social tension is the division among Belgian Catholics themselves — good-willed people producing discord by "consulting others" rather than heeding unified Church guidance. A secondary tension runs between capital and labour: the document acknowledges different classes exist by divine order but insists on "friendly cooperation," cautioning workers to maintain respect for employers and employers to treat workers with "just kindness and prudent care." The deepest structural tension is between the existing Christian social order (grounded in religion, law, and hierarchy) and Socialism, which the text says is "violently" striving to "throw both divine and human laws into confusion."

## Unstructured

**Flow of argument:** The letter moves from a diagnosis of a pastoral problem (Catholic discord in Belgium) through a call to structural remedy (a bishops' congress), to a vision of the goals of united Catholic action, and ends with a sharp polemical warning against Socialism. It is relatively brief and direct — more of a directive than a sustained theological argument.

**Tone:** Predominantly authoritative and directive. Leo XIII speaks with the voice of a superior addressing subordinates, using phrases like "We suggest," "We strongly support," "warn them to refrain completely." There is a warm pastoral register in the opening (noting his "special friendship" for Belgium and recalling their past loyalty) and the closing blessing, but the body is firmly instructional.

**Key themes and framings:**
- *Unity as instrumental and ecclesial*: Catholic concord is not merely a virtue but the precondition for effective social action. The document draws on the schools debate as a concrete historical example: unity worked then, disunity is now failing. This makes Catholic unity a practical and political argument, not just a spiritual one.
- *The social question as inseparable from religion*: The document insists that "this question cannot be regarded from one standpoint only" — it touches "external goods," religion and morals, civil law, and class rights and duties simultaneously. This framing resists any purely economic or secular reading of the social question.
- *Gradualism and moderation*: The text affirms that "every improvement contributes to stability and leads to greater benefits if it is introduced gradually and moderately" — signalling a conservative reformist stance opposed to radical social change.
- *Socialism as the negative pole*: The letter names Socialism explicitly in paragraph 11 as the enemy of both religion and the state, citing *Rerum Novarum* as prior testimony to this alarm. Belgian Catholic unity is ultimately framed as a bulwark against this threat.
- *The clergy as social agents*: Priests are specifically tasked with being "wary in the face of novel opinions," with soothing and uniting men's minds, and with recalling "the duties of Christian citizens." The clergy are cast as the first line of social stabilisation.

**Notable framing:** The document uses the language of "fruits" — Catholic social action should yield "fruits," and the current discord is a hindrance to the expected harvest. This organic metaphor positions Catholic engagement as cultivation rather than revolution, consistent with the gradualist tone throughout.
