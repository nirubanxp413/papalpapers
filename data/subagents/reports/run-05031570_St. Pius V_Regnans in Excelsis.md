# Regnans in Excelsis

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual | Social | Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The pope, acting from his supreme apostolic authority as sole earthly governor of the Church, formally excommunicates and deposes Elizabeth I of England for heresy, the usurpation of spiritual jurisdiction, and the persecution of Catholics.
- **Summary:** The document opens by asserting the absolute, divinely-granted authority of the papacy over all peoples and kingdoms. It then catalogs a series of religious and ecclesiastical offences attributed to Elizabeth I — including abolishing the Mass, installing heretical ministers, suppressing Catholic clergy, and compelling oaths of spiritual obedience to herself — framing these as an ongoing, escalating persecution of the faithful. Having exhausted persuasion and diplomatic contact (pious admonitions, Catholic princes' appeals, papal nuncios all rebuffed), Pius V declares he is compelled to act with "the weapons of justice." The bull concludes with a juridical sentence: Elizabeth is declared a heretic, excommunicated, deprived of her crown, and her subjects absolved of all oaths and duties of obedience to her.
- **Response to:** The document itself says it responds to: Elizabeth's seizure of the English crown and usurpation of the title of supreme head of the Church in England; her reversal of the Catholic restoration under Queen Mary; the abolition of the Mass, Catholic rites, celibacy, fasts, and sacramental practices; the ejection and imprisonment of Catholic bishops and clergy; the imposition of heretical (Calvinist) rites upon subjects by force of law; the refusal to receive papal nuncios or heed Catholic princes' admonitions; and the general growth in power of the "ungodly" worldwide who corrupt with "wicked doctrines."
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs all nobles, subjects, and peoples formerly bound by oaths to Elizabeth to consider themselves absolved of those oaths and to refuse obedience to her orders, mandates, and laws — on pain of excommunication for those who comply with her. It also directs that authenticated copies of this bull shall carry full legal force across all nations in and out of judicial proceedings.
- **Tension:** The central tension is jurisdictional and spiritual: the claim of the papacy to hold supreme authority over all earthly rulers — including the power to depose monarchs — in direct collision with Elizabeth's claim to sovereign and supreme headship of the Church within England. A secondary tension runs between the duty of Catholic charity and patience (the document expresses reluctance and regret at acting against a monarch whose ancestors "so well deserved of the Christian community") and the duty of justice, which the pope frames as ultimately compelling him to act. A further tension exists between the unity of the body of Christ and the fragmentation caused by heresy, which the document frames as an existential threat to salvation itself.

## Unstructured

**Key themes and flow of argument:**

The document is structured as a legal-theological proceeding in miniature. It opens with a doctrinal foundation: Christ entrusted the Church exclusively to Peter and his successors, giving the pope jurisdiction "over all peoples and kingdoms." This is not incidental framing — it is the jurisprudential basis for everything that follows. Without this claim, the bull has no standing.

The argument then pivots to the specific case. Elizabeth is introduced not as a legitimate monarch but as a "pretended queen" and "servant of crime," language that immediately signals the document's refusal to grant her any legitimacy. The catalogue of her offences in sections 1–2 is cumulative and deliberate: each action (abolishing the Mass, installing heretics, forcing oaths, imprisoning clergy) is presented as compounding the last, building a picture of systematic, willful destruction of Catholicism in England. The reference to Henry VIII as "a deserter" and the contrast with Mary as "the lawful queen of famous memory" positions Elizabeth within a dynastic-religious narrative in which Catholic legitimacy was restored and has now been overthrown again.

Section 3 marks the rhetorical and juridical turn. The pope frames his action as reluctant but necessary — he has tried patience, prayer, persuasion, and diplomacy, and all have failed. Elizabeth has not merely erred; she has "fixed and set" her mind. This hardness of heart is the theological trigger that converts pastoral concern into juridical action. The phrase "weapons of justice" is carefully chosen: it acknowledges that force of some kind is being deployed, but frames it as justice rather than aggression.

Sections 3–5 deliver the formal sentence in layered steps: excommunication of Elizabeth and her adherents; deprivation of her title, lordship, and privileges; absolution of all her subjects from their oaths of obedience; and a positive command to those subjects to disobey her, backed by the threat of excommunication for compliance.

**Tone:**

The document is overwhelmingly authoritative and juridical rather than pastoral. There is no appeal to Elizabeth's conscience, no offer of reconciliation, no path back. Unlike many papal documents of the period that balance condemnation with an invitation to return, this bull is a closing of a door rather than an opening. The sole pastoral register is the brief expression of regret in section 3 — but even this is immediately overridden by the assertion of necessity. The tone toward Elizabeth's subjects is more commanding than consoling: they are told what they must not do, and warned of consequences for non-compliance.

**Notable framing:**

The framing of Elizabeth as existing within a global network of "ungodly" forces — not an isolated national problem but a sanctuary for the "most pernicious of all" — universalizes what might otherwise seem a local jurisdictional dispute. The document presents the problem as cosmic in scale (the unity of the body of Christ, the salvation of souls) even while its legal instrument is precisely targeted at one named individual. The phrase "outside of which there is no salvation" in the opening line anchors the entire argument: if the Church is the sole vehicle of salvation, then anything that disrupts its unity or authority is not merely a political offence but a mortal threat to souls.

The closing provision in section 6 — that authenticated copies shall have the force of the original — reflects a practical awareness that the bull's authority must be enforceable across jurisdictions where the original document cannot easily be produced. This detail, seemingly administrative, underscores the document's self-understanding as a legal instrument with universal reach.
