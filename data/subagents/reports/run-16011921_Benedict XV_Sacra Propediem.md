# Sacra Propediem

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The seventh centenary of the Franciscan Third Order is an occasion to revive its lay spiritual movement as the remedy for both the moral decay and the social ruptures — class warfare, post-war disorder, materialism — afflicting contemporary society.
- **Summary:** Benedict XV marks the 700th anniversary of the Third Order of St. Francis by calling all Catholics to join or renew their membership in it. The document argues that the Franciscan spirit — fraternal charity, poverty of spirit, penance, and peace — is precisely what the current age needs, paralleling the disorders of Francis's own era. The pope links personal moral reform (chastity, modesty, detachment from riches) directly to social restoration (ending class conflict, achieving lasting peace). Tertiaries are presented as the agents through whom Christian principles can re-enter the "current of social life."
- **Response to:** (1) The "horrible war" that had "kindled in almost the entire world" a conflagration now still smouldering; (2) "the interior crisis which the nations are going through" arising from "forgetfulness and prolonged disdain of Christian principles"; (3) class conflict — "this fight for the sharing of goods which sets in conflict the different classes of society" threatening "a universal catastrophe"; (4) moral decay characterised by "an unlimited desire of riches and an insatiable thirst for pleasures" and indecent dress; (5) the misrepresentation of Francis by "certain moderns" and "Modernists."
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) Expansion of Third Order chapters in "every city, town, and even in each village"; (2) affiliation of existing Catholic associations (youth, workers, women) to the Third Order; (3) personal reform by Tertiaries in embracing poverty of spirit, self-denial, and bearing the cross; (4) Tertiary Sisters modelling "holy modesty" in dress to reform female morals broadly; (5) spreading "good words of Christ" to those who have "gone out from the road"; (6) a centenary Triduum with plenary indulgences to spur devotional renewal.
- **Tension:** (1) The "spirit of the world" — materialism and pleasure-seeking — versus the Spirit of Christ; (2) rich versus proletariat: the document names an ongoing class war and decries "unbridled luxury" set against poverty; (3) authentic Franciscan obedience to Rome versus the Modernist reconstruction of Francis as a figure of "vague and vain religiosity"; (4) human treaty-making for peace (diplomatic/political settlements) versus the only durable peace, which the document says must be grounded in interior pacification of hearts.

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument.** The document opens with a personal, almost warm note: Benedict recalls receiving the Tertiary habit himself in 1882. This grounds the papal exhortation in lived devotion before it becomes prescription. From that personal opening the document moves outward in concentric circles — Francis's historical achievement (the Third Order as the first institution to make religious life accessible to lay people "in the whirlpool of the world") → the rule as updated by Leo XIII → the contemporary crisis → the specific remedies Tertiaries can apply → practical directives for the centenary celebrations and indulgences.

**Dual register: spiritual and social are inseparable.** The document refuses to treat moral reform and social peace as two separate agendas. Fraternal charity is described both as "the spiritual rule of his children" and as what "rendered naturally the greatest service to the entire human family." Interior conversion is "but an instrument" for awakening "love of Christian wisdom" in society. This tight weaving of inner virtue and outward social consequence is the document's central argumentative move.

**Diagnosis of the age.** Paragraphs 12–19 form a concentrated social diagnosis. War is still smouldering ("the smoking hearth of this ill-extinguished conflagration"). Class war threatens "a universal catastrophe." Then the document pivots to cultural-moral decay: "an unlimited desire of riches and an insatiable thirst for pleasures" mark the age; "a lamentable retrogression leads it back to the ignominies of ancient paganism." The language is sharp — "shameful stigma," "revolting corruption," "shocking" dances. Women's immodest dress receives unusual prominence (paragraph 19), named as a direct offence against God and an occasion of sin brought even to the Eucharist.

**The peace argument.** Paragraph 15 makes an explicit distinction between human diplomatic peace ("laborious treaty-making of human prudence") and the peace Christ brings. Accords between states or classes "will not be durable" unless founded on "the pacification of hearts," which requires mastery of the passions — and this is precisely what Franciscan formation achieves. This frames the Third Order not as a pious side-project but as a structural social necessity.

**Tone.** The document is more pastoral than juridical in register — it appeals, hopes, and invites rather than commands. Benedict uses "We cherish the hope," "We are sure," "it is desirable." The indulgences at the end are a reward structure rather than a penalty structure. Yet the pastoral warmth coexists with blunt moral denunciation (paragraphs 18–19), giving the text an internally contrasted tone: tender towards Francis and the Tertiaries, severe towards the age's vices.

**Anti-Modernist aside.** Paragraph 3 inserts a brief but pointed rebuttal of Modernist appropriations of Francis — the Francis who is "guarded in his obedience to the Apostolic See" is the real one; the Modernist version is "assuredly neither Francis of Assisi nor a saint." This is not the document's main concern but signals that the Franciscan revival being urged is an explicitly hierarchically-anchored one.

**Notable framing.** The centenary is framed as providential timing: just as Francis arose in "such troubled times" to defend the Church, so now the Third Order responds "marvelously to the needs of society." The parallel between the thirteenth-century crisis and the post-WWI moment is explicit and structural, not merely rhetorical — it is the document's main justification for urgency.
