# Cum illud semper

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Church must rigorously select the most worthy candidates for pastoral office through standardised competitive examinations, and the appeal process established to protect justice must be reformed so it cannot be weaponised by unworthy or unscrupulous candidates.
- **Summary:** Benedict XIV addresses persistent abuses in the system for appointing parish rectors. The document begins by asserting the Church's longstanding concern that pastoral care not be entrusted to the unworthy, then recounts how the Council of Trent and later Pius V established competitive examinations and a right of appeal to protect against unjust appointments. The bulk of the letter diagnoses how the appeal mechanism has been perverted — rejected candidates abuse it to delay, exhaust, and ultimately displace legitimately elected pastors — and prescribes a detailed, standardised examination procedure. It closes by extending these norms to benefices reserved to the Apostolic See, urging bishops to exercise diligent vigilance so that further legislation does not become necessary.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to (a) a corrupted opinion that Tridentine decrees only prohibit appointing the unworthy rather than requiring the election of the most worthy; (b) the abuse of the Pian Constitution's appeal mechanism ("in devolutivo"), whereby rejected candidates routinely convoked elected pastors to lengthy second and third examinations, causing grave harm to parishes and to justice; (c) the absence of a standardised written examination format, which left no reliable record against which to adjudicate appeals; and (d) the document's own earlier 1721 circular letter (drafted when Benedict XIV was Secretary of the Sacred Congregation for the Council), which it now supplements and in part supersedes.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document is directed prospectively at all bishops and ordinaries, prescribing: uniform written examination procedures (same questions, same time, same room, candidates writing and signing their own answers); evaluation criteria extending beyond doctrinal knowledge to encompass character, prudence, seriousness and prior service; strict deadlines for filing appeals; transmission of full examination acts to appeal judges; a three-judgment cap on appeals; and special procedures for benefices reserved to the Apostolic See. It also addresses appeal judges and the Cardinal Prefect of the Sacred Congregation of the Council as enforcement actors.
- **Tension:** Two interlocking tensions run through the document. First, a tension between justice-as-procedure (the right of appeal, intended to correct unjust episcopal choices) and justice-as-outcome (the right appointment of a genuinely worthy pastor, which the appeal process in practice obstructs). Second, a tension between the bishop's privileged local knowledge of his clergy — including secret knowledge of crimes — and the formal rules of evidence available to appeal judges, who are "entirely ignorant of the facts" and are therefore liable to be misled by testimonials assembled after the fact.

## Unstructured

**Argument flow.** The document has a clearly structured rhetorical arc. It opens by asserting a foundational ecclesiastical principle ("the whole structure of the family wavers if what is demanded of the body is not found in the head"), then traces the historical deformation of a good law: Trent mandated competitive exams; Pius V added the right of appeal; the appeal mechanism was then exploited until the remedy became a weapon. Benedict XIV presents himself as the expert who, as a junior official in 1720–21, diagnosed this problem and drafted the 1721 circular — and who now, as Pope, has both the authority and the experience to impose a definitive remedy. This autobiographical move (sections 5–8) is rhetorically significant: it grounds the new legislation in lived pastoral experience rather than abstract canonical reasoning.

**Tone.** Predominantly legislative and technical — this is a canonical instruction more than a spiritual exhortation. The prose is careful and procedural, moving through numbered rules. Yet pastoral concern breaks through at key points: the image of the elected pastor forced to leave his parish "like a husband leaving his bride to strangers" (section 7), and the insistence that doctrinal excellence alone does not make a good pastor. The document names the problem with striking directness: "learned pastors, not fitting pastors" are being imposed on parishes.

**Dual evaluation standard.** The document's most substantive theological-pastoral contribution is its insistence that fitness for pastoral office requires a double assessment — doctrinal knowledge and the whole constellation of moral, prudential and experiential qualities (gravità, prudenza, bontà di costumi, ossequio verso la Chiesa, prior service). Sections 10–14 catalogue the ways the existing appeal system systematically reduces the contest to a purely academic one, thereby privileging those who study hardest after rejection over those who have lived well and served faithfully. This is not merely an administrative complaint: it reflects a vision of the priesthood in which character and conduct are constitutive of fitness, not merely supplementary to it.

**Secrecy and episcopal authority.** A notable procedural innovation is the provision (section VII of the prescriptions, section 16) allowing a bishop who knows of a hidden crime disqualifying a candidate to communicate this secretly to the appeal judge, or — if the bishop doubts the judge's discretion — to the Cardinal Prefect of the Congregation directly. This represents an attempt to preserve episcopal authority against the formal evidential rules that would otherwise require a bishop to either reveal the secret in open proceedings or remain silent and see a bad appointment confirmed. The document explicitly defends bishops against the presumption that they act from "aversion or favour," invoking the Johannine image of the shepherd who knows his sheep.

**Notable framing.** The document consistently frames abuses as the result of individual bad faith ("the fraud and malice of men") rather than structural defects, even as it proposes structural remedies. It also conspicuously avoids abolishing the appeal right altogether, explicitly rejecting that option as contrary to the mind of Trent (section 15), and instead works within the existing canonical framework to constrain misuse. The final sections extending the new rules to apostolic-reserve benefices give the encyclical a comprehensive scope — no appointment of a soul-bearing benefice is exempt from the standardised competition procedure.
