# Incredibili

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document condemns the anti-Church laws of the government of New Granada, praises those who resisted persecution, calls for the repentance of clerics who complied, and invokes divine aid for the Church amid grave civil oppression.
- **Summary:** Pius IX addresses the Archbishop of Santa Fe de Bogotá and his suffragan bishops to lament the "incredible grief" caused by the New Granada government's systematic persecution of the Church. He enumerates the specific laws and their effects — confiscation of Church property, forced exile of bishops, clergy, and religious, the banning of papal rescripts, and mandatory oaths of obedience to anti-Church laws — and by apostolic authority formally condemns and abrogates those laws. He praises the bishops, faithful clergy, nuns, and Catholic laity who resisted, censures the ecclesiastics who complied, and concludes with fervent prayer that God will deliver the Church from its afflictions and bring all peoples to know Him.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to: laws and decrees passed by the New Granada government approximately two years prior that confiscated all Church property; required government consent for priests to exercise ministry; banned religious communities of men and women; forbade promulgation of papal letters and rescripts; declared liberty for non-Catholic sects; and imposed an oath of obedience to the republic's constitution under penalty of exile. It also responds to the arrest, imprisonment, military escort, and exile of the bishops themselves, the imprisonment and death of priests in exile, the violent expulsion of nuns from their convents, and the scandal of "some ecclesiastics" who obeyed the government and took the illicit oath.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The government of New Granada is directed to "open their eyes," recall the censures inflicted by Apostolic Constitutions and General Councils on invaders of Church rights, and pity their own souls. Clerics who complied are exhorted to "return quickly to the paths of justice and truth" and are pointed toward those who retracted their oath as the model to follow. The bishops are encouraged to continue defending the Church's cause, doctrine, rights, and liberty with the same constancy they have already shown. More broadly, the document directs prayer toward a future in which "all peoples, races, and nations come to know, adore, and fear" God and His Son, suggesting a universal evangelistic aspiration beyond the immediate crisis.
- **Tension:** (1) State authority vs. Church authority: the civil government claims the power to regulate, confiscate, and exile the Church's persons and property, while the document insists those laws are contrary to "all divine and human laws" and have no validity. (2) Clerical fidelity vs. clerical capitulation: the text names the scandal of ecclesiastics who "did not hesitate to obey and support the perverse laws," setting them in sharp tension against the bishops and clergy who suffered exile and imprisonment rather than comply. (3) Temporal suffering vs. eternal salvation: the faithful are described as "in great peril of losing eternal salvation" because they have been deprived of sacraments and pastors — the real stakes of the political conflict are framed as souls, not merely institutional rights.

## Unstructured

*Incredibili* is a tight, forensic document with a pronounced prosecutorial register. Pius IX opens by cataloguing, almost as a legal brief, the specific provisions of New Granada's anti-Church legislation: the requirement of government consent for ministry, the confiscation and sale of Church property, the suppression of religious communities, the ban on papal communications, the oath of fidelity, and the penalties of exile or imprisonment for refusal. The list functions rhetorically as an indictment before the papal verdict delivered in §4, where Pius formally "condemns," "abrogates," and declares "entirely invalid" everything the government has done. The language of apostolic authority is invoked explicitly and repeatedly — the document is not pastoral consolation but a juridical act.

The tone shifts notably in §§5–7, where Pius pivots from condemnation to praise. He distinguishes three groups: the bishops themselves ("good soldiers of Christ Jesus"), the devoted clergy who "suffered most patiently monstrous persecutions," and the nuns who "firmly cling to their heavenly Spouse" while praying even for their persecutors. This tripartite praise is both an act of episcopal solidarity and a public record — the encyclical names what happened so that it is not forgotten.

The document is framed throughout by the motif of grief: "incredible grief" opens the letter, and sorrow and groaning recur as the proper response of any person "animated by Catholic and human sensibilities." This framing is not merely emotive; it positions the reader as a moral witness obligated to react.

The prophetic citation from Isaiah 58:1 — "Cry, cease not, lift up thy voice like a trumpet" — is the document's key interpretive move. Pius identifies his own apostolic role with the Old Testament prophet's mandate to denounce sin openly and fearlessly. This grounds the condemnation in a scriptural necessity rather than mere political reaction.

The concluding section (§8) moves from forensic to eschatological, widening the horizon from New Granada to "almost everywhere on earth," suggesting the persecution is not an isolated case but part of a larger pattern of Church affliction. The prayer for universal acknowledgment of God ends the letter on a note that transcends the specific crisis — though the crisis itself is never minimized.

There is no pastoral softening of the condemnation, nor any diplomatic ambiguity about the government's guilt. The document's argument is linear: here are the crimes (§1), here is the suffering they caused (§2), here is the scandal of clerical compliance (§3), here is the formal condemnation (§4), here is the praise for those who held firm (§§5–7), and here is the appeal to God for relief (§8). The structure mirrors the rhetorical movement from wound to remedy.
