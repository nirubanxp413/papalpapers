# Venimus in altitudinem

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** At the frightening outset of his pontificate, Clement XIII proclaims a Jubilee of prayer, fasting, and almsgiving as the Church's spiritual weapons against the grave wounds afflicting Catholicism and the Apostolic See.
- **Summary:** This brief (September 11, 1758, year one of Clement XIII's pontificate) opens with the pope expressing deep anxiety and trembling at the state of the Catholic Church and the Apostolic See, using the nautical metaphor of having entered "deep waters" ("venimus in altitudinem") amid fierce contrary winds. He declares that the remedy lies not in cataloguing the evils but in turning to God through prayer, fasting, and almsgiving. In response, he announces a Jubilee with plenary indulgence, setting out detailed conditions — participation in a solemn procession through Rome, visits to designated churches, fasting, sacramental confession, reception of the Eucharist, and charitable giving — extended to faithful throughout the world and adapted for those in enclosure, illness, travel, or imprisonment. The document closes with canonical provisions overriding existing privileges and instructions for bishops to promulgate the letter.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to what it describes as the painful state ("lo stato penoso") of the Catholic religion and the Apostolic See at the moment Clement XIII assumed office; it alludes to "many horrific wounds" inflicted on the faith, Church discipline, and Church rights ("tante orrende ferite inflitte alla nostra fede, alla nostra disciplina e ai nostri diritti"), without specifying their nature. It also follows the ancient custom of Roman Pontiffs of granting jubilee indulgences at the beginning of a pontificate.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs the faithful toward penitential practice: fasting on the Ember Days (Quattro Tempora), church visits, sacramental confession and Eucharist, and almsgiving. It prescribes that bishops and ordinaries immediately and without delay promulgate and distribute the letter throughout their dioceses, cities, and territories. It anticipates a future in which God's mercy, invoked through these acts, will sustain the new pontificate through its difficulties.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the overwhelming weight of the Church's troubles at the start of the pontificate (described in language of trembling, anguish, grief, and weeping) and the confidence that God's omnipotent mercy transcends human understanding and can heal even the most grave, stubborn, and desperate conditions. A secondary tension exists between the expansive grant of indulgences and absolutions (including from reserved cases and censures) and the careful legal exclusions for unrepentant, publicly irregular, or excommunicated persons.

## Unstructured

The document is structured in two distinct movements: a personal confessional opening (§1) and a canonical juridical body (§§2–10). The tonal shift between the two is striking.

**Opening movement — the pastoral lament (§1):** The opening paragraph is unusually affective for a formal papal brief. Clement XIII describes himself as having entered "high seas" ("venimus in altitudinem") with "unleashed contrary winds," reaching out to God with "unspeakable laments." The rhetoric is one of trembling, anguish, grief, and tears. He does not specify the evils but insists they are visible to any careful observer — "tante orrende ferite" (so many horrific wounds) to faith, discipline, and rights. Yet the passage pivots immediately to hope: no illness is so grave, stubborn, or desperate that God's ineffable mercy cannot heal it. The remedy declared is the traditional triad of prayer, fasting, and almsgiving, framed as the "arms" ("le nostre armi") with which the Church resists evil. This framing positions the Jubilee as a spiritual campaign rather than merely a ritual celebration.

**Juridical body — the Jubilee proclamation (§§2–10):** The remainder of the document is technical and canonical. It grants plenary indulgence under precise conditions: for Romans, participation in the solemn procession (September 17) from Santa Maria degli Angeli to Santa Maria Maggiore, plus church visits, fasting, confession, Eucharist, and almsgiving within specified weeks. For those outside Rome, the same indulgence is extended via visits to churches designated by local ordinaries. Provisions are carefully made for those unable to fulfil all conditions — religious in perpetual enclosure, the sick, prisoners, and travellers — allowing confessors to commute or defer the required acts. Confessors are granted broad faculties to absolve from censures, even in cases reserved to the Holy See, with the notable exception of public irregularities and unrepentant persons under censure (§8). A comprehensive derogation clause (§9) explicitly overrides existing papal constitutions, rules, and privileges that might otherwise limit these grants.

**Tone:** The document is simultaneously deeply pastoral in its opening — almost confessional and vulnerable — and rigorously authoritative in its canonical sections. The opening paragraph reads as genuine personal anguish; the subsequent paragraphs read as precise legal instruments. This dual register — the suffering shepherd and the lawgiving pontiff — is the document's defining rhetorical feature.

**Notable framing:** The title phrase "Venimus in altitudinem" is drawn from Psalm 68 (69):3 ("I have come into the depths of water, and the flood has overwhelmed me"), casting Clement's accession not as a triumph but as an immersion in danger — a self-consciously humble and anxious entry into office. The Jubilee is framed not as a celebration of papal power but as a collective cry for divine mercy at a moment of institutional distress. The document does not name any specific enemy, schism, heresy, or political crisis, keeping the threat deliberately general ("so many evils," "so many wounds"), which gives the lament a universal rather than polemical character.
