# Dum Multa

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document condemns Ecuador's civil marriage and divorce laws as violations of both divine law and Church authority, while calling the bishops to continued resistance and pastoral vigilance.
- **Summary:** Leo XIII addresses the bishops of Ecuador to formally condemn civil marriage and divorce legislation recently enacted there. The document reaffirms that Christian marriage is a sacrament subject exclusively to Church authority—not civil power—that it is indissoluble by divine will ("What God has joined, let no man divide"), and that it benefits society through morality, family stability, and the dignity of women. The Pope decries Ecuador's abolition of a canonically constituted diocese and episcopal appointments made without the Holy See, framing all these acts as grave injustices against Church rights. He urges the bishops to persevere in defending those rights, to educate the faithful in Catholic teaching, and to pray to the Sacred Heart of Jesus for better times.
- **Response to:** The enactment in Ecuador of civil marriage and divorce laws opposed to "divine right" and Church discipline; the abolition of a diocese constituted under canon law; the appointment of bishops to vacant dioceses without Holy See privilege; the failure of the Ecuadorian government to repair previously condemned injustices despite papal forbearance.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Continued episcopal resistance and public protest against anti-Church legislation; ongoing catechesis of the faithful in Catholic moral teaching and reverence for Church authority; intercessory prayer to the Sacred Heart of Jesus for the restoration of religious tranquility in Ecuador.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between civil authority (which claims jurisdiction over marriage via civil marriage and divorce laws) and Church authority (which holds that the sacramental dimension of Christian marriage belongs exclusively to the Church). A secondary tension exists between the Pope's earlier hopes for reconciliation with Ecuador and the government's continued—indeed escalating—legislative hostility.

## Unstructured

*Dum Multa* is a brief but sharply focused document, essentially a formal papal protest and letter of encouragement addressed to the bishops of Ecuador. Its tone is simultaneously authoritative and pastoral: authoritative in its clear doctrinal declarations and its explicit condemnation of specific laws; pastoral in its expressions of solidarity with the bishops' suffering and its encouragement not to be "disheartened."

The argument proceeds in three tight movements. First (§1), Leo establishes the factual grievances: Ecuador has not only failed to remedy prior injustices but has compounded them with civil marriage legislation, the suppression of a diocese, and irregular episcopal appointments. The tone is one of measured disappointment—"the hopes which We had … have perished miserably."

Second (§2), the document pivots to doctrinal grounding, restating the theology of Christian marriage as sacrament. Leo cites Christ's elevation of marriage, his abrogation of divorce ("What God has joined, let no man divide"), and the consequent exclusive Church jurisdiction over the marriage bond itself (while conceding civil authority over civil effects). This section frames the Ecuadorian laws not merely as political disagreements but as violations of divine institution. Notably, Leo links marriage's indissolubility to concrete social benefits—morality, spousal love, family stability, protection of children, dignity of women, civil prosperity—making the argument simultaneously theological and social-utilitarian.

Third (§3), the document turns to exhortation: the bishops are to continue their resistance without yielding, teach and form the faithful, and pray to the Sacred Heart. The appeal to the Sacred Heart (to whom Ecuador has been "solemnly dedicated above all nations") is a specifically devotional and affective element, suggesting that political resolution requires not only ecclesiastical resistance but spiritual intercession.

The document is notably compact—three paragraphs—and functions more as a formal statement of condemnation and solidarity than an extended theological treatise. Its framing throughout privileges Church rights and divine law over state sovereignty in matters of marriage, and treats the bishops' earlier protests as fully legitimate and praiseworthy. There is no diplomatic hedging toward the Ecuadorian government; the condemnation is total ("We … totally condemn the so-called civil marriage laws").
