# Diuturnum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Civil authority derives from God alone, not from popular consent, and therefore obedience to rulers is a religious duty — but rulers themselves are accountable to God for just governance.
- **Summary:** Leo XIII addresses what he describes as an acute crisis: the violent rejection of civil authority across Europe, culminating in political assassination and the spread of socialism, nihilism, and communism. He argues at length — from Scripture, Church Fathers, and natural reason — that the right to rule originates in God, not in the people. While the form of government and the designation of rulers may legitimately involve popular choice, the authority itself is divine in origin. Citizens must therefore obey as a matter of conscience and religious duty, not merely from fear of punishment. The Church is presented as the indispensable guardian of this order, restraining both rebellious subjects and tyrannical rulers. A single exception to obedience is granted: commands openly contrary to natural or divine law need not be obeyed, but even then resistance must be passive (withdrawal, not sedition).
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to a wave of assassinations and revolutionary violence in Europe, naming the murder of Emperor Alexander II of Russia (March 1881) as the immediate horror that fills Europe with dread; the broader intellectual roots of this violence are traced to the Protestant Reformation's doctrinal innovations, eighteenth-century French philosophical "popular sovereignty" theories (Rousseau's social contract), and their downstream products: communism, socialism, and nihilism. The text also responds to what Leo calls the long-running "war waged against the divine authority of the Church."
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs rulers to govern with equity, paternal charity, and justice, knowing they will answer to God; it directs citizens to reject forbidden sects, abhor conspiracy, and obey rulers as God's ministers; it directs bishops to teach these doctrines actively in their flocks; and it urges rulers to grant the Church freedom as the surest guarantor of social stability.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the Church's claim that authority comes from God (and thus demands conscience-based obedience) and the modern liberal-revolutionary claim that authority comes from the people (and thus can be revoked by them). A secondary tension runs between unconditional obedience to rulers and the legitimate refusal to obey commands that violate divine or natural law — the document holds both poles simultaneously. A third tension underlies the document: the Church wishes to be seen as the friend of both rulers and peoples, yet it must rebuke both overreaching rulers and rebellious subjects.

## Unstructured

**Key themes and flow of argument**

*Diuturnum* opens in alarm: political authority is visibly collapsing in Europe. Rulers are being assassinated; sedition is rampant; "popular passions now reject, with more boldness than formerly, every restraint of authority." Leo immediately frames this as the predictable end-point of a long intellectual trajectory — specifically, the erosion of the doctrine that authority comes from God.

The argument proceeds in three movements:

1. **Theological and philosophical grounding (§§4–13).** Leo establishes, through Old Testament citations, the Gospels, Paul's Letter to the Romans, Augustine, Chrysostom, and Gregory the Great, that political power is a "communication of divine power." He directly refutes social-contract theory, arguing that the popular-sovereignty doctrine rests on a "falsehood and a fiction" — people did not create themselves as social beings, and no contractual transfer of rights can generate the moral authority that compels the conscience of citizens.

2. **Historical testimony: the early Church (§§18–21).** Leo marshals the example of early Christians obeying pagan emperors — even unjust ones — as concrete proof that the Church has always taught civil obedience. He cites Athenagoras, Tertullian, and the Epistle to Diognetus to show Christians as the most reliable subjects of the Roman Empire. The martyrs are presented as the paradigm: they refused orders that violated God's law, but they did so by passive refusal and joyful acceptance of death, never by sedition.

3. **Diagnosis of modernity and appeal to rulers and bishops (§§23–27).** The Protestant Reformation is identified as the intellectual break that led directly (via the Enlightenment philosophers) to the French Revolution and then to communism, socialism, and nihilism. Repression by law alone cannot restore order, because "fear is a weak foundation" (quoting Aquinas). Only religion can produce the interior disposition — conscience — that sustains genuine obedience. Therefore rulers should ally with the Church rather than suppress it.

**Tone**

The tone is emphatically authoritative: Leo speaks as guardian and interpreter of Catholic doctrine, issuing instructions to bishops, rulers, and peoples. The register is not primarily pastoral or consolatory; it is diagnostic and legislative. Yet it modulates into something almost urgent or anxious in the opening paragraphs — "grave anxiety," "from hour to hour" — as though the institutional order is genuinely imperilled. The historical sections (early Christians) have a warmer, exemplary quality.

**Notable framing**

- The document carefully separates *who holds* power (which may legitimately be determined by popular choice or national custom) from *the source of* the power itself (always divine). This is a sophisticated move to defuse the charge that the Church opposes democracy as a form.
- The single legitimate ground for disobedience — commands against natural or divine law — is framed as a narrow exception that does not compromise the general duty of obedience, and even then the acceptable response is passive non-compliance, not revolt.
- The Church is positioned as a neutral mediator above both throne and people, restraining tyranny and rebellion alike. This self-presentation serves an obvious apologetic purpose at a moment when liberal states are curtailing Church power.
- The lineage "Reformation → Enlightenment philosophy → Revolution → socialism/communism/nihilism" is stated explicitly and treated as self-evidently causal, without qualification.
