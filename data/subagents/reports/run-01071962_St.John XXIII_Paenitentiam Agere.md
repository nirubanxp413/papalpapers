# Paenitentiam Agere

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** Penance — both internal repentance and external mortification — is the indispensable spiritual preparation the faithful must undertake before and during the Second Vatican Council.
- **Summary:** John XXIII writes to his bishops in the months leading up to the opening of the Second Vatican Council (October 1962), urging the entire Church — clergy and laity alike — to embrace prayer and penance as the proper disposition for receiving God's grace at this solemn assembly. Drawing on a continuous scriptural and patristic line from Moses through the Prophets, John the Baptist, Christ, the Apostles, and the Council of Trent, the Pope argues that God has always required penitential preparation before any great encounter with His people. He distinguishes between internal repentance (confession, Eucharist, detestation of sin) and external penance (voluntary mortification, patient acceptance of suffering), insisting that both are necessary. The letter concludes with practical directives: a solemn novena to the Holy Spirit in every diocese, special preaching, and an appeal to bishops to mobilise their faithful so that souls may be ready to receive the Council's graces.
- **Response to:** The imminent opening of the Second Vatican Council, which the Pope had already proclaimed in the Apostolic Constitution *Humanae salutis*; the precedent set by Innocent III (Fourth Lateran), Gregory X (Second Lyons), and Pius IX (First Vatican) of calling the faithful to penance before an Ecumenical Council; and what the document describes as widespread immersion in "earthly pleasures" and a weakening of the "nobler powers of the human spirit" among contemporary Christians.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The forthcoming Council sessions themselves; the hoped-for renewal of Catholic faith, charity, and moral life; the reunion of separated Christians with the Apostolic See ("all who are at present separated from this Apostolic See may be impelled to strive actively and sincerely for union"); and the broader missionary extension of "the kingdom of God in the world."
- **Tension:** The document names two related tensions. First, a tension between the baptismal dignity Christians have received (clothed in Christ's beauty, called to holiness) and the way many of them "forget the greatness of their calling" and defile it through sin — making the sacrament of Penance a "laborious kind of baptism" needed to recover what was freely given. Second, a tension between the contemporary drift toward "immoderate quest for earthly pleasures" that "debases and weakens the nobler powers of the human spirit" and the austere self-denial modelled by the martyrs and saints, which the Pope calls Christians to recover in view of the Council.

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument.** The letter moves in three broad waves. It opens with doctrinal foundation (§§1–17): penance is the divinely established precondition for any fruitful encounter between God and humanity, demonstrated sequentially through Moses, the Prophets, Joel, John the Baptist, Christ, Peter, and Paul, then anchored ecclesially in the Council of Trent and the Lenten liturgy. The second wave (§§18–27) is historical-institutional: John XXIII situates himself in a line of popes who required penitential preparation before each previous Ecumenical Council, then issues concrete directives (diocesan novena to the Holy Spirit, preaching campaigns, works of mercy). The third wave (§§28–43) is practical-ascetic: a careful anatomy of penance into internal contrition (§28) and external mortification (§§29–33), with the voluntary acts of penance valued especially because they spring from "a free and generous resolve of the will." The letter closes with an exhortation to bishops to transmit this appeal to every category of the faithful — priests, religious, children, the sick.

**Key themes from the text.**

1. *Penance as precondition for divine encounter.* The document's governing logic is typological: just as Moses required expiation before the Law, just as the Prophets required penitential prayer before God's interventions, just as Christ began his preaching with "Do penance," so the Church must penitentially prepare itself before the Council can bear fruit.

2. *The Council as occasion, not subject.* The Council is not described theologically; it is treated primarily as the occasion that makes the call to penance urgent. The substantive content John XXIII associates with the Council is moral renewal and the hope of Christian reunion — ends that depend entirely on the interior state of souls.

3. *Participation in Christ's sufferings as ecclesial act.* The Pope draws on Colossians 1:24 ("I fill up those things that are wanting of the sufferings of Christ in my flesh, for his body, which is the Church") to frame voluntary penance not merely as personal discipline but as a contribution to building up the whole Church — making individual asceticism a directly ecclesial, even missionary, act.

4. *Two-tier penance.* The distinction between inevitable sufferings accepted with resignation (§30) and freely chosen voluntary mortification (§§31–33) is explicit and deliberate. The latter is valued more highly because it is a "sacrifice" — something given freely, not merely endured.

5. *Reunion of separated Christians.* The document explicitly names as one of the hoped-for fruits of the Council's prayer and penance that "all who are at present separated from this Apostolic See may be impelled to strive actively and sincerely for union." This is the closest the letter comes to a social or ecclesio-political dimension, but it remains framed in wholly spiritual terms (the grace obtained through prayer and penance).

**Tone.** Overwhelmingly pastoral rather than legislative. John XXIII explicitly renounces threatening language ("We cannot find it in Us to threaten or abuse them, for the love We bear them is a father's love"), preferring the register of fatherly appeal. The scriptural quotations are woven in as living voices still "ringing in our ears" (Joel) rather than proof-texts. The governing affect is urgency combined with confidence: the Pope is certain the Council will succeed if the spiritual preparation is made.

**Notable framing.** The letter is dated on the Feast of the Most Precious Blood of Our Lord Jesus Christ (1 July 1962), a detail the closing line makes explicit — reinforcing the blood-penance-redemption cluster that runs through the whole document (washing robes in the blood of the Lamb, Christ pouring out His most precious blood, the sacrament of Penance restoring baptismal splendor). This liturgical dating is itself a framing device, anchoring the text's argument in the paschal mystery.
