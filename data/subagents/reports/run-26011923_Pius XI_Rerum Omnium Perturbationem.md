# Rerum Omnium Perturbationem

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The universal call to holiness — accessible to every Christian in any state of life — is demonstrated through the life, writings, and apostolic method of St. Francis de Sales, whose virtue of meekness is also proposed as a remedy for contemporary social disorder.
- **Summary:** Issued on the Third Centenary of the death of St. Francis de Sales, this encyclical uses the saint's biography and writings to refute the longstanding "prejudice" that genuine sanctity is unattainable except by a select few. Pius XI argues that holiness is both commanded and accessible to all, and directs bishops to spread the Salesian spirit — particularly meekness — as a corrective to a world distracted by business, passion, and politics, naming Francis de Sales the Patron of all Writers.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to: (1) the contemporary world's "disorders" and "unmeasured desires" identified in a prior encyclical as rooted in souls; (2) the "prejudice, deeply rooted" since Francis de Sales' own lifetime and "not destroyed even today," that genuine sanctity is impossible or reserved for a spiritual elite; (3) the "apostasy of mankind from the Church" whose "sad and disastrous effects are deplored even to the present hour," traced to the heresies of the Reformation; (4) widespread neglect of the soul among the faithful, who are "immersed in business," given to "satisfaction of their passions," or "completely devoted to the welfare of the public" while forgetting eternal life.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The encyclical directs: (1) bishops to instruct their flocks throughout the centenary year that holiness is obligatory and possible for all, following Salesian teaching; (2) the faithful to acquire meekness as both a spiritual virtue and a source of "perfect peace and concord both in family life and among nations"; (3) Catholic journalists and writers to imitate Francis de Sales — never compromising truth, maintaining clarity and beauty of style, and always writing with charity even in controversy; (4) universal distribution and reading of *Philothea* and *Treatise on the Love of God*; (5) diocesan triduums and novenas through 28 December 1923.
- **Tension:** The central spiritual tension is between the common assumption that Christian perfection is an "exclusive possession of a few great souls" and the Church's insistence — grounded in "Be ye therefore perfect" (Matt. v, 48) — that all are called and capable. A secondary social tension runs through the document: the world pulls people toward wealth, sensuality, and politics, while the Church calls them toward interior holiness and sanctification of ordinary life. A third tension — historical but treated as ongoing — is between heretical fragmentation (the Reformation's legacy) and Catholic unity, with Francis de Sales' controversial method of charity-over-polemic offered as the model for re-integration.

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument.** The encyclical opens by linking contemporary social disorder directly to neglect of interior life, then pivots to the centenary occasion as a providential remedy. The structure is essentially a hagiographical argument: first Pius XI narrates Francis de Sales' character (meekness as acquired, not innate virtue — the detail about bile turned to stone at embalming is offered as physical proof of lifelong self-mastery), then his apostolic works in La Chablais against Calvinist heresy, then his major writings (*Philothea*, *Treatise on the Love of God*, *Controversies*), and finally a set of practical directives to bishops. The saint's life functions as evidence for the encyclical's doctrinal claim: if Francis — naturally hot-tempered — could master himself to such a degree, the command to holiness is genuinely realizable by any Christian.

**Key themes from the text.** The document's dominant motif is the *democratization of sanctity*: "The law of holiness embraces all men and admits of no exception." The word "all" (omnes / "all") recurs insistently. Francis de Sales is valued precisely because he "gave the lie to a prejudice" — holiness as elite — and showed that the devout life is "perfectly possible in every state and condition of secular life." A secondary theme is meekness (*mansuetudo*) as simultaneously a personal virtue and a social force: the encyclical claims meekness "tends primarily to settle the differences both public and private which so often separate us" and asks whether it might not produce "perfect peace and concord both in family life and among nations." This connects the spiritual argument to the opening concern about worldly disorders, giving the encyclical its Mixed character.

**Tone.** The tone is strongly authoritative in its doctrinal and juridical moments (the formal declaration of Francis de Sales as Patron of Writers is stated as an act of Apostolic authority) but decidedly pastoral in its hagiographical sections. The saint is presented with warmth and some rhetorical vividness — the snow-sleeping missionary, the preacher who continues when his audience walks out — making the document feel more inspirational than legislative. The closing exhortation to prayer is notably personal.

**Notable framing.** The Reformation is named as the origin of the current apostasy, and Francis de Sales' polemical *Controversies* is praised for defeating heresy through "charity which was ever the controlling motive." Yet the encyclical's emphasis throughout is not on condemning opponents but on proposing a Salesian method of irenic, stylistically excellent, truth-committed writing. The application to Catholic journalists and writers is explicit and practical: they are to inherit Francis de Sales' approach to controversy — never minimizing truth, but always demonstrating "the highest principles and moved only by Christian charity." This frames the centenary celebration not as backward-looking commemoration but as a living formation program for the contemporary Church.
