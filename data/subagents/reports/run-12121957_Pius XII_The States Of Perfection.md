# The States Of Perfection

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** Christian perfection — defined as total charity-driven union with God and consecration to His service — is universally called for, but its institutional realization through "states of perfection" requires principled adaptation that preserves the founder's spirit, religious obedience, and docile relationship with the Holy See.
- **Summary:** Delivered as an address to the Second General Congress of States of Perfection, this document draws a careful distinction between "perfection" (a universal Christian calling) and "states of perfection" (canonical forms such as religious orders and secular institutes). Pius XII affirms that while the essential definition of Christian perfection is immutable — rooted in charity and total self-offering to God — its practical application through religious communities must adapt to modern conditions. The address then treats three concentric relationships: the individual member to their community (obedience, personality development), communities to one another (collaboration, common welfare), and communities to the Holy See (trust, transparency, docility). Charity is named as the "bond of perfection" and the ultimate resolution of all tensions.
- **Response to:** The document responds to: (1) tensions and objections arising from adaptation movements within religious communities — specifically complaints that religious obedience damages human dignity and hinders personality development ("infantilism"); (2) a perceived tendency in some states of perfection to avoid close contact with the Holy See; (3) accusations that the Holy See pursues excessive "centralization"; (4) the complexity of coordinating increasingly international associations of major Superiors across twenty-five countries.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs toward: (1) a renewed, theologically grounded practice of obedience understood as loving submission to God through visible authority rather than merely external constraint; (2) mature, adult formation of religious — explicitly citing Paul's "put away the things of a child"; (3) sincere collaboration and "common welfare" among communities, modeled on Paul's body-of-Christ analogy; (4) frank and transparent communication with the Holy See as the "directing center of the Church"; (5) fidelity to founders' constitutions as the objective expression of a community's spirit.
- **Tension:** (1) Between the universality of Christian perfection and its restriction to formal "states of perfection" — the text insists perfection can exist outside canonical states, while also affirming the states' privileged role. (2) Between the imperative to adapt religious life to modern conditions and the inviolability of essential elements and approved constitutions. (3) Between religious obedience and the modern value of personal autonomy and full personality development — the document acknowledges this as a live concern and addresses objections that obedience induces "infantilism." (4) Between the legitimate authority of major Superiors and the supreme governance of the Holy See — the text rejects "centralization" as a caricature while firmly maintaining the See's "directing center" role.

## Unstructured

**Document type and occasion.** This is not an encyclical but an allocution — a papal address delivered to the Second General Congress of States of Perfection in December 1957. Its rhetorical mode is therefore semi-formal and pedagogical: Pius XII is not promulgating new doctrine but "throwing light" on principles and resolving objections. The tone oscillates between authoritative (citing Canon Law, Apostolic Constitutions, previous papal addresses) and pastoral (quoting Matthew 11:29, "Come to me, all you who labor…"), aiming to reassure while also correcting.

**Conceptual architecture.** The address is tightly structured in two halves. Part I establishes the philosophical and theological foundations of Christian perfection: it is not identical with institutional "states of perfection," it is universally called for, it consists essentially in charity and self-offering, and it admits of no revision in its essential definition. This move is rhetorically important — it disarms any attempt to fundamentally renegotiate what perfection means, while simultaneously opening space for adaptive change at the level of practice. Part II then treats three relational levels — individual member/community, community/community, community/Holy See — with the bulk of substantive argument devoted to the first (obedience) and the last (relationship with Rome).

**Obedience as the central argumentative problem.** The longest and most argumentatively developed section concerns objections to religious obedience — namely, that it degrades human dignity and fosters emotional/intellectual "infantilism." The pope's response is twofold: (a) scripturally, citing Paul's call to mature manhood in Ephesians and 1 Corinthians; and (b) theologically, reframing obedience not as subjection to another person but as abandonment into the hands of God mediated through legitimate authority. The concern is not dismissed but taken seriously and then recontextualized. This is the document's most diagnostic passage for understanding tensions inside mid-twentieth century religious life.

**Framing of adaptation.** The word "adaptation" (and "revision") appears throughout as a live issue: the Congress is explicitly about adapting states of perfection to present-day requirements. Pius XII endorses adaptation at the level of practical means and organization, but uses the document to police its limits — most notably by insisting that the "spirit of the founder as expressed in the Constitutions approved by the Church" is the objective norm that neither Superiors nor Chapters can arbitrarily override. Tradition and adaptation are held in tension with the founder's charism as the resolving principle.

**Holy See and centralization.** The document directly addresses the complaint that Rome is overly centralist. Pius XII's response is definitional: "centralization" in the pejorative sense (taking everything into Rome's hands) is disavowed, but the Holy See's role as "directing center of the Church" is non-negotiable. This is a characteristic Pius XII maneuver — conceding vocabulary while defending substance. The passage about avoiding contact with Rome (citing *Humani Generis*, 1950) suggests a specific disciplinary concern about communities operating too independently.

**Scriptural texture.** The address is unusually dense in Pauline citation: 1 Corinthians 12 (body of Christ), 1 Corinthians 13 (putting away childish things), Ephesians 4 (mature manhood), Colossians 3:14 ("charity is the bond of perfection"), Luke 22:26 (greatest as servant), Matthew 11:29 (come to me, all who labor). The Colossians verse becomes the homiletic close, giving the entire address a unifying liturgical frame: all adaptation, all obedience, all collaboration resolves into charity.

**Tone summary.** Authoritative but not harsh. The pope speaks as a teacher clarifying principles ("It is important, first of all, to recall…"; "We do not intend to enter into details"), not as a prosecutor. Objections to obedience are characterized as arising from "misinterpretation" or "practical errors" — not malice. The emotional register is one of encouragement toward an ideal already being pursued, with gentle correctives on specific deviations.
