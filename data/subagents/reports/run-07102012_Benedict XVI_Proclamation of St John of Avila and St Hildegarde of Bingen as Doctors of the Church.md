# Proclamation of St John of Avila and St Hildegarde of Bingen as "Doctors of the Church"

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The New Evangelization — understood as re-proposing the Christian faith to a dechristianized world — requires holiness, conversion, and the witness of saints as its indispensable engine.
- **Summary:** Delivered at the opening of the Thirteenth Ordinary General Assembly of the Synod of Bishops (coinciding with the launch of the Year of Faith), the homily frames the New Evangelization as the Church's urgent pastoral priority. Benedict distinguishes it from both ordinary parish evangelization and the *Missio ad Gentes*, locating it specifically toward baptized Catholics who have drifted from the faith. He draws on the newly proclaimed Doctors — St John of Avila (reform, priestly formation, scripture) and St Hildegard of Bingen (prophecy, creation, music, feminine spiritual authority) — as paradigmatic evangelizers. The crisis of marriage is presented as inseparable from the crisis of faith, and personal conversion/reconciliation with God is named as the essential precondition for any outward evangelization.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to (1) the dechristianization of historically Christian regions, (2) the crisis of marriage ("going through a profound crisis" in "the oldest regions evangelized"), (3) the crisis of faith linked to that marriage crisis, and (4) the need for a renewed impulse given by Vatican II — marking the fiftieth anniversary of its opening.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) The deliberations of the Synodal Assembly over the three weeks following the homily; (2) the broader Year of Faith; (3) improved formation of priests, religious, and laity (echoing St John of Avila); (4) a sincere desire for conversion and reconciliation as the precondition for evangelization; (5) placing marriage as not merely an object but a "subject" of the New Evangelization.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the Church's universal missionary mandate and the internal hemorrhage of baptized Catholics who live "without reference to the Christian life." A secondary tension exists between the glory of holiness (saints as evangelizers) and the "fragility, even sin" of many Christians that actively obstructs evangelization. The text also notes — without resolving — the tension between the indissolubility of marriage as Gospel sign and the "painful reality of many marriages which, unhappily, end badly."

## Unstructured

The homily operates on several registers simultaneously, knitting together a programmatic opening address, a liturgical reflection on scripture (marriage in Genesis/Mark, the suffering Christ in Hebrews), and two hagiographical portraits — all in service of a single thesis: the New Evangelization is the defining challenge of the present moment, and it proceeds only from genuine holiness and personal conversion.

**Flow of the argument:** Benedict opens by situating the occasion (Synod opening + Year of Faith + Vatican II anniversary), then anchors everything in the person of Jesus Christ — "in every time and place, evangelization always has as its starting and finishing points Jesus Christ." He distinguishes the three modes of evangelization (ordinary, *Missio ad Gentes*, New Evangelization) before presenting the new Doctors as historical exemplars of evangelical dynamism. The marriage section follows as a case study in how spiritual crisis and social crisis interlock. The saints/holiness section then offers the positive prescription. The homily closes with a call for conversion as the sine qua non for credible outward witness.

**Tone:** Predominantly authoritative-pastoral. The address to "Dear Brother Bishops" signals a collegial register, but the programmatic framing ("this theme reflects a programmatic direction for the life of the Church") gives it institutional weight. The language of "crisis" (faith, marriage) is frank without being alarmist; the overall mood is one of sober confidence.

**Notable framing:** 
- Marriage is explicitly called "a Gospel in itself, a Good News for the world of today" — a striking rhetorical move that elevates a sacrament to an evangelical instrument.
- The two new Doctors are introduced not as historical curiosities but as "radiant figures" whose charisma illuminates the present task: John of Avila for priestly reform and scriptural depth; Hildegard for prophetic discernment, love of creation, and spiritual authority as a woman — the latter implicitly presenting feminine holiness as equally central to the Church's evangelical tradition.
- The phrase "the Church exists to evangelize" is stated without qualification, centering mission as the Church's constitutive purpose rather than an optional program.
- The text never frames the New Evangelization as primarily sociological or institutional; it is consistently grounded in grace, the Holy Spirit, and the example of the saints — signaling that structural reforms are subordinate to spiritual renewal.
- Blessed John Paul II is named at the close as a paradigm of the New Evangelization, linking the present Synod to the pontificate that coined the term.
