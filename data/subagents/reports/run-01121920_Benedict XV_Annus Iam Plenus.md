# Annus Iam Plenus

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Social
- **Theme (one sentence):** A renewed appeal for charitable donations to relieve the hunger and suffering of children in Central Europe in the aftermath of war, framed around the approaching feast of Christmas.
- **Summary:** Benedict XV writes to bishops one year after a prior encyclical appeal for children suffering hunger and destitution in Central Europe. He reports that while some charitable response was generated, conditions have not improved and in some regions have worsened due to renewed conflict, civil collapse, and ongoing scarcity. He calls again for a specific collection of alms — to be taken up on the Feast of the Holy Innocents (28 December) or the preceding Sunday — directing particular attention to the children of wealthier parts of the world as potential donors to their suffering peers, and pledges 100,000 Italian lire from his own resources.
- **Response to:** The continued and worsening humanitarian crisis among children of Central Europe following the war; the inadequacy of the previous year's relief effort (which "has perhaps neither succeeded in reaching those parts of Europe where necessity pressed hard, nor, where help was given, has it always been adequate to the actual need"); renewed outbreaks of war in some regions; civil collapse and "frightful and disgraceful massacres" that have rendered families destitute.
- **Prescriptive towards:** A concrete charitable collection on the Feast of the Holy Innocents or the preceding Sunday; engagement of children in wealthier cities as donors; direction of funds either to the Pope or to the "Save the Children Fund"; cultivation by parents of "sentiments of humanity and holy compassion" in their children through this occasion.
- **Tension:** The tension between the prosperity and comfort experienced by children in wealthier parts of Europe and the destitution of children in war-affected Central Europe. A secondary tension runs between the urgency of physical need (hunger, disease, death) and the spiritual occasion of Christmas — the document attempts to resolve this by framing material charity as the most fitting response to the birth of Christ.

## Unstructured

*Annus Iam Plenus* is a short, pastorally direct document — more a letter of practical instruction than a doctrinal encyclical. Its argument is linear and emotionally driven rather than philosophically developed.

**Flow of argument:** The letter opens with an accounting of the previous year's appeal: money was collected, the "Save the Children Fund" was praised, yet conditions in Central Europe remain dire or have worsened. This backward look serves to establish both the legitimacy of the cause and the insufficiency of what has already been done, creating the grounds for a second appeal. The middle section turns to the Christmas occasion as both rhetorical opportunity and theological motivation — Christ born in poverty, "the House of Bread," the Divine Infant who "being rich became poor" — and specifically addresses wealthier children as natural partners in charity to their suffering peers. The letter closes with practical directives: diocesan announcements, a specified collection date, and Benedict's own personal pledge of 100,000 lire.

**Key themes:**
- *Universal fatherhood and non-discriminatory charity:* The appeal explicitly extends to children "of whatsoever nationality they may be," grounded in the principle that all bear "the image of God." This universalism is named explicitly and is rhetorically central to the call.
- *Children as both recipients and agents of charity:* Unusually, the document addresses not only bishops and their flocks in general but specifically invites children in prosperous cities to act as donors. The image of "desolate children... straining suppliant hands" to "happier children" who might share their savings is vivid and deliberately child-to-child in its framing.
- *Christmas as the theological ground for material relief:* The document weaves the Nativity narrative throughout — Bethlehem as "House of Bread," the Infant Jesus as one "who became poor," the shepherds receiving blessing in return for their gifts. Charitable giving is cast not merely as humanitarian duty but as the fitting and most acceptable offering to the Child Jesus at Christmas.
- *Institutional acknowledgment:* Benedict explicitly names and commends the "Save the Children Fund" (a non-Catholic, secular humanitarian organisation), which is a notable gesture of cross-institutional cooperation embedded in a papal document.

**Tone:** The document is pastoral and emotionally warm rather than authoritative or doctrinal. It is written in the register of a father appealing to his children rather than a sovereign issuing commands. The use of rhetorical questions ("And shall we think that these last are endowed with such a spirit as to be unwilling...?"), the evocation of maternal grief ("no mother who should look upon her little ones with weeping eyes"), and the image of the smiling Infant Jesus rewarding charitable children all signal a deliberate pastoral affect aimed at moving hearts rather than defining doctrine.

**Notable framing:** The document twice invokes "universal fatherhood" — once for the Pope ("the universal fatherhood which it is Our office to sustain") and once for God ("the common father of so many innocent children") — creating a nested structure in which papal charity mirrors and channels divine charity. The pledge of personal funds at the end reinforces the lead-by-example logic explicitly stated: "after exhorting the faithful by Our words, We may stir their generosity by Our example."
