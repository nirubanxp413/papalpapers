# Super soliditate

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** A formal condemnation of a pamphlet that denied the supreme and divinely instituted authority of the Roman Pontiff over the universal Church, in defense of Petrine primacy as an article of Catholic faith.
- **Summary:** Pius VI responds to a pamphlet by Joseph Valentin Eybel titled *Quid est papa?* (1782), which had been reprinted and translated into multiple languages. The brief systematically refutes Eybel's claim that all bishops hold equal authority to the Pope and that the Church should be governed as a republic with the Pope serving merely as a directorial president who exhorts and corrects but does not hold supreme jurisdiction. Drawing on a chain of Church Fathers (Cyprian, Chrysostom, Jerome, Augustine, Ambrose, Bernard), ecumenical councils (Chalcedon, Fourth Lateran, Trent, Florence, Lyons), and even Gallican prelates, the Pope argues that the primacy of the Apostolic See is a divinely established dogma of continuous tradition. The document concludes by formally condemning and banning Eybel's pamphlet, imposing penalties of suspension *a Divinis* for clergy and excommunication for laity who read, retain, or reprint it.
- **Response to:** The spread and repeated reprinting of Eybel's pamphlet *Quid est papa?* (Vienna, 1782), which argued that any bishop is equal in power to the Pope, that the Church should be administered like a republic with the Pope as a mere president, that papal reservations (absolutions, dispensations, benefices, appeals) are unjust usurpations of episcopal rights, and that the "rock" of Matthew 16 refers to Peter's faith or confession rather than his person and office.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The faithful are directed to reject Eybel's pamphlet, remain united to the Apostolic See as "the mother and teacher of all Christians," and understand that challenge to the Roman Pontiff's supreme authority necessarily destabilizes legitimate episcopal succession throughout the world. Booksellers, printers, and all persons are ordered to surrender any copy of the pamphlet to local Ordinaries under penalty of censure.
- **Tension:** The core tension is between two ecclesiological models: (1) a hierarchical-monarchical Church in which supreme jurisdiction flows from Christ through Peter to the Pope and only derivatively to bishops by ecclesiastical right, versus (2) a collegial-republican model in which all bishops receive equal power directly from Christ and the Pope functions only as a coordinating figurehead. The document also surfaces a tension between the continuity of patristic tradition (which the Pope claims unambiguously supports primacy) and the selective, polemical use of the same Fathers by opponents to support episcopal parity.

## Unstructured

**Occasion and adversary.** The document is almost entirely reactive. Eybel is named explicitly and repeatedly; the Pope describes him as someone "already too well known for pamphlets condemned long ago" (*già troppo noto per opuscoli condannati da tempo*). The Pope had initially chosen not to condemn the 1782 pamphlet, hoping it would fade into obscurity, but the decision by Eybel's supporters to reprint and translate it into multiple languages (including vernacular Greek) forced action. The brief is thus as much a disciplinary instrument as a doctrinal one.

**Doctrinal argument structure.** The document proceeds through a long catena of authorities marshaled against Eybel's specific claims. The Pope enumerates Eybel's errors at length in §4 before systematically dismantling them: on the "rock" passage (§9, Augustine and Ambrose); on the equality of apostolic power (§§6–16); on the illegitimacy of papal reservations (§14, Council of Trent); on indulgences (§15, Council of Constance/Martin V). The cumulative effect is an exhaustive display of doctrinal consensus across fifteen centuries.

**Tone: authoritative and polemical.** The document is entirely authoritative rather than pastoral. There is no consolation, encouragement, or appeal to the faithful's piety except instrumentally. The Pope's language toward Eybel is harsh: "wretched writer" (*infelice scrittore*), "reckless and blind temerity" (*precipitosa e cieca temerarietà*), "shameless calumny" (*impudente calunnia*). The pastoral note, when it appears (§22–23), is framed in terms of protecting souls from being led astray — not as a call to renewal but as justification for condemnation.

**Key framing device: the Petrine unity principle.** The document's central theological claim is that the Roman See is not merely the first in honor but the principle of unity itself: "Christ wished that the bond of unity be held firm and tight in this Principate of the Apostolic See." Therefore, any attack on the See is ipso facto an attack on the unity of the Church — and by extension on Christ's design. This framing allows the Pope to characterize Eybel's ecclesiological argument not as a legitimate theological opinion but as an assault comparable to that of ancient heretics.

**Use of Gallican witnesses.** Strategically notable is the Pope's deployment of Gallican authorities against Eybel (§§17–20). Eybel had invoked Gallican doctors as supporters of episcopal independence; Pius counters by citing Caesarius of Arles, Avitus of Vienne, Hincmar of Reims, Ivo of Chartres, and the 1681 Gallican assembly itself, all attesting to the primacy of the Apostolic See. The 1681 Gallican prelates' statement is quoted verbatim: "He is the head of the Church, the centre of unity; he has over us the primacy of authority and jurisdiction... Whoever would dissent from this truth would be schismatic, even heretic." This co-optation of the Gallicans is a pointed rhetorical move, depriving Eybel of one of his anticipated support bases.

**Penal conclusion.** The document closes not with doctrinal synthesis but with a detailed juridical structure: condemnation of the pamphlet as containing propositions that are "false, scandalous, rash, injurious, schism-inducing, erroneous, heresy-inducing, heretical, and others already condemned by the Church." The censures are automatic (*ipso facto*), require no further declaration, and are reserved to the Pope and his successors for absolution — with the sole exception that any confessor may absolve the excommunication *in articulo mortis*. This level of juridical precision signals that the document functions primarily as a formal act of the Roman Inquisition rather than a teaching letter.
