# Gravissimum supremi

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** Sacred Missions — organized campaigns of itinerant preachers sent throughout the dioceses — are presented as the essential and proven remedy for the widespread moral corruption and religious ignorance afflicting the Kingdom of Naples, especially in remote, mountainous areas.
- **Summary:** Writing to the bishops of the Kingdom of Naples on 8 September 1745, Benedict XIV observes that many people, particularly in rural and mountainous regions far from episcopal centers, are living lives devoid of virtue and Christian practice. Drawing on his own long administrative experience (as Promoter of the Faith, Secretary to the Sacred Congregation for the Council of Trent, and as bishop of Ancona and Bologna), and citing the example of canonized saints who promoted missions, he orders a coordinated programme of Sacred Missions across all the dioceses of the kingdom. He assigns organizational authority to Cardinal Spinello, Archbishop of Naples, enlists the Dominicans and Jesuits for more distant regions, requests the cooperation of King Charles of the Two Sicilies, and instructs bishops themselves to make personal pastoral visits rather than remaining at their seats.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to (1) reports of moral dissolution — "parecchi erano smarriti, soprattutto in montagna… trascorrono una vita lontana da ogni virtù" — reaching the pope through the complaints of the faithful; (2) bishops' negligence in visiting their own dioceses and in summoning missionaries; and (3) a broader pattern the pope observed in his prior administrative roles, where dioceses suffering from "costumi corrotti" were failing to call upon missionary help.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document is directed toward a concrete pastoral campaign: systematic Sacred Missions throughout Neapolitan dioceses, coordinated by Cardinal Spinello, staffed by Vincentians, Pii Operai, Dominicans, and Jesuits, and supplied with extraordinary faculties from the Holy See. It also anticipates (and warns against) the possibility that bishops will remain inactive at their seats while populations remain unreached, threatening "provvedimenti della Provvidenza Apostolica" for such negligence.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the duty of bishops to tend to their flocks and their apparent failure to do so — especially for populations "molto separate dalla Città" who live without adequate instruction in faith and morals. A secondary tension exists between the scale of moral corruption ("molte anime… siano precipitate verso la perdizione per mancanza del mezzo") and the limited capacity of individual bishops acting alone, which the pope resolves by calling in supplementary missionary forces.

## Unstructured

**Core argument and flow**

The letter opens by defining the twofold duty of the Supreme Apostolate: to spread the faith and to maintain it where it already exists — and then immediately narrows to the second duty, noting that maintaining faith requires not only correct doctrine but "principi che si devono manifestare coi fatti per mostrare vita e costumi consentanei alla Religione cristiana." The pope quickly establishes the problem: far from episcopal centres (especially in mountainous areas), people are living corrupt lives that invite divine wrath. The rest of the letter is a sustained argument that Sacred Missions are the appropriate, time-tested, and now necessary instrument to correct this.

**Tone**

The tone is predominantly authoritative and directive rather than pastoral or consoling. Benedict XIV writes from a position of accumulated personal experience — he lists his prior roles at length (Promoter of the Faith, Secretary to the Congregation for the Council of Trent, bishop of Ancona, then Bologna for a decade) to establish his own credibility as a witness to the effectiveness of missions. This is not a speculative document; it is a practical order backed by evidence. There is nonetheless a note of pastoral urgency when he speaks of souls perishing "per mancanza del mezzo" (for lack of the means of instruction), and a note of sorrow ("scriviamo non senza lacrime e tristezza").

**Key framing devices**

1. **Historical precedent and saintly example:** The pope anchors his command in the practice of revered predecessors and canonized saints — Vincenzo de' Paoli (who founded the Congregation of Missionaries), Giovanni Francesco Regis (who personally crossed harsh mountains to instruct the ignorant), Saint Charles Borromeo (whose Oblates in Milan produced great fruit), and popes such as Benedict XIII. The implicit argument is: this is not innovation — it is a well-attested Catholic tradition.

2. **Scripture as mandate:** He cites Christ's sending of the apostles to preach (Mk 3:15), Paul's statement "Christ sent me not to baptize but to evangelize" (1 Cor 1:17), and the Acts of the Apostles on the priority of the word — to frame missionary preaching as a constitutive obligation, not an optional supplement.

3. **Practical logistics as spiritual substance:** Unusually for a document of this kind, Benedict XIV devotes considerable space to operational arrangements — Cardinal Spinello's coordinating role, the mobilization of Dominican and Jesuit provincials for the Sannio and Calabria, the granting of extraordinary faculties, the disinterested conduct expected of missionaries (no gifts, no vanity). By doing this he signals that the failure of past efforts was partly organizational and that spiritual renewal requires structural support.

4. **The king as auxiliary:** The invocation of King Charles of the Two Sicilies near the end is notable: the pope asks bishops to call on royal authority "if necessary" to enable missions to proceed. This frames civil power as a legitimate — even necessary — instrument of pastoral ends, while keeping the authority relationship clear (the king is a "Carissimo Figlio in Cristo" who assists, not directs).

5. **The bishop's personal presence:** The closing image of King Jehoshaphat (2 Chr 17; 19) traveling personally among his people is used to rebuke bishops who remain in their seats. The pope explicitly notes that he knows "per certo" this negligence has occurred and warns that apostolic remedies will be applied if it continues — a pointed pastoral threat.

**Notable absence:** The document makes no reference to specific heresies, Protestant movements, or doctrinal controversies. The problem is entirely one of moral laxity and pastoral neglect rather than heterodox belief — which explains why "Sacred Missions" (preaching, confession, example) rather than doctrinal clarification is the prescribed remedy.
