# Novae hae litterae

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Pope Pius VI issues a final canonical admonition — threatening excommunication within sixty days — against French clergy who accepted the Civil Constitution of the Clergy, consecrated or accepted illegitimate "intruded" bishops, and swore the civic oath, while simultaneously praising those who endured exile, imprisonment, and persecution in fidelity to the Holy See.
- **Summary:** Written as a follow-up to Pius VI's earlier letter of 13 April 1791, *Novae hae litterae* opens by distinguishing two responses to that prior admonition: joy at the many bishops, canons, professors, and laypeople who remained steadfast under severe persecution, and grief at those who persist in schism. The Pope catalogues the spread of the schism — from eight illicitly consecrated "intruded" bishops at the time of his first letter to the occupation of nearly all dioceses of France — condemns the deceptions used by the constitutional bishops (including the fabricated letter pretending papal approval and the work *Accord des vrais principes*), refutes the two main excuses offered for ignoring his earlier letter (alleged inauthenticity and lack of civil publication), and then announces a peremptory admonition: sixty days for the first warning, sixty more for the second. He names specific individuals — Charles-Maurice de Talleyrand, Bishop of Autun, as the principal author of the schism; the Archbishop of Sens; and the Bishops of Viviers and Orléans — alongside all intruded bishops, their vicars, and complicit pastors. He suspends formal excommunication out of paternal mercy but confirms earlier suspension penalties and warns that if repentance is not demonstrated he will proceed according to canonical norms. The letter closes with exhortations to the faithful and legitimate clergy to pray, fast, avoid all contact with intruders and their writings, and remain united with their legitimate pastors.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to: (1) the Civil Constitution of the Clergy, which the Pope had already called "the source and origin of all poisoned errors" in his April 1791 letter; (2) the consecration of "intruded" bishops without canonical election or mission; (3) the civic oath sworn by clergy; (4) the spread of the schism from eight intruded bishops to near-total occupation of French dioceses; (5) the deceptions circulated by constitutional bishops — specifically their denial of the authenticity of papal letters and their insistence on a "civil form" of publication as a precondition for obedience; (6) the publication of *Accord des vrais principes de l'Eglise... sur la Constitution Civile du Clergé de France* (Paris, 1791), which the Pope calls a collection of "erroneous, schismatic and heretical" propositions; (7) letters sent to him by intruded bishops feigning communion with the Holy See.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates and directs toward: (1) the retraction by schismatic clergy of the civic oath and their sacrilegious consecrations, modelled on historical examples of retraction (the monks and bishops at the Council of Rimini, the French monk Leporius, the priest Lucidius, Jean Gerson, Pierre de Marca, and Archbishop Fénelon); (2) the formal excommunication and public notification of schismatics if they do not comply within the allotted sixty-day periods; (3) the re-establishment of legitimate pastors in their sees; (4) the avoidance by the faithful of all contact with intruders and their writings; (5) intensified prayer, penance, and fasting by the faithful.
- **Tension:** The document names or clearly implies the following tensions: (1) *Spiritual authority vs. temporal power* — the National Assembly legislated on church structure ("Costituzione civile del clero") and the Pope insists that such civil legislation cannot govern "causa maior" spiritual matters, quoting Valentinian Augustus to the effect that papal authority in the Church needs no imperial sanction; (2) *Legitimate hierarchy vs. intruded hierarchy* — throughout the letter the Pope contrasts "pastori legittimi" (legitimate pastors) with "intrusi" (intruders) who lack canonical election, ordination, and mission; (3) *Pastoral mercy vs. canonical justice* — the Pope explicitly wrestles with this throughout §18–27, comparing the Church to a mother who cannot forget her rebellious children ("come una madre non può dimenticarsi del suo bambino"), deferring excommunication out of mercy while insisting severity is necessary if repentance does not follow; (4) *Feigned unity vs. real schism* — the document devotes considerable attention (§12–16) to the constitutional bishops' pretence of communion with Rome, comparing their strategy to that of Photius writing to Pope Nicholas, Luther writing to Leo X, and Pier Paolo Vergerio writing to Julius III.

## Unstructured

**Overall argument and flow:**

The letter is structured as a formal canonical document moving through three broad phases: (a) acknowledgement and praise, (b) condemnation and doctrinal refutation, (c) final warning and call to repentance.

§§1–6 open with expressions of joy. The Pope thanks those who endured exile and imprisonment ("alcuni fra voi, tollerando con animo invitto l'esilio fuori dalle vostre chiese"), quotes Saint Leo the Great on shared suffering, praises the canons, professors of the Sorbonne, rectors of seminaries, and laypeople, and notes with measured satisfaction the resignations of some intruded bishops — though he tempers even that joy because the intruder of Rouen abdicated without retracting the sacrament or detesting his error.

§§7–16 pivot to sorrow and then to doctrinal argument. The Pope names the four bishops who "persevered in error" — Autun, Sens, Viviers, Orléans — and invokes Saint Leo's principle that negligent rectors bear the sins of their subjects. He then describes the catastrophic spread of the schism: from eight illicitly consecrated bishops to near-total occupation of French dioceses (§8), using Saint Athanasius's lament over Alexandria as a rhetorical touchstone ("In tutta la terra non s'è mai udito nulla di simile"). He quotes an ancient Roman synod condemning bishops who invaded other dioceses and applies it forcefully to the current situation. He characterises the constitutional bishops as a "profane and sacrilegious" regime that corrupts the sacraments, drives out legitimate pastors, and subjects the Church's divine authority to temporal power (§11). §§12–16 then expose the intruders' two main strategies: denial of the authenticity of papal letters, and demand for civil publication as a prerequisite for obedience — both dismissed as pretexts to enable the schism to grow unchecked.

§§17–18 transition to the impending response. The Pope states he *could* have excommunicated the contumacious clergy already (more than eleven months have passed since the first warning) but has withheld the sentence out of paternal mercy and in hope of repentance, confirming only the earlier suspension.

§§19–26 constitute the formal canonical admonition: a numbered list of those warned — consecrators (Talleyrand and two co-consecrators named explicitly), all intruded bishops, the four legitimate bishops who swore the oath, intruded pastors and vicars, and more broadly all who promoted or swore the Civil Constitution. The two 60-day windows are set out.

§§27–30 close with pastoral exhortation — the Pope invokes the "viscere di Gesù Cristo," historical examples of French retractors, and appeals to legitimate bishops, canons, faithful laity to pray, fast, and maintain unity with their legitimate pastors.

**Tone:**

The document is predominantly *authoritative* and juridical — it quotes canon law, synodal decisions, and papal predecessors extensively — but it is interwoven with a notably *pastoral* and even *anguished* register: the Pope explicitly states he writes "non senza gran pianto e lamento" and uses the maternal imagery ("come una madre non può dimenticarsi del suo bambino") to explain the delay in excommunication. This tension between authority and mercy is itself thematically central to the letter, not merely rhetorical.

**Notable framing:**

The Pope consistently frames the schism not as a political dispute but as a spiritual catastrophe provoked by "filosofi innovatori" who "costituivano la maggior parte dell'Assemblea Nazionale" (§27) — making the revolutionary philosophical current, rather than the state as such, the deeper target of his criticism. Historical precedents (the Arian controversy, the Council of Rimini, Photius, Luther, Fénelon) are deployed throughout to situate the crisis within an unbroken pattern of schism and retraction, implying both that the current crisis is recognisable and that resolution through penitence is genuinely available. The letter ends not with condemnation but with a blessing and the hope that "la mano di Dio non si arresterà sopra gli intrusi e gli scismatici."
