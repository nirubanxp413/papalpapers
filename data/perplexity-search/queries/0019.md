## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Ad Beatissimi Apostolorum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The encyclical calls for the restoration of Christian principles — especially brotherly love, respect for authority, and detachment from material goods — as the only true remedy for the concurrent catastrophes of world war and deep social unrest.
- **Summary:** Issued at the very opening of Benedict XV's pontificate in November 1914, this encyclical surveys the condition of the world and the Church and finds four intertwined causes of societal breakdown: the absence of mutual charity among people, disregard for legitimate authority, class hatred and the conflict between rich and poor, and excessive desire for material wealth. Against the backdrop of the First World War, the pope argues that these social evils share a common root in the abandonment of Christian teaching, and he calls on bishops, clergy, and all Catholics to restore Christian fraternity, ecclesiastical unity, doctrinal orthodoxy, and obedience to authority as the path to both worldly peace and eternal salvation.
- **Response to:** The text explicitly responds to the outbreak of what it calls "the most disastrous war" then raging across Europe and the world (§3–4); the general collapse of respect for authority in states and families (§9); class conflict between proletariat and property-owners, with strikes and civil tumults (§12); the spread of Modernism, which Benedict renews the condemnation of from Pius X (§25); internal Catholic divisions caused by the use of partisan labels distinguishing one group of Catholics from another (§24); and the unresolved "Roman Question" — the loss of the Papal States and the Pope's temporal freedom (§31).
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates and directs toward: the conclusion of peace among nations and a negotiated resolution to the war (§4, §30, §32); the restoration of brotherly love as the guiding principle of Benedict's entire pontificate (§8); renewed obedience by clergy to their bishops and by Catholics to the Holy See (§28–29); the suppression of Modernism and adherence to traditional Catholic doctrine (§25); the growth and faithful discipline of Catholic associations (§26); proper formation of the clergy (§27); and a resolution of the Pope's temporal status (§31).
- **Tension:** The central spiritual tension is between the Christian ideal of universal brotherhood ("All you are brethren," Matt. xxiii.8, cited in §6) and the actual reality of nations at war and classes at each other's throats (§7, §12). A related social tension runs between the principle that all authority derives from God and therefore demands obedience (§9–10) and the observable fact that rulers have separated their governance from religion, thereby undermining the very authority they rely upon (§11). There is also a tension within the Church between the unity Catholics are called to and the factional divisions — including Modernism, partisan labels, and priestly insubordination — that actually prevail (§22–25, §28).

## Unstructured

**Opening and scope.** The document opens with Benedict reflecting on his sudden elevation to the papacy and his immediate pastoral impulse toward "the whole human race" (§1–2). This universal frame — humanity as one flock — is the theological premise from which every subsequent argument flows. It is notably not addressed to secular rulers first but to bishops, yet it repeatedly appeals beyond the Church to "those in whose hands are placed the fortunes of nations" (§4).

**The war and its cause.** Paragraphs 3–4 describe the war in vivid, almost apocalyptic terms, citing Matthew xxiv ("nation shall rise against nation"). Benedict does not diagnose the war's political or national causes; instead, he locates it within a deeper moral and spiritual failure. He makes this explicit in §5: "the absence from the relations of men of mutual love … the authority of rulers is held in contempt … injustice reigns in relations between the classes … the striving for transient and perishable things" — all four are symptoms whose root is the abandonment of Christian principles.

**Structure of the argument.** The body of the encyclical (§5–18) is organized around these four causes, treated in sequence: (1) lack of charity / brotherly love (§6–8); (2) contempt for authority (§9–11); (3) class hatred and social conflict (§12–13); (4) greed and materialism (§14–18). Each section moves from diagnosis to prescription, and each prescription converges on restoring Christian teaching. The rhetorical logic is cumulative: the four social diseases are presented as a single systemic failure of Christianity's removal from public life.

**Tone.** The tone is simultaneously pastoral and authoritative. Benedict uses the first person plural ("We") throughout in the papal manner, but the emotional register is one of sorrow and paternal urgency rather than condemnation. He describes himself as "filled with bitter sorrow" (§3) and frames his appeals as coming from "a friend and a father" (§4). When he does issue commands — renewing the condemnation of Modernism (§25), insisting on obedience to bishops (§28) — the language sharpens noticeably.

**The Modernism section.** Paragraph 25 stands out as unusually direct. Benedict explicitly inherits and renews Pius X's condemnation of Modernism as "the synthesis of all heresies," and goes further by warning against the "spirit of Modernism" — a preference for novelty over tradition in liturgy, institutions, and personal piety. He invokes the maxim "Let there be no innovation; keep to what has been handed down" as a near-absolute rule in matters of faith, softened only by the formula "Old things, but in a new way" for disciplinary matters. This sets a clear doctrinal and cultural posture for his pontificate.

**Catholic unity and internal discipline.** Paragraphs 22–29 turn from the world to the internal life of the Church. Benedict is concerned about Catholics who use "qualifying appellations" to distinguish factions (§24) — implicitly referencing political-religious labels like "liberal Catholic" or "integral Catholic" in use at the time. He insists that "Catholicism does not admit of more or less." He also addresses priestly insubordination to bishops, reflecting his awareness that even within the clergy the spirit of independence that plagues civil society has made inroads.

**The Roman Question.** Paragraph 31 briefly but pointedly raises the unresolved status of the papacy since the loss of the Papal States, framing it not as a political grievance but as a structural impediment to the Church's freedom that also harms the peace of nations. Benedict explicitly says he renews the protests of his predecessors "not by human interest, but by the sacredness of our office."

**Closing.** The encyclical ends (§30–32) by returning to its opening subject — the war — and by invoking the intercession of the Virgin Mary and the God who "makes peace." The circularity is deliberate: the document begins and ends with the same longing for peace, framing all the internal Church concerns of the middle sections as ultimately ordered toward that same end.

**Notable framing.** The most distinctive rhetorical move is the claim that the ongoing world war is not merely a political catastrophe but the visible symptom of a civilizational spiritual failure — and therefore that the Church's internal renewal and the world's external peace are parts of a single project. This gives even the technical sections on clergy formation and episcopal obedience an urgency beyond ecclesiastical housekeeping.
