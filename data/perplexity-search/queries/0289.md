## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Auctorem fidei

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** A papal condemnation of 85 specific propositions drawn from the Acts and Decrees of the diocesan Synod of Pistoia (1786), defending both orthodox doctrines on grace and the sacraments and the hierarchical authority of Rome against episcopalist, Jansenist, and Gallican errors.
- **Summary:** Pius VI opens by invoking the image of Christ as "author of the faith" sustaining the Church against persistent conspiracies, then narrates how Bishop Scipione de' Ricci of Pistoia-Prato convened a diocesan synod that, despite appearing reformist, disseminated condemned doctrines. After an extended review process involving multiple cardinals, bishops, and theologians, the Pope enumerates 85 propositions from the Synod with individual censures (ranging from "heretical" to "rash," "erroneous," "schismatic," or "pernicious"). The condemned propositions fall into two broad clusters: (1) ecclesiological — improper attribution of authority to the lay community, bishops, or national councils at the expense of the Roman Pontiff and apostolic constitutions; and (2) theological/doctrinal — errors on grace, original sin, immortality, the sacraments, penance, indulgences, sacred images, religious orders, liturgical practice, and oaths. The document closes with a blanket prohibition of the Synod's Acts and related works, threatening excommunication for non-compliance.
- **Response to:** The text explicitly responds to: (a) the Acts and Decrees of the Synod of Pistoia (1785), which the Pope says Ricci assembled under the influence of "maestri di perversa sapienza"; (b) the revival of previously condemned doctrines associated with Bajus, Quesnel, Luther, Calvin, Peter of Osma, Wicleff, and the Jansenist current; (c) the Gallican Declaration of the Assembly of 1682, which the Synod illicitly incorporated into its decrees on faith; (d) Ricci's personal refusal of the Pope's invitation to come to Rome for discussion.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs all faithful, patriarchs, archbishops, bishops, and inquisitors to: refuse and suppress the condemned propositions; accept the hierarchical prerogatives of the Roman Pontiff and General Councils over diocesan synods; maintain received liturgical practice, sacramental discipline, and the traditional system of religious orders; and understand church authority as divinely constituted rather than delegated upward from the community.
- **Tension:** (1) Papal/apostolic supremacy vs. episcopal autonomy — the text repeatedly condemns the Synod's claim that bishops receive all necessary governing rights directly from Christ, independent of superior hierarchical power. (2) Apostolic continuity vs. "reform" — the Pope frames Ricci's changes not as renewal but as disruptive "novità" introduced under the pretext of returning to antiquity, while actual approved ancient practice is being discarded. (3) Authoritative doctrinal definition vs. ambiguous synodal language — a significant structural concern is the Synod's use of equivocal wording that conceals condemned meanings: "l'errore, nascosto fra senso e senso, s'insinui negli animi più facilmente." (4) Roman universalism vs. national/Gallican particularism — the adoption of the Gallican Articles of 1682 is treated as a jurisdictional and doctrinal attack on the Apostolic See.

## Unstructured

**Overall shape of the argument.** The document has a juridical-narrative structure rather than a theological treatise structure. Paragraphs 1–10 are an extended preamble: Pius invokes the Pauline image of enduring contradiction as Christ endured it, then recounts the story of Ricci's appointment, betrayal of his oath of obedience, convening of the Synod, the ensuing scandal among "persons of authentic and pious religion," the lengthy review process, and the Pope's patient pastoral attempts to bring Ricci back in person. Only in paragraph 11 does the document pivot to its operative core: the enumeration and condemnation of 85 propositions with censures. The closing paragraphs add supplementary condemnations (the Trinity formulations, the Gallican declaration, the Louvain and Utrecht materials) and promulgate the legal prohibition.

**Doctrinal clusters condensed.** The 85 condemned propositions cluster into roughly nine topic areas, as signposted by the document's own headings:
1. Church authority and the transmission of power (Props. I–XII): power does not flow from the community upward to pastors; the Pope is not a "ministerial head" in the Synod's reductive sense; bishops cannot override universal discipline unilaterally; diocesan synods cannot sit in judgment over apostolic constitutions.
2. Papal peace/historical calumnies (Props. XIII–XIV): the Jansenist "Peace of Clement IX" interpretation is condemned as dishonoring that pope.
3. Grace, original sin, and nature (Props. XV–XXIV): errors recalling Bajanism and semi-Pelagianism — the state of innocence was not natural but gratuitous; immortality was a gift not a condition; the law does not make people transgressors by impossibility; grace is not merely illuminating without also moving the will; there is no intermediate affection between dominating concupiscence and dominating charity.
4. Sacraments — baptism, Eucharist (Props. XXVII–XXXIII): conditional baptism, the essential role of participation in the Eucharistic sacrifice, the necessity of explicitly naming transubstantiation, the application of Mass fruits, single-altar practice, liturgy in the vernacular.
5. Penance, absolution, censures (Props. XXXIV–L): orderly penance, authority to absolve, the binding force of excommunication (not merely external), ipso facto sentences, the discipline of reserved cases.
6. Holy Orders and clerical discipline (Props. LI–LVII): ordination by saltum, titles of ordination, requiring baptismal innocence for priesthood, the legitimacy of Mass stipends and stola offerings, minor orders.
7. Marriage and betrothal (Props. LVIII–LX): betrothal is not a merely civil act; the Church, not the state, has original authority to establish matrimonial impediments.
8. Worship, devotions, images, feasts, oaths (Props. LXI–LXXV): adoration of Christ's humanity, devotion to the Sacred Heart of Jesus, sacred images and their titles (including the Virgin's titles), the use of set numbers of prayers, the value of Missions and Exercises, liturgical Latin, Scripture reading obligations, devotional works already condemned (Quesnel), feast day transfers by bishops, and clerical oaths.
9. Religious orders (Props. LXXX–LXXXIV): the Synod's three principles that monastic life is incompatible with the hierarchy, that the variety of orders causes confusion, and that private monasteries are politically dangerous "small monarchies" — all condemned as false and calumnious against approved orders and their founders.
10. National councils (Prop. LXXXV): a national council cannot resolve controversies of faith with unchallengeable judgment; this claim is condemned as schismatic and heretical.

**Tone.** The document is predominantly authoritative and juridical, not pastoral. The pastoral register appears only in the preamble (paragraphs 7–9), where Pius describes his reluctance to condemn and his earnest attempts to reconcile Ricci pastorally. Once the condemnations begin, the tone is formal, technical, and exhaustive. Each condemned proposition is given a precise censure-label (ERETICA, FALSA, TEMERARIA, SCANDALOSA, ERRONEA, SCISMATICA, etc.), sometimes in combinations of four or five terms. This style is a deliberate response to the Synod's own rhetorical ambiguity: the document explicitly criticizes innovators who hide error under "fraudolenti giri di parole" and therefore counteracts with maximum definitional clarity.

**Notable framing devices.** The document deploys a consistent rhetoric of precedent: almost every condemnation cites the Synod's deviation not from abstract principle but from a named prior condemnation (Luther's articles, Bajus, Quesnel, the Council of Trent, previous papal constitutions). This frames the Synod not as a genuine reform effort but as a systematic recycling of already-condemned heresies — "resuscitare errori già condannati." The opening appeal to the Letter to the Hebrews ("Tenendo fisso lo sguardo su Gesù, autore e perfezionatore della fede") frames the entire exercise as fidelity under persecution, with the Pope himself as the suffering but resolute guardian of the apostolic deposit.

**The Gallican dimension.** The supplementary condemnation of the Gallican Declaration of 1682 (already condemned by Innocent XI and Alexander VIII) embedded in the Synod's decree on faith is treated with particular gravity — it is described as "insidiously inserted" to lend the Synod's errors the authority of the French Church, which itself is said to have been slandered by the association.
