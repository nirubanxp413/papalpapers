# Inter Praecipuas

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document condemns biblical societies and the distribution of unapproved vernacular Scripture translations as instruments of heresy and religious indifferentism, threatening both the faith of Catholics and civil order.
- **Summary:** Gregory XVI addresses all Catholic bishops to warn them against biblical societies—primarily founded in England and spreading widely—which publish and distribute vernacular translations of Scripture without ecclesiastical oversight, encouraging readers to interpret the Bible independently of Church authority and tradition. The document catalogues prior papal condemnations and Church legislation on this matter, then specifically condemns a new organization called the "Christian League" founded in New York in 1843, which it identifies as seeking to spread religious liberty (cast as indifference to religion) among Romans and Italians, with political as well as spiritual subversive intent. Bishops are directed to suppress these societies, confiscate unapproved Bibles, intensify preaching, and seek the recovery of those already seduced.
- **Response to:** The founding and spread of Protestant biblical societies distributing unapproved vernacular Bibles; prior heretical tactics of Lutherans, Calvinists, and Jansenists in distorting Scripture; the founding of the "Christian League" in New York (June 12, 1843) targeting Italian Catholics; repeated prior condemnations by Pius VII, Leo XII, and Pius VIII that had not fully suppressed the threat.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Continued vigilance and suppression of biblical societies by bishops; confiscation of unapproved vernacular Bibles and condemned books; intensified preaching and qualified public reading of Scripture under Church supervision; cooperation with civil authorities (especially Italian princes) to prevent the spread of religious liberty and its political consequences; pastoral outreach to recover those who have been seduced.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the Church's claimed role as the sole authoritative interpreter and distributor of Scripture versus the biblical societies' program of wide, unguided lay reading of vernacular Bibles. A second tension runs through the document between religious authority and "liberty of conscience," which the text frames as indifference to religion and a gateway to political sedition. A third tension is institutional: the Church insists it actively promotes Scripture knowledge through proper channels, while the societies falsely accuse it of suppressing Scripture.

## Unstructured

The document opens with an unambiguous polemical posture: biblical societies are described as "an army on the march" conspiring against Catholic truth. The argument flows in several recognizable moves:

**Historical grounding:** A substantial portion (paragraphs 3–6) is devoted to establishing that the Church has always promoted Scripture—through the Council of Trent, provincial synods, Benedict XIII's Roman Council, and ongoing episcopal reporting—and that restrictions on vernacular translations were always disciplinary responses to heretical abuse, not suppression of Scripture itself. This is clearly a defensive counter-narrative against the societies' charge that Rome keeps Scripture from the people.

**Heresy genealogy:** The document explicitly traces a lineage: Lutherans and Calvinists → Jansenists → biblical societies → Christian League. Each is shown to share the same core tactic—using Scripture, freed from Church interpretation, as a lever against Catholic doctrine and authority. The citation of Tertullian ("by artifice" and "perverse interpretation") gives this genealogy patristic legitimacy.

**The New York threat:** The most specific and urgent passage (paragraph 9) describes intelligence received about the Christian League founded in New York, with detailed knowledge of their strategy: targeting Italians at home and abroad, using poverty or novelty-seeking as recruitment vectors, distributing corrupt Bibles and Protestant Reformation histories (naming specific titles by Merle d'Aubigné and John Cric). This intelligence-report quality section gives the encyclical a notably pragmatic, almost administrative tone alongside the theological argument.

**Political dimension:** Gregory explicitly links religious indifferentism to political revolution (paragraphs 13–14), arguing that the Christian League itself admits that "liberty of conscience" is intended as a font of "political liberty." The appeal to Italian princes as natural allies against the societies rests on this logic: undermining Catholic unity destabilizes civil obedience. This political framing is stated explicitly by the text, not inferred.

**Tone:** The document is firmly authoritative throughout—it issues condemnations, confirms prior legislation, and commands bishops rather than merely advising them. There is a brief pastoral register in the closing paragraphs (paragraph 12's call to recover those seduced, paragraph 15's call to prayer), but the dominant mode is legislative and polemical. The document closes with a call to prayer to Mary "who has the power to end all heresies," giving the final word a devotional rather than triumphalist closure.

**Notable framing:** The phrase "religious liberty, or rather an insane desire for indifference concerning religion" is the document's most pointed rhetorical move—collapsing the Enlightenment concept of religious freedom into a pathological label. This framing positions any claim to individual scriptural interpretation as not merely theologically wrong but psychologically disordered and socially dangerous.
