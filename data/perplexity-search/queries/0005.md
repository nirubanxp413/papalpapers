## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The letter announces and justifies the newly corrected Greek Euchologion, using each of its four prefatory admonitions as occasions to defend the Roman primacy, correct sacramental abuses, and affirm that the Apostolic See preserves rather than abolishes legitimate Eastern liturgical rites.
- **Summary:** Benedict XIV writes to Eastern-rite Catholic clergy ("Uniates") to announce the completion of the corrected Greek Euchologion, printed by the Congregation for the Propagation of the Faith in 1754 after a process begun under Urban VIII. The letter proceeds through four admonitions placed at the front of the new edition: (1) priests must commemorate the Roman Pontiff—and their Catholic bishop or patriarch—in the Mass, but must omit schismatic or heretical hierarchs; (2) the faithful must be catechized about the difference between the Greater Entrance (unconsecrated gifts) and the entry of the Presanctified (consecrated), so that reverence shown at the former is not confused with adoration due only to the Eucharist; (3) Extreme Unction is to be administered only to the seriously ill, and the chrism requires only oil and balm (though additional spices remain licit); (4) Eastern prayers for ritual cleansing do not revive Mosaic legal obligations, provided they are performed as Church custom, not as observance of the abrogated old Law. Throughout, the Pope insists that the Apostolic See's intent is to retain every Eastern usage that contains no error, while eliminating interpolations and correcting misunderstandings.
- **Response to:** The document states it responds to: (a) long-standing errors and interpolations that had crept into successive printed editions of the Euchologion; (b) a process begun by Philip IV of Spain's petition to Urban VIII in 1631, carried through eighty-two sessions but never completed under previous popes; (c) specific disputed questions that arose in Benedict XIV's own Congregations (1745–1748) about the Greater Entrance rite, oblation by deacons, Extreme Unction for the healthy, and post-partum ritual; (d) Greek popular confusion between the Greater Entrance and the procession of the Presanctified; (e) schismatic interpolations (attributed in part to Theodore Balsamon) that introduced the sacramental formula into the reconciliation rite; (f) criticism by various authors (Arcudius, Le Brun, Tournefort) that the Greater Entrance invited "idolatry."
- **Prescriptive towards:** The letter is directed toward: (a) ensuring all Eastern-rite Catholic priests use the 1754 corrected edition exclusively and conform any future reprints to it; (b) active episcopal catechesis of laity about the non-consecrated nature of gifts at the Greater Entrance; (c) strict limitation of Extreme Unction to the gravely ill; (d) maintaining Eastern customs (greater entrance, deacon's oblation at the prothesis, post-partum rites, abstinence from blood and strangled meat) while explicitly grounding them in Church custom rather than Mosaic law; (e) continued effort to draw schismatics back to Catholic unity, framed as the underlying pastoral motive of the entire Euchologion project.
- **Tension:** Two overlapping tensions run through the text: (1) *Unity vs. diversity of rite* — the document consistently navigates between the Apostolic See's authority to correct and the commitment never to Latinize legitimate Eastern practices; the Pope repeatedly insists "no innovations should be made" even where some advisors urged abolition of contested ceremonies. (2) *Catholic communion vs. schism* — commemoration of the Roman Pontiff in the Mass is presented as the defining outward sign of being in or out of Catholic unity; the entire first admonition treats this liturgical act as a public profession of ecclesial allegiance, with the omission of the Pope's name read as a declaration of schism.

## Unstructured

**Overall shape of the argument.** The letter is organized around four admonitions placed in the preface of the corrected Euchologion, each of which the Pope elaborates at length. The document is less a theological treatise than an annotated promulgation: Benedict XIV explains why each admonition was inserted, rehearses the historical and patristic evidence on which it rests, and records the decisions of his Congregations (with dates and minutes cited precisely). The tone is a blend of the administrative and the pastoral — authoritative in its decrees, but consistently framed as care for Eastern Christians rather than imposition from outside.

**Commemoration of the Pope (Admonitions 1, §§9–28).** The longest section. Benedict marshals historical evidence — from the erasure of Pope Felix III's name by Acacius of Constantinople in 484, through the Councils of Lyons (1274) and Florence (1434), to letters of Peter of Antioch (1053) — to establish that commemoration of the Roman Pontiff in the diptychs is ancient Greek practice and that its omission was always the mark of schism, not a neutral liturgical variation. He also addresses whether kings and emperors (including infidel Turkish rulers) may be commemorated, concluding that the nature of prayer does not forbid it, and that Greek Catholics understood themselves as praying for Christian princes rather than for the Turks in practice. The rhetorical logic is cumulative: the commemoration is not a Latin imposition but a recovery of authentic Eastern practice disrupted by schism.

**The Greater Entrance (Admonition 2, §§29–43).** Benedict lays out the two entrances, explains the source of popular confusion (the Presanctified procession uses the same body postures with actually consecrated bread), and then systematically defends the retention of the Greater Entrance rite against those who wanted it abolished. The defence draws on the theology of intention (latria is determined by the mind, not by the outward act), on patristic precedent (Leo Allatius, St. Thomas, Suarez), and on pastoral prudence: abolition would be "extremely odious to the Greek church" and inconsistent with the Latin principle of preserving Eastern customs intact. The resolution — no change to the rite, but mandatory catechesis — is presented as mirroring the Council of Trent's approach to images (session 25): keep the practice, instruct the people.

**Extreme Unction and Chrism (Admonition 3, §§44–58).** This section is partly administrative (Extreme Unction only for the gravely ill; chrism valid with oil and balm even without additional herbs) and partly theological-historical, dealing at length with whether the formula "the sign of the gift of the Holy Spirit" used in reconciling apostates implies a repetition of Confirmation. Benedict reviews the scholarly debate at length and resolves it by reference to the Grottaferrata Euchologion (Cardinal Bessarion's manuscript), which does not include the sacramental formula in the reconciliation rite; the Congregation decreed conformity to that ancient manuscript.

**Ritual purity and the abrogation of Mosaic law (Admonition 4, §§59–75).** The final section addresses prayers in the Euchologion that invoke categories of clean and unclean (fouled wells, animals dying in a church, women after childbirth). The Pope distinguishes three successive considerations: (1) Mosaic food laws are abrogated and cannot be observed as such; (2) the Church may re-impose external disciplinary rules based on the same matters if done on its own authority; (3) Eastern abstinence from blood and strangled meat, and post-partum observances, fall into category (2) — they are Church custom rooted in apostolic tradition, not Judaizing. The section closes with an extended treatment of post-partum churching, noting the Greek practice (obligatory exclusion for forty days) differs from the Latin (advisory only) but need not be changed because it is performed with Christian prayers and intent, not as Mosaic legal obligation.

**Tone and framing.** Throughout, the register is *authoritative but irenic*. The Pope repeatedly positions the Apostolic See as the protector of Eastern rites against uninformed Latin critics who "condemn everything in the sacred Greek rites which they discover to be dissimilar and not in agreement with the Latin rite" (§7). He names scholars, cites manuscript sources with precision (Barberini codex, Grottaferrata Euchologion), and acknowledges competing scholarly opinions rather than simply decreeing. The closing paragraphs make explicit the letter's pastoral goal: to demonstrate the "real love" of the Apostolic See for Eastern Catholics and to sustain their "holy union" while hoping that schismatics will one day be drawn back.

**Notable framing device.** The document repeatedly uses the phrase "no innovations should be made" as the outcome of Congregational deliberation — applied to the Greater Entrance, to the deacon's oblation, to the post-partum rite. This rhetorical pattern enacts the letter's governing claim: the Roman correction process is not a Latinization but a conservative restoration of the most ancient Eastern sources, with additions and interpolations removed and nothing legitimate touched.
