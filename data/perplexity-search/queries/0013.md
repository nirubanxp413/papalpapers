# Responses to Certain Questions of the United States Conference of Catholic Bishops Concerning Artificial Nutrition and Hydration

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Artificial nutrition and hydration for patients in a vegetative state constitutes ordinary and proportionate care that is morally obligatory as an expression of the fundamental human dignity of every person.
- **Summary:** This document issues two formal responses from the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith, approved by Pope Benedict XVI, addressing questions posed by the United States Conference of Catholic Bishops regarding the moral status of artificial nutrition and hydration for patients in a "vegetative state." The first response affirms that providing food and water — even by artificial means — is in principle an ordinary and proportionate means of preserving life and therefore morally obligatory, provided it accomplishes its proper finality of nourishing and hydrating the patient. The second response affirms that even when physicians judge with moral certainty that a patient in a "permanent vegetative state" will never recover consciousness, withdrawal of nutrition and hydration is not permissible, because such a patient retains fundamental human dignity and is therefore owed ordinary and proportionate care.
- **Response to:** The document responds directly to questions submitted by the United States Conference of Catholic Bishops concerning the moral permissibility of withdrawing artificial nutrition and hydration from patients in vegetative or permanent vegetative states — particularly in cases where medical professionals judge recovery of consciousness to be impossible.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs Catholic moral practice toward maintaining that artificial nutrition and hydration must continue as long as it accomplishes its proper finality (hydration and nourishment), and that the permanent vegetative state diagnosis — even when made with moral certainty — does not justify withdrawal. It prescribes ongoing care as the default obligatory norm.
- **Tension:** The text reveals a tension between medical-clinical judgments (specifically the authority of "competent physicians" who judge with "moral certainty" that a patient will never recover consciousness) and the Church's moral teaching that fundamental human dignity persists regardless of such clinical prognosis. A secondary tension exists between the concept of "ordinary and proportionate" care — which the document affirms artificial nutrition and hydration typically constitutes — and circumstances where it may not accomplish its proper finality, which are acknowledged as legitimate exceptions.

## Unstructured

This is a terse, highly authoritative document — a formal magisterial response (responsio) rather than a pastoral letter or encyclical. The format itself signals its nature: two precisely worded questions followed by two single-word answers ("Yes" and "No"), each with a brief explanatory commentary. There is no pastoral warmth, narrative, or extended theological development; the tone is that of a juridical or doctrinal ruling.

The central conceptual move is the classification of artificial nutrition and hydration as "ordinary and proportionate" care rather than "extraordinary" or "disproportionate" means. This distinction is foundational in Catholic moral theology regarding end-of-life care, and by firmly placing artificial nutrition and hydration in the ordinary category for vegetative-state patients, the document forecloses the moral argument for withdrawal that relies on characterizing such interventions as burdensome or disproportionate.

The document grounds its position in the concept of "fundamental human dignity," which it names explicitly in the response to the second question. This framing is significant: the argument is not primarily about the likelihood of recovery or the patient's subjective experience, but about the intrinsic worth of the person regardless of their cognitive or conscious state. The permanent vegetative state is acknowledged as a real clinical category, but is presented as morally irrelevant to the obligation of care.

The phrase "ordinary and proportionate means of preserving life" is the load-bearing phrase throughout. The responses do acknowledge that there are circumstances under which artificial nutrition and hydration would not be obligatory — namely, when they "cannot be assimilated by the patient's body or cannot be administered to the patient without causing significant physical discomfort," or when they cease to "accomplish [their] proper finality." These carve-outs are narrow and physiological rather than prognostic or qualitative-of-life based.

The document is explicitly reactive: it exists because the USCCB submitted questions, implying that there was uncertainty or debate within the U.S. Catholic context about the permissibility of withdrawal in these cases. The document resolves that debate with finality, closing interpretive space rather than opening it.

The approval by Pope Benedict XVI is explicitly noted, lending the responses papal authority beyond that of the Congregation alone, which strengthens their binding character within Catholic moral teaching.
