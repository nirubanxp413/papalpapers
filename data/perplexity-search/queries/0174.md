## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Quam Religiosa

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** A directive to the bishops of Peru to resist a newly enacted civil marriage law, defending the Church's exclusive sacramental jurisdiction over Christian marriage against encroachment by the state.
- **Summary:** Leo XIII writes to the Peruvian episcopate expressing sorrow and indignation at a recently passed Peruvian law which, under the pretext of regulating marriages among non-Catholics, effectively introduced civil marriage and permitted civil ceremonies for mixed marriages even in cases where the Holy See had determined a dispensation inappropriate. The Pope reaffirms Catholic teaching that Christian marriage is a sacrament entirely subject to Church authority, references his earlier encyclical *Arcanum divinae sapientiae* for doctrinal detail, notes that the Tridentine decree *Tametsi* has long been promulgated and observed in Peru, and directs the bishops to instruct the faithful and actively lobby Peruvian legislators to reverse or amend the offending decree.
- **Response to:** A "recently disseminated law in Peru" which introduced civil marriage in effect — even if not by name — and which "putting aside the authority of the Church" allowed civil ceremony for mixed marriages contrary to Holy See determinations; the document frames this as a "violation of the obedience due to Our dignity and to the authority divinely invested in Our supreme ministry."
- **Prescriptive towards:** Bishops are directed to (1) instruct the faithful fully on the Church's teaching on marriage and its bearing on eternal salvation; (2) use their authority and personal relationships to influence Peruvian legislators, so that civil marriage laws contain "nothing contrary to the teaching of the Church" and the recent decree is not left to stand unchanged.
- **Tension:** Church jurisdiction vs. civil jurisdiction over marriage — the document draws a sharp line: civil leaders have authority over "human affairs which led to marriage and generally concern civil matters," but "in the truly Christian marriage, they have no authority." A secondary tension exists between Peru's historically strong Catholic identity (praised at the opening) and its government's move toward secular regulation of marriage.

## Unstructured

The document is short and tightly focused — six paragraphs, no theological digressions. Its structure is essentially: praise → complaint → doctrinal grounding → pastoral/political directive → blessing. The tone is unmistakably authoritative rather than pastoral; Leo writes as a sovereign addressing subordinates about a compliance failure, not as a spiritual guide offering encouragement.

The opening paragraph is notable for its strategic function: Leo establishes Peru's Catholic credentials (long allegiance to the faith, a recent national Catholic congress, special papal honors granted to its bishops) before pivoting to censure. The implicit logic is: Peru has been favored; this law is therefore a betrayal of that identity and those privileges.

The doctrinal core (§4) is the densest section. Leo's argument against civil marriage is sacramental: Christ "translated the duty of nature into sacraments," and since Christian marriage is a sacrament, it is ontologically outside civil jurisdiction. The practical good of marriage — "tranquil and happy life for the spouses, stronger family harmony, children raised more correctly" — is presented as flowing from its sacred character, not from civil regulation. The reference to *Arcanum divinae sapientiae* (1880) positions this letter as an enforcement action downstream of established doctrine rather than a fresh theological argument.

The invocation of *Tametsi* (Trent, Session 24) is pointed: Leo notes it "was not only promulgated in Peru, but has been enforced for a long time and faithfully observed," which makes the new civil marriage law doubly problematic — it contradicts not just universal Church teaching but existing Peruvian legal and ecclesiastical practice.

The directive to bishops in §5 is notably political: they are to cultivate relationships with legislators ("enter into their favor"), appeal to national pride through the examples of Peruvian saints Turibius and Rose, and extract a commitment that the decree will be altered. The document treats the bishops as diplomatic agents of the Holy See as much as spiritual shepherds.

The overall framing treats civil marriage as a category error — a misappropriation of civil authority into a domain that belongs structurally and theologically to the Church. The word "recently" (applied to the offending law) signals urgency; the pope wants action before this becomes entrenched precedent.
