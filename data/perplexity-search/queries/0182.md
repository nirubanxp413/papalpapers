## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Commissum Divinitus (Gregory XVI, 1835)

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Civil authority has unlawfully encroached on ecclesiastical power — over doctrine, sacraments, discipline, and papal jurisdiction — and must be firmly repudiated.
- **Summary:** Addressed to the clergy of Switzerland, this document formally condemns the articles produced at a January 1834 assembly in Baden (canton of Aargau), which Gregory XVI charges with subordinating Church governance to secular civil power. The document argues from divine institution, patristic authority, and the nature of Christ's commission to the apostles that the Church's powers of teaching, governing, and sacramental ministry are entirely beyond the reach of the state. It specifically condemns the Baden articles for giving civil authority the right to approve synods, oversee seminaries, remove clerics, regulate marriage (including blessing mixed marriages), abolish monastic exemptions, and even erect or unify dioceses — all without reference to Rome. It concludes with a formal apostolic condemnation and an exhortation to the Swiss clergy to resist these encroachments.
- **Response to:** The January 1834 assembly at Baden in the canton of Aargau, which produced articles defining the temporal power's role in ecclesiastical affairs; the subsequent enactment of civil laws confirming those articles; and a broader pattern of enemies of the Church who "cunningly try many things" by claiming to protect lay rights while eroding Catholic teaching and discipline.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The Swiss clergy (bishops, priests, and ministers of the sacraments) are directed to stand firm, preach against error, defend the rights of the Holy See, reject "profane novelties," and ensure that the faithful cling to Catholic faith and submit to Church authority. Secular rulers are implicitly directed to acknowledge that Church power is beyond their jurisdiction, and that obedience to civil law is owed on conscience — but never at the cost of abandoning the faith.
- **Tension:** The central tension is the competing claims of civil authority and ecclesiastical authority over the same social and spiritual terrain — marriage, education, worship, clerical appointment, monastic governance. A secondary tension exists between the desire for public order ("the tranquility of the citizens and the welfare of the Church: these two things cannot be separated") and the need to resist state encroachment. A third, doctrinal tension is named explicitly: "indifferentism" — the idea that religious differences between marriage partners are irrelevant — is condemned as "absurd and impious" and as an ideological foundation underwriting the Baden articles.

## Unstructured

**Flow of argument:** The document opens with Gregory framing his duty as universal pastor, then narrows quickly to the specific trigger: the Baden assembly of January 1834. He explains why he waited before acting (he initially hoped civil leaders would reject the articles themselves), then pivots to systematic doctrinal argument before issuing a formal condemnation and pastoral exhortation.

The doctrinal core (paragraphs 4–14) is dense and organized around concentric circles of ecclesiastical authority: first, the Church's divinely instituted power of magisterium and governance (¶4); second, its complete independence from civil rule, proved from the apostles' defiance of the Sanhedrin and from Ossius of Cordoba's letter to Emperor Constantius (¶5–6); third, specific violations committed at Baden — sacramental authority, marriage, synods, seminaries, monastic exemptions (¶7–9, 12–13); fourth, papal primacy and its grounding in Peter's commission, with extensive quotation from Leo the Great and Jerome (¶10–11); fifth, the Baden articles' particular assault on papal jurisdiction in the Swiss dioceses (¶13–14).

**Tone:** Strongly authoritative and juridical. The register is that of a formal condemnation document — phrases like "We reprove and condemn," "We decree that they should forever be considered condemned," and "with the fullness of the apostolic power" signal that this is not pastoral guidance but a binding act of magisterial authority. The closing paragraphs (¶17–21) shift briefly to a paternal and consolatory register ("We give thanks… who comforted Us with you"), but even there the exhortation quickly returns to martial imagery: "unsheathe the sword of the spirit," "stand as a wall," "fight for the cause of God."

**Notable framing:** Gregory frames the Baden participants not merely as politically mistaken but as agents of deception — they "pretend to have as their only purpose the protection of the rights of the laity" while secretly pursuing "erroneous and wicked teachings." The assembly is not treated as a sincere if misguided reform effort but as a hostile act whose premises were already condemned (the final condemnation explicitly states the articles "proceed from condemned premises"). This framing elevates the stakes from a jurisdictional dispute to a spiritual battle.

**Indifferentism as the ideological root:** The document singles out indifferentism — the principle that religious differences do not matter — as the necessary theoretical underpinning for the Baden articles on mixed marriage. This is significant because it ties a practical disciplinary dispute (who blesses mixed marriages) to a larger condemned doctrine, suggesting the Baden agenda is not merely administrative but philosophically corrosive.

**Papal primacy as the pivot:** A substantial portion of the text is devoted not just to the Church-vs-state division but to establishing that, within the Church, authority flows through Rome. The Baden articles are condemned both for subordinating the Church to the state and for implying that episcopal jurisdiction does not require papal confirmation. Gregory uses Leo the Great, Jerome, and the example of Peter's commission ("feed my sheep") to insist that any fracture of unity from the Roman See is spiritually catastrophic — "whoever dares to depart from the unity of Peter might understand that he no longer shares in the divine mystery."
