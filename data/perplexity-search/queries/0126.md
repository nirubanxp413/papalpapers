# Cum populi

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Pope calls on the Bishop of Aleria and his Corsican diocese to resist the Civil Constitution of the Clergy, which the Pope declares contrary to Church dogma and order, and to fulfil their canonical duties under threat of schism and ecclesiastical sanction.
- **Summary:** This brief (breve) is a short directive letter from Pius VI to Bishop Giovanni of Aleria. The Pope states that the people of the bishop's diocese have been deceived into embracing the Civil Constitution of the Gallican Clergy promulgated by the National Assembly, which he holds to be contrary to both dogma and the ancient and modern ordering of the Church. Because he has already written at length on the duties of each rank of the Church in a separate letter to the Cardinals, Archbishops, Bishops, chapters, clergy, and people of France, Pius VI transmits copies of that letter to the bishop, so that neither the bishop nor his flock will be ignorant of how to act amid this "subversion of ecclesiastical interests." The brief closes with an apostolic blessing and an exhortation to the bishop to let the gravity of the persecution inflame his pastoral zeal.
- **Response to:** The promulgation by the French National Assembly of the Civil Constitution of the Clergy, which the document states the populations of the bishop's territory have embraced after being deceived ("irretiti dagli inganni di altri"). The document also implicitly responds to the broader "terrible storm" unleashed against the Catholic religion.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The bishop, the diocesan chapters, parish priests, clergy, and laity of Aleria — and by the closing note, the bishops of Ajaccio, Sagone, Nebbio, and Mariana (all Corsican dioceses). Each is directed to learn their canonical duties from the enclosed letter and to act accordingly, on pain of being regarded as complicit in schism and subject to canonical sanctions against "corruptors of doctrine and discipline."
- **Tension:** The central tension is between obedience to the Church's canonical and dogmatic order on one side, and the civil-ecclesiastical authority claimed by the French National Assembly on the other. The document also names an implicit pastoral tension: the flock has been "ensnared by the deceptions of others," placing the bishop in the position of having to reclaim a flock already drawn toward the schismatic constitution. A secondary tension is between danger/persecution (the "terrible storm" against the Church) and the call to heightened pastoral zeal — the Pope frames grave peril as a spur rather than a reason for retreat.

## Unstructured

**Argument flow and tone:**  
The document is very short — essentially a cover letter accompanying a longer, separately issued papal letter. Its rhetorical structure is tight: (1) statement of the problem (the population has embraced the Civil Constitution, which violates dogma and ecclesiastical order); (2) assertion that Pius VI's "paternal solicitude" cannot allow ignorance of duty; (3) reference to the fuller letter already issued to France; (4) statement of purpose — copies are being sent so the bishop and his flock know how to act; (5) exhortation to the bishop to let the danger inflame his zeal; (6) closing apostolic blessing.

The tone is firmly authoritative but not punitive in itself. Pius VI speaks from a position of pastoral responsibility ("paterna sollecitudine") and frames the transmission of the letter as a service — he does not want the bishop or the flock to be left uninformed. The threat of canonical sanctions is stated factually rather than issued as a new decree: those who become "subject to schism" will face the sanctions the canons already provide for "corruptors of doctrine and discipline."

**Key framing choices:**  
- The laity's embrace of the Civil Constitution is attributed not to their own initiative but to having been "ensnared by the deceptions of others" ("irretiti dagli inganni di altri") — a framing that preserves the flock as victims and focuses blame on external deceiving agents.
- The document uses the phrase "sovvertimento degli interessi ecclesiastici" (subversion of ecclesiastical interests) to characterise what the Civil Constitution represents, positioning it as an attack on an established order rather than merely a theological disagreement.
- The "terrible storm" metaphor ("terribile bufera") implies a force of nature or divine trial, not merely a political dispute — elevating the stakes beyond the civil realm.
- The final exhortation reverses the logic of danger: the more grave the peril and persecution, the more the bishop's "ardent religious vocation" should be "stimulated and inflamed." Danger is recast as spiritual intensifier.

**Scope and audience:**  
Though addressed to the Bishop of Aleria, the closing note shows it was simultaneously sent to the bishops of all four Corsican dioceses (Ajaccio, Sagone, Nebbio, Mariana). This is therefore a coordinated circular for Corsica, not a purely individual communication. The document's substance is largely carried by the referenced longer letter to France; this brief functions as a transmission mechanism to extend that letter's authority to the Corsican church.

**Notable absence:**  
The document makes no direct theological argument against the Civil Constitution — it asserts opposition to dogma and Church order as a given, and defers all substantive elaboration to the longer letter. This is consistent with its cover-letter function, but it means the document itself rests almost entirely on assertion of authority rather than argumentation.
