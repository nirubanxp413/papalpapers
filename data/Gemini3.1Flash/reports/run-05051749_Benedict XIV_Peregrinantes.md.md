### Context
*Peregrinantes* (1749) was issued by Benedict XIV (Prospero Lambertini) to announce the 1750 Jubilee. Historically, it follows the Peace of Aix-la-Chapelle (1748), which concluded the War of the Austrian Succession. Benedict XIV, a canonist and diplomat, sought to pivot Europe from the exhaustion of dynastic warfare toward a unified Catholic identity.

### Reflective
*   **Post-War Exhaustion:** The document reflects on the "long war" that had ravaged Europe, framing the cessation of hostilities as a divine window for spiritual recovery.
*   **Institutional Continuity:** It explicitly references the historical evolution of the Jubilee (from Boniface VIII’s centennial to the 25-year cycle established by later popes), positioning the Church as the sole stable institution amidst shifting secular borders.
*   **Pagan-to-Christian Rome:** It reflects on the historical transformation of Rome, using the city’s physical architecture as a retrospective argument for the Church's triumph over antiquity.

### Prospective
*   **Mobilization:** Prescribes a "spiritual war" against secular vices (luxury, cupidity, and intellectual license).
*   **Diplomatic Expectation:** Explicitly mandates that Catholic princes provide safe passage and provisions for pilgrims, attempting to bind secular state infrastructure to the Church's logistical needs.
*   **Eschatological Warning:** Predicts the "Judgment Day" as a looming horizon, using it to pressure the faithful into immediate compliance with the Jubilee requirements.

### Temporal gradients

## Unstructured

### 1. Historical Context and Secular Engagement
Benedict XIV was a "scholar-pope" whose reign was defined by a pragmatic, legalistic approach to the Enlightenment. *Peregrinantes* is notable for its attempt to re-assert the Church’s role as the arbiter of European peace. By calling for a Jubilee immediately following the Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle, Benedict attempted to "re-brand" the end of the War of the Austrian Succession as a moment of divine grace rather than mere political exhaustion.

### 2. The "Spiritual War" as Political Metaphor
The document’s shift into military terminology is not merely devotional. By framing the Jubilee as a "new kind of war," Benedict was attempting to redirect the martial energies of the European nobility—recently demobilized from the Austrian Succession conflict—toward the Church’s internal agenda. This was a strategic effort to maintain the Church's relevance in a post-Westphalian political order where secular rulers were increasingly asserting autonomy.

### 3. Diplomatic Efficacy
The appeal to Catholic princes to protect pilgrims was a test of the Church's remaining soft power. Historical records from 1750 indicate that while major Catholic powers (France, Austria, Spain) formally acknowledged the Jubilee, their cooperation was often perfunctory. The "secular world" was already moving toward the secularization of travel and border control, and the Pope’s request for special provisions for pilgrims was increasingly viewed as an archaic diplomatic courtesy rather than a binding political obligation.

### 4. Reception and Legacy
The 1750 Jubilee was a success in terms of attendance, but it did not achieve the Pope's goal of a "re-conversion" of Europe. Contemporary accounts (e.g., travelogues of the Grand Tour) suggest that while Rome was crowded, the pilgrims were often viewed by the local Roman populace and visiting elites as a source of economic revenue rather than a spiritual movement. The document’s attempt to bridge the gap between the "pilgrim" and the "citizen" failed to halt the rising tide of secularism that would characterize the late 18th century.
