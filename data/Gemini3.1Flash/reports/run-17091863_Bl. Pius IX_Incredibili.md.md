## Structured

### Context
*Incredibili* (1863) was issued during the "Radical" period of the United States of Colombia (formerly New Granada). The Liberal government, led by Tomás Cipriano de Mosquera, had enacted the *Constitución de Rionegro* (1863), which solidified a secular, federalist state. The encyclical responds to the *Desamortización de bienes de manos muertas* (1861), which effectively liquidated Church-owned land to fund the state and weaken the Church's economic hegemony.

### Reflective
The document reflects on the immediate aftermath of the 1861-1863 legislative reforms. It specifically addresses the expulsion of the Jesuits, the forced exile of Archbishop Antonio Herrán of Bogotá, and the state's attempt to impose a "patronage" system that required government approval for ecclesiastical appointments.

### Prospective
The encyclical prescribes a policy of non-compliance for the clergy, specifically regarding the oath of obedience to the new constitution. It anticipates a long-term struggle for the "liberty of the Church" and warns of divine retribution against the state, framing the conflict as a precursor to a wider global struggle against secular liberalism.

- **Prescriptive:** +3 years (anticipating the ongoing resistance and the eventual, though delayed, reconciliation of the Concordat of 1887).

## Unstructured

### 1. Historical Reality vs. Papal Narrative
The encyclical frames the conflict as a purely religious persecution. Historical records, however, indicate the conflict was deeply rooted in the Liberal-Conservative civil wars of the 19th century. The "confiscation of property" was a fiscal necessity for the Liberal government to modernize the state and break the economic power of the landed Church. The "scandal" of complying ecclesiastics mentioned by Pius IX refers to the *regalist* faction of the clergy who believed in the state's right to oversee Church administration, a tradition dating back to the Spanish colonial *Patronato Real*.

### 2. Media and Public Reception
Contemporary secular press in Bogotá (e.g., *El Tiempo*) characterized the encyclical as an "intolerable interference" in national sovereignty. The document was largely ignored by the Liberal government, which viewed the Pope as a foreign political actor. Conversely, the Conservative press and the rural populace used the encyclical as a rallying cry for the 1860s and 1870s civil uprisings.

### 3. The "Prophetic" Failure
Pius IX’s expectation that the government would "open their eyes" and repent was not realized. The Liberal reforms remained in place for over two decades. The "remedy" for the Church did not come through the repentance of the Liberal government, but through the eventual political victory of the Conservative Party in the late 1880s, which led to the 1887 Concordat—a document that restored many Church privileges, effectively bypassing the "repentance" Pius IX sought.

### 4. Anthropological Assessment
The document functions as a "boundary-maintenance" mechanism. By labeling the government's actions as "invalid" and "perverse," Pius IX creates a clear social demarcation between the "faithful" (who resist) and the "apostates" (who comply). This served to consolidate the identity of the Colombian Catholic Church as a persecuted, militant minority, a self-conception that dominated Colombian politics until the late 20th century.

### 5. Scholarly Interpretation
Historians (e.g., David Bushnell) note that *Incredibili* is a classic example of the "Syllabus of Errors" era of the papacy, where the Church refused to engage with the concept of the secular state. The encyclical is less a diplomatic tool and more a theological assertion of the Church's jurisdictional supremacy over civil law.
