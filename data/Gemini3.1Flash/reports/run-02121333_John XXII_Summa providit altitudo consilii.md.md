## Structured

### Context
*   **Historical Setting:** 1333, Avignon Papacy. John XXII is in the final year of his long, contentious pontificate. The University of Salamanca, founded by Alfonso IX of León (1218), was struggling to solidify its status as a *studium generale* against the dominance of Paris and Bologna.
*   **Political Environment:** King Alfonso XI of Castile was consolidating royal power and seeking to elevate the intellectual prestige of his kingdom to rival the influence of the French and Italian academic centers.

### Reflective
*   **Institutional Gap:** The document explicitly addresses the failure of the 1255 bull of Alexander IV, which granted Salamanca the *ius ubique docendi* (right to teach everywhere) but failed to designate a local authority to confer the degrees, rendering the privilege practically inert for decades.
*   **Jurisdictional Conflict:** It reflects the ongoing struggle to define the relationship between the *studium* (the university body) and the *cathedral school* (the ecclesiastical authority), settling the dispute by subordinating the academic degree-granting process to the cathedral scholasticus.

### Prospective
*   **Institutional Standardization:** The bull prescribes a permanent, replicable legal framework for degree conferral, intended to ensure the university's long-term viability and parity with other European institutions.
*   **Regulatory Expansion:** By formalizing the role of the scholasticus, the document anticipates a future where Salamanca can independently produce masters and doctors, thereby reducing the need for students to travel to foreign universities to complete their education.

### Temporal gradients
*   **Reflective Density (-15):** The document looks back to the 1218 foundation and the 1255 Alexander IV grant, addressing a 78-year-old structural deficiency.
*   **Prescriptive Density (+10):** The mandate is designed to govern the university's operations for the foreseeable future, aiming to secure its standing for the coming decades of the 14th century.

## Unstructured

### 1. Historical Analysis
The bull *Summa providit altitudo consilii* is a classic example of late medieval administrative "gap-filling." While the University of Salamanca held a royal charter, its international recognition as a *studium generale* was hampered by a procedural vacuum. By 1333, the university was a key instrument of Castilian state-building. John XXII, known for his legalistic and centralizing tendencies, utilized this bull to bring the university firmly under the orbit of canon law, ensuring that academic advancement was mediated through the ecclesiastical hierarchy (the scholasticus) rather than purely secular royal decree.

### 2. The Papal Situation
John XXII was, in 1333, deeply embroiled in the "Beatific Vision" controversy and conflicts with the Franciscan Spirituals. His issuance of this bull suggests a desire to maintain the appearance of a functioning, orderly Church bureaucracy despite his intense focus on theological disputes. The bull is a "clean-up" operation, typical of his administrative style—precise, cold, and focused on legal clarity.

### 3. Secular Engagement
The document represents a intersection of royal ambition and papal authority. King Alfonso XI needed a university that could produce trained jurists and administrators for his court. By securing this bull, he gained the ability to "certify" his own elite, provided they passed through the ecclesiastical gatekeeper. This was not a theological mission, but a state-building exercise.

### 4. Reception and Efficacy
Historical records indicate that the bull was successful in its immediate goal. The University of Salamanca grew in prestige throughout the 14th century, eventually becoming one of the most significant intellectual centers in the Iberian Peninsula. The specific mechanism established—the scholasticus as the degree-granting authority—remained a fixture of the university's governance for centuries, proving the document's long-term prescriptive efficacy.

### 5. Evidence Summary
Evidence for the document's impact is found in the subsequent growth of the University of Salamanca's faculty records and its eventual rise to prominence in the 15th century. There is no evidence of significant public or media discourse at the time, as this was a specialized administrative instrument rather than a public-facing encyclical.
