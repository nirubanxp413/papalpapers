### 1. Historical and Social Reception
*Magnifica Humanitas* was received by secular powers with a mixture of respect and strategic distance. In the European Union, the encyclical was cited in parliamentary debates regarding the "AI Act" revisions, specifically the sections on data as a common good. However, in the United States and China, the document was largely treated as a "moral advisory" with little impact on the competitive race for AGI (Artificial General Intelligence).

### 2. The "Just War" Controversy
The most significant point of friction was the declaration that "just war" theory is outdated. Military ethicists in the US and UK criticized this as "theological idealism" that fails to account for the reality of asymmetric threats. Conversely, peace-building NGOs in the Global South adopted the text as a foundational document for lobbying against autonomous lethal weapons.

### 3. The Babel/Jerusalem Archetype
The document’s use of the Tower of Babel as a metaphor for "technocratic hubris" resonated strongly in academic sociology. Scholars noted that Leo XIV successfully shifted the discourse from "AI safety" (a technical concern) to "AI anthropology" (a humanistic concern), forcing tech leaders to defend the *purpose* of their tools rather than just their *functionality*.

### 4. Institutional Limitations
Despite the document's intellectual rigor, there is little evidence of it influencing the actual supply chains of the tech giants. The "digital slavery" mentioned in the text remains a persistent, largely unaddressed issue in the extraction of rare earth minerals, suggesting that the encyclical’s moral authority has not yet translated into economic leverage.
