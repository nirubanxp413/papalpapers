## Unstructured

### 1. Historical and Political Context
*Divini Amoris Scientia* was issued in the context of John Paul II’s broader project to consolidate the identity of the Church at the end of the second millennium. While the document is ostensibly theological, it functions as a strategic cultural intervention. By elevating a 19th-century French nun to the status of "Doctor of the Church," the Vatican sought to reassert the relevance of contemplative, non-academic wisdom in an era increasingly dominated by technocratic and secular rationalism.

### 2. Reflective Analysis
The document is a retrospective on the "Little Way." It interprets the 20th-century history of the Church as a validation of Thérèse’s doctrine. It specifically addresses the "Jansenist" ghost—the lingering influence of a theology of divine justice that the Vatican viewed as a barrier to modern evangelization. By framing Thérèse as the antidote, the document reflects on the failure of previous, more rigid pedagogical models within the Church.

### 3. Prospective Analysis
The document attempts to shape the "New Evangelization" by offering a model of holiness that is accessible to the laity. It prescribes a shift away from intellectualized theology toward a "science of love." However, evidence of this having a measurable impact on secular society is absent. While the document was highly influential within Catholic devotional circles, it did not penetrate secular political or social discourse, remaining an intra-ecclesial instrument.

### 4. Reception and Efficacy
There is no evidence of this document being cited in secular policy, parliamentary debate, or international relations. Its reception was confined to the Catholic press and academic theological circles. The "prophecy" that Thérèse would be a guide for the new millennium was realized in terms of popular piety (her relics toured the world extensively in the early 2000s), but this did not translate into the broader social or political influence the Vatican often seeks through its encyclicals.

### 5. Investigative Summary
The document is a classic example of "symbolic governance." By naming a Doctor of the Church, the papacy utilizes its historical authority to signal a shift in pastoral priorities. It is less a policy document and more a cultural marker, intended to provide a "soft" face to the Church as it approached the year 2000.
