## Structured

### Context
*Levate* (September 27, 1867) was issued during the final, volatile phase of the Risorgimento. The "Piedmontese" threat mentioned is the Garibaldian expedition of 1867, which culminated in the Battle of Mentana (November 1867). Simultaneously, the document addresses the aftermath of the 1863 January Uprising in Poland, where the Russian Empire intensified the Russification of the Catholic Church to dismantle Polish national identity.

### Reflective
The encyclical serves as a formal record of the Holy See’s diplomatic and canonical grievances. It reflects on:
- The failure of the 1864 *Convention of September* between Italy and France, which Pius IX viewed as a betrayal of the Papal States' sovereignty.
- The systematic dismantling of the Uniate Church in the Russian Empire, specifically the forced conversion of Ruthenian Catholics.
- The administrative isolation of the Pope from his dioceses in the East, which he interprets as a rupture of the "natural law" of ecclesiastical communication.

### Prospective
- **Prescription:** A universal call for a three-day triduum of prayer to invoke divine intervention against secular encroachment.
- **Anticipation:** The Pope predicts that the "triumphal impiety" of the secular state will lead to the destruction of social order, warning that the state’s interference in church governance is a precursor to total moral collapse.
- **Outcome:** The prayer campaign had negligible impact on the geopolitical trajectory; the Papal States were annexed in 1870, and the Russian suppression of the Polish Church continued unabated until the 1905 Edict of Toleration.

### Temporal gradients
- **Reflective (-5):** The document is heavily anchored in the immediate past (1863–1867), specifically the legislative acts of the Russian government and the military movements of the Garibaldini.
- **Prospective (+2):** The gaze extends slightly into the near future, anticipating the imminent fall of Rome and the long-term struggle of the *Kulturkampf* era.

## Unstructured

### 1. Historical Reality vs. Papal Narrative
The encyclical frames the conflict as a spiritual battle against "diabolical" forces. Historical records from the period (e.g., British Foreign Office dispatches from Rome) suggest a more pragmatic reality: the Italian government was struggling to restrain Garibaldi’s volunteers to avoid a direct conflict with France, which still garrisoned Rome. The Pope’s rhetoric of "ravening wolves" obscures the complex diplomatic maneuvering where the Papacy was attempting to maintain temporal sovereignty in an era of rising nationalism.

### 2. The Russian-Polish Dimension
The specific mention of the "Ecclesiastical College" in St. Petersburg refers to the *Roman Catholic Ecclesiastical College* established by the Russian Empire to exert state control over the Church. Historians (e.g., Norman Davies) note that this was a deliberate policy to sever the link between Polish Catholics and the Vatican, effectively turning the Church into a state department. Pius IX’s protest was legally accurate regarding canon law but politically impotent against the Tsarist autocracy.

### 3. Reception and Impact
There is no evidence that *Levate* influenced the military or political decisions of the Piedmontese government or the Russian Empire. In the secular press of 1867 (e.g., *The Times* of London), the encyclical was treated as a predictable expression of papal intransigence. It was largely ignored by secular powers, who viewed the Pope’s claims of "natural law" as incompatible with the emerging Westphalian model of the sovereign nation-state.

### 4. The "Act" of Jurisdiction
The Pope’s decision to "insert an Act" into the encyclical to maintain jurisdiction over suppressed dioceses is a significant administrative maneuver. It represents an attempt to use the *printed word* as a legal instrument to bypass the physical blockade of the Russian state, demonstrating the Church's reliance on its global communication network when its local physical presence was compromised.

### 5. Anthropological Assessment
The document functions as a "lamentation of the sovereign." Pius IX, having lost the ability to exercise power through traditional diplomatic or military channels, shifts his authority into the realm of the symbolic and the spiritual. By calling for global prayer, he attempts to create a "virtual" community of the faithful that transcends the borders of the hostile states that are physically isolating him.
