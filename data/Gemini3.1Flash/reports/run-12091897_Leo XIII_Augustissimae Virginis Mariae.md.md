The encyclical functions as a spiritual mobilization order during a period where the Church felt increasingly isolated from the levers of secular power. By framing the Rosary as a "public" and "universal" act, Leo attempted to reclaim a form of public influence that the Church could no longer exercise through traditional diplomatic or political channels.

### 3. The "Secret Society" Tension
Leo’s obsession with "wicked societies" is the primary bridge between his theology and his politics. He viewed the Rosary Sodality as a direct, mirror-image competitor to the organizational structures of Freemasonry and secular labor unions. He sought to provide the laity with a sense of belonging and "associational" identity that was explicitly Catholic, thereby inoculating them against secular political movements.

### 4. Reception and Legacy
The document was highly influential within the internal structure of the Church, leading to a surge in Rosary Sodality enrollments in the early 1900s. However, it failed to stem the tide of secularization in Europe. Historians of religion (e.g., O'Malley) note that while Leo’s Marian devotion was deeply personal, his attempt to turn it into a "military" force for the Church was largely a defensive reaction to the loss of the Papal States and the rise of the modern secular state.
