### 2. Reflective Analysis
The document is a classic example of "devotional consolidation." By 1960, the proliferation of specific devotions had created a crowded liturgical landscape. John XXIII’s reflective gaze is directed at the 19th-century popes who elevated these devotions to combat secularism and rationalism. He is essentially "tidying up" the devotional house before the Council, ensuring that the Precious Blood is recognized as the foundational element of the other, more popular devotions.

### 3. Prospective Analysis
The encyclical’s prospective power is limited to the sphere of individual piety. It suggests that "social life" will become "more peaceable" through the conversion of the individual. This reflects a pre-conciliar view that social order is a direct byproduct of private virtue, rather than the result of structural or political reform. History shows that this specific devotion did not become a primary driver of social change in the 1960s; rather, the Church’s focus shifted toward the social justice themes of *Gaudium et Spes*.
