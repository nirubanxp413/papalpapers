# Theology of the Body

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Greek-Catholic Church of Romania embodies the vocation of unity between East and West, sustained through martyrdom, memory, and constant return to the Trinitarian source of the Church.
- **Summary:** Written to mark the third centenary of the Union of the Greek-Catholic Church of Romania with Rome (1700–2000), this Apostolic Letter by John Paul II celebrates the history of Romanian Greek-Catholics as a living sign of ecclesial unity. The document traces the historical journey toward union with Rome, honours the martyrs of the communist era, calls for a purification of memory through rigorous historical examination, and urges the Church to renew its quest for Christian unity — especially with the Orthodox Church — in the spirit of the Second Vatican Council and within the broader Jubilee of the Year 2000.
- **Response to:** (1) The three-hundredth anniversary of the Union of the Greek-Catholic Church of Romania with the Apostolic See (Synods of Alba Iulia, 1697–1700); (2) the suffering and suppression of the Greek-Catholic Church under communist totalitarianism in the second half of the 20th century; (3) John Paul II's own pastoral visit to Romania (May 1999); (4) the Jubilee of the Year 2000, which the document describes as "a year of grace" calling for renewal and purification of memory.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) Historical and theological research to document the martyrs and revisit the Provincial Synods of 1872, 1882, and 1900; (2) full reception of the Second Vatican Council's teaching by the Greek-Catholic Church of Romania; (3) renewed ecumenical dialogue with the Romanian Orthodox Church at both national and local levels; (4) rethinking the specific vocation of Eastern Churches in communion with Rome within a broader ecumenical horizon; (5) deeper prayer and return to the Trinitarian source as the foundation for all renewal.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between division and unity — the historic wound of the East-West schism set against the Greek-Catholic Church's calling to bridge Byzantium and Rome. A second tension runs between the Church's past (martyrdom, suppression, incomplete reception of Vatican II) and its future (ecumenical reconciliation, purified identity, renewed mission). The document also names the tension between the *mysterium iniquitatis* (communist persecution) and the *mysterium pietatis* (faithful witness unto death).

## Unstructured

Despite its title "Theology of the Body" — which appears to be a cataloguing or filing label rather than a description of the document's content — this Apostolic Letter has nothing to do with John Paul II's famous catechetical series on human sexuality. It is, in fact, an address to the Greek-Catholic Church of Romania on the occasion of the third centenary of its Union with Rome, dated 7 May 2000.

**Core argument and flow:**

The letter moves through five movements: (1) *Memory and presence* — the Pope evokes his 1999 visit to Romania as a kind of Emmaus encounter, Christ walking with the Church through history; (2) *History and unity* — the Romanian people are presented as uniquely positioned to mediate between the Christian East and West, having received both Byzantine and Roman heritages; (3) *Witnesses and martyrs* — the communist suppression of the Greek-Catholic Church is framed as a participation in the *mysterium pietatis*, a witness of martyrdom that shines before the universal Church; (4) *Purification of memory* — the Jubilee context requires honest historical examination, not merely celebration; (5) *A sign of unity* and *prayer* — the Church's future lies in ecumenical openness, reception of Vatican II, and constant return to the Trinitarian source through prayer.

**Tone:**

The tone is predominantly pastoral and affective — the Pope addresses the Greek-Catholics as "dear brothers and sisters," recalls personal emotion at hearing *Hristos a înviat!* ("Christ is risen!"), and speaks of kneeling at the tombs of their martyrs. Yet the letter is also directive and prescriptive, especially in sections 10–12, where it issues specific calls for theological research, historical revision, and ecumenical dialogue. This combination of pastoral warmth and magisterial direction is characteristic of John Paul II's epistolary style.

**Notable framing:**

- The *Jubilee of the Year 2000* serves as the overarching frame: this centenary is presented as providentially coinciding with the Jubilee, lending the celebration a universal and eschatological dimension.
- The Greek-Catholic Church is not treated as a marginal or disputed community but as "an eloquent witness to the unity of the whole Church" — a bridge that embodies the complementarity of Eastern and Western Christianity.
- The martyrs are described in explicitly apocalyptic language drawn from Revelation (the "great multitude clothed in white," the "white stone with a new name"), elevating them from historical victims to heavenly intercessors.
- John Paul II explicitly connects the blood of Greek-Catholic martyrs with the cause of Christian unity: "Their blood, shed for Christ and with Christ, is a sure foundation for seeking the unity of the whole Christian ecumene." Suffering is thus reinterpreted as ecumenical capital.
- The document acknowledges institutional failures without elaborate apology — the Church's children have "committed inconsistencies with regard to the teaching of the Gospel" — and frames the Jubilee as a moment of collective purification rather than self-congratulation.
- The final call to prayer is grounded in Trinitarian theology: the "ultimate root" of the Greek-Catholic Church, and of all unity, is the Holy Trinity itself.
