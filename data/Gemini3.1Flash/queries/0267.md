## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Exeunte Iam Anno

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The moral corruption of the age — rooted in rationalism, materialism, and the abandonment of faith — can only be healed by a return to genuine Christian virtue, personal self-mortification, and prayer.
- **Summary:** Written on Christmas Day 1888 at the close of Leo XIII's jubilee year (his 50th anniversary of priesthood), this letter begins with gratitude for the widespread devotion shown to the Holy See and then turns into a sweeping pastoral diagnosis of contemporary moral decay. The document traces a causal chain from wrong intellectual principles (rationalism, materialism, atheism) through moral corruption to social disorder (socialism, communism, nihilism), and proposes the remedy in personal Christian virtue, daily struggle against concupiscence, prayer, and the witness of the clergy. It closes with a prayer for the restoration of peace and right order under God.
- **Response to:** The jubilee year celebration and the signs of renewed faith it revealed; the widespread abandonment of Christian morals in public and private life as Leo observes it — luxury, greed, pride, contempt for the poor; the spread of rationalism, materialism, and atheism in education and public discourse; the consequent rise of socialism, communism, and nihilism; the exclusion of ecclesiastical authority from public schools; the weakening of prayer as a living habit.
- **Prescriptive towards:** A universal renewal of Christian virtue and self-discipline by all Catholics; the daily struggle against concupiscence as a non-optional duty ("not a counsel but a duty") for every Christian; the practice of prayer as the necessary support for virtue; the clergy setting an exemplary standard of learning, innocence of life, and self-abnegation; the restoration of the Church's rights in society so that the Gospel's power may be experienced; the spark of reviving faith growing into a transforming flame.
- **Tension:** (1) Worldly self-indulgence and pride vs. the Christian duty of mortification and self-denial; (2) Rationalist/materialist philosophy (which denies the soul's immortality and God's authority) vs. Catholic faith as the only true foundation for justice and social order; (3) The Church's divinely appointed role as guide and guardian vs. its marginalisation by states that have removed ecclesiastical authority from education and public life; (4) The apparent prosperity of unjust regimes vs. the theological insistence that injustice is always ultimately punished.

## Unstructured

The document is structured in two large movements. The first (§§1–9) is diagnostic: beginning with the thanksgiving of the jubilee year, Leo pivots quickly to a survey of widespread moral failure. His rhetoric is emphatically causal — wrong ideas produce wrong lives, wrong lives produce social disorder. The chain is explicit: "rationalism, materialism, atheism, have begotten socialism, communism, nihilism." The tone here is grave and authoritative; Leo names the intellectual sources of contemporary evil (denial of God, denial of the soul's immortality, exaltation of unaided reason, materialist reduction of human nature) and shows how each severs the moral foundations that make virtue possible.

The second movement (§§10–15) is prescriptive and pastoral. Leo shifts from diagnosis to remedy, and the tone becomes more exhortatory — almost homiletic. The core prescription is the ancient ascetic ideal restated with theological precision: Christian life is necessarily a battle against concupiscence, and this battle is not optional for any Christian. He quotes the Council of Trent directly to establish that concupiscence remains after baptism and must be fought daily. The document insists this struggle is not just for monks or the devout minority but for every Christian, including — especially — those most exposed to worldly temptation.

Prayer receives sustained treatment (§§11–12): it is presented not as pious supplement but as the necessary power-source of virtue. Leo invokes Augustine and Chrysostom, uses filial metaphors (God as loving father), and ends with an insistence that without prayer the whole moral project collapses.

The clergy section (§14) is notable for its high demands: priests must embody the "light of the world" not merely in learning but in exemplary innocence, self-denial, and a Bernardine contempt for self. The standard cited is not competence but holiness.

The document closes with a nautical image of the Church as a ship in a storm, Leo as helmsman fixing his gaze on the "Divine Pilot." This frames the entire document as an act of episcopal courage under adversity — the encyclical is itself offered as an example of not despairing despite the scale of the evil described.

The overall tone is Mixed: pastorally warm in its exhortations (the "loving parent" register of §5), but theologically stern in its insistence that there is no shortcut to Christian virtue and no purely political solution to moral disorder. The argument is fundamentally theological-moral rather than political, though the social consequences of irreligion are prominently described. The document does not engage specific political events or institutions by name; its analysis operates at the level of ideas and their moral consequences.
