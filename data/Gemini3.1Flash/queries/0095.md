# Quanto in dolore

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Pope calls on the assembled French bishops to take courageous action in defence of the Church against the suppression of the Jesuits, the silencing of Catholic defenders, and the broader encroachment of secular power upon ecclesiastical authority in France.
- **Summary:** Addressed to the archbishops and bishops of France gathered in extraordinary assembly, Clement XIII laments the grave afflictions visited upon the Catholic Church in France: defenders of the faith are silenced while innovators attack dogma with impunity; ministers are imprisoned and exiled; control of religious education has been taken from bishops; and the Society of Jesus, long a bulwark of Catholic faith, has been persecuted, dispersed, and publicly condemned by lay authority. The Pope urges the bishops to deliberate together, to approach the Most Christian King on behalf of the Church, and to show the same zeal and fortitude their episcopal office demands, assuring them of his full apostolic support.
- **Response to:** The persecution and dispersal of the Society of Jesus (Jesuits) in France; the silencing of Catholic defenders and the imprisonment and exile of sacred ministers; the removal of episcopal oversight over the education of youth; attacks on apostolic constitutions and Church decrees by "innovators"; injurious acts against the Holy See and the episcopate; the broader struggle between ecclesiastical and secular power in France.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The bishops convening in Paris are directed to deliberate on measures to strengthen the Church's wavering power in the kingdom; to bring the matter before the King, impressing on him the gravity of the harm done to the Church and the danger of worse consequences if the Church is not promptly freed from these injuries; and to show episcopal courage and constancy in defending the faith.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between ecclesiastical authority (papal and episcopal) and secular/lay power: defenders of the faith are suppressed while innovators attack freely; lay authority has declared religious vows invalid, a matter the document insists belongs solely to the Church. A secondary tension exists between silence and speech — the Pope describes having waited in silence for divine assistance but now finds that silence can no longer be sustained. There is also a tension between despair at present evils and hope founded on divine assistance and the King's known piety.

## Unstructured

The document is a brief (breve) rather than an encyclical in the strict sense, addressed specifically to the French bishops assembled in the extraordinary General Assembly of the Gallican Clergy in 1762. Its occasion is precise and urgent: the suppression of the Jesuits in France, with wider grievances about the condition of the Church there woven in.

The tone is simultaneously lamenting and exhortatory. Clement XIII opens with a register of shared suffering ("In quanto grande dolore Ci troviamo"), positioning himself and the bishops as co-sufferers who understand each other's pain. This establishes solidarity before turning to call for action. The emotional register is high throughout — grief, fear, indignation, and fervent hope are all invoked — but the document's rhetorical purpose is unmistakably to galvanise the bishops to act.

The argument moves in a clear progression: (1) a description of the Church's plight in France, with specific grievances enumerated; (2) the particular scandal of the Jesuit suppression, treated as both spiritually and institutionally outrageous; (3) an explicit call for the bishops to deliberate at their Paris assembly and approach the King; (4) assurance of papal backing and divine intercession.

The Jesuit section (paragraph 3) is the most polemically charged. Clement defends the Society by rehearsing its legitimate credentials: approved at the Council of Trent, praised and privileged by many popes, valued by the bishops themselves. The public burning of Jesuit books and writings "by the hand of the Executioner" is presented as a public humiliation of the Church itself. The charge that lay authority has presumed to declare religious vows invalid strikes at a core ecclesiological principle — that the Church alone judges matters pertaining to vows.

The document's framing of enemies is notably broad: it distinguishes Catholics who love the Church and are silenced, "innovators" who attack dogma with impunity, unnamed adversaries who rejoice at the discord between ecclesiastical and secular power (implied to be Protestants or unbelievers), and implicitly a French civil authority that has acted against both the Jesuits and episcopal prerogatives. Yet the Pope's appeal to the King is not adversarial — he appeals to the King's known piety and his status as "Most Christian Prince and First-born Son of Holy Mother Church," framing the royal intervention as both possible and expected.

The closing paragraphs are pastoral and affective: Clement commits himself to defend the bishops with full apostolic authority and promises unceasing prayer, especially at the sacrifice of the Mass. The closing benediction reinforces the pastoral register.

Overall, the document is authoritative in its ecclesiological claims but pastoral and fraternal in its direct address to the bishops. It does not issue formal condemnations or impose obligations but seeks to rouse, encourage, and coordinate episcopal action within a crisis largely driven by French domestic politics. The Jesuit suppression is the proximate occasion; the deeper subject is the boundary between spiritual and temporal authority.
