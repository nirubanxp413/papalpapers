## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Charitas

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Pope asserts the exclusive authority of the Apostolic See over episcopal appointments and Church governance in response to the French National Assembly's Civil Constitution of the Clergy, which he declares heretical, schismatic, and null.
- **Summary:** *Charitas* is a formal papal declaration issued in the midst of active schism in France. Pius VI recounts the series of events leading from the National Assembly's Civil Constitution of the Clergy (1790) through his warnings to King Louis XVI, the refusal of most French bishops to take the civil oath, and finally the sacrilegious consecrations of new bishops by oath-taking prelates beginning on February 24, 1791. The document formally suspends the oath-takers and nullifies all elections and consecrations made under the Constitution, while simultaneously exhorting faithful bishops, canons, clergy, and Catholic laity to reject the intruders and remain in unity with the Holy See.
- **Response to:** The French National Assembly's Civil Constitution of the Clergy, which the document states restructured the Church by giving elections of bishops to lay municipal assemblies (including "laity, heretics, unbelievers, and Jews"), mandating a civil oath for all clergy under pain of removal, stripping the Apostolic See of its role in appointing bishops, and enabling the Assembly Executive to authorize consecrations — all of which the Pope had warned King Louis XVI against in correspondence dating to July 1790.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs (a) all clergy who took the oath to abjure it within forty days or face suspension; (b) the named spurious bishops (Expilly, Marolles, Saurine, Massieu, Lindet, Laurent, Heraudin, Gobel) not to exercise any episcopal jurisdiction; (c) faithful bishops to remain with their churches and resist the intruders; (d) canons to maintain their chapters independently of false bishops; (e) parish priests and curates to hold their appointments as valid regardless of civil removal; (f) Catholic laity to shun the intruders, avoid communion with them in divine worship, and "stay close to Us."
- **Tension:** The central tension is between civil/political authority (the National Assembly legislating Church structure, compelling oaths, and appointing bishops) and ecclesiastical/spiritual authority (the exclusive right of the Apostolic See to confirm and appoint bishops, as affirmed by the Council of Trent). A secondary tension runs between pastoral patience — the Pope repeatedly describes himself as restrained, consulting cardinals, writing to the king, offering forty-day windows to repent — and the canonical severity he feels compelled to invoke once schism is actually enacted. There is also a tension between the unity of the French Church (described as nearly unanimous in resistance) and the few ambitious or weak individuals who capitulate, whose defection the document treats as uniquely dangerous precisely because they supply the episcopal consecrations that make schism structurally viable.

## Unstructured

**Central argument and flow:** The document unfolds as a detailed chronological narrative before pivoting to formal canonical decrees. Pius VI opens with a statement of governing principle drawn from Paul's characterisation of love (*caritas*): love is patient, but when errors escalate to schism, love itself demands that the erring be confronted with "their horrible sin and the heavy canonical penalties." This framing is important — every act of severity in the document is rhetorically grounded in pastoral love, not punitive zeal.

The narrative occupies roughly the first two-thirds of the letter (sections 2–19). Pius traces his correspondence with Louis XVI beginning July 1790, the responses of the French episcopate (130 of 131 bishops opposing the Constitution), the Assembly's escalating decrees requiring oaths and enabling lay-elected bishops, and finally the specific consecrations of February and early March 1791 — naming each officiant and each newly-consecrated figure. The naming is itself a rhetorical act: it personalises the schism, ensures a public record, and signals that these individuals are known to Rome and held accountable.

**Tone:** The document combines two distinct registers. In the narrative sections the tone is almost diplomatic in its precision — dates, names, letters, consultations with cardinals, all meticulously cited to demonstrate that Rome acted with care and transparency, not haste. In the prescriptive sections (20–27) the language shifts to formal canonical declaration: "We declare," "We proclaim," "We rescind, efface, and abrogate." The closing addresses to bishops, canons, priests, and laity (28–32) adopt a distinctly pastoral and even affectionate tone — "beloved sons," "venerable brothers" — contrasting with the harshness of the middle section's denunciations.

**Key framing moves:**
1. *Schism as political construction:* The document frames the schism not as a spontaneous religious rupture but as engineered by the Assembly. The Assembly "knew well" that no legitimate bishop would consecrate lay-elected men, so it deliberately sought out ambitious or weak bishops to break the deadlock. The schism is thus presented as a political project using ecclesiastical tools.
2. *The oath as origin:* The civil oath is called "the poisoned source and origin of all errors and the chief cause of the sorrow of the French Catholic church" (section 20). All subsequent canonical penalties flow from this single act; abjuring the oath is the key to returning to communion.
3. *Apostolic See as sole locus of episcopal jurisdiction:* The document repeatedly and specifically invokes Trent (Session 24, chap. 1, de Reformat.) to ground the claim that no bishop, metropolitan, or civil body may consecrate or appoint bishops without papal mandate. This is both a theological claim and a direct rebuttal to the constitutional argument that metropolitan-led elections restore ancient practice.
4. *Historical precedent:* Leo the Great is quoted three times; the schism of Utrecht is invoked; and specific patristic precedents (Theodosius usurping Juvenal's see, Ischyras rewarded for betrayal at Tyre) are adduced. The effect is to frame the French crisis not as unprecedented but as a recurrent pattern in Church history, one that the See has previously survived and corrected.
5. *Preservation of the faithful as primary pastoral goal:* The final sections make clear that the most urgent concern is protecting ordinary Catholics from receiving sacraments from invalid ministers and from being spiritually misled. The language of "ravening wolves," "thieves," and "intruders" frames the schismatic clergy not primarily as theological opponents but as dangers to souls.

**Notable absence:** The document explicitly states it "purposely pass[es] by matters relating to the civil government" (section 17), positioning itself as acting solely within ecclesiastical competence. This rhetorical move attempts to separate the Church's canonical response from any appearance of political opposition to the Revolution — though the practical effect of the decrees (nullifying the Assembly's ecclesiastical legislation) is inherently political.
