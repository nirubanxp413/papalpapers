## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Quare lacrymae

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The execution of King Louis XVI by the French revolutionary assembly was an act of anti-Catholic hatred, rendering him a martyr in the tradition of Mary Queen of Scots, with the deeper roots lying in the deliberate propagation of impious books, false liberty, and false equality that simultaneously destroyed the monarchy and persecuted the Church.
- **Summary:** Addressed to his "Venerable Brothers," Pius VI responds to the beheading of Louis XVI on 21 January 1793 with grief and condemnation. He argues that the true cause of the king's death was hatred of the Catholic faith — not any genuine political crime — and builds a theological argument for recognising Louis as a martyr by analogy with Mary Queen of Scots, whose martyrdom his predecessor (referred to in the third person in a quoted passage) had analysed. The document traces the revolution's origins to Calvinist agitation, the writings of "perverse philosophers" (Voltaire named explicitly), and the seduction of the people with the false slogans of liberty and equality, all of which the pope claims to have warned against in a prior encyclical (*Inscrutabile divinae*, 1775). It ends with a summons to celebrate solemn funeral rites for Louis XVI.
- **Response to:** The execution of Louis XVI by the National Assembly (21 January 1793); the Civil Constitution of the Clergy, which the pope states he had already rejected in his response of 10 March 1791 signed by thirty bishops; the long-running campaign of Calvinist and philosophe agitation against the Catholic Church and the French monarchy described in the text; and earlier papal failures to persuade French Catholics to destroy "poisonous books."
- **Prescriptive towards:** The pope summons his "Venerable Brothers" to celebrate solemn obsequies for Louis XVI in the papal chapel. Beyond this ritual act, his prescription is largely exhortatory and theological: he implores that the innocent blood of Louis may intercede for France to recognise its crimes, and invokes the model of his predecessor Clement VI, who pursued the murderers of King Andrew of Sicily with grave spiritual penalties. He expresses the apostolic duty — citing Bernard of Clairvaux to Pope Eugene IV — to strive for the conversion of unbelievers, the retention of converts, and the return of the lapsed.
- **Tension:** (1) Legitimate monarchical authority vs. popular sovereignty — the document argues the Assembly had "no authority and no right" to condemn the king, and characterises rule by the people as inherently irrational and cruel. (2) The Catholic Church's authority over public life vs. the revolutionary state's claim to legislate on Church organisation (the Civil Constitution of the Clergy). (3) True liberty — understood as order, law, and obedience — vs. the "philosophical liberty" the document says corrupts morals and subverts institutions. (4) Martyrdom theology: whether Louis XVI, who at some point signed the Civil Constitution, can still be considered a martyr — the document resolves this by analogy with cases of retracted error (St Cyprian, Jesuit John de Britto) and by pointing to his documented repentance in his testament.

## Unstructured

**Overall character.** *Quare lacrymae* is an allocution — a formal papal address to the College of Cardinals — rather than an encyclical circulated to all bishops. Its occasion is entirely specific: the guillotining of Louis XVI. The title, "Why these tears," is answered in the opening sentence: the pope says he can barely speak through his grief. The tone throughout is one of controlled outrage alternating with theological argumentation; it is more juridical than pastoral, building a legal-theological case for martyrdom rather than offering comfort.

**The martyrdom argument as the document's spine.** The entire middle section (§§3–11) is a sustained attempt to prove that Louis XVI died a martyr in the technical theological sense — not simply a man killed unjustly, but a witness to the Catholic faith killed *in odium fidei*. Pius VI uses a three-part structure: (a) the precedent of Mary Queen of Scots, whose martyrdom a predecessor pope had tentatively endorsed for the same reason; (b) the assertion that the real motive behind the charges was Louis's refusal to exile non-juring Catholic priests and his stated intention to restore Catholic worship; (c) the resolution of the apparent obstacle (Louis's signing of the Civil Constitution) by analogy with martyrs who had previously erred and then retracted in blood. The argument is lawyerly and careful, not triumphalist.

**Philosophy and the Revolution.** The document explicitly names Voltaire and his *Life* as evidence that the revolution was conceived as a campaign against "religious and priestly power" precisely because, in the revolutionaries' own logic, monarchy and Church were "so bound together that once one was destroyed the other must necessarily fall." The pope also cites an unnamed 1787 book arguing it was praiseworthy to kill a prince unwilling to embrace the reformed religion. These are treated not as background context but as the causal chain: books → corrupted minds → revolution → regicide.

**Liberty and equality as false labels.** §§7–8 offer a sustained critique of both revolutionary watchwords. "Philosophical liberty" is described as aimed at "corrupting souls, depraving morals, subverting the order of laws and all institutions." Equality is dismissed as producing anarchic plurality with no moderating authority — compared to a musical ensemble that degenerates into noise when no one keeps time. Both critiques are drawn verbatim from the 1775 encyclical *Inscrutabile divinae*, which the pope cites at length, framing the current disaster as the predicted consequence of an earlier warning ignored.

**France as fallen champion.** §12 is the document's most rhetorical passage: a direct apostrophe to France, "called by Our predecessors the mirror of all Christendom and the firm pillar of the Faith... how far you are from Us today." The reversal is made sharper by §13: "You who demanded to have a Catholic king... precisely because he was Catholic, you killed him." This framing — France as apostate nation rather than merely misguided — is more condemnatory than the rest of the text and distinguishes the tone here from, say, a pastoral letter.

**Limits of papal power acknowledged.** In §16, the pope admits with unusual candour that the French have not only ignored his warnings but have insulted him, and even forged letters in his name. He explicitly limits what he can do: he will leave in their "miserable depravity" those who choose to persist; he hopes Louis's blood will intercede. The lack of any practical disciplinary action (no excommunication decreed, no formal condemnation beyond what was already given) is striking and is acknowledged within the text itself.

**Tone.** Authoritative in register but with a marked vein of lamentation throughout. The document opens and closes with grief, not command. Its authority is exercised primarily through theological argument and historical analogy rather than through canonical decree. The dominant affect is sorrow that previous warnings went unheeded.
