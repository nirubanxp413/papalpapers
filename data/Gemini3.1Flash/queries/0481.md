## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# A Concilio Constantinopolitano I

*On the Anniversaries of the First Council of Constantinople and the Council of Ephesus*

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The 1600th anniversary of the First Council of Constantinople and the 1550th anniversary of the Council of Ephesus are occasions to renew and deepen faith in the Holy Spirit as the living source of the Church's unity, mission, and ongoing renewal.
- **Summary:** Written on the Solemnity of the Annunciation (March 25, 1981), this Apostolic Letter invites the world's bishops to mark two conciliar anniversaries by celebrating a special Pentecost gathering in Rome — first at St. Peter's Basilica for the Holy Spirit, then at the Basilica of Saint Mary Major for Mary as Theotokos. John Paul II argues that the Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed's affirmations about the Holy Spirit and the Incarnation ("Et incarnatus est de Spiritu Sancto ex Maria Virgine") remain the living doctrinal foundation of the Church, binding today's Church to the early centuries, linking the ancient councils to Vatican II, and providing the spiritual energy needed for authentic renewal and ecumenical progress.
- **Response to:** The letter responds to two specific historical anniversaries (the 16th centenary of the First Council of Constantinople, 381 AD, and the 1550th anniversary of the Council of Ephesus, 431 AD). It also responds to the incomplete implementation of the Second Vatican Council's renewal, to ongoing Christian divisions (East and West), and to "fluctuations of human thought" and "the spirit of the world" threatening doctrinal fidelity.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The letter anticipates a collegial Pentecost celebration in Rome drawing representatives from all Episcopal Conferences, Eastern Patriarchates, and metropolitan provinces. It directs bishops to communicate these anniversaries to their local churches on Holy Thursday and other occasions. More broadly, it points toward the approach of the third millennium and the continued work of Church renewal, ecumenical unity, justice, peace, priestly and religious vocations, and lay apostolate — all to be carried out "in the Holy Spirit."
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the enduring, unchanging truths of faith defined in the ancient councils and the risk of abandoning or diluting those truths under pressure from "a certain regard for the spirit of the world." A second tension lies in the Church's longing for Christian unity — the document notes that the councils belonged to an "undivided Church," yet divisions have occurred that still prevent full communion. A third tension is internal: the need to carry out Vatican II's renewal "maturely and in a regular way" while remaining perfectly faithful to the Spirit's guidance, warning against distortions of the Council's teaching.

## Unstructured

**Argument flow:** The letter moves in five numbered sections. Part I establishes the theological significance of the First Council of Constantinople and its Creed formulation on the Holy Spirit as the "Lord and giver of life." Part II adds the Council of Ephesus and its Christological/Mariological teaching — the two natures of Christ, soteriology ("what is not assumed is not saved"), and the title Theotokos for Mary. Part III draws these two ancient councils into direct continuity with Vatican II, arguing that Vatican II's ecclesiology is deeply rooted in and illuminated by these earlier dogmatic definitions. Part IV makes the pastoral proposal concrete: a two-part Pentecost celebration in Rome (morning at St. Peter's, afternoon at Santa Maria Maggiore). Part V delivers the practical pastoral charge to bishops to carry the message to their local churches.

**Key theological moves:** The document's central argumentative move is to refuse any rupture between ancient and contemporary faith. The Creed recited "by so many generations" is not a relic but a "living" expression; the Holy Spirit who inspired the Nicene and Ephesian Fathers is the same Spirit "rejuvenating the Church by the power of the Gospel" today. John Paul II explicitly quotes Lumen Gentium at length (nos. 4, 56, 58, 59, 63) to demonstrate that Vatican II itself is the contemporary form of the same doctrinal tradition.

**Mary's role:** The Marian dimension is substantial but secondary to the pneumatological (Holy Spirit) theme. Mary appears as the creature "most closely associated with the work of salvation," the first cooperator with the Spirit, the Theotokos whose motherhood establishes a "permanent maternal link with the Church," and the archetype of the Church's own spiritual motherhood. Her presence in the Upper Room at Pentecost (Acts 1:14) explicitly connects the Ephesus anniversary to the Holy Spirit celebration.

**Tone:** Predominantly pastoral and invitational rather than juridically authoritative. The letter addresses the bishops as "dear and venerable brothers" and frequently uses the first-person plural ("we shall gather," "we shall draw"). It is also theologically dense — quoting patristic sources (St. Ambrose), multiple scripture passages, and Vatican II — suggesting it functions simultaneously as catechesis for bishops and as a template for preaching to the faithful. The closing call to prayer is warm and urgent, invoking the Spirit as "fons vivus, ignis, caritas (living water, fire and love)."

**Ecumenical framing:** A notable feature is the letter's explicit ecumenical aspiration. The councils of Constantinople and Ephesus are presented as expressions of "the undivided Church," and their commemoration is offered as a means of "mutual understanding with our beloved brothers in the East and in the West." The ecumenical path is described as one of "prayer, humility, and trust," with the shared Creed and shared pneumatology as the most promising ground for convergence.

**Notable framing — the approaching millennium:** The document situates itself at "the close of the second millennium," looking toward the third. This eschatological-historical awareness lends urgency: the anniversaries are not antiquarian exercises but preparation for the Church's future mission. Christ as "the center of history" — "the same yesterday and today and for ever" (Heb. 13:8) — grounds this forward movement in an unchanging anchor.
