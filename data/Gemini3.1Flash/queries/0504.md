## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Non ambigimus (Benedict XIV, 30 May 1741)

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The erosion of Lenten fasting discipline through reckless dispensations poses a serious danger to the faithful and to the honour of the Catholic Church, and must be arrested by the bishops through zealous pastoral action.
- **Summary:** Benedict XIV writes to the entire episcopate to lament that the ancient and apostolically mandated practice of Lenten fasting has been "almost completely eliminated" by the excessive, indiscriminate, and often trivial granting of dispensations. He identifies this collapse as a source of real spiritual harm — causing scandal among orthodox Catholics, mockery from heretics, and drawing down divine punishment on peoples. The brief instructs bishops to restore strict standards: dispensations must require genuine medical necessity confirmed by two physicians, broad communal dispensations must be sought from Rome, and those who genuinely cannot fast must compensate with prayer, alms, and other pious works.
- **Response to:** The document responds explicitly to a widespread breakdown in the observance of Lenten and other prescribed fasting days. The Pope states that reports reaching him document: dispensations granted "everywhere, indiscriminately, for futile and non-urgent reasons"; public banquets and forbidden meals openly held during fast periods "with impunity"; and a general climate in which some places presume or demand dispensations "impudently and arrogantly" without seeking approval from the Holy See.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document is directed toward restoring episcopal vigilance and correcting abusive dispensation practices. It anticipates: (1) bishops personally leading by word and example to revive the faithful's desire for fasting; (2) rigorous application of the rule requiring two physicians to confirm necessity before any individual dispensation; (3) cases requiring communal or categorical dispensations being referred to the Apostolic See; (4) those lawfully dispensed being limited to a single meal and redirecting to almsgiving and prayer to make reparation.
- **Tension:** The document names a tension between pastoral accommodation (the granting of legitimate dispensations for genuine necessity) and the abuse of that accommodation, which has become so habitual that it amounts to the practical abolition of a fundamental penitential discipline. There is also an implied reputational tension: the Pope notes that the laxity causes "just recriminations from those who follow orthodox religion, while followers of heresies mock and exult" — placing Catholic fasting discipline in a competitive confessional frame, where internal laxity becomes external scandal.

## Unstructured

**Central argument and flow.** The brief opens by grounding Lenten fasting in the deepest possible authorities: prefigured in the Law and Prophets, consecrated by Christ's example, transmitted by the Apostles, and universally prescribed by sacred canons. This historical and theological preamble establishes fasting not as a disciplinary custom that might be adjusted, but as a constitutive mark of the Church ("quasi contrassegno della nostra milizia" — "as if the badge of our militia"). From this high ground the Pope descends into present reality: the practice has been "quasi completamente eliminata" — language that implies something close to crisis rather than mere slippage.

**The dispensation problem as structural, not incidental.** The document's diagnostic centre is not that individual Catholics lack piety, but that the institutional mechanism of dispensation has been corrupted: granted too easily, too broadly, for insufficient reasons, and sometimes without reference to Rome. The Pope even mentions having had to grant a dispensation in Rome himself during the current year for urgent reasons, but insists he expressly decreed that no unlawful banqueting was to result from it — modelling how a legitimate dispensation should be handled. The problem is systemic ("eccessiva facilità di dispensare ovunque, in modo indiscriminato") and structural.

**Tone: authoritative with pastoral warmth.** The document is not primarily punitive. Benedict addresses the bishops as "Nostra gioia e Nostra corona" ("our joy and our crown") and speaks of his own "sincera e assillante preoccupazione" (sincere and pressing concern). He does not threaten censures; instead he appeals to conscience ("facciamo divampare nel cuore dei fedeli il desiderio…"), and closes by asking the bishops to ensure that those who cannot fast still find ways to seek forgiveness through piety and alms. The register shifts between authoritative instruction (explicit rules about physicians, appeals to Rome) and pastoral exhortation (rekindle fervour, set an example).

**Notable framing: fasting as spiritual warfare and social marker.** The text frames fasting not only as personal penance but as a collective act of distinction from enemies — "i nemici della Chiesa" — and a shield against divine wrath ("allontaniamo i fulmini della divina vendetta"). Non-observance is thus not merely a personal failing but a communal spiritual wound with collective consequences: "le sventure dei popoli, le sciagure mortali, pubbliche e private, non trovano origine altrove" ("the misfortunes of peoples, public and private mortal disasters, find their origin nowhere else"). This raises the stakes of the argument from devotional discipline to cosmic and social consequence.

**Confessional dimension.** The Pope mentions that heretics "si fanno beffe ed esultano" (mock and exult) at Catholic laxity. This brief rhetorical move embeds the fasting question in the broader counter-Reformation context of Catholic credibility and discipline vis-à-vis Protestant critique — without elaborating it at length.

**What the document does not address.** The text does not discuss the content of Lenten fasting rules in detail (no definition of what constitutes a fast day meal), does not name specific regions or dioceses, and does not propose formal canonical penalties for violations. The emphasis throughout is on re-educating bishops and restoring their zeal, rather than legislating new norms.
