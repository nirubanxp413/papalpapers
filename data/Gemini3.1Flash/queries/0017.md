# Immortale Dei

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The encyclical argues that the Christian constitution of the State — in which civil and ecclesiastical authority each exercise sovereignty within their proper sphere, in ordered harmony — is the only rational foundation for genuine public welfare and true human liberty, against the modern liberal-rationalist doctrines that would exclude God and the Church from political life.
- **Summary:** Leo XIII develops a comprehensive political theology asserting that all legitimate authority derives from God, that the State therefore has a duty of public religion (specifically toward the one true religion, Catholicism), and that Christ established the Church as a perfect and independent society with its own jurisdiction. These two powers — civil and ecclesiastical — are not rivals but are ordained by God to act in concert, each supreme in its own domain, with the Church holding priority wherever souls and salvation are at stake. The encyclical then diagnoses the "novel conception of law" arising from sixteenth-century religious upheaval and the political revolutions that followed: popular sovereignty without reference to God, unlimited liberty of conscience and press, indifferentism among religions, and the enforced separation or subjugation of the Church by the State. Leo repudiates these as philosophically incoherent and practically destructive, while carefully noting that the Church does not condemn any particular form of government and permits toleration of diverse religious customs where the common good demands it. Catholics are instructed to hold firm to papal teaching, to participate actively in civil life, and to work within existing institutions to restore a Christian ordering of society.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly names the following as what it responds to: (1) the ancient and renewed "hackneyed reproach" that the Church is hostile to civil government and state welfare; (2) the "novel conception of law" produced by the sixteenth-century "passion for innovation" (the Protestant Reformation) which then spread into philosophy and political theory; (3) the revolutionary principles of popular sovereignty, unlimited freedom of thought and publication, religious indifferentism, and the total exclusion of God from civil governance; (4) the practical policies of secular states — suppression of religious orders, spoliation of Church property, state control of Catholic marriage, denial of the Church's status as a perfect society, and forced separation of Church and State; (5) prior condemnations by Gregory XVI (*Mirari Vos*, 1832) and Pius IX (*Quanta Cura* / *Syllabus*, 1864), which Leo invokes as binding precedent.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs Catholics to: (1) accept without deviation all that the Roman Pontiffs have taught or shall teach on matters of public authority and religion; (2) reject the various modern "liberties" — of conscience, press, and indifferentist worship — whose corrupting results are already evident; (3) take an active and prudent part in municipal and national politics, precisely to introduce Christian wisdom into the veins of the State; (4) resolve internal Catholic political disputes and party rivalries in submission to the Holy See; (5) support religious education of youth through public provision; (6) maintain unity of doctrine and action under the guidance of the apostolic see and bishops. The document also implies a hoped-for future in which rulers will voluntarily restore the harmonious union of Church and State that once characterized Christian Europe.
- **Tension:** (1) Between the Church as a perfect and independent society with divine authority and the modern secular State's claim to unlimited sovereignty — a direct jurisdictional conflict, especially over marriage, education, and Church property. (2) Between genuine human liberty (ordered by truth and virtue, protected by the Church) and the "liberty of self-ruin" embodied in liberal doctrines of unlimited freedom of conscience and expression. (3) Between the obligation of Catholics to obey legitimate civil authority and the impossibility of obeying laws that violate Christian teaching — the text acknowledges that Catholics in some nations may face situations where political engagement itself is not expedient. (4) Between a nostalgic ideal of the once-harmonious Christian commonwealth (the medieval synthesis Leo invokes) and the present reality of states that "seem anxious to recede further and further" from Christian principles. (5) Internal to Catholicism: the text warns against Catholics accusing one another of bad faith over legitimate differences in political opinion (e.g., form of government), while still requiring absolute unity on doctrinal matters.

## Unstructured

*Immortale Dei* is a sustained and architecturally careful argument. Leo XIII opens by inverting the charge against the Church — not that the Church is hostile to the State, but that the State, by expelling the Church, harms itself. The document proceeds in three broad movements.

**The constructive case (§§3–22):** Leo builds from natural law upward. Man is social by nature; society requires authority; all authority comes from God. The Church is a perfect society of divine institution, with its own law-making and judicial powers, distinct from but not opposed to civil society. The two powers — priesthood and kingdom — are like soul and body: different in nature and end, but meant to act in ordered harmony. Leo's tone here is philosophical and declarative, drawing on St. Augustine, Ivo of Chartres, and scripture. He invokes a historical golden age when "States were governed by the philosophy of the Gospel," producing the great achievements of Christian Europe (§21). This golden age functions as both proof and aspiration: it demonstrates that the Christian model works, and sets up the contrast with the present disorder.

**The polemical diagnosis (§§23–33):** The sixteenth-century Reformation is named as the root cause of subsequent political disorder — it first disrupted religious unity, then philosophically infected all of civil society, producing the revolutionary principles of popular sovereignty, unlimited liberty, and religious indifferentism. Leo's analysis is structural: these errors are not random but form a coherent (if false) system. Popular sovereignty that excludes God logically produces state atheism; religious indifferentism logically produces the rejection of all religion; unrestrained liberty of press and thought logically degrades morals. He argues these are not merely theologically wrong but "repugnant to reason" and practically destructive — seditions become legally justified, public order is perpetually imperiled.

**The practical and pastoral instructions (§§36–49):** Having condemned the system in principle, Leo carefully distinguishes what is and is not condemned in practice. No particular form of government is condemned; popular participation in government may in some circumstances be obligatory; the Church does not force conversions; toleration of diverse religious practice by rulers for the common good is permissible. Catholics may disagree on prudential political matters without impugning their faith. The pastoral tone sharpens here: Leo addresses Catholics directly, urging participation in politics not as compromise but as infiltration of existing institutions with Christian virtue — he cites Tertullian's boast that Christians already pervaded all Roman institutions. The goal is not withdrawal but redemption of the civic order from within.

**Tone:** Primarily authoritative and magisterial — Leo writes as legislator and judge, cataloguing errors, invoking predecessors, citing scripture and Fathers. But there are pastoral registers: the closing §50 turns to prayer, and the address to the bishops throughout ("venerable brethren") softens the register at key moments. The document never concedes the legitimacy of the opposing position on its own terms; it consistently argues that liberal political theory is not merely imprudent but irrational and contrary to nature.

**Notable framing:** Leo consistently frames the Church's position not as a claim to worldly power but as a defense of the human person's highest good and of true liberty. The modern state's claim to protect "liberty" is reframed as enslavement — slavery to error, passion, and ultimately atheism. The Church, conversely, is presented as the original and continuing guardian of genuine human freedom, civilization, and progress (§§38–39). This is a deliberate rhetorical move to contest the Enlightenment narrative in which the Church is the enemy of liberty and progress.
