## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Decet Quam Maxime

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Church ministers must exercise all spiritual functions entirely free of charge, and Clement XIV enacts detailed binding rules for the Bishops of Sardinia to eradicate the entrenched practice of demanding fees for ordinations, sacraments, benefices, marriages, burials, and diocesan visitations.
- **Summary:** Addressed directly to the Bishops of Sardinia at the request of King Charles Emmanuel, *Decet Quam Maxime* is a sweeping administrative decree cataloguing and prohibiting the many ways in which ecclesiastical officials — from bishops down to notaries — had been extracting payments for spiritual and administrative acts. Drawing heavily on scripture, Church Fathers (Ambrose, Gregory the Great), the Council of Trent, and accumulated rulings of the Sacred Congregations, Clement XIV specifies, item by item, what may never be charged, what narrow exceptions exist (chiefly for unsalaried notaries and chancellors), and what maximal fees apply in those exceptions. The document combines moral condemnation of avarice with precise canonical tariffs, framing unlawful fee-taking as simony — a corruption that destroys ecclesiastical discipline and shames the episcopate.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to: (1) persistent "abuses" and "depraved customs" in Sardinian diocesan curias despite prior papal and conciliar legislation; (2) the failure of Innocent XI's 1678 compilation to achieve universal compliance, partly because it was claimed to apply only within Italy; (3) bishops' reliance on justifications such as "ancient and inveterate custom," the need to reward curia ministers, and insufficient means of support; (4) a direct petition from King Charles Emmanuel of Sardinia for papal intervention; and (5) the gradual spread of fee-taking practices from minor ministry to bishops themselves, sometimes codified in synodal constitutions.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates and directs towards: (1) uniform discipline across all Sardinian dioceses, replacing the existing diversity of fees and practices; (2) full compliance by bishops, vicars general, chancellors, notaries, and household members with the specific tariffs and prohibitions enumerated; (3) bishops actively instructing and restraining subordinates, not merely abstaining from abuses themselves (citing Ambrose: "the hands of your household must also be restrained"); (4) eradication of any custom, however ancient or embedded in synodal constitutions, that violates these norms; and (5) a model of priestly ministry in which freedom from financial interest is itself a public witness to the faithful.
- **Tension:** (1) *Spiritual authority vs. economic necessity*: the document acknowledges that curial ministers lack stipends and must somehow be supported, yet insists the spiritual character of ministry categorically forbids charging for it — resolved by permitting narrow, capped fees for notaries and chancellors only. (2) *Apostolic law vs. entrenched local custom*: ancient diocesan customs, sometimes ratified by synodal authority, are directly declared null and void ("crimes are the more heinous the longer they shackle the spirit," quoting Alexander III). (3) *Episcopal dignity vs. suspicion of avarice*: bishops praised for their piety are nonetheless told their customary revenues include unlawful exactions, and the letter insists they will not be materially harmed if they comply.

## Unstructured

**Document type and tone**

*Decet Quam Maxime* is a regulatory rescript rather than a doctrinal encyclical. Its tone is authoritative throughout — detailed, legally precise, and backed by an extensive apparatus of canon law citations, conciliar decrees, and Sacred Congregation rulings. Where it is pastoral, the pastoral register is brief and framing: the opening invocation of Christ's command to "freely give what you have freely received" (Mt 18:8) and the closing exhortation that bishops place their "true gain" in the worship of God and the feeding of the flock. The bulk of the text reads more like a legal schedule than a spiritual letter.

**Flow of the argument**

The document opens with a theological foundation: ministers of the Church must be free from any suspicion of avarice, as commanded by Christ, Paul, and Peter. It then describes the failure of prior legislation (including Innocent XI's 1678 decree) to correct entrenched abuses, and announces the pope's intervention prompted by the Sardinian king's request. From §3 onwards, the text proceeds topically through the main categories of ecclesiastical life in which fee-taking had occurred:

1. **Holy orders** (§§3–7): No bishop or official may accept anything for conferring any order, with a narrow exception for unsalaried notaries/chancellors (capped at ten obols per dimissorial letter).
2. **Examination of ordained priests** (§8): Fees charged before allowing a newly ordained priest to celebrate first Mass are declared baseless and must be eliminated.
3. **Conferral of benefices** (§§9–15): Detailed rules for free conferral, with limited chancellor fees for edicts, letters of institution, and taking possession, all specified to the julio.
4. **Convents and religious houses** (§§35–36)**: No fees for any act relating to nuns' admission, profession, governance, or daily governance of religious life.
5. **Sacraments** (§§20–24): Administration of all sacraments — including baptism, confirmation, extreme unction, and the distribution of holy oils — must be entirely gratis; examples of Sacred Congregation rulings rejecting even symbolic charges are cited.
6. **Marriage** (§§17–19): All matrimonial acts from dispensations to the proclamation of banns are to be free; only the unsalaried chancellor may receive small fees for specific written acts.
7. **Funerals and burials** (§§25–27): Demanding payment for burial ground or for location of burial is condemned as profiting from grief (Gregory the Great is quoted at length); voluntary offerings for pious purposes are permitted.
8. **Episcopal visitations** (§§28–34): Bishops may accept only "frugal sustenance" during visitation, not money for examining wills, reviewing accounts, or transportation by horse; a series of Sacred Congregation rulings from 1637 to 1768 is marshalled to close each loophole.
9. **Judicial and penal matters** (§§39–40)**: Fines must go to pious uses, not episcopal private gain; ecclesiastical judges may not charge for delivering sentences in spiritual cases.
10. **Miscellaneous acts** (§§37–38): A final enumeration covers preaching licenses, dispensations from feast-day servile work, approving relics and indulgences, letters of poverty, searches of episcopal archives, excommunication letters, pontifical rights, and apostolic letters — all gratis or with minimal chancellor fees.

**Key framing devices**

- *Simony* is the organizing concept. Fee-taking for spiritual acts is not treated merely as a financial irregularity but as a canonical crime carrying *ipso facto* penalties.
- *Custom cannot excuse corruption*: the letter explicitly addresses and rejects the defense of ancient custom at both its opening (describing how bishops "put forward various excuses, such as ancient and inveterate custom") and its close (quoting Alexander III that crimes are worse the longer they persist).
- *The king's role*: the explicit mention of Charles Emmanuel of Sardinia as the one who "requested" these decrees is notable — it frames the intervention as responsive to civil authority seeking ecclesiastical reform, a co-operative rather than adversarial church-state framing.
- *Bishops are not blamed*: Clement XIV carefully absolves the Sardinian bishops of personal responsibility for the abuses (§2: "you can in no way be blamed; rather you are worthy of commendation"), attributing them to historical accumulation and ministerial subordinates — a diplomatic framing that makes compliance easier to embrace.
- *Precision as a tool of reform*: the extreme specificity of the permitted fees (ten obols, five julios, one aureus, sixteen-and-a-half julios) reflects a reform strategy of removing all interpretive discretion that might allow abuses to re-enter under a new guise.
