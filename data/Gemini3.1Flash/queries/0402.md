## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Libentissime quidem

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual | Social | Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The encyclical clarifies and enforces the rules of Lenten fasting and abstinence, addressing abuses in the granting of dispensations and establishing precise conditions under which bishops may legitimately exempt individuals or entire communities from these obligations.
- **Summary:** Benedict XIV addresses the erosion of Lenten fasting discipline across the Catholic world. He recounts his earlier apostolic letters of 1741 (Non ambigimus and In suprema) which required that any dispensation to eat meat during fasting periods must be paired with the obligation of a single daily meal and the prohibition against mixing meat and fish at the same table. He then reproduces his 1744 letter to the Archbishop of Compostela, answering seven specific doctrinal questions about these rules. He further examines what constitutes a legitimate cause for granting collective dispensations to entire cities or dioceses — rejecting high food prices and the presence of soldiers as sufficient causes — and affirms that genuine epidemic disease, attested in writing by physicians, may justify such broad dispensations. He calls on bishops to stop the habit of routinely and too easily granting communal Lenten exemptions, and closes with an appeal for renewed pastoral vigilance.
- **Response to:** The widespread relaxation of Lenten fasting discipline in Italian cities and dioceses; routine grants of collective exemptions from abstinence for whole communities; theological disputes among Spanish theologians about the application of the 1741 apostolic letters; a letter from the Archbishop of Compostela (since deceased) raising seven specific doubts; a request from the Prefect of Jesuit missions in Santo Domingo regarding dispensations for remote mission populations; and recurring epidemic chest diseases across Europe (1730, 1733, 1740) that prompted medical-based dispensation requests.
- **Prescriptive towards:** Bishops are directed to cease granting collective Lenten dispensations too easily; to require written medical testimony before allowing communal exemptions; to enforce the rule of a single daily meal and the prohibition on mixing meat and fish for anyone holding a meat dispensation; to resolve doctrinal doubts through reference to the Apostolic See rather than individual theological opinion; and to reinvigorate popular observance of Lenten discipline as a matter of mortification of the flesh.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the practical accommodation of human weakness (illness, poverty, food scarcity, the presence of armies) and the integrity of the Church's ancient fasting discipline. A secondary tension exists between episcopal and theological autonomy — particularly of Spanish theologians who generated competing opinions — and the normative authority of the Apostolic See. A third tension is implied between the pastoral responsibility to care for the faithful and the risk of enabling the progressive dissolution of penitential practice through habitual over-dispensation.

## Unstructured

The document is structured as a papal administrative letter with strong legislative intent. Its tone is unmistakably authoritative: Benedict XIV explicitly positions himself as one who resolves controversies rather than invites further debate, and he expresses impatience with the "cavils" and "sophisms" of theologians who complicate what he regards as clear law. At the same time, the letter contains genuine pastoral warmth — he praises bishops who have responded faithfully, acknowledges his own heavy burden of duties, and closes with a blessing that is affectionate rather than merely formal.

The argument moves in three distinct stages. First, the pope rehearses the regulatory history: his 1741 letters, the subsequent theological controversies, and the resolution provided through the Sacred Tribunal of the Penitentiary and his personal letter to the Archbishop of Compostela. The reproduction of the Compostela letter in full is a deliberate pedagogical device — it makes canonical answers available to all bishops, not just those who have access to the Italian theological commentaries that have already published it. The seven questions and answers are crisp and declarative, leaving no room for interpretation: the single-meal rule applies at all times fasting is prescribed; the prohibition on mixing meat and fish is absolute; the Bulla Crociata is a separate instrument that must be interpreted on its own terms.

The second stage examines the broader abuse: whole cities and dioceses have been obtaining standing exemptions from Lenten abstinence, some for many years running, effectively nullifying the discipline for entire populations. Benedict systematically dismantles the main arguments offered to justify such collective exemptions — high fish or egg prices, the presence of troops — arguing that neither constitutes a genuine necessity comparable to individual illness. He invokes Innocent III and the eighth Council of Toledo to anchor his criteria in canonical tradition, and provides a positive rule: genuine collective dispensation is justified only where food necessary for fasting observance is truly unavailable, or where epidemic disease threatens the whole population, and then only with written medical attestation.

The third stage is the case study of Santo Domingo, which serves as a limiting example of a legitimate broad dispensation — remote location, documented food scarcity, inability to seek annual recourse to Rome — granted under strict conditions including the single-meal rule and the binding of the mission prefects' consciences.

Throughout, the pope draws on his own pre-pontifical experience as Archbishop of Bologna, citing his earlier Mandatum and Institutiones as sources he has already thought through these questions carefully. This self-referencing lends the document an unusual texture: it reads partly as administrative consolidation of a long-running reform effort rather than an entirely new pronouncement. The document's final exhortation — urging bishops to rediscover through practice that Lenten abstinence causes no actual harm — reflects a conviction that the problem is at least partly one of misperception and habit, not just theological confusion.
