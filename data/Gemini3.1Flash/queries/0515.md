## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Ad nostras manus

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Pope warns that a man claiming to be both Bishop of Agra and Apostolic Vicar in France is a fraudulent usurper never appointed by Rome, and orders bishops to protect the faithful from him and reject all his acts as null and sacrilegious.
- **Summary:** A brief but urgent letter, *Ad nostras manus* responds to a document called the "Manifeste de l'armée chrétienne et royale" (June 1793), which directed deposed French clergy to turn to a self-styled "Bishop of Agra and Apostolic Vicar" residing at Saint-Laurent sur Sèvre for guidance. Pius VI acknowledges the Christian and royal army's praiseworthy intention of restoring Catholicism in France, but insists this particular channel of authority is illegitimate: no such bishop was ever appointed or recognized by Rome. The man identified as "Guillot de Folleville" has usurped both titles; his acts are null and sacrilegious. The Pope mobilizes the French episcopal hierarchy — especially the bishops of Poitiers and Saint-Pol de Léon — to alert their diocesans, and calls on the pseudo-bishop himself to repent and submit to canonical discipline. The letter closes with pastoral reassurance: the Apostolic See will supply legitimate help to any locality left without proper ecclesiastical authority by the ongoing persecution, and the Pope prays for the bishops' eventual return to their sees.
- **Response to:** The letter explicitly responds to the circulation of the "Manifeste de l'armée chrétienne et royale au peuple français à Clisson, ce premier Juin 1793," which directed deposed priests and vicars to seek guidance from the claimed "Bishop of Agra, Apostolic Vicar." The document also responds to the broader context (stated in §5) of bishops having been forcibly expelled from their sees and the spiritual needs of the faithful being disrupted by "the persecution of adversaries."
- **Prescriptive towards:** The letter directs (a) all French bishops to warn their diocesans against the pseudo-bishop; (b) the bishops of Poitiers and Saint-Pol de Léon in particular to act with full zeal given their geographic proximity; (c) Guillot de Folleville himself to cease all usurped functions, abandon his "empio crimine," and seek absolution from the Church under the terms of the sacred canons. Looking forward, the Pope commits the Apostolic See to providing "prompt and legitimate help" to any region left without lawful ecclesiastical authority, and expresses hope that the bishops will soon return freely to their sees.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the urgent need to restore and sustain Catholic religious life in France (a goal the Pope explicitly praises in the Christian and royal army's manifesto) and the equally urgent need to maintain legitimate apostolic authority and hierarchical order — the very condition required for any authentic restoration to be possible. A secondary tension is acknowledged in §5: bishops have been coerced into abandoning their sees, leaving the faithful potentially bereft of legitimate pastors, a pastoral emergency that the Apostolic See must address through extraordinary delegation.

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument.** The letter is compact (five numbered paragraphs) and moves in a tight logical arc: (1) a suspicious document has arrived claiming a certain Apostolic Vicar can guide deposed clergy; (2) the Pope cannot verify the manifesto's authenticity, but regardless, the named individual holds no papal mandate; (3) all bishops must warn the faithful; (4) the pseudo-bishop must repent and submit; (5) the Pope acknowledges the real pastoral crisis left by episcopal exile and pledges the Apostolic See's ongoing support.

**Tone.** The document is primarily authoritative and juridical — Pius VI is issuing a formal canonical warning against an invalid exercise of episcopal and apostolic authority. The language of nullity and sacrilege ("atti sacrileghì... viziati dal marchio della nullità") is precise canonical terminology. Yet the closing paragraph shifts to an almost pastoral register: "voglia il Signore che i Nostri comuni voti si compiano" ("may the Lord grant that our common desires be fulfilled"), expressing genuine pastoral affection for the French Church.

**Notable framing — distinguishing intention from channel.** Pius VI is careful not to condemn the royalist and Christian army's goal. He says their intention to restore Catholicism "seems praiseworthy to the highest degree" ("non senza la massima lode"). His objection is entirely structural: by routing clergy through an unauthorized claimant, they are actually widening "a broader path to error" and causing "grave mystification" for the faithful. This framing allows the Pope to align himself with the Catholic restoration cause while simultaneously asserting that the Petrine office alone confers authentic apostolic mandate.

**Apostolic succession as the anchor.** The entire logic of the letter rests on the principle that ecclesiastical authority flows from legitimate appointment by Rome. Without that, even well-intentioned pastoral work is sacrilegious — null by definition. The pseudo-bishop's name and location are named specifically, and local bishops are directed to act on this by both proximity and canonical obligation.

**Geographical specificity.** The reference to Saint-Laurent sur Sèvre (in the diocese of Poitiers, near the diocese of Saint-Pol de Léon) and the explicit singling out of the bishops of Poitiers and Brittany give the letter a highly targeted, operational quality unusual for a general circular. It is addressed broadly (Cardinals, Archbishops, Bishops, all clergy and people of France) but its practical instruction is geographically focused on western France — the Vendée-Poitou-Bretagne region where the "armée chrétienne et royale" was actively operating.

**Silence on the manifesto's political content.** The letter makes no theological or political comment on the royalist military campaign itself, the Revolution, or the question of legitimate civil government. Its scope is strictly ecclesiastical: the validity of a specific claimed appointment. This is a notable restraint — the document is not a political manifesto in reply to a political manifesto, but a jurisdictional clarification in response to an unauthorized pastoral claim.

- **Do not extrapolate.** If media coverage, academic citation, or historical documentation is thin or absent, say so plainly (`insufficient evidence`, `no reliable sources located`) and leave fields sparse. Do not fill gaps with plausible narrative.
- **Distinguish source types** throughout the report — never conflate them:

  - **Media** — newspapers, magazines, broadcast, modern digital journalism; report what was *said* in public discourse, not necessarily what *was*.
  - **Academic scholarship** — peer-reviewed or established historiography interpreting past events (label as interpretation, not primary fact).
