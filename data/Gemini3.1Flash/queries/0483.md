# Fides et Ratio (as filed under Evangelium Vitae)

*Encyclical Letter of John Paul II to the Bishops of the Catholic Church on the Relationship Between Faith and Reason (1998)*

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** Faith and reason are not rivals but two complementary wings by which the human spirit rises toward truth; their separation impoverishes both and must be overcome.
- **Summary:** The document argues that a profound and original unity exists between philosophical reason and Christian faith, each needing the other for its full flourishing. Drawing on Scripture, the Patristic tradition, Aquinas, and the history of philosophy, John Paul II diagnoses a contemporary "crisis of meaning" rooted in modern philosophy's abandonment of metaphysics and ultimate truth. He calls philosophers to recover wisdom's sapiential horizon, cautions theology against fideism and rationalism alike, and insists that genuine metaphysical enquiry—one capable of reaching being, God, and moral truth—is indispensable both for understanding Revelation and for answering the deepest questions of human existence.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly responds to (a) the modern philosophical turn away from metaphysics toward subjectivism, relativism, nihilism, scientism, pragmatism, and historicism; (b) the crisis of confidence in universal and absolute truth widespread in contemporary culture; (c) the centenary of Leo XIII's *Aeterni Patris* and the First Vatican Council's *Dei Filius*; and (d) errors within theology itself, including fideism, uncritical biblicism, and the neglect of philosophical formation.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs theologians, philosophers, seminary formators, and scientists toward: renewing philosophy's sapiential dimension and metaphysical range; restoring the harmony between faith and reason; pursuing "a new evangelization" that engages contemporary cultures through philosophical dialogue; and ensuring that scientific and technological progress remains ordered to genuinely human ends. It specifically anticipates the challenges of the "new millennium."
- **Tension:** The central tension is between authentic philosophical reason—oriented toward being, ultimate truth, and the absolute—and the dominant currents of modern and postmodern thought, which the document describes as having "wilted under the weight of so much knowledge" and retreated into subjectivism, immanentism, and nihilism. A secondary tension runs between the legitimate autonomy of philosophy and its necessary openness to what Revelation can offer; and between the Church's duty to discern errors in philosophical systems and its lack of any official philosophy of its own.

## Unstructured

The encyclical opens with its most famous line—"Faith and reason are like two wings on which the human spirit rises to the contemplation of truth"—which serves as the structural and rhetorical pivot of the entire argument. Everything that follows is an elaboration of this metaphor: what happens when one wing is clipped, how the wings were once harmonized, and what must be done to restore their joint flight.

**Flow of argument.** The document moves in a spiral rather than a straight line. It begins with Scripture (Wisdom literature, Paul) to establish that the human being is constitutively a seeker of truth, then surveys the historical encounter between Christianity and Greek philosophy (Justin, Clement, Origen, Augustine, Aquinas), before diagnosing the modern separation of faith from reason—idealism, positivism, nihilism, the "end of metaphysics"—as the root of a contemporary "crisis of meaning." The final chapters draw programmatic conclusions for both philosophy and theology. The encyclical is addressed formally to bishops but in practice to three overlapping audiences: theologians (who must not neglect philosophy), philosophers (who must not abandon metaphysics), and the broader culture (which has lost confidence in the possibility of truth).

**Key recurrent moves.** John Paul II consistently avoids condemning philosophy wholesale while targeting specific errors: *eclecticism* (using incompatible philosophical fragments without internal coherence), *historicism* (making truth relative to its historical moment), *scientism* (dismissing non-empirical knowledge as meaningless), *pragmatism* (subordinating ethical principles to technical utility), and above all *nihilism* ("the denial of all foundations and the negation of all objective truth"). Each is shown to flow logically from the prior abandonment of metaphysics.

**Tone.** The encyclical is strongly authoritative in its diagnoses—"I make this strong and insistent appeal"—yet pastoral and dialogical in its posture: the Church "follows the work of philosophers with interest and appreciation" and explicitly excludes no one from the invitation to dialogue. It appeals to philosophers as allies, not adversaries, and frames philosophical enquiry as a genuinely noble human vocation.

**Notable framing.** The document treats the relationship between faith and reason as *circular*, not hierarchical: reason prepares the ground for faith (the Anselmic *credo ut intellegam*), and faith in turn expands reason's horizons beyond what it could reach unaided (*intellego ut credam*). Saint Thomas Aquinas is held up as the supreme historical model of this synthesis—not because the Church canonizes Thomism, but because "in him the demands of reason and the power of faith found the most elevated synthesis ever attained by human thought." The final image—Mary as *Seat of Wisdom* and model of authentic philosophy—closes the document with a contemplative and Marian register, framing philosophical submission to truth as analogous to the Virgin's *fiat*.

**Implicit urgency.** Throughout, the text is conscious of standing at a historical threshold: "the threshold of the third millennium." The proliferation of philosophical systems, the "fragmentation of knowledge," the rise of postmodern doubt, and the erosion of shared moral foundations all create a moment the document treats as both perilous and opportune—a moment that demands exactly the kind of renewed faith-reason synthesis the encyclical proposes.
