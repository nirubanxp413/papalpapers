# Instruction Concerning the Criteria for the Discernment of Vocations with Regard to Persons with Homosexual Tendencies in View of Their Admission to the Seminary and to Holy Orders

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual | Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Church must exercise careful discernment to ensure that candidates for holy orders possess the affective maturity and qualities — including the absence of deep-seated homosexual tendencies — necessary to sacramentally represent Christ as head, shepherd, and spouse of the Church.
- **Summary:** This Instruction establishes binding norms for the admission of candidates to seminaries and holy orders, specifically prohibiting those who practice homosexuality, manifest deep-seated homosexual tendencies, or support "gay culture." It grounds this exclusion in the theological claim that the priest sacramentally represents Christ as bridegroom of the Church, which requires a particular form of affective maturity, and it assigns responsibility for discernment to bishops, seminary rectors, spiritual directors, and candidates themselves. While affirming that persons with homosexual tendencies must be treated with respect and not subjected to unjust discrimination, the document insists the Church retains the right and duty to determine who is suitable for ordination, and that concealing homosexuality to gain admission constitutes grave dishonesty.
- **Response to:** The document explicitly situates itself as a response to "the current situation" — described as making more urgent the question of whether to admit candidates with deep-seated homosexual tendencies — and to the accumulation of prior Magisterial teaching since Vatican II, including the Catechism of the Catholic Church, *Pastores dabo vobis*, and multiple Congregation documents on priestly formation and sexual ethics.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The Instruction is directed toward future seminary admissions processes; it anticipates and directs bishops, major superiors, seminary rectors, and spiritual directors to apply stricter discernment criteria, and requires candidates with transitory (non-deep-seated) homosexual tendencies to have clearly overcome them at least three years before ordination to the diaconate.
- **Tension:** Several tensions are explicitly named or clearly implied: (1) between the Church's call to treat persons with homosexual tendencies with respect and sensitivity while simultaneously barring them from ordination; (2) between the candidate's own freedom and desire for priesthood and the Church's sovereign authority to determine suitability; (3) between the secrecy obligations of the spiritual director and his duty to dissuade unsuitable candidates; (4) between the candidate's right to privacy and the obligation of honest self-disclosure to Church authorities.

## Unstructured

**Key Themes**

The document's central theological argument is built on the concept of *configuration to Christ*: through ordination, the Holy Spirit configures the priest to Christ as head, shepherd, and — crucially — *spouse* of the Church. This spousal framing does the heaviest conceptual work in the document. Because the priest must be a "living image" of Christ the Bridegroom, a particular form of affective ordering is treated as a precondition for valid priestly identity. Affective maturity — defined as the capacity to relate correctly to both men and women and to develop "spiritual fatherhood" — is presented as the anthropological hinge on which the exclusion of homosexual candidates turns.

**Flow of the Argument**

The document proceeds through four logical steps:
1. Theological foundation: the priest as sacramental icon of Christ the Bridegroom requires affective maturity and spiritual fatherhood.
2. Doctrinal statement: homosexual acts are grave sins; deep-seated homosexual tendencies are "objectively disordered" — language drawn directly from the Catechism and the CDF's *Homosexualitatis problema*. Persons with such tendencies deserve respect but not ordination.
3. Institutional procedure: discernment is a shared responsibility distributed among bishop, rector, spiritual director, confessor, and candidate — each with distinct but complementary duties.
4. Candidate obligation: the candidate bears primary responsibility for his own formation and must not conceal his homosexuality, as doing so constitutes a breach of the "spirit of truth, loyalty and openness" required for ministerial priesthood.

**Tone**

The tone is firmly authoritative — this is a normative Instruction, not a pastoral guideline. It draws extensively on Canon Law (citing specific canons), prior Congregation documents, Synodal teaching, and Catechism passages to establish that the norms articulated are not novelties but applications of existing, stable Church teaching. The vocabulary is technical and juridical (e.g., "morally certain judgment," "internal forum," "serious doubt"). There is a brief pastoral register in the acknowledgment that persons with homosexual tendencies must be "accepted with respect and sensitivity" and that "every sign of unjust discrimination" should be avoided — but this is offered as a parenthetical carve-out within a document otherwise structured to enforce exclusion.

**Notable Framing**

The document is careful to distinguish between "deep-seated" homosexual tendencies (permanently disqualifying) and "transitory" tendencies (disqualifying unless overcome at least three years before diaconal ordination). This distinction softens the absolute character of the prohibition while preserving it structurally. The document also pre-empts the charge of unjust discrimination by citing the CDF's own *Some Considerations Concerning the Response to Legislative Proposals on Nondiscrimination of Homosexual Persons*, asserting that declining to ordain those without necessary qualities is not discriminatory. The framing thus positions the exclusion as a matter of fitness for a specific role rather than as a moral judgment against persons as such — though the theological language of "objective disorder" in the cited Catechism passages complicates this distinction. The document closes by grounding its authority in explicit papal approval by Benedict XVI (dated August 31, 2005), lending it the weight of pontifical confirmation.
