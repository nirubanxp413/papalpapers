# Ubi primum accepimus

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Pope Clement XIII endorses Archduke Joseph of Austria as King of the Romans and urges the assembled Electors of the Holy Roman Empire to use their gathering as an occasion to defend the Christian faith and the rights of the Church.
- **Summary:** Writing to the Electors of the Holy Roman Empire convened at Frankfurt, Clement XIII expresses great joy at the planned election of a King of the Romans, explicitly naming Archduke Joseph of Austria as his preferred candidate on account of Joseph's exceptional intellect, royal virtues, and hereditary piety drawn from both the Lorraine and Austrian bloodlines. The Pope then pivots to a spiritual charge: beyond the election itself, the Electors should dedicate their assembly to the protection of the Christian Religion and the "most holy rights of the Church." He closes by introducing his Apostolic Nuncio, Archbishop Nicholas of Traianopolis, as his personal representative and asking the Electors to receive and trust him as they would the Pope himself.
- **Response to:** The news that the Electors were gathering in Frankfurt to elect a King of the Romans ("Non appena venimmo a conoscenza che da parte vostra si pensava di eleggere il Re dei Romani"); the need to "sustain and strengthen" the Empire with new support; and an implied concern that the assembly might not give sufficient attention to the protection of religion and the Church's rights.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The election of Archduke Joseph of Austria specifically; the deliberate defense of Christian faith and Church rights by the assembled Electors ("prendiate quelle decisioni che agli occhi di tutti dimostrino che Voi avete voluto consultarvi per l'incolumità della fede e per i santissimi diritti della Chiesa"); and the proper reception of, and deference to, the Apostolic Nuncio as the Pope's personal voice at the diet.
- **Tension:** The document implicitly navigates the tension between temporal imperial authority and the spiritual authority of the Holy See — the Pope does not command the Electors but "supplicates and implores" them ("vi supplichiamo e scongiuriamo"), framing obedience to the Nuncio as a demonstration of Catholic devotion rather than political submission. A secondary tension exists between the hope that a strong Catholic emperor will benefit the Church and the uncertainty that the Electors will act with the requisite piety; the Pope's urgency ("ogni pensiero, con comune passione") implies this is not guaranteed.

## Unstructured

**Genre and occasion.** This is a brief (Breve), a relatively intimate papal letter, not a formal encyclical. It is addressed directly and personally to the Prince-Electors gathered at Frankfurt — a highly specific, occasion-driven document rather than a general teaching letter. Its brevity is deliberate; the Pope himself says he will not "dilungarci oltre" (prolong the matter further) and instead delegates his full argument to the Nuncio.

**Flow of argument.** The brief moves in three steps. First, a declaration of joy and hope: the planned election signals the possibility of a stronger Empire that will in turn strengthen the Church and the Catholic world ("anche la stessa Chiesa e l'intero mondo Cattolico avranno forze più valide"). Second, a personal endorsement: Joseph of Austria is named and praised — his intellect, his royal habits, his inherited piety from the Lorraine and Austrian houses. The Pope states plainly that he "greatly desires" this election and that he "rightly promises himself great and illustrious things" from Joseph's virtues. Third, a spiritual charge and a diplomatic introduction: the Electors are urged to protect the faith, and the Nuncio is formally commended to them.

**Tone.** The tone moves between pastoral warmth and mild diplomatic pressure. The Pope uses affectionate address ("dilettissimo figlio in Cristo," "Venerabili Fratelli e diletti Nobili Figli") and characterises his wishes as hope and supplication rather than decree. Yet the framing is pointed: receiving the Nuncio with honour will be a "sign of your devotion" to the Holy See, and the Electors' piety will be demonstrated by how they act — implying that failure to act correctly would be a failure of Catholic identity. The verb "scongiuriamo" (we implore/adjure) adds urgency.

**Notable framing.** The document ties the health of the Empire directly to the health of the Church: a strong King of the Romans is not primarily a political benefit but a "new aid" (nuovo soccorso) for the Church and the Catholic world. Conversely, the Electors' religious obligations are framed as naturally belonging to their political assembly — protecting religion is presented not as an extra demand but as the obvious purpose of their gathering. The personal qualities of Archduke Joseph (piety, royal discipline, noble bloodline) are the explicit criteria by which the Pope judges him fit — there is no appeal to dynastic right or legal succession; the warrant is entirely moral and religious.

**Limits of the text.** The document does not name any specific threat to religion or to the Church's rights, nor does it explain what "protecting the faith" would concretely require of the Electors. These specifics are deferred entirely to the Nuncio, whose oral communication is given equal authority to the Pope's own words. The brief thus functions partly as a credential letter for Archbishop Nicholas, with the substantive papal agenda reserved for direct diplomatic conversation.
