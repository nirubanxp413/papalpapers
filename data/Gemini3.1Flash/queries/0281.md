# Levate

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Church faces coordinated assault from hostile secular powers in Italy, Russia, and Poland, and the faithful must respond with united prayer to defend her liberty and unity.
- **Summary:** Pius IX writes to the global episcopate to describe two converging crises: the military and political threat to Rome by the Piedmont government, whose forces are approaching the city and arming rebels against the Holy See; and the systematic suppression of Catholic dioceses, bishops, and institutions in the Russian Empire and Polish Kingdom — including forced episcopal removals, the closure of the Catholic Academy of Warsaw, and a new governmental "Ecclesiastical College" designed to intercept and filter all communications between the faithful and Rome. The Pope frames both as manifestations of the same diabolical spirit, calls on bishops to organize three days of public prayer throughout their dioceses within six months, and grants a plenary indulgence for the faithful who participate.
- **Response to:** (1) The military advance of Piedmont forces on Rome, arming insurgents and fortifying approaches to the city; (2) Russian imperial decrees suppressing the Polish dioceses of Podlachia and Kamieniec, expelling their bishops, and establishing a government-controlled "Roman Catholic Ecclesiastical College" in St. Petersburg to intercept papal communications; (3) The closing of the Catholic Academy of Warsaw and threats to the Ruthenian dioceses of Chelm and Belzium; (4) The case of priest Woicicki, who accepted diocesan administration from civil authorities in defiance of ecclesiastical penalties.
- **Prescriptive towards:** A universal three-day period of public prayer in all dioceses (within six months; one year for overseas dioceses); continued episcopal vigilance over clergy and faithful; prayerful intercession that God thwart the plans of hostile powers and recall persecutors to repentance.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between the supreme authority of the Apostolic See (over doctrine, episcopal jurisdiction, and communication with the faithful) and the claims of secular governments to regulate, filter, or suppress those same ecclesiastical functions. A second tension runs between the pope's pastoral duty to communicate with and provide for the faithful in persecuted regions and his practical inability to do so ("since We cannot communicate with those faithful"). A spiritual tension also underlies the document: the pope is confident in God's ultimate protection yet is compelled to name the real danger and sorrow of the Church's present condition.

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument.** The document moves in three phases. It opens with a vivid, emotionally charged denunciation of Rome's immediate danger — "phalanxes of rebels," "ravening wolves," men "animated by a diabolical spirit" — and identifies the Piedmont government as actively enabling the assault. The second, longer phase shifts to Russia and Poland, cataloguing specific decrees and institutional acts of suppression in precise legal language (named dioceses, dates, decree content). This second phase is more administrative than rhetorical: the Pope is formally notifying the episcopate of documented actions and of the remedial canonical measures he has taken (inserting an Act via the printed word to maintain jurisdiction over the suppressed dioceses). The third phase is the call to prayer and the grant of indulgences, which closes the document on a pastoral and consoling note.

**Tone.** The opening passages are notably emotional and martial — "triumphal impiety," "ravening wolves," "the standard of Satan." The middle section becomes cooler and more juridical as the Pope enumerates specific governmental acts. The conclusion is pastoral and affirming. The overall posture is one of embattled but tranquil confidence: "We tranquilly await whatever events may bring … We place all Our hope in the God of Our salvation."

**Notable framing.** The Pope explicitly links the Italian crisis and the Russian-Polish crisis as expressions of the same underlying hostility ("We have other evils to deplore, too"), unifying disparate geopolitical situations under a single theological narrative: the Church under assault from secular powers aligned with evil. The Governmental College in St. Petersburg receives particularly detailed condemnation: it is described as destroying the Church's constitution, injuring "our supreme Pontificate," and trampling natural law — suggesting the Pope regards the interception of communications between the faithful and Rome as not merely an administrative inconvenience but a fundamental ecclesiological violation. The document also makes conspicuous use of physical geography and proximity — the enemies are "before the very Chair of Peter" — to convey the existential nature of the threat.

**Quotable anchors from the text:**
- "Lift up your eyes, venerable brothers. Look about you and grieve at the evil abominations which now defile unhappy Italy."
- "They plan to raise the standard of lies in this beloved city of Ours, before the very Chair of Peter, the center of Catholic truth and unity."
- "We cannot communicate with those faithful … We have been compelled to insert an Act in Our publication to provide for the exercise of legitimate jurisdiction."
- "This schismatic secular power destroys the constitution of the Catholic Church and subverts ecclesiastical discipline."
- "In these calamitous difficulties, no one fights for Us except Our Lord and God."
