## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# In apostolatus culmine

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Pope grants apostolic authority to found a full university in Santo Domingo, framing it as the institutional completion of the Dominicans' missionary conversion of the island's indigenous population.
- **Summary:** This is a founding bull (bula de fundación) issued by Paul III in October 1538, establishing the University of Santo Domingo in the convent of the Dominican friars on the island of Hispaniola. The document narrates that the Dominicans had already converted "innumerable persons of both sexes" from idolatry to the Christian faith, and that a studium already existed in the city but lacked the apostolic privileges needed to confer degrees equivalent to those granted by the universities of Spain. The bull formally erects a University of Doctors, Masters, and Scholars, modeled on the University of Alcalá, complete with seal, treasury chest, degrees (Bachelor, Licentiate, Doctorate, Master), and governance by a Regent/Rector — with all the privileges, immunities, and exemptions enjoyed by the Spanish universities of Alcalá and Salamanca.
- **Response to:** (1) The prior condition of the island's inhabitants as infidels who "worshiped idols" (*idola colebant*), which the Dominicans sought to "eradicate root and branch" (*radicitus extirpare*). (2) The city of Santo Domingo's characterization as lying in a "very remote region, completely ignorant of sacred letters" (*in regione valde remota, ac sacrarum literarum prorsus ignara*). (3) The existing studium's lack of sufficient apostolic privileges and indults to promote scholars to degrees equivalent to those of the Spanish universities, which the petition explicitly identifies as a gap.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) The city will be further adorned and its population multiplied as a result of the university (*dicta Civitas multiplicatis propterea illius habitatoribus per amplius decoraretur*). (2) The inhabitants of the city and surrounding islands will be "more fervently instructed in the Christian religion" (*in Religione Christiana ferventius instruerentur*). (3) The Provincial, Prior, and friars will be "more readily invited to greater works of virtue and charity" (*ad majora virtutum, et caritatis opera impendendum promptius invitarentur*). (4) Scholars — both secular and religious, from anywhere — may pursue and receive degrees in any lawful faculty of letters.
- **Tension:** The document holds two tensions in productive balance. First, a geographic and institutional tension: the city is "very noteworthy" (*admodum insignis*) yet lies in a "very remote region completely ignorant of sacred letters," receiving a large multitude from neighboring islands, yet lacking the academic legitimacy conferred by apostolic privileges. Second, a jurisdictional tension: the islands are subject to the kingdoms of Spain and their universities, yet the local studium lacks the privileges enjoyed by those universities — a gap the bull closes by explicit derogation of any contrary privileges, statutes, or apostolic letters, even those granted *motu proprio*.

## Unstructured

**Nature of the document.** This is not an encyclical or pastoral letter in the classical sense — it is a formal papal bull (*bula*) constituting a legal act: the erection and institution of a university. Its tone is almost entirely juridical and formulaic, organized in the dense, clause-stacked style of curial Latin. The pastoral and missionary rationale is offered as preamble and justification (*narratio*), but the operative substance (*dispositio*) is a precise grant of rights, privileges, and governance structures.

**Missionary conversion as the founding premise.** The document opens its narrative of the petition by rehearsing what the Dominicans had already done: the inhabitants of the Ocean Islands (*Insularum Maris Oceani*) had been infidels and idol-worshippers; the friars had "directed innumerable persons of both sexes from among these same inhabitants under the washing of regeneration to the worship of the Christian religion by divine grace" and "gathered very abundant fruits in the Lord's field by preaching the Word of God ceaselessly." The university is thus presented not as an independent intellectual project but as the natural next phase of a completed evangelization — moving from conversion to formation.

**The studium already exists; the problem is legal standing.** The document reveals that a *studium* had already been erected with apostolic authority (*apostolica auctoritate noviter erectum viget*), but that it lacked the privileges and indults of the Spanish universities sufficient for the promotion to the usual degrees (*graduum concedi solitorum in Universitatibus studiorum Generalium Regnorum Hispaniae*). The petition is therefore not for something entirely new but for full juridical equivalence — to close the gap between a functioning school and a fully accredited institution.

**Modeling on Alcalá.** Throughout both the petition and the grant, the University of Alcalá (*oppido de Alcala, Toletanae Diocesis*) is the explicit template. The bull grants the new university the same privileges as Alcalá and Salamanca. This is a specific institutional reference the document makes explicitly and repeatedly, suggesting the Dominicans saw Alcalá — a relatively recent, humanist-inflected Spanish university — as their benchmark.

**Governance and procedure.** The university is to be governed by a *Regens seu Rector*. Degrees require prior examination (*praevio examine*) with four doctors in the relevant faculty present, nominated by the Prior and Regent. The Regent or the Bishop of Santo Domingo may promote scholars in the absence of doctors. Lecturers may be recruited and assigned, with salaries provided from university funds when resources allow. The university may make, amend, and reform statutes. All of this is stated in operational terms, not aspirational ones.

**Derogation clause.** The bull's closing sections are notably forceful in preempting legal objections: it explicitly derogates all contrary apostolic constitutions, council ordinances, privileges, pragmatic sanctions, and municipal statutes — including those explicitly stating they cannot be derogated — "this one time, specially and expressly." The anathema formula concludes: anyone who presumes to contravene this document will incur "the indignation of Almighty God and of the blessed Apostles Peter and Paul." This is standard curial formula but signals that the grant was designed to be legally unassailable.

**Flow of argument.** The document's logic runs: (1) we hold the apostolic seat and care for its fruits; (2) the Dominicans in Santo Domingo have converted the islanders and gathered abundant spiritual fruit; (3) the city is remote and lacks sacred learning; (4) a studium exists but lacks full standing; (5) a full university would beautify the city, deepen Christian instruction, and reward the friars' labors; (6) therefore we erect and institute one, with full privileges. The spiritual mission provides the moral warrant; the institutional gap provides the practical occasion; the grant provides the legal solution.

**Tone.** Authoritative and juridical throughout. There is no pastoral warmth or dialogue — this is a legal instrument. The closest the document comes to personal voice is in its opening meditation on the fruits that flow from the study of letters (*literarum studio*), and in its stated desire to favor the Provincial, Prior, and friars with "special favors and graces."
