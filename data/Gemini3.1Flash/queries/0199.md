## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Unam Sanctam

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Church is one and indivisible, and all temporal power is necessarily subordinate to the spiritual authority of the Roman Pontiff, submission to whom is declared absolutely necessary for salvation.
- **Summary:** *Unam Sanctam* opens by asserting the unity, holiness, catholicity, and apostolicity of the Church as a matter of faith, drawing on biblical imagery (Noah's ark, the seamless tunic, the one sheepfold) to argue that there can only be one mystical body with one head — Christ, represented on earth by Peter and his successors. It then develops a "two swords" doctrine: both the spiritual and temporal swords belong to the Church, but the temporal is exercised *on behalf of* the Church and must be subordinated to the spiritual. From this hierarchical principle the bull concludes that the spiritual power has authority to establish, correct, and judge all temporal rulers, while the highest spiritual power — the Roman Pontiff — can be judged by God alone. The document closes with the absolute dogmatic declaration that subjection to the Roman Pontiff is necessary for every human creature's salvation.
- **Response to:** The text responds to claims (attributed in the text to "the Greeks or others") that certain peoples or rulers are not subject to Peter and his successors; it also implicitly counters any argument that the temporal sword operates independently of ecclesiastical authority, as the bull specifically addresses those who "deny that the temporal sword is in the power of Peter."
- **Prescriptive towards:** The bull directs all temporal powers — kings and soldiers — to accept that their authority is exercised only "at the will and sufferance of the priest," and that if the terrestrial power errs it will be judged by spiritual power. It ends with a universal, binding declaration that every human creature must be subject to the Roman Pontiff for salvation.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between two claims to supreme authority — temporal (kingly/political) power and spiritual (papal/ecclesiastical) power — which the document insists must be resolved by the unconditional subordination of the former to the latter. A secondary tension involves the unity of the Church versus schismatic or non-subject communities ("the Greeks or others") who resist Petrine authority.

## Unstructured

**Flow of argument**

The bull proceeds through three interlocking moves. First, it establishes ecclesial unity through a cascade of scriptural types and citations: the dove of the Canticles, Noah's ark (one pilot, one cubit, everything outside destroyed), the seamless tunic, the one sheepfold with one shepherd. Unity is not merely organizational but ontological — a "monster" would have two heads, so the Church can have only one. This sets the ground for the second move.

Second, the document deploys the "two swords" metaphor drawn from Luke 22:38. The apostles said "behold, here are two swords" — and Christ said "it is enough," meaning both swords are in the Church. The spiritual sword is wielded *by* the Church directly; the temporal sword is wielded by kings and soldiers but *on behalf of* and at the sufferance of the Church. Pseudo-Dionysius's hierarchical cosmology is invoked: the divine law ordains that lower things reach their end through higher intermediaries. Therefore temporal power is below spiritual power as a matter of universal divine order, not merely ecclesiastical preference.

Third, the bull draws out the judicial consequence: if temporal power errs, it is judged by spiritual power; if a lower spiritual power errs, it is judged by a higher one; if the supreme spiritual power errs, it is judged by God alone — citing 1 Corinthians 2:15 ("the spiritual man judgeth of all things and he himself is judged by no man"). Resistance to this ordained power is equated with resistance to God's ordinance, and the alternative (two co-equal powers) is branded as Manichaean heresy. The closing sentence — "it is absolutely necessary for salvation that every human creature be subject to the Roman Pontiff" — is framed not as policy but as defined dogma.

**Tone**

The tone is predominantly authoritative and declaratory rather than pastoral. The language is juridical and theological throughout: the bull "declares, proclaims, defines." Scriptural citation is dense and treated as probative proof-text rather than illustration. There is virtually no exhortatory or consoling register; this is a document that asserts and commands.

**Notable framing**

- Salvation language is deployed to give the jurisdictional claim ultimate weight: outside the one Church there is "neither salvation nor the remission of sins"; and at the close, subjection to the pope is "absolutely necessary for salvation." The hierarchical-political argument is thus framed as a soteriological necessity, not merely an administrative preference.
- The Manichaean charge is rhetorically significant: any assertion of two equally ultimate powers (God and a temporal sovereign standing apart from the Church) is equated with the dualist heresy of two equal ultimate principles. This forecloses the opponent's logical escape by labeling the alternative metaphysically heretical.
- Biblical typology does heavy lifting. Noah's ark is the most fully elaborated type: one ark, one cubit finish, one pilot = one Church, one pope. The destruction of everything outside the ark is cited without softening, implying that those outside the Church's unity face the same fate.
