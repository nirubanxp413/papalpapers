# Cum multorum charitate

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** A papal Jubilee is proclaimed — first for Italy, then extended to France — as a spiritual remedy for grave dangers and calamities, framed around communal prayer, fasting, and gratitude for the recovery of King Louis XV.
- **Summary:** Benedict XIV issues this brief (*Breve*) to proclaim a Jubilee for the peoples of Italy and adjacent islands, who are afflicted by many calamities, and to extend it to France and its provinces. The primary spiritual prescription is prayer and fasting as the "one safe refuge" against the evils of a difficult age in which charity has grown cold and human malice abounds. The extension to France is specifically motivated by a providential event: the recovery of King Louis XV from a grave illness, for which fervent thanksgiving is owed to God. The faithful of France are directed to offer solemn prayers for the health of their ruler and for the lasting stability of the kingdom.
- **Response to:** The document itself cites (1) a general moral and spiritual crisis — "charity is lacking in many, and the malice of men abounds" — making the times "most difficult"; (2) calamities afflicting the peoples of Italy and adjacent islands; (3) the recent serious and much-lamented illness of King Louis XV of France, from which he has recovered.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs (1) the peoples of Italy to participate in the Jubilee through prayer and fasting to implore divine mercy; (2) the peoples of France to render solemn thanksgiving to God for the king's recovery and to pray continually for his health and for the stability of the kingdom, trusting always in the Lord.
- **Tension:** A spiritual tension is named between the coldness of human charity ("langue la carità di molti") and the abounding malice of men on one side, and the mercy of God that can be moved by prayer and fasting on the other. A secondary tension is implicit between the fragility of political order (the king's illness, the stability of the kingdom) and the divine sovereignty over rulers ("Colui che è il solo Signore dei regnanti").

## Unstructured

*Cum multorum charitate* is a brief, almost formulaic document — a breve rather than a full encyclical — but it distils a characteristic Benedictine XIV posture: measured, pastorally pragmatic, and alert to the intersection of spiritual life and political reality.

The opening frame is notably pessimistic about the moral temperature of the age. Benedict does not specify what calamities afflict Italy, but his rhetoric places prayer and fasting as the only reliable shelter against an era of widespread moral decay. The phrase "siamo minacciati dai più gravi pericoli" ("we are threatened by the gravest dangers") is sweeping and left unspecified — it may encompass war, plague, or broader social disorder — but the document does not name any explicitly. The text appeals to the authority of "all the principles of both laws, confirmed by the teachings of the Prophets and the Apostles," lending this prescription a universal, timeless quality rather than grounding it in a specific crisis.

The structural pivot comes in section 2, where the Jubilee is extended to France. The motivation shifts from general calamity to a specific, joyful event: the miraculous-seeming recovery of King Louis ("il Cristianissimo Re Lodovico"). The tone here becomes warmly political-theological — the king is described as "Nostro carissimo figlio in Cristo" (our dearest son in Christ), placing him within a familial ecclesial order. The papal role is that of spiritual father mediating between God's providence and a grateful Catholic kingdom.

The prescriptive content for France is notably royalist in orientation: the people are to pray that God "accumulate days upon days" for the king and, crucially, that God hold the king's heart in His hand ("tenendo in mano il suo cuore") and guide him according to divine commandments. This framing places royal virtue and royal longevity within a providential framework: the king's fidelity to God is the basis for the kingdom's stability.

The tone overall is authoritative-pastoral: it does not argue at length, but issues commands wrapped in exhortation. The document closes with a pragmatic instruction about the circulation of authorised copies — a sign that the brief is meant for wide distribution across territories where the original cannot physically travel.

The argument's flow is: (1) general spiritual crisis → (2) Jubilee as remedy for Italy → (3) extension to France on the basis of a specific providential event → (4) directive to France for thanksgiving and intercession → (5) logistical provision for distribution. It is brief, confident, and assumes the authority of the papal office without extensive justification.
