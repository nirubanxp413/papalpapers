## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Annus Qui Hunc

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document directs bishops to ensure the physical cleanliness of churches and the proper, devout, and distinctly non-theatrical conduct of the Divine Office—including plainchant, polyphonic music, and the use of instruments—in preparation for the upcoming Holy Year Jubilee.
- **Summary:** Addressed to bishops of the Papal States on the eve of the 1750 Jubilee, *Annus Qui Hunc* instructs them to prepare for an influx of foreign pilgrims by ensuring their churches are clean and decently furnished (regardless of wealth), that the canonical Hours are sung or recited attentively and according to rule, and that polyphonic music and musical instruments conform to a grave, devout, and intelligible style entirely distinct from theatrical singing. Benedict XIV grounds each directive in conciliar decrees, patristic authority, and the example of eminent predecessors, and closes with a call for priests to celebrate Mass and conduct themselves in public with proper dignity.
- **Response to:** The document responds to: (1) the occasion of a forthcoming Jubilee year, occasioned by the end of a war and the brokering of peace among warring princes; (2) the observable state of some diocesan churches as ruined, filthy, or lacking in proper ornaments; (3) negligent recitation of the canonical Hours by those bound to them; (4) the perceived infiltration of theatrical or profane styles of singing and instrumental music into sacred functions; and (5) the existing controversies among ecclesiastical authors over the legitimacy of polyphonic music and musical instruments in the liturgy.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates the arrival of large numbers of foreign pilgrims and directs that: churches be made clean and orderly before they arrive; the Divine Office be sung with due attention and deliberateness; polyphonic music and instruments be kept grave, devout, and intelligible (with specific instruments—such as timpani, horns, oboes, flutes—explicitly excluded); theatrical singing be removed; and orchestral interludes, where permitted, be grave and appropriate to the liturgical season (especially during Holy Week). Bishops are urged to publish edicts implementing these norms, beginning a broader reform of church music.
- **Tension:** The principal tension is between sacred decorum and the intrusion of theatrical or profane styles into worship—i.e., the concern that what pilgrims hear in churches of the Papal States will not differ from what they hear in opera houses and theatres, thereby scandalising the devout and providing no corrective example. A secondary tension exists between poverty (which may prevent costly ornaments) and the inarguable obligation of cleanliness. A third tension runs through the extended treatment of music: between those ecclesiastical authorities who condemn polyphonic music and instruments entirely, and those who permit them—with Benedict steering a middle course that permits but strictly regulates, while condemning any music that obscures the words being sung or mimics theatrical entertainment.

## Unstructured

**Occasion and framing.** The letter opens by naming its occasion precisely: the following year (1750) is a Jubilee, and peace having been concluded among warring princes, large gatherings of foreign pilgrims are expected in Rome and throughout the Papal States. The Pope's pastoral anxiety is twofold—that visitors not be offended by what they find, and that they carry home "motivations and examples of virtues." This framing of foreign scrutiny gives the document its rhetorical urgency: reform is not merely an internal matter but a matter of public witness.

**Three practical goals.** The document is organized around three explicit objectives: (1) the cleanliness and decent furnishing of churches; (2) the proper, attentive, and regular recitation or singing of the canonical Hours; and (3) the purging of theatrical style from polyphonic music and instruments. Each section accumulates a dense battery of authorities—Scripture, Fathers, councils (Trent, Lateran IV, Vienne, Toledo, Milan, Cambrai, Cologne, Saumur), decretals (John XXII, Alexander VII, Innocent XI, Innocent XII), and named theologians and canonists (Bellarmine, Bona, Suárez, Bauldry, Drexel, Feijóo, van der Lindt, and many others)—to establish that the norm being required is not a novelty but a recovery of existing discipline.

**Cleanliness as non-negotiable minimum.** On the first point, Benedict is careful to distinguish between wealth (which not all can attain) and cleanliness (which admits no excuse). He cites Bellarmine, Jerome, and his predecessor Benedict XIII as witnesses that even the poorest Capuchin churches demonstrate that poverty and filth are not synonymous. The rhetorical target is bishops and clergy who maintain splendid personal residences while allowing their churches to fall into squalor.

**Divine Office: attentiveness and pace.** On the second point, the document stresses that merely completing the obligation of the canonical Hours without mental attention does not satisfy the obligation (citing Innocent III and the gloss on "diligently" and "devoutly"). It specifies that chant must not be rushed, that pauses must be observed, and that plainchant (*cantus firmus*) is the backbone upon which ecclesiastical discipline rests. The comparison between well-ordered monastic churches (which attract the faithful) and neglected collegiate and parish churches (which do not) is deployed as a pastoral argument for reform.

**Music: a long and balanced argument.** The bulk of the document is devoted to music, and Benedict's handling is notably scholarly and even-handed before arriving at firm conclusions. He surveys the entire controversy—those who oppose polyphonic music entirely (Aelred of Rievaulx, Aquinas in certain readings, Marcellus II), those who approve it (John of Salisbury, Antoninus, the Council of Trent's final position, Bellarmine, Suárez, Baronius, Bona), and the unanimity on one point: that theatrical singing in churches is condemned by all sides. His argument is therefore structured as: (a) the debate is legitimate; (b) it has been resolved by prior pontifical and conciliar authority in favour of permitting polyphony under regulation; (c) the abuse—theatrical style—is condemned by everyone; therefore (d) bishops must act to suppress the abuse while retaining licit practice.

**The intelligibility criterion.** A recurrent and specific criterion runs through the musical sections: the words must be heard and understood. Benedict quotes councils (Cambrai, Cologne, Toledo, Milan) and individual authors (van der Lindt, Drexel, Augustine's *Confessions*) to establish that polyphonic music whose complexity obscures the text defeats its own purpose, since the purpose of singing in church is to move hearts toward God through the meaning of the words, not merely through sonic pleasure. The story of Augustine weeping at church hymns—and what he would feel hearing unintelligible polyphony—is deployed as a particularly affective conclusion to this argument.

**Specific practical rulings on instruments.** Benedict names permitted instruments (violone, violoncello, bassoon, violas, violins—those which support and sustain voices) and prohibited ones (timpani, natural horns, trumpets, oboes, flutes, piccolos, harps, mandolins—those which produce theatrical sound). Orchestral interludes are tolerated where customary, provided they are grave and not distracting. Music especially unsuited to its liturgical context—he singles out Holy Week, citing the case of Lucca under Pius V—is firmly condemned.

**Tone.** The tone is authoritative-pastoral: Benedict writes as a legislating pope who is also a learned scholar. The density of citation (ecclesiastical and patristic) gives the document a quasi-academic texture, but the underlying register is exhortatory and moderately urgent. The closing paragraphs return to the practical situation of individual dioceses and end on a note of collegial solidarity ("embracing Your Fraternity within the bowels of Christ") combined with apostolic blessing.

**Notable framing.** The document consistently presents Rome as the source and model from which correct liturgical practice radiates outward (the Charlemagne exemplum reinforces this). The Jubilee occasion turns what might have been a routine pastoral letter into a document with heightened stakes: the Church's credibility before the watching world is at issue.
