## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Omnibus Compertum

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** Pope Leo XIII calls the Greek Melchite bishops to restore and consolidate internal unity through charity, proper hierarchical deference, and a plenary council, so that the Church may better serve its mission and attract dissidents back to Rome.
- **Summary:** Written in July 1900 to the Greek Melchite Patriarch, archbishops, and bishops, this letter acknowledges the Pope's longstanding affection and concrete support for the Greek Melchite church (a seminary in Jerusalem, college funding in Rome, extended patriarchal jurisdiction), then addresses a recent internal dispute that was resolved when the bishops visited Rome. To prevent future disagreements, Leo XIII prescribes three measures: preserving the patriarch's rights while urging him to exercise authority with fraternal charity, reminding bishops of their proper submission to the patriarch with appeal to Rome as a final recourse, and mandating the convening of a plenary council to codify rights, discipline, liturgy, and mission needs. The letter closes with an appeal for unity grounded in charity, framing harmony within the Melchite church as instrumental to the wider goal of reconciling Eastern dissidents with the Apostolic See.
- **Response to:** A recent internal dispute ("certain minor disagreements") among the Greek Melchite bishops that had arisen before their visit to Rome, where peace was restored through the Pope's exhortations. The document also responds to the broader ongoing effort—begun at the start of Leo's pontificate and expressed in the constitution *Orientalium*—to draw Eastern Christians into closer communion with the See of Peter.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The convening of a plenary council (as soon as possible) to settle rights of patriarch and bishops, clergy discipline, monastic order governance, mission needs, liturgical matters, and ecclesiastical laws. Also directed toward the long-term goal of reconciling Eastern dissidents (non-Catholic Eastern Christians) with the Catholic Church, which the document explicitly states is advanced by internal harmony among Eastern Catholics.
- **Tension:** The core tension is between patriarchal authority and episcopal autonomy within the Greek Melchite church—the letter must simultaneously defend the patriarch's rights and privileges while admonishing the patriarch not to dominate his bishops, and remind the bishops to submit to him. A secondary tension exists between the Eastern churches' distinct identity and their integration into Roman Catholic structures (e.g., routing unresolved disputes to the Holy See).

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument:** The letter follows a clear pastoral-administrative arc. It opens with a recitation of Leo's prior investments in the Greek Melchite church—seminary, college, jurisdictional grant—establishing paternal authority grounded in demonstrated care. It then acknowledges the recent dispute and its resolution, using that moment of restored peace as the occasion to institutionalize safeguards against future conflict. The three numbered prescriptions (I, II, III) move from the personal (how the patriarch should relate to bishops), to the relational (how bishops should relate to the patriarch), to the institutional (a formal council). The letter closes by elevating the stakes: harmony is not merely a domestic church matter but is tied to the evangelization and reconciliation of all Eastern dissidents.

**Tone:** The tone is authoritative but warmly paternal. Leo uses first-person affective language throughout ("nothing has been more precious, more sacred to Us," "We urge and beseech you from the bottom of Our heart") while still issuing clear prescriptions. The use of Scripture (1 Peter 5:3) and Bernard of Clairvaux ("May love accomplish more than power") softens the hierarchical directives with spiritual and patristic grounding.

**Notable framing:** The document frames Eastern Catholic unity explicitly as a missionary instrument: "None of you is unaware of how much harmony of spirit, heart, and opinion affects the good of the whole Church and assists in the reconciliation of dissidents." This means the Melchite bishops' internal behavior is presented not as a local matter but as directly bearing on the broader Catholic mission toward the Eastern Christian world. The appeal to ancestors ("imitating the example of their ancestors") and the comparison to other Eastern churches that benefited from national councils also frames the prescription as tradition-affirming rather than externally imposed. The document is notably brief—no doctrinal exposition, no polemics—functioning as a disciplinary-administrative letter with pastoral warmth.
