## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Laborem Exercens

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** Human work is the fundamental key to the social question because its true meaning is rooted in the dignity of the person as subject, not in the economic value of its output — and this truth demands the priority of labour over capital in every social and spiritual order.
- **Summary:** Written on the ninetieth anniversary of *Rerum Novarum*, *Laborem Exercens* argues that work is not merely an economic activity but a constitutive dimension of human personhood, grounded in the Genesis mandate to "subdue the earth" as beings made in God's image. The document develops a distinction between work in the "objective sense" (what is produced, the role of technology and capital) and work in the "subjective sense" (the worker as a free, creative, responsible person), insisting that the subjective always takes precedence. From this personalist foundation the document condemns both liberal capitalism and Marxist collectivism for the same underlying error — "economism" and "practical materialism" — which treats labour as a commodity and the worker as an instrument rather than a subject. The encyclical then enumerates workers' rights (just wages, rest, unions, employment, safety, protection for migrants and the disabled), grounds them in the broader framework of human rights, and closes with a spirituality of work that connects human labour to participation in God's creative activity, the redemptive suffering of the Cross, and the hope of the Resurrection.
- **Response to:** The encyclical explicitly states it responds to (1) the ninetieth anniversary of Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* and the whole tradition of Catholic social teaching that followed (*Quadragesimo Anno*, *Mater et Magistra*, *Pacem in Terris*, *Populorum Progressio*, *Gaudium et Spes*); (2) the historically unresolved conflict between capital and labour that arose with industrialisation and was ideologically crystallised in the antinomy between liberalism/capitalism and Marxism/collectivism; (3) new technological conditions — automation, electronics, microprocessors — threatening mass unemployment and demanding a "reordering and adjustment of the structures of the modern economy"; (4) widening global inequality between developed and developing nations, including newly decolonised countries; and (5) persistent violations of workers' rights, including unjust wages, unsafe conditions, landlessness in agriculture, discrimination against migrants and disabled persons.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document anticipates and directs action toward (1) the formation of just labour policies at national and international levels, with the State as primary "indirect employer" bearing responsibility for full employment; (2) structural reform of ownership — neither rigid private capitalism nor state collectivism, but forms of joint ownership, worker participation in management, and "shareholding by labour"; (3) adequate protection of agricultural workers, disabled persons, and migrant workers through legislation and social institutions; (4) the development of a Christian "spirituality of work" that permeates ordinary daily activity and links labour to salvation; (5) universal progress guided by *Populorum Progressio*'s vision, measured by growing recognition of workers' dignity rather than material accumulation alone.
- **Tension:** (1) *Labour vs. capital*: the encyclical identifies the foundational tension of the modern era as the systematic separation and opposition of labour and capital, which it traces to the philosophical error of "economism" and "practical materialism" — present in both capitalist liberalism and Marxist collectivism. (2) *Person vs. system*: every economic and ideological system risks reducing the human person from a subject of work to an instrument of production, and the document insists this reversal is morally inadmissible regardless of the system's ideological label. (3) *Objective vs. subjective dimensions of work*: the gains of technology and material productivity constantly threaten to eclipse the personal, creative and dignifying character of work. (4) *Justice vs. solidarity*: worker solidarity is affirmed as morally legitimate but must not collapse into class egoism or become an instrument of political parties — it must remain oriented toward the common good.

## Unstructured

**Central argument and flow**

The encyclical is structured as a sustained philosophical and theological meditation in five parts, building from anthropological foundations toward concrete social prescriptions and culminating in a spirituality of work.

Parts I–II establish the conceptual framework. Work is defined not instrumentally but ontologically: it is the means by which the human being, created as God's image, exercises dominion over visible creation. The distinction between "objective" work (what is done, the product, technology) and "subjective" work (the person who acts) is the pivotal analytical tool of the entire document. John Paul II returns to it repeatedly to diagnose errors and propose remedies. The claim "work is for man, not man for work" is the moral axiom from which everything else flows.

Part III ("Conflict between Labour and Capital") is the document's most polemical section. It situates the encyclical squarely within the Cold War ideological confrontation but refuses to take either side. Both capitalism and Marxism are convicted of the same "error of economism" — treating the human worker as a production factor rather than a person. This is called an "error of materialism", first practical, then philosophical. The document insists that the priority of labour over capital is not an ideological slogan but a metaphysical truth: capital is itself accumulated past labour, so labour is always primary. From this follows the document's nuanced position on property: private ownership is not an absolute right (contra rigid capitalism), but mere state collectivisation does not achieve true "socialisation" either (contra Marxism) — what matters is whether ownership structures genuinely serve the worker as subject.

Part IV ("Rights of Workers") moves into applied social ethics. It introduces the concept of the "indirect employer" — the network of institutions, states, and international bodies that condition the actual terms of employment. This framing is notable for extending moral responsibility far beyond the individual firm to include multinationals, international trade relationships, and state labour policies. The list of rights is detailed: just wages (including a "family wage" or equivalent social provision), rest, health and safety, pension, union membership and the right to strike (within limits), special attention to agricultural workers, disabled persons, and migrants.

Part V ("Spirituality of Work") integrates the social analysis into a theological vision. Work is participation in God's ongoing creative activity; Christ as a carpenter is "the gospel of work" made flesh. The toil inherent in work is connected to the Cross; the new good produced by work anticipates the new earth of the Resurrection. The spirituality is presented not as a retreat from social engagement but as its deepest motivation.

**Tone**

The document is notably authoritative in its philosophical claims (the priority of labour over capital is stated as "an evident truth that emerges from the whole of man's historical experience") while being pastoral in its attention to concrete workers — the farmer, the miner, the intellectual facing unemployment, the mother, the disabled person, the migrant. It is also deliberately synthetic: John Paul II explicitly situates the text within the "organic development" of Catholic social teaching rather than presenting it as a rupture or novelty.

**Notable framing choices**

- The repeated return to Genesis ("subdue the earth") as an exegetical anchor gives the social argument a theological depth that distinguishes it from secular labour theory.
- The "error of economism" framing allows the document to condemn both liberal capitalism and Marxist collectivism without endorsing either — a characteristically JPII "third way" position rooted in personalism.
- The "indirect employer" concept is a sophisticated institutional analysis that anticipates later discussions of global supply chains and corporate accountability.
- The document's treatment of women's work is ambivalent: it affirms women's rights not to be discriminated against in the workplace while simultaneously affirming a mother's domestic role as her "specific" and "irreplaceable" vocation — framing that the text acknowledges could be read as constraining but insists must not become coercive or penalising.
- Work as participation in the Cross and Resurrection is not merely pious decoration but serves as the theological answer to why toil is not merely a curse: it has redemptive meaning precisely because Christ himself was a man of work.
