# Romanus Pontifex

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The Pope grants Portugal perpetual dominion over newly explored African territories and the right to conquer, enslave, and convert their peoples, framing colonial expansion as a spiritual mission in service of God and the Universal Church.
- **Summary:** Issued by Nicholas V in January 1455, this bull formally extends earlier papal grants to King Alfonso V of Portugal and Infante Henry the Navigator, confirming Portugal's exclusive rights to explore, possess, and exploit the African coast and all future acquisitions from the capes of Bojador and Não southward through Guinea. The document simultaneously grounds this grant in spiritual language — the salvation of souls, propagation of the orthodox faith, overthrow of Saracens and infidels — while enacting concrete temporal and commercial arrangements: prohibition of rival navigation, authorization of trade with infidels (with carve-outs for weapons), permission to found churches, and imposition of excommunication on those who interfere. The bull frames the conquests already made, including the forcible taking of "Guineamen and other negroes," as legitimate acts flowing from prior papal authority.
- **Response to:** The document itself identifies several immediate concerns it responds to: (1) Portugal's decades of costly naval and military expeditions along the African coast, during which "very many of their natural subjects have perished"; (2) the fear that "strangers induced by covetousness" would sail to the same territories, either to profit or to undermine Portugal's work; (3) the risk that rivals might supply "arms, iron, wood for construction" to infidels, making them stronger enemies; and (4) the need to close legal gaps in earlier grants (from Martin V and Eugenius IV) so they explicitly cover Ceuta and all acquisitions past and future.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The bull directs future action in several ways: (1) continued Portuguese exploration and conquest toward "the Indians who are said to worship the name of Christ," in hopes of allying with them against Saracens; (2) the conversion of "gentile or pagan peoples" encountered along the route; (3) the founding of churches and sending of clergy to new territories; (4) universal compliance from "all the faithful of Christ" — regardless of rank, up to emperors and kings — with Portugal's exclusive navigation and trade rights, under penalty of excommunication and interdict.
- **Tension:** The document contains at least two notable tensions: (1) Between spiritual mandate and temporal appetite — the bull insists the conquests are motivated by "zeal for the salvation of souls," yet its operative clauses deal primarily with property rights, tribute, trade monopolies, and penal sanctions against commercial rivals. The spiritual framing is used to consecrate and protect very material acquisitions. (2) Between universalism and exclusion — the opening declares the Pope "seeking and desiring the salvation of all," yet the bull's legal machinery creates a system in which entire peoples can be "reduced to perpetual slavery" and their lands permanently appropriated, with no mechanism for their own consent or recourse.

## Unstructured

The bull opens in the register of pastoral universalism: the Roman Pontiff is portrayed as a father who surveys "all the several climes of the world" and desires the salvation of every soul. This framing immediately establishes the theological authority on which everything else rests — the Pope as "vicar of Jesus Christ," whose power extends over temporal rulers precisely because his concern is eternal. The rhetorical move is important: it is not conquest that legitimates the Pope's grant, but the Pope's universal spiritual mandate that legitimates the conquest.

The document then narrates Portugal's cumulative investment — twenty-five years of expeditions, loss of life, enormous expense — as evidence of sincere faith and a basis for reward. Henry the Navigator is cast as "a Catholic and true soldier of Christ," and his work is described as praiseworthy and "worthy of perpetual remembrance." This is hagiographic framing in service of a property claim: by presenting Portugal's activities as heroic sacrifice for the faith, the bull makes any interference with them appear not just politically hostile but morally and spiritually wrongful.

The core legal mechanism is a confirmation and extension of prior grants: authority previously given is now declared to encompass all past and future acquisitions along the African coast and beyond. The right to "invade, search out, capture, vanquish, and subdue all Saracens and pagans" and "reduce their persons to perpetual slavery" is quoted from a prior letter as already granted; this bull simply ensures it applies broadly. The language is dry and legalistic, a sharp contrast to the devotional opening — the document shifts comfortably between prayer and property law.

The bull's treatment of trade is notably pragmatic. Portugal is authorized to conduct commerce with Saracens and infidels in nearly all goods, with the explicit exceptions of iron instruments, construction wood, cordage, ships, and weapons — items with direct military utility. The concern here is not theological purity but strategic: Portugal must not inadvertently arm its enemies. The text is frank that commercial activity in "infidel" regions is expected and sanctioned.

The enforcement mechanism is robust and explicitly hierarchical. Excommunication applies to individuals; interdict applies to entire communities. No one — "even [those] endued with archiepiscopal, episcopal, imperial, royal, queenly, ducal, or any other greater ecclesiastical or worldly dignity" — is exempt. The archbishop of Lisbon and the bishops of Silves and Ceuta are commanded to publicly proclaim these penalties on Sundays and feast days "while a large multitude of people shall assemble." The bull is not just a diplomatic document; it is designed for public performance as a deterrent.

The tone throughout is authoritative rather than pastoral. There is no exhortation to love, mercy, or discernment toward the peoples being conquered; they are categorized as Saracens, infidels, pagans, or gentiles, and their conversion is described as a hoped-for outcome rather than a condition of the enterprise. The sole pastoral note is the instruction that clergy sent to new territories may "hear confessions" and "administer the ecclesiastical sacraments" — directed at the Portuguese settlers, not the conquered populations. The document's spiritual energy is oriented almost entirely inward, toward the Christian community it addresses, rather than outward toward those it claims to be saving.
