# Rite Expiatis

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document uses the Seventh Centenary of St. Francis of Assisi's death to call Catholics to imitate Franciscan virtues—voluntary poverty, humility, obedience, penance, and charity—as the authentic remedy for the spiritual and social disorders of the present age.
- **Summary:** Issued on the occasion of the 700th anniversary of St. Francis of Assisi's death, Pius XI presents Francis as a providential reformer raised up by God to renew both the Church and civil society in a time of heresy, social strife, and moral corruption. The encyclical surveys Francis's personal virtues (poverty, humility, obedience, chastity, divine love) and his institutional legacy (First, Second, and Third Orders) as a model of evangelical reform that "changed the face of the earth." The Pope draws a direct parallel between the social ills of Francis's era and those of 1926, urging all three Franciscan Orders and the wider Catholic world to revive the Franciscan spirit as a living antidote to secularism, materialism, and pride. The celebration of the centenary is framed not merely as historical commemoration but as an active summons to imitation.
- **Response to:** (1) The moral and social corruption of the contemporary age—described as marked by "concupiscence of the flesh and the pride of life"—which the text explicitly compares to the turbulent 13th-century world Francis reformed. (2) Distortions of Francis's image: the document directly rebuts those who portray Francis as an opponent of Church discipline, a proto-liberal or nationalist hero, a mere nature-poet, or a precursor of "false liberty." (3) The extended Jubilee Year already underway, whose fruits of penance and renewal the centenary is meant to crown. (4) Leo XIII's encyclical *Auspicato* (1882) and Benedict XV's *Sacra Propediem*, which established the precedent of papally-urged Franciscan centenary observances.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) Bishops and clergy: to preach the Franciscan spirit and actively promote enrollment in the Third Order. (2) All three Franciscan Orders: to renew fidelity to their founding Rule as a living protest against materialism and pride, and to restore Christian society. (3) Lay Catholics generally: to reject secularist admiration of Francis stripped of his spiritual content, and instead to imitate his poverty, humility, and penance. (4) The centenary celebrations themselves: to present an historically accurate, spiritually demanding Francis—not a romantic or nationalist symbol—so that pilgrims and participants are moved to genuine moral reform.
- **Tension:** (1) *Authentic vs. distorted Francis*: the text explicitly names and resists attempts by "defenders of modern error," nationalists, and secularists to appropriate Francis as a symbol of liberty, nature-love, or civic virtue while ignoring his austerity and Church obedience. (2) *Evangelical poverty vs. contemporary materialism*: Francis's voluntary poverty and self-mortification is held against "an insatiable desire for riches," ostentatious display, and the worldly lives of those who "roll in the very mud of voluptuousness." (3) *Obedience to the Church vs. rebellion*: the document stresses that Francis's reform succeeded precisely because it operated through, not against, papal authority—an implicit contrast with reformers who "rebelled against the teachings and authority of the Holy See" and ended in "licentiousness and vice" and social disorder.

## Unstructured

**Key themes and flow of argument**

The document opens by linking the centenary to the ongoing Jubilee Year: both are moments of spiritual renewal, and the centenary is meant to consummate the Jubilee's fruits. This framing immediately establishes that the document's purpose is not antiquarian but pastoral and urgent.

Pius XI then asserts Francis's unique Christological resemblance: "there has never been anyone in whom the image of Jesus Christ and the evangelical manner of life shone forth more lifelike and strikingly than in St. Francis." This is a strong opening claim that sets the register—Francis is not merely admirable but uniquely representative of the Gospel.

The historical parallel is the structural spine of the document. Paragraphs 6–9 paint a vivid portrait of 13th-century society: heresy, internecine warfare, class exploitation, clerical corruption, and political chaos. The passage is notably detailed about social injustice—"between lords and vassals… relations in every sense of the world foreign to the spirit of humanity," the oppression of the poor, greed, usury. This detailed sociological account of Francis's era functions rhetorically to mirror the present: the Pope does not have to state explicitly that 1926 resembles the 1220s—the parallel is constructed through accumulation. Paragraph 10 then introduces Francis as the providential answer: he "shone as the sun" into this darkness.

The central virtue sequence (paragraphs 12–28) moves through poverty, humility, obedience, chastity, penance, and divine love. Each virtue is carefully grounded in quotation from primary Franciscan sources (Thomas of Celano, St. Bonaventure, the Rules of the Orders, Francis's own writings). This gives the document a quasi-hagiographical texture alongside its teaching authority. The treatment of poverty is the most extended and theologically dense: it distinguishes Francis's voluntary, love-motivated poverty from the "forced and unlovable poverty preached by some ancient philosophers," and links it explicitly to Christ's kenosis ("although he was rich made Himself poor for us"). The text quotes Francis's own Rule on poverty as a kind of beatitude for the friars.

The treatment of obedience (paragraphs 22–25) is politically pointed. The Pope spends considerable energy refuting those who claim Francis was a proto-Protestant or proto-liberal: he went personally to Innocent III for papal approval of his Rule, prescribed obedience to the Roman Pontiff in the Rule itself, and "held in veneration the clergy." The implicit argument is that authentic Franciscan reform is ecclesial reform, never anti-institutional rebellion—and that rebellions against the Church lead historically to civil disorder.

Paragraphs 30–36 describe the social fruits of Francis's reform through the Third Order in terms that approach a theory of social transformation: the Third Order exempted members from feudal oaths of vassalage and military conscription, provided legal protections for the poor, and created a new civic moral order grounded in the Gospel. The language here shifts from purely spiritual to explicitly social: the Third Order is described as "a shield of the rights of the poor and the weak against the abuses of the rich and the powerful." This is one of the document's strongest passages connecting Franciscan spirituality to structural social change—yet the change is framed as flowing from interior conversion, not from political contestation.

Paragraphs 39–41 engage the cultural reception of Francis in the 19th and early 20th centuries with a critical edge. The Pope acknowledges and even appreciates admiration for Francis's poetry (the Canticle of the Sun), his love of nature, his patriotism, and his universal fraternity—but systematically subordinates each to the spiritual core: "all of this is quite admirable but it is the least that is to be praised in our Saint." The risk named is that sentimentalized or politicized admiration becomes a means of self-justification for those who live contrary to Francis's actual teaching.

**Tone**

The tone is predominantly pastoral but carries a firm teaching authority throughout. Pius XI addresses bishops as co-workers ("your services, Venerable Brothers") and uses hortatory language ("We pray," "We desire," "We expect"). But the document is equally assertive when correcting misreadings of Francis or naming contemporary social pathologies. The use of extended historical narrative (the 13th-century context) and thick quotation from Franciscan primary sources gives the encyclical a learned, almost encyclopedic quality unusual for a purely devotional text. The overall effect is that of a pope who has internalized Franciscan piety personally (he mentions having received the badge of the Third Order himself) and who writes about Francis with evident warmth and specificity, not merely as an institutional occasion.

**Notable framing**

The document closes by identifying the centenary with a providential moment in Pius XI's own pontificate: "it seems that God in His goodness and mercy has ordained that Our pontificate shall not pass without the happiest fruits for the Catholic Church." This self-insertion is not incidental; it frames the Pope's role as analogous to Francis's—a providential instrument of reform in a troubled age.

The repeated contrast between imitation and mere admiration ("either imitate that which you praise or do not permit yourself to praise that which you do not care to imitate") functions as the document's moral nerve. The centenary is only valuable if it produces transformation; celebration without imitation is condemned as self-contradiction.
