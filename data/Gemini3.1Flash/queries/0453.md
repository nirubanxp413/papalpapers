## Step 3 theme summary (from prior pass — use as framing, verify externally)

# Coelestis Pastor

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document formally condemns 68 propositions attributed to Miguel de Molinos whose "prayer of quiet" taught that spiritual perfection requires total passivity of soul, abandonment of active virtue and sacraments, and tolerance of demonic-induced carnal acts as non-sinful.
- **Summary:** Pope Innocent XI opens by asserting the Church's mission to guard the faithful from "poisonous ideas" and "age-old errors," then proceeds to detail how Molinos—under the pretext of the "interior way" and the "prayer of quiet"—had spread doctrines leading "the faithful from true religion and from the purity of Christian piety into terrible errors and every indecency." After legitimate inquisitorial investigation and consultation with theologians and cardinals, the pope enumerates 68 propositions held by Molinos, condemns them collectively as heretical, suspect, erroneous, scandalous, and blasphemous, and sentences Molinos to "harsh and perpetual confinement." The decree bans all of Molinos' books and writings in any language, threatens those who read or keep them with perpetual loss of titles and offices, and confirms the condemnation in permanent constitutional form so that it will not be forgotten over time.
- **Response to:** The document responds explicitly to the teaching and practice of Miguel de Molinos, who "under pretext of the prayer of quiet, but actually at variance with the teaching and practice of the holy fathers from the very beginnings, was teaching false doctrines by word and writings." It references a judicial procedure culminating in a sentence of 28 August 1687, and a public abjuration ceremony on 3 September 1687, indicating the document responds to an already-concluded inquisitorial process.
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document prescribes permanent vigilance: it prohibits all future speaking, writing, disputing, holding, or practicing of the condemned propositions or anything like them; it orders anyone holding Molinos' works to surrender them immediately for burning; and it binds violators with "supreme anathema." By reconfirming the condemnation through a new Constitution intended to be perpetually valid, it anticipates ongoing danger of the same errors resurfacing and directs future pontiffs and inquisitors to enforce these censures.
- **Tension:** The central tension named in the text is between total passive abandonment of the self to God (Molinos' "interior way") and the active exercise of free will in virtue, prayer, sacraments, and moral accountability that the pope upholds as true Catholic piety. Specific subsidiary tensions include: the role of confession and external sacramental life versus the claim that "the interior way is separated from confession"; the authority of bishops and directors over souls versus Molinos' claim that episcopal governance of the interior is "a new doctrine worthy of ridicule"; and the question of moral culpability when bodily acts are attributed to demonic violence rather than personal consent (propositions 41–53).

## Unstructured

**Overall argument and flow**

The document opens with a theological prologue establishing the pope's authority as successor to Peter, whose charge is to ensure the faithful "not feed on poisonous ideas." This sets the justificatory frame for the entire condemnation: what follows is not persecution but pastoral protection. The body of the document then works through two distinct movements: first, a narrative of how Molinos came to be investigated and condemned; second, an enumeration of his 68 propositions without editorial gloss, allowing their content to condemn itself in the reader's eyes.

The 68 propositions fall into discernible clusters:

1. **Radical passivity of will** (props. 1–16, 20–21, 34–35): The soul must cease all active operation, seeking, or thanksgiving. Any act originating from human will—even prayer, petitioning God, or giving thanks—is presented by Molinos as an obstacle to God's action and thus an offense. The logic is that God alone acts; the soul must become "an inanimate body."

2. **Rejection of traditional spiritual disciplines** (props. 17–19, 23, 26–33, 36–40): The three-way spiritual path (purgative, illuminative, unitive) is called "the greatest absurdity ever spoken about in mystical theology." Sensible devotion, feast-day observances, meditation, vocal prayer including the Our Father, works of penance, acts of love toward the Virgin and the saints, and even the humanities of Christ are dismissed or forbidden for "interior souls."

3. **Moral passivity regarding carnal and sinful acts** (props. 41–53): The most alarming cluster asserts that demons can physically move the bodies of "perfect souls" to commit carnal acts without those souls sinning, because consent is absent. Molinos claims even mutual carnal acts between two persons can occur this way, and that confessing them is unnecessary—indeed harmful. He appeals to biblical figures (Job, David, Jeremiah, Samson, Judith, Elijah, Elisha, Paul) as precedents.

4. **Claims of sinlessness and mystical death** (props. 54–63): Advanced souls reach a state where they no longer sin at all (even venially), no longer feel anything, and cannot confess because God himself supplies the grace of the sacrament. This "mystical death" extinguishes self-love and with it all capacity for sin.

5. **Authority and governance of the interior** (props. 64–68): Theologians are less fit for contemplation than the ignorant; bishops have no jurisdiction over the interior life; directors who demand that souls manifest their interior state are perpetrating "a manifest deception" inspired by Satan.

**Tone**

The document is predominantly authoritative and juridical, not pastoral or hortatory. The pope does not invite reflection or persuasion; he promulgates binding condemnations with precisely specified canonical penalties. Phrases like "we have condemned, banished, and denounced," "ipso facto and perpetually," "supreme anathema reserved to ourselves," and "prohibited and condemned all books" establish the tone of definitive juridical decree. The inclusion of a public shaming ceremony (Molinos standing on a platform in penitential dress at Santa Maria sopra Minerva) and the mandate to read the sentence from the pulpit reinforces the performative, exemplary dimension of the condemnation.

**Notable framing**

The pope frames Molinos' teaching as fundamentally deceptive: done "under pretext" of piety while actually subverting it. The preamble's language—Christ protecting the Church "against the gates of hell"—implies that Molinos' doctrine is not merely mistaken but diabolically inspired, an irony given that Molinos himself attributed carnal violations to demonic "violence." The condemnation of the propositions about demonic compulsion (41–53) is particularly notable: by attributing sinful acts to Satan while denying the need for confession, Molinos in effect created a system where spiritual directors could claim authority to excuse any behavior as involuntary, insulated from sacramental accountability.

The document's insistence on perpetual force—"shall have force perpetually," re-confirmed so that "the passage of years may cause the memory of this condemnation to become erased"—suggests awareness that Quietist impulses might recur, and that this text is intended as a permanent doctrinal marker, not merely a response to one individual.
