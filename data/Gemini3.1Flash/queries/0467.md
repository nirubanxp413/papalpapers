# Quae Ad Nos

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Spiritual
- **Theme (one sentence):** Leo XIII urges the bishops of Bohemia and Moravia to unite against active enemies of the faith who are drawing large numbers away from Catholic practice.
- **Summary:** Written to the bishops of Bohemia and Moravia, this brief letter expresses Leo's alarm at daily reports of Catholics departing from their faith. He calls for a joint episcopal meeting to coordinate a common defense, insists that deliberations be submitted for papal approval, and warns that internal clerical factionalism is weakening the very forces needed to resist the threat. The letter closes with an appeal for unity and a papal blessing.
- **Response to:** The document responds to "the departure of large numbers from the rites and practices of Catholicism" and to deliberate, well-resourced opposition — "enemies of the faith" who "spare neither labor nor money" to destroy the flock. It also addresses existing internal division: "partisan zeal which causes the holy clergy among you to be split apart."
- **Prescriptive towards:** The document directs the bishops toward: (1) an immediate joint meeting of all bishops of Bohemia and Moravia; (2) reporting their deliberations and decisions to Rome for apostolic approval; and (3) actively rooting out clerical factionalism so that episcopal unity can serve as a bulwark for the faith.
- **Tension:** The central tension is between an organised external threat to Catholic practice and an internally divided clergy whose disunity undermines any coordinated response. The letter frames this as urgent: the danger is described as common and growing daily, yet the instrument of defense — the united episcopate — is fractured by partisan zeal.

## Unstructured

*Quae Ad Nos* is a short, sharply focused administrative letter rather than a doctrinal encyclical. Its argument is essentially military in metaphor: the faith is under siege, the pastors are the defenders, and a divided army cannot hold the line. Leo reaches for urgency ("the magnitude of the danger persuades Us to goad the willing") while diplomatically acknowledging that dioceses differ in their exposure ("not all of your dioceses are in the same circumstances").

The tone is authoritative but not censorious. Leo does not rebuke the bishops directly; he acknowledges their effort and reframes his intervention as supplementary encouragement. The phrase "to goad the willing" is notably candid — he concedes they are already motivated, but the stakes justify papal pressure.

The structural move of the letter is to convert local, diocesan responses into a coordinated, Rome-supervised operation. The requirement that "the nature of the deliberations and decisions… be referred to Us so that they may be sanctioned by the apostolic approval" is a quiet assertion of central oversight. Unity at the episcopal level is not sufficient on its own; it must flow through Rome.

The internal factionalism among the clergy receives notable emphasis given the letter's brevity. Leo names it as a specific problem — "partisan zeal" — and directly links it to military weakness: it "divides and enervates the forces." The document implies that the external threat and the internal schism are mutually reinforcing dangers.

There is no theological development here; the document is entirely practical-ecclesial. Its register is pastoral concern filtered through hierarchical command. The closing apostolic blessing underscores that the relationship between Leo and these bishops is one of fatherly care, even as the content is directive.
