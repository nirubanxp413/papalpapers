# Quartus Supra

## Structured

- **Key theme:** Mixed
- **Theme (one sentence):** The document defends the supreme jurisdictional primacy of the Apostolic See — in doctrine, discipline, episcopal elections, and church property — against an Armenian schismatic faction in Constantinople that falsely claims the Catholic name while rejecting Roman authority, and calls faithful Armenians to resist deception and remain in Catholic unity.
- **Summary:** Addressed to Armenian Catholics in communion with Rome on 6 January 1873, *Quartus Supra* responds to a schism that had erupted among Armenians in Constantinople circa 1870: a faction elected a false patriarch, seized churches by force, expelled the lawful patriarch Anthony Peter IX, and accused the Holy See of overstepping its authority with the Constitution *Reversurus* (1867). Pius IX systematically refutes each objection — that the schismatics are genuine Catholics, that their condemnation was unjust, that the dispute is merely disciplinary rather than doctrinal, and that the Pope's rules on episcopal elections and church-property alienation violate Ottoman imperial rights — marshalling patristic testimony, conciliar canons, and the precedents of his predecessors to demonstrate that communion with and submission to the See of Peter is the very definition of Catholic identity, in East as well as West.
- **Response to:** (1) The new Armenian schism originating in Constantinople about 1870, involving the election of a false patriarch and violent seizure of Catholic churches; (2) the neo-schismatics' public "declaration of faith" of 6 February 1870, which they used to claim Catholic status while evading full submission to Rome; (3) the influence of previously-condemned Jansenist and Pistoian teachings that had been quietly circulating in the East since the 1806 Synod of Carcapha; (4) accusations that the Constitution *Reversurus* (1867) and its rules on episcopal election and property alienation infringed Ottoman imperial rights; (5) the neo-schismatics' argument that an excommunication sentence they deemed unjust need not be obeyed (a position the document links to Jansenism and Wyclif); and (6) twenty-four years of stalled efforts to strengthen Catholic discipline in the East since the 1848 apostolic letter *In suprema*.
- **Prescriptive towards:** (1) Armenian Catholics wavering under pressure from the schismatics — addressed directly to hold firm, avoid those who "grow daily worse," and not admit to their gatherings anyone in communion with the faction; (2) the Ottoman imperial government — asked to restore the exiled patriarch and to recognise that allowing "the Catholic religion" while denying the Pope's jurisdictional primacy is self-contradictory; (3) future episcopal elections in the Armenian patriarchate — the procedure of synodal recommendation plus papal confirmation is affirmed as the proper ongoing norm; (4) the wider Church — the document positions the Armenian case as an illustration of the universal principle that no national or popular law can override Church governance.
- **Tension:** (1) *Universal Church authority vs. Eastern particular-church autonomy* — the neo-schismatics insist their churches have customs and canons that Rome cannot override; Pius IX counters that even patriarchal prerogatives derive from and depend on Petrine authority. (2) *Ecclesiastical governance vs. civil/Ottoman power* — the document explicitly demarcates "the things of Caesar" from "the things of God," arguing that episcopal elections and church-property management are ecclesiastical by nature and beyond imperial jurisdiction, while simultaneously asking the Ottoman emperor for practical help. (3) *The name "Catholic" as contested ground* — both sides claim it; the document argues the label is meaningless without full submission to the Apostolic See, calling the schismatics' self-identification a "chief deceit." (4) *Discipline as separable from doctrine vs. inseparable from it* — the schismatics argue the dispute is only about discipline; Pius IX insists "discipline is often closely related to doctrine and has a great influence in preserving its purity," so resistance to disciplinary authority is already heresy.

## Unstructured

**Flow of the argument**

The letter opens by recalling the Pope's 1848 letter to Eastern Catholics and lamenting that, instead of the hoped-for reunion, a "new disaster" has arrived — the Armenian schism in Constantinople. This framing positions the document as a grief-laden but firm intervention, not an aggressive opening move.

The argument then moves in concentric rings, each addressing a different layer of the schismatics' position:

1. *Who they are* (§§2–5): The schism's origins and concrete actions are narrated — the false patriarch, the seized churches, the exile of the lawful shepherd. St. Cyprian is invoked on how the devil disguises schism as light.

2. *Their claim to the Catholic name* (§§6–11): The neo-schismatics' published "declaration of faith" is exposed as deceptive because it professes only what they choose to profess. True Catholic identity requires satisfying the Apostolic See, not selective confession. This section draws on a dense chain of patristic citations (Irenaeus, Cyprian, Optatus, Maximus the Confessor, the Council of Constantinople 536, the Eighth Ecumenical Council) to show that Eastern bishops themselves historically acknowledged Roman supremacy.

3. *Their claim that it is only a dispute about discipline* (§12): Pius IX appeals to his own Constitution *Reversurus* and to Pius VI's teaching that "discipline is often closely related to doctrine." Resistance to legitimate prelates is schism by definition, regardless of doctrinal posture.

4. *Their accusation that Rome exceeded its power* (§13–15): The document concedes the First Vatican Council's definition of papal primacy is recent but insists the underlying teaching is ancient; Chalcedon and its aftermath are cited at length.

5. *The specific grievances: episcopal elections* (§§24–32): The Pope explains the *Reversurus* procedure — synodal recommendation of three names, papal confirmation — point by point, refuting the charge that it strips the laity of rights (the laity never had the right to elect bishops), and documenting that in practice he has exercised restraint, never yet appointing outside the recommended list.

6. *The specific grievances: church property* (§§43–44): The alienation-prohibition is shown to be an ancient canonical obligation already sworn to by Eastern patriarchs on their return to union, not a novel power-grab.

7. *The accusation of infringing imperial rights* (§§37–41): The "render unto Caesar" principle is applied: civil power governs civil affairs; ecclesiastical affairs — including episcopal appointments and church property — are not civil affairs. The interference of secular rulers in Church governance is traced to Arianism.

8. *Exhortation to the faithful* (§§50–51): The letter closes pastorally, urging the Armenian Catholics to stand firm as their ancestors did under persecution, to preserve "the unity of faith in the bond of peace."

**Tone**

The document is predominantly authoritative and juridical — long sections read as formal legal rebuttals, with numbered propositions and precedents. Yet the tone modulates: the opening and closing are genuinely pastoral ("We must rather grieve and lament"), the Armenian audience is addressed as "venerable brothers and beloved children," and the Pope explicitly says he has "long and fervently besought God" for reconciliation and would receive returning schismatics "with love and full charity." The mix is characteristic: the authority of a supreme judge, the affect of a distressed father.

**Notable framing**

- The document consistently maps the Armenian schismatics onto ancient heretical types: Acacians, Jansenists, Arians, even Wyclif. This is not presented as polemic for its own sake but as a pattern-recognition argument — the same errors recur, the Church has condemned them before, and the condemnation stands.
- The appeal to the Ottoman emperor is notable in its political delicacy: Pius IX thanks him for past protections of Catholics, asks for practical restoration of the lawful patriarch, and simultaneously insists the Emperor has no authority over Catholic ecclesiastical governance. The document threads this needle by distinguishing civil prefectship (which the Emperor can grant or withdraw) from episcopal jurisdiction (which he cannot touch).
- The text draws an implicit connection between the Armenian schism and larger Eastern separatism: the Synod of Carcapha (1806), Jansenism, and Pistoia are all cited as upstream causes, suggesting the current crisis is the eruption of long-suppressed currents rather than a sudden break.
- The document ends by quoting St. Cyprian on how testing "exercises your hearts and minds" so that "the unimpaired faith of the approved way may shine with clear light" — framing the schism not as a catastrophe but as a providential trial.
